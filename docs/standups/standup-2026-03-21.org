## What the Bifibration Structure Is Telling You

The fact that your architecture is a **bifibration** (not just a fibration) is the most important structural observation here. Let me unpack why.

A fibration gives you cartesian liftings — the ability to *pull back* information from higher strata to lower strata. "Stratum n+1 needs something from stratum n; here's the canonical way to get it."

An opfibration gives you opcartesian liftings — the ability to *push forward* information from lower strata to higher strata. "Stratum n has completed; here's the canonical way to deliver its results to stratum n+1."

A **bifibration** gives you *both simultaneously*. And this bidirectionality is not just a convenience — it has deep structural consequences.

### The Adjunction at Every Transition

In a bifibration, for every morphism f : n → n+1 in the base (a stratum transition), the pullback functor f* : E_{n+1} → E_n and the pushforward functor f_! : E_n → E_{n+1} form an **adjunction**: f_! ⊣ f*. The pushforward is left adjoint to the pullback.

This means that pushing results forward and pulling queries backward are not independent operations — they're *dual*. The pushforward sends information "as freely as possible" (left adjoint = free construction), while the pullback retrieves information "as conservatively as possible" (right adjoint = forgetful/restriction). The adjunction ensures they're compatible: anything you push forward and then pull back gives you at least what you started with (the unit of the adjunction), and anything you pull back and then push forward gives you at most what you'd get directly (the counit).

For your system, this means: the non-monotonic transition between strata has a precise algebraic characterization as an adjunction. The negation-as-failure step, the accumulation step, the assumption revision step — each is an adjoint pair, not just "some function between lattices." This constrains the space of valid transitions and gives you tools for proving properties of the transitions.

### The Quantale Enrichment

The enrichment of each fiber in a quantale is capturing something subtle. In an ordinary category, morphisms between objects either exist or don't — it's a binary relationship. In an enriched category, the "hom-object" between two objects is not a set but an element of the enriching structure. Here, the hom-object between two cells is a *quantale element* — it carries information about the *strength* or *quality* of the connection, not just its existence.

Concretely: the morphism (propagator) from cell A to cell B isn't just "there exists a propagator connecting them." It's "there exists a propagator connecting them, and the quantale element describing it tells you what lattice transformation it performs, what effects it may have, what resources it consumes." The enrichment makes the *computational content* of propagators visible to the categorical structure.

This is why the quantale enrichment is the right choice rather than, say, enrichment in plain lattices. The quantale's tensor (⊗) captures sequential composition of propagators — "first apply this propagator, then that one" — while the lattice join (⊔) captures parallel composition — "apply both propagators and combine their contributions." Ordinary lattice enrichment would give you only the parallel story. Quantale enrichment gives you both.

## What This Means for a Computational Substrate

Here's where I want to push beyond description into prescription. If your architecture *is* a bifibration enriched in a quantale, then you can systematically derive capabilities from the categorical structure.

### Kan Extensions: Computing What You Don't Have

Every adjunction gives rise to **Kan extensions** — the categorical notion of "best approximation." Given the adjunction f_! ⊣ f* at each stratum transition, you automatically get:

**Left Kan extension** along the pushforward: given partial information at stratum n, compute the *best possible* information at stratum n+1 without waiting for stratum n to complete. This is **speculative computation** — stratum n+1 starts working with the best current approximation of stratum n's results, refining as more information arrives.

**Right Kan extension** along the pullback: given a requirement at stratum n+1, compute the *minimal sufficient* computation at stratum n to satisfy it. This is **demand-driven computation** — stratum n only computes what stratum n+1 actually needs, not everything it could.

You may already be doing both of these informally. The categorical structure tells you they're not just heuristics — they're the *unique best* strategies, determined by the universal properties of the adjunction.

For Prologos, this means:
- Speculative evaluation of higher strata before lower strata complete (with guaranteed soundness — the left Kan extension is the best approximation)
- Lazy evaluation of lower strata based on demand from higher strata (with guaranteed minimality — the right Kan extension computes only what's needed)
- Both strategies are compatible (because they come from the same adjunction)

### Ends and Coends: Global Invariants from Local Structure

In an enriched category, **ends** and **coends** are the enriched analogues of limits and colimits. For your quantale-enriched fibers:

The **end** ∫_c Hom(c, c) over all cells c in a fiber gives you the **global invariant** of the propagator network — the "best simultaneous constraint" that all cells satisfy. This is essentially the fixpoint of the network, characterized categorically.

The **coend** ∫^c Hom(c, c) gives you the **trace** of the network — the total "computational work" performed across all cells. In the quantale, this is the tensor product of all propagator contributions, which characterizes the total effect of running the network.

These aren't just abstract nonsense. The end tells you "what the network knows" (the fixpoint). The coend tells you "what the network did" (the effect trace). Both are computable from the enriched category structure, and both are natural targets for optimization.

### The Grothendieck Construction as Architecture Template

Your bifibration is (by the Grothendieck construction) equivalent to a **pseudofunctor** from the stratification poset to the 2-category of quantale-enriched categories. This means your entire system can be specified as:

1. For each stratum n: a quantale-enriched category E_n (the fiber — a propagator network)
2. For each transition n → n+1: a pair of adjoint functors (f_!, f*) between E_n and E_{n+1}
3. Coherence conditions: the adjunctions compose correctly across multiple transitions

This is a **modular specification format**. To add a new stratum or a new kind of non-monotonic transition, you specify the fiber and the adjoint pair, and the Grothendieck construction assembles the total system. You don't need to reason about the global architecture — the categorical machinery handles the assembly.

For your implementation, this suggests a design pattern. Each subsystem (logic engine, type checker, session checker, effect tracker) is specified as a fiber. Each inter-subsystem connection is specified as an adjoint pair. A generic "bifibration assembler" takes these specifications and constructs the unified propagator network. Adding a new subsystem means providing a new fiber and its adjunctions — the rest is automatic.

## The Deeper Implication: A Computational Substrate Based on Bifibrations

If you take the categorical structure seriously as a *design principle* rather than just a *description*, you get something more ambitious than Prologos as currently conceived.

### Computation as Sections of a Bifibration

A **section** of a fibration p : E → B is a functor s : B → E such that p ∘ s = id_B. In other words, it's a choice of object in each fiber, varying coherently across the base. For your system, a section is a **complete computation** — an assignment of cell values at every stratum that respects all the transitions.

Running a Prologos program means *finding a section of the bifibration*. The propagator network at each stratum searches for the fiber component of the section (the fixpoint). The stratum transitions connect the components. The whole computation is a section.

This reframing is powerful because sections of fibrations have well-studied properties:

- **Existence**: does a section exist? (Is the program solvable?)
- **Uniqueness**: is the section unique? (Is the program deterministic?)
- **Obstructions**: what prevents a section from existing? (What causes failure? — these are cohomological obstructions, and they connect to your error reporting)

### Obstructions as Error Theory

This is speculative but potentially very fruitful. In algebraic topology, the obstruction to extending a section from one level to the next lives in a **cohomology group** of the base with coefficients in the fiber. If a section exists up to stratum n but can't be extended to stratum n+1, the obstruction is an element of H^{n+1}(B, π_n(fiber)).

For your system, this would mean: the reason a program fails at stratum n+1 is characterized by a cohomological invariant that lives in a specific algebraic structure determined by the fiber (the propagator network at that stratum) and the base (the stratification). This invariant would give you not just "the program failed" but "the program failed because of *this specific structural reason*," classified algebraically.

I don't know if this is practically useful yet. But the fact that your architecture has the right categorical structure to even *ask* the question is remarkable.

### Transport and Parallel Transport

In differential geometry, a fibration (fiber bundle) comes with a **connection** — a rule for parallel transporting elements of the fiber along paths in the base. The connection tells you how to "move" a computation from one control state to another while preserving some invariant.

Your cartesian and opcartesian liftings *are* the connection. They define parallel transport of information along stratum transitions. The curvature of this connection (the failure of parallel transport to be path-independent) would measure the **non-commutativity of your non-monotonic transitions** — does it matter in what order you perform negation, accumulation, and assumption revision?

If the curvature is zero (flat connection), the order doesn't matter and you have more freedom in scheduling. If the curvature is nonzero, the order matters and you need to respect it. The categorical structure gives you a precise way to detect and characterize this.

### Enriched Bifibrations as a Programming Model

The most ambitious possibility: rather than using the bifibration structure as an implementation technique *for Prologos*, expose it as a **programming model *in Prologos***.

A Prologos programmer would be able to:

- Define custom fibers (domain-specific propagator networks with custom lattices)
- Define custom base categories (domain-specific control structures)
- Define adjoint pairs between fibers (domain-specific non-monotonic transitions)
- Compose these into bifibrations (domain-specific layered computation)
- Run computations by finding sections (domain-specific problem solving)

This would make Prologos not just a language with propagators and dependent types and session types, but a **language for defining computational substrates** — a meta-language for specifying and running layered monotonic-over-non-monotonic computations in arbitrary domains.

The quantale enrichment means the programmer can specify not just *what* computations are possible but *what they cost*, *what effects they have*, *what resources they consume* — all within the same framework.

## On Kan Extensions and the Practical Challenge

Let me be more concrete about what left and right Kan extensions would actually mean in your architecture, and why your concern about "taking turns" is well-founded but possibly addressable.

### The CALM Theorem Constraint

You're grounded in CALM (Consistency As Logical Monotonicity): monotonic computations can be parallelized without coordination; non-monotonic operations require coordination (synchronization barriers). Your threshold propagators at stratum boundaries are exactly the coordination points CALM demands.

The worry about speculative evaluation is: if stratum n+1 starts working before stratum n has quiesced, stratum n+1 might be operating on incomplete information. When stratum n eventually completes and delivers its full fixpoint, stratum n+1 might need to retract work done on the incomplete information. That retraction is non-monotonic — it violates the within-stratum monotonicity guarantee.

This is a real concern. But there's a way through it that's consistent with your existing architecture.

### Speculative Evaluation via Your ATMS

You already have the mechanism. Your ATMS maintains parallel worldviews. A speculative evaluation of stratum n+1 is just **another worldview** — one that assumes stratum n's current (incomplete) state is the final state.

When stratum n actually completes, one of two things happens. Either the assumption was correct (stratum n's final fixpoint matches what stratum n+1 was working with), in which case the speculative worldview is validated and its results become real. Or the assumption was wrong, in which case the speculative worldview is pruned — a nogood — and stratum n+1 restarts with the correct fixpoint.

The key insight: **the speculation is not a violation of monotonicity within stratum n+1.** It's a new assumption in the ATMS. The ATMS is already your mechanism for managing hypothetical reasoning. Speculative evaluation is just one more hypothesis: "stratum n is done and its result is X."

```markdown
ATMS Worldview A: "stratum n is complete with fixpoint F_partial"
  → stratum n+1 propagates under this assumption
  → reaches fixpoint F_{n+1}^A

ATMS Worldview B: (ground truth, waiting for stratum n to actually complete)
  → stratum n completes with actual fixpoint F_actual

If F_partial = F_actual:
  → Worldview A is validated, F_{n+1}^A is the real result
  → No wasted work

If F_partial ≠ F_actual:
  → Worldview A is pruned (nogood)
  → Stratum n+1 restarts with F_actual
  → Work on Worldview A is discarded (O(1) with CHAMPs — just drop the reference)
```

The CHAMPs make this cheap. The speculative worldview is a persistent data structure sharing almost everything with the ground truth. Discarding it is free. The ATMS already tracks assumption dependencies. The only new thing is treating "stratum n is complete" as an ATMS assumption rather than a hard synchronization barrier.

### Demand-Driven Evaluation via Threshold Propagators

The right Kan extension (demand-driven evaluation) is actually closer to what you already do. When stratum n+1 needs a specific value from stratum n, it installs a threshold propagator that fires when that value is available. This is a **pull** — stratum n+1 is saying "I need at least this much information from stratum n."

The right Kan extension refines this: instead of waiting for stratum n's *complete* fixpoint, stratum n+1 computes *what it actually needs* from stratum n and installs targeted threshold propagators for exactly that. Stratum n might not need to reach full fixpoint — it only needs to stabilize the specific cells that stratum n+1 depends on.

This is a partial relaxation of the synchronization barrier. Instead of "wait for stratum n to fully quiesce," it's "wait for stratum n to stabilize the cells I care about." The CALM theorem still applies — you're still synchronizing on the non-monotonic boundary — but the synchronization is finer-grained.

### The Combined Pattern

```markdown
Stratum n running (not yet complete)
    │
    ├── Stratum n+1 installs targeted thresholds (right Kan: demand-driven)
    │   → fires as soon as needed cells stabilize
    │   → stratum n+1 begins partial computation
    │
    ├── Stratum n+1 speculatively assumes current state is final (left Kan: speculative)
    │   → tracked as ATMS assumption
    │   → speculative results available immediately
    │
    └── Stratum n actually completes
        → targeted thresholds confirmed (demand-driven results are sound)
        → speculative assumption validated or pruned
        → stratum n+1 either keeps speculative work or restarts
```

This doesn't violate "taking turns." The strata still have a logical ordering. The monotonic guarantee within each stratum is preserved. The ATMS manages the speculative assumptions. The threshold propagators manage the demand-driven dependencies. What changes is that the turns *overlap* — stratum n+1 can start working (speculatively or on-demand) before stratum n has finished, with well-defined semantics for what happens when stratum n's final answer arrives.

Whether this is worth implementing depends on your workloads. If strata are typically fast and the pipeline is short, the overhead of managing speculative worldviews might exceed the savings from parallelism. If strata are expensive and there's significant pipeline depth, the overlap could be substantial. Benchmark before committing.

## On Gödel Completeness and Obstructions

Your approach to completeness — prove decidability or provide a fuel-bound escape — is exactly right. The fuel-bound for infinite negation at runtime is the pragmatic answer to the undecidability of the halting problem applied to your specific context.

The connection to obstructions: when the fuel bound is hit, you have an **obstruction to computing a section of the fibration.** The program tried to extend its computation from stratum n to stratum n+1 (via negation), but the computation at stratum n didn't terminate. The obstruction is the non-terminating fixpoint computation at stratum n.

In your architecture, this obstruction is observable as a specific cell (or set of cells) that never stabilized — the propagators kept firing but the lattice values kept changing (or the negation kept cycling). The derivation tree for the obstruction would show: "cell X depends on ¬Y, Y depends on ¬X, neither stabilized within the fuel bound." This is already good error reporting. The categorical framing just gives it a name (obstruction to section extension) and a classification (the type of the obstruction corresponds to the type of non-termination — cyclic negation vs. infinite domain vs. divergent computation).

The fact that you can guarantee completeness structurally for most of your system — only infinite runtime negation escapes the structural guarantee — is strong evidence that the lattice-based architecture is doing real work. In a traditional Prolog system, non-termination lurks everywhere (any recursive predicate can diverge). In your system, the lattice structure (finite height, ascending chain condition) guarantees termination within each monotonic layer, and the stratification structure guarantees termination across layers — except at the single point where the runtime can introduce unbounded negation chains.

## Kan Extensions and the BSP Engine

Since you're specifically thinking about this, let me make it concrete for your BSP design.

### What Left Kan Extension Means for BSP

In your BSP engine, computation proceeds in supersteps: all propagators fire in parallel (computation phase), then cells synchronize (communication phase), then a barrier checks for quiescence. The ATMS manages worldviews on the second layer.

A left Kan extension along the pushforward from stratum n to stratum n+1 would mean: given the *current* (incomplete) state of stratum n at the end of a superstep, compute the best approximation of what stratum n+1 would receive if stratum n were already complete.

In BSP terms, this is: **at each barrier, speculatively initialize stratum n+1's cells with the current partial fixpoint of stratum n, tagged as an ATMS assumption.** Stratum n+1's propagators can begin firing in the *next* superstep, in parallel with stratum n's continued convergence.

The key property of a left Kan extension is that it's the *best* (most informative) approximation that's consistent with what's known. In your lattice setting, this means: take the current cell values at stratum n (which are below or equal to the eventual fixpoint, since propagation is monotonic) and push them forward. Everything stratum n+1 derives from this partial information is *sound* — it's a lower bound on what it would derive from the complete fixpoint. When stratum n actually completes, stratum n+1 either confirms its speculative work or needs to join in additional information (but never retract, because the speculation was a lower bound).

This is the crucial point: **if the pushforward is monotone (which it is, because your inter-strata transitions preserve lattice ordering), then speculative results from a partial fixpoint are always below the results from the complete fixpoint.** Stratum n+1 never needs to retract speculative work — it only needs to *continue* propagating when more information arrives from stratum n's completion.

This means you don't need the ATMS for the speculation. The monotonicity of the pushforward guarantees that the speculative results are sound. You just need to detect when stratum n's completion delivers new information beyond what was already speculatively forwarded, and trigger additional propagation in stratum n+1.

For BSP specifically:

```markdown
Superstep k:
  Stratum n: propagators fire (approaching fixpoint)
  Stratum n+1: propagators fire on speculatively forwarded values

Barrier k:
  Stratum n: synchronize cells
  Stratum n+1: synchronize cells
  Check: did stratum n's cells change since last forward to n+1?
    If yes: forward new values to n+1 (monotone join into n+1's cells)
    If no: stratum n has quiesced; speculative work is confirmed

Superstep k+1:
  Stratum n: (if not yet quiesced) propagators fire
  Stratum n+1: propagators fire (on newly forwarded values, if any)
```

This gives you **pipelined strata** within the BSP framework. Each barrier does double duty: synchronizing within-stratum cells *and* forwarding between-stratum approximations. The cost is minimal — one additional check and potential join per barrier per stratum boundary. The benefit is that stratum n+1 starts converging before stratum n finishes, and all the speculative work is guaranteed sound.

### What Right Kan Extension Means for BSP

The right Kan extension is dual: given a *requirement* at stratum n+1, compute the minimal information needed from stratum n.

In BSP terms: stratum n+1 registers **threshold interests** in specific cells of stratum n. Instead of forwarding all of stratum n's state at each barrier, you only forward cells that stratum n+1 has expressed interest in. Stratum n's propagators might not need to converge fully — they only need to stabilize the cells that stratum n+1 cares about.

This is demand-driven evaluation within BSP:

```markdown
Setup:
  Stratum n+1 analyzes its propagators to determine which stratum-n cells
  they actually read. Registers threshold interests on those cells.

Barrier k:
  For each threshold interest:
    If the stratum-n cell has stabilized (value unchanged since last barrier):
      Forward to stratum n+1 (the demand is satisfied)
    Else:
      Continue (stratum n hasn't stabilized this cell yet)

  Stratum n+1 fires propagators only for the cells whose demands are newly
  satisfied.
```

The right Kan extension guarantees **minimality** — stratum n+1 gets exactly the information it needs, no more. This reduces communication volume at barriers (only forwarding demanded cells) and potentially allows stratum n to quiesce earlier (it can stop when all demanded cells are stable, even if other cells are still changing).

### Combining Them in BSP

The left and right Kan extensions are adjoint, which means they can work together:

- Right Kan identifies *which* cells to forward (demand-driven)
- Left Kan determines *when* to forward them (as early as possible, speculatively)
- The adjunction guarantees compatibility: speculative forwarding of demanded cells gives the same result as waiting for completion and then extracting what's needed

For your BSP engine, this translates to:

```markdown
Superstep structure:
  1. Propagators fire in parallel (all strata, all worldviews)
  2. Cells synchronize (within-stratum communication)
  3. Barrier check:
     a. For each stratum boundary:
        - Forward demanded cells that have changed (left Kan + right Kan)
     b. For each stratum:
        - Has it quiesced? (all cells stable)
     c. Global:
        - Have all strata quiesced? (termination)
  4. ATMS layer:
     - Prune inconsistent worldviews
     - Propagate nogoods
```

This is still BSP — the barrier is still there, strata still take turns in the sense that information flows through barriers. But the pipelining means strata overlap in time, and the demand-driven filtering means barriers are cheaper.

## Structures Worth Exploring

Let me suggest categorical structures based on where your implementation actually is, not where it could theoretically be.

### Traced Monoidal Categories (You Already Have One)

Your document identifies the session type subsystem as a traced monoidal category. This is worth developing further because traced monoidal categories have a deep connection to **feedback and fixpoints** that's directly relevant to your propagator architecture.

The trace operation in a monoidal category captures feedback: given a morphism f : A ⊗ U → B ⊗ U, the trace Tr(f) : A → B "loops U back," connecting the U output to the U input. This is exactly what your duality propagators do — they connect dual session endpoints, creating a feedback loop.

The connection to propagators: a propagator network with feedback (cycles) is a traced monoidal category. The trace is the fixpoint computation. The monoidal product is parallel composition of independent subnetworks. This gives you a categorical framework for reasoning about *which* propagator network cycles are well-founded (the trace exists) and which diverge (the trace doesn't exist).

For the BSP engine specifically: the Int construction (Joyal, Street, Verity) builds a *compact closed category* from a traced monoidal category. Compact closed categories have a notion of *duality* that's richer than what you currently use for session types. Every object has a dual, and morphisms from A to B correspond to morphisms from I to A* ⊗ B (where A* is the dual of A). This might give you a cleaner treatment of bidirectional session types — instead of special-casing duality propagators, duality becomes a structural feature of the category.

### Polynomial Functors

This is the structure I think could be most immediately useful for your next engineering phase.

A polynomial functor is a functor of the form P(y) = Σ_{i ∈ I} y^{B_i}, where I is a set of "positions" and B_i is a set of "directions" at each position. Polynomial functors form a category (with natural transformations as morphisms) that has remarkable closure properties — it's closed under composition, has a monoidal structure, and supports a notion of "wiring diagram" that directly models your propagator network architecture.

The connection: **a propagator network is a morphism of polynomial functors.** Each cell is a position. Each propagator is a direction at a position (it reads from some cells and writes to one). The network topology is a polynomial functor map. Composing networks (connecting the output cells of one to the input cells of another) is composition of polynomial functors.

David Spivak's work on polynomial functors and wiring diagrams provides:

- A formal language for specifying network topologies
- Composition operations that are guaranteed to produce well-formed networks
- A type system for networks (the polynomial functor's type signature tells you what interfaces a network exposes)
- A notion of "network morphism" that captures refinement (replacing a subnetwork with a more detailed implementation)

For Prologos, polynomial functors could give you the **module system for propagator networks** — each module exposes a polynomial functor interface (these are my input cells, these are my output cells, these are the types), and composition of modules is composition of polynomial functors. The Grothendieck construction you identified as validating falls out as a special case of polynomial functor composition.

### Operads and Multicategories

Your propagator networks have a feature that ordinary categories don't capture well: propagators have **multiple inputs**. A propagator that reads from three cells and writes to one is not a morphism in the usual sense (which goes from one object to one object) — it's a *multimorphism*, going from three objects to one.

The right categorical structure for this is an **operad** or **multicategory**. Objects are cell types (lattices). Multimorphisms are propagators. Composition is network assembly — plugging the output of one propagator into an input of another.

The operad structure gives you:

- A clean account of propagator arity (how many inputs, how many outputs)
- Associativity of composition (plugging networks together in different orders gives the same result)
- A substitution principle (replacing a cell with a subnetwork that computes that cell's value)

The colored operad (also called symmetric multicategory) is the most general version: different cell types (different lattices) are different colors, and propagators are typed by their input and output colors. This captures your heterogeneous networks where type cells, session cells, and multiplicity cells all coexist with propagators that cross between domains.

### Sheaves on Your Network Topology

Your CHAMPs give you persistent, versioned cell values. A cell's history — its sequence of values across propagation steps — is a **presheaf** on the poset of time steps (or more precisely, on the poset of network versions). The entire network's history is a **sheaf** on the network topology: local consistency (each cell's history is a valid lattice ascending chain) glues together to give global consistency (the network's evolution is a valid propagation trace).

Sheaf theory could give you:

- **Cohomological invariants** of your network's computation. The zeroth cohomology H⁰ is the global sections — the fixpoints of the network. Higher cohomology measures obstructions to extending local solutions to global ones. This connects to your error reporting: a type error that manifests as a local inconsistency (one cell reaches ⊤) but whose cause is global (an interaction between distant parts of the program) would show up as a nonzero first cohomology class.

- **Descent theory** for distributed computation. If you eventually distribute your propagator network across machines, sheaf descent tells you when local computations on each machine can be assembled into a global computation. The descent condition is: the local fixpoints agree on shared cells. This is exactly the consistency condition you'd need for distributed propagators.

- **Cosheaves for information flow.** While sheaves track "what's known" (restriction from global to local), cosheaves track "what's communicated" (extension from local to global). Your Galois connections between subsystems might be naturally understood as cosheaf maps — they extend local information (within one subsystem) to global information (across subsystems) in a way that's compatible with the network topology.

### Profunctors

A profunctor from category C to category D is a functor C^op × D → Set. Profunctors generalize functors and relations, and they compose via a coend formula that resembles matrix multiplication.

For your system, profunctors could model the **interfaces between subsystems**. The Galois connection between the session type subsystem and the QTT subsystem, when you eventually implement it as live propagators, would be a profunctor: for each session type s and each QTT usage context u, the profunctor gives you the set of valid bridges between them.

Profunctor composition would then give you: if you have a bridge from sessions to QTT and a bridge from QTT to type inference, the composite profunctor gives you the induced bridge from sessions to type inference. This is the transitive closure of your Galois connection network, computed categorically.

The advantage over computing bridges ad hoc: profunctor composition has a universal property (it's the coend), which means the composite bridge is *the best possible* — it loses no information that could be preserved by any chain of bridges. This is a strong optimality guarantee for your inter-subsystem communication.

## One More Thing

Your document's methodology section — "a system inhabits a categorical structure when the objects and morphisms correspond to implemented data structures and transitions, the functorial laws are enforced by the implementation, and the additional structure is load-bearing" — is a contribution in its own right. I've never seen this criterion stated this clearly in the applied category theory literature. Most papers in the area are happy to note that a categorical structure is "compatible with" or "can model" a system, without asking whether the structure is actually load-bearing.

You should keep applying this criterion. The gap between "this could be modeled as X" and "this *is* X because removing X would break the system" is the gap between category theory as metaphor and category theory as engineering. You're on the right side of that gap.
