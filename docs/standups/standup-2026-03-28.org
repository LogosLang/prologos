Module theory is a rich area that spans abstract algebra, lattice theory, and computer science. Let me build it up carefully, because the connections to your work are deeper than they might appear at first glance.

## What Module Theory Is

### The Algebraic Foundation

A module is a generalization of a vector space. Where a vector space has scalars from a field (like the real numbers), a module has scalars from a ring (which might not have division).

Formally, a left R-module M over a ring R is an abelian group (M, +) equipped with a scalar action R × M → M satisfying:

- r · (m₁ + m₂) = r · m₁ + r · m₂ (scalar distributes over module addition)
- (r₁ + r₂) · m = r₁ · m + r₂ · m (module addition distributes over scalar addition)
- (r₁ · r₂) · m = r₁ · (r₂ · m) (scalar action is associative)
- 1 · m = m (unit scalar acts as identity)

The ring R acts on the module M by "scaling" its elements. The module elements can be added together. The interaction between scaling and addition is governed by distributivity.

### Why This Matters Beyond Algebra

The module concept abstracts a pattern that appears everywhere: a structured set of things (the module elements) acted on by an algebra of transformations (the ring). The transformations compose (ring multiplication), they can be added (ring addition), and they interact with the module's own structure (distributivity).

Examples that are secretly modules:

**Abelian groups** are Z-modules (modules over the integers). Scaling by n ∈ Z means adding an element to itself n times.

**Vector spaces** are F-modules (modules over a field). This is the classical case.

**Ideals in a ring** are modules over the ring itself. The ideal I ⊆ R is an R-module under the ring's own multiplication.

**Representations of a group** are modules over the group algebra. A group G acting on a vector space V is the same as a k[G]-module structure on V.

## Module Theory on Lattices

### Lattice-Ordered Modules

A lattice-ordered module (l-module) is a module M that is also a lattice, where the module operations are compatible with the lattice ordering. Specifically:

- M is a lattice under some partial order ≤
- Addition is order-preserving: if m₁ ≤ m₂ then m₁ + n ≤ m₂ + n
- Scalar action by positive elements is order-preserving: if r ≥ 0 and m₁ ≤ m₂ then r · m₁ ≤ r · m₂

This is where the connection to your work begins. Your propagator network cells hold lattice values. If those lattice values form a module over some ring of transformations, then the transformations (propagators) have algebraic structure that constrains how they can interact.

### Quantale Modules

A quantale module is a complete lattice M equipped with an action of a quantale Q on M:

Q ⊗ M → M

satisfying:

- q ⊗ (⊔ S) = ⊔ {q ⊗ s | s ∈ S} (action distributes over arbitrary joins)
- (q₁ ⊗ q₂) ⊗ m = q₁ ⊗ (q₂ ⊗ m) (action is associative)
- e ⊗ m = m (unit of quantale acts as identity)

You already have quantales in your architecture (effect ordering, session types). The quantale module structure says: your cell values (in lattice M) are acted on by your effect/session algebra (quantale Q), and this action is well-behaved (distributes over joins, associates).

This is directly relevant. When a session type step "transforms" a cell value — sending a message changes the session state, which constrains what types are valid — that transformation is a quantale action on the type lattice. The type lattice is a module over the session quantale.

### Residuated Lattices and Their Modules

A residuated lattice is a lattice equipped with a multiplication (·) and two division operations (\ and /) satisfying:

a · b ≤ c if and only if a ≤ c / b if and only if b ≤ a \ c

The divisions (residuals) are the "best approximation" of undoing the multiplication. This is the algebraic structure underlying linear logic's multiplicative connectives, and it connects directly to your linear types and resource tracking.

A module over a residuated lattice inherits the residuation: the scalar action has a "best approximation of inverse," which means you can ask "what scalar would produce this result?" and get a well-defined (lattice-theoretic) answer.

For your system: if your propagators form a residuated lattice (which they do, if the propagator algebra has the right structure), then you can ask "what input would produce this output?" — which is backward reasoning, which is what your logic engine does. The residuation IS the logical inverse of forward propagation.

## Main Findings of Module Theory Relevant to You

### Finding 1: Submodule Lattice

The submodules of a module M form a lattice under inclusion. This is a fundamental structural result: the "parts" of a module are organized as a lattice.

### Finding 2: Decomposition Theorems

Module theory has powerful decomposition results. The most relevant:

**Direct sum decomposition**: M ≅ M₁ ⊕ M₂ ⊕ ... ⊕ Mₖ, where the Mᵢ are "simple" (indecomposable) modules. This says: every module can be broken into irreducible pieces.

For your system: every propagator network can be decomposed into irreducible subnetworks that interact only through their boundaries. The decomposition is the natural modularization of the network. Your subsystems (type inference, session checking, effect tracking) are the irreducible components. The Galois bridges are the boundary interactions.

**Krull-Schmidt theorem**: for modules with both chain conditions (ascending and descending), the direct sum decomposition is unique up to isomorphism and reordering. This means the decomposition into irreducible pieces is canonical — there's one right way to decompose.

For your system: if your propagator networks satisfy both chain conditions (which they do, if your lattices have finite height and your networks are finite), then the decomposition into subsystems is canonical. This supports your intuition that the architecture you've found is "natural" — it's the unique decomposition of the network into irreducible components.

### Finding 3: Morita Equivalence

Two rings R and S are Morita equivalent if their module categories are equivalent: R-Mod ≅ S-Mod. Morita equivalent rings have "the same module theory" — they produce the same structured collections of things, even if the rings themselves look different.

For your system: two different lattice substrates might be Morita equivalent — they produce the same structured propagator networks, even if the lattices themselves have different elements. This means you could swap out one lattice for another (perhaps simpler, or more efficient) without changing the network's behavior, as long as the lattices are Morita equivalent.

This is relevant to your optimization story. If you find that a complex lattice (used for precision) is Morita equivalent to a simpler one (used for efficiency), you can use the simpler one without loss of expressiveness. The Morita equivalence guarantees that everything you could compute with the complex lattice, you can also compute with the simple one.

### Finding 4: Tensor Products of Modules

The tensor product M ⊗_R N of two R-modules is the "universal bilinear construction." It captures "all the ways M and N can interact through R."

For your system: the tensor product of two propagator networks (over a shared lattice substrate) is the network that captures all the ways the two networks can interact. If your subsystems interact through a shared lattice (which they do — the type lattice is shared between type inference and session checking), the tensor product describes the full interaction space.

The tensor product is also how you'd formalize your cross-domain bridges categorically. A Galois connection between lattice M and lattice N, mediated by ring R (the shared operations), is a morphism in the tensor product M ⊗_R N.

### Finding 5: Homological Algebra

Module theory is the foundation of homological algebra — the study of "obstructions" to extending partial solutions. The key constructions:

**Exact sequences**: 0 → A → B → C → 0 says "B is built from A and C, and the way they fit together is classified by an extension group Ext¹(C, A)."

For your system: the obstructions to extending a partial computation from one stratum to the next (your "error theory" from the fibration analysis) live in extension groups. The extension group tells you not just "can this extend?" but "in how many ways can it extend?" — which is relevant to ambiguous type inference (multiple valid extensions) and error reporting (the obstruction classifies the type of failure).

**Derived functors**: Ext and Tor are derived functors that measure "how far a functor is from being exact." In your system, a propagator that's "almost monotone" (monotone on most inputs, non-monotone on a few edge cases) would have nontrivial derived functors that measure the non-monotonicity.

- **Modules form a lattice.** The submodule lattice orders modules by "contains." Module A ≤ Module B if everything defined in A is also defined in B.

- **Cached loading is module memoization.** The .pnet file is a serialized module. Loading it is faster than recomputing it. ### Application 3: The Submodule Lattice as Network Organization

The submodule lattice of your propagator network gives you a formal tool for understanding and organizing the network's structure.

Each submodule is a self-contained subnetwork. The submodule lattice tells you:

- **Which subnetworks are independent** (their meet is trivial — they share no cells). Independent subnetworks can be computed in parallel.

### Application 4: Residuated Lattices for Bidirectional Reasoning

Your propagator networks are already bidirectional — information flows both ways through cells. The residuated lattice structure formalizes this.

A propagator f : A → B computes forward: given input a, produce output f(a). The residual f \ b computes backward: given desired output b, what input is needed? The residuation guarantees that these are consistent: a ≤ f \ b if and only if f(a) ≤ b.

For your type inference: if you know the result type of a function application and the function's type, the residual tells you the required argument type. This IS type inference — computing the argument type from the result type and the function type. The residuated lattice structure says this is algebraically well-defined and has a unique best answer (the residual is the greatest element satisfying the constraint).

For your logic engine: backward chaining (given a goal, find premises that prove it) is residuation. Forward chaining (given premises, derive conclusions) is the forward action. The residuated lattice structure unifies forward and backward reasoning in a single algebraic framework.

For your narrowing: narrowing an unknown (given a function application where the argument is unknown, enumerate constructors that make the function reduce) is a form of residuation. The residual of the reduction function at the target value gives you the set of arguments that produce that value.

### Application 5: Homological Module Theory for Error Reporting

Your error reporting could benefit from homological algebra. When type inference fails, the failure is an obstruction — something that prevents the construction of a consistent type assignment. Homological algebra classifies obstructions.

The simplest case: a short exact sequence 0 → A → B → C → 0 represents "B is an extension of C by A." If the sequence doesn't split (B ≠ A ⊕ C), then there's an obstruction to decomposing B. The obstruction lives in Ext¹(C, A).

For your type checker: when two type constraints can't be simultaneously satisfied, the failure is an obstruction to extending a partial type assignment. The Ext group classifies the failure — it tells you not just "these constraints are incompatible" but "the incompatibility is of this specific algebraic type." Different types of incompatibility correspond to different Ext elements, which could drive different error messages.

This is speculative but potentially powerful. If you could classify type errors by their homological type (Ext¹ for simple incompatibility, Ext² for incompatibility that arises from the interaction of three or more constraints, etc.), you'd have a principled error taxonomy that goes beyond ad hoc error message engineering.

## The Endomorphism Ring Decomposition for SRE

Your colleague's observation that the four SRE relations correspond to sub-rings of the endomorphism ring is algebraically precise and worth unpacking further, because it has implications for how you implement and extend the SRE.

The endomorphism ring End(M) of a module M consists of all structure-preserving maps from M to itself. For your type lattice M, these endomorphisms are the transformations that the SRE can apply. The ring operations are:

- **Multiplication** (composition): applying two transformations in sequence
- **Addition** (pointwise join): combining the effects of two transformations

The four relation types decompose this ring into components with distinct algebraic signatures:

**Equality endomorphisms** satisfy f(x) = x for all relevant x. They're the kernel of the "how much does this change things" measure. In your SRE, equality is structural unification — two cell-trees are merged by identifying corresponding cells. The endomorphism is the identity on the merged cells. This is the simplest algebraic component.

**Subtyping endomorphisms** satisfy f(x) ≤ f(y) whenever x ≤ y. They preserve the lattice ordering. In your SRE, subtyping propagates covariance and contravariance through type constructors. When you decompose `PVec Int <: PVec Nat`, the subtyping endomorphism on PVec *distributes* over the constructor — it becomes a subtyping endomorphism on the parameter. This distribution is the module-theoretic direct sum decomposition applied to the endomorphism.

The variance annotations on your type constructors (covariant, contravariant, invariant) are telling the SRE which sub-ring of endomorphisms to use for each parameter position. Covariant parameters use the order-preserving sub-ring. Contravariant parameters use the order-reversing sub-ring. Invariant parameters use the identity sub-ring (equality only). This is not an ad hoc design choice — it's the canonical decomposition of the endomorphism ring restricted to that constructor.

**Duality endomorphisms** satisfy f(x ⊔ y) = f(x) ⊓ f(y) — they swap joins and meets. They're antitone (order-reversing). In session types, duality swaps send and receive, swaps internal and external choice. The reason duality is the hardest SRE path is algebraically fundamental: antitone maps don't distribute over direct sums the same way monotone maps do. For a direct sum M = A ⊕ B, a monotone map decomposes as f = f_A ⊕ f_B (apply independently to each component). An antitone map may not decompose this way — it can *mix* the components, mapping elements of A into B and vice versa. This mixing is exactly what session duality does: the dual of a send (which lives in the "output" component) is a receive (which lives in the "input" component).

For your SRE implementation, this means duality decomposition requires tracking cross-component interactions that equality and subtyping decomposition don't need. The SRE's structural decomposition must handle not just "decompose each component independently" (which works for monotone relations) but "decompose with component swapping" (which is needed for antitone relations). This is a genuinely harder algorithmic problem, which validates your experience that duality is the hardest path.

**Rewriting endomorphisms** satisfy f(f(x)) = f(x) — they're idempotent. Applying the rewrite twice gives the same result as applying it once. In your system, this is normalization — reducing a term to normal form. Once the term is in normal form, reducing again doesn't change it. The idempotent sub-ring is the set of "projection-like" maps that collapse the lattice onto a sublattice of normal forms.

The idempotence condition is important for termination: if your rewriting endomorphisms are idempotent, the rewriting process converges in at most one step (per cell). If they're not idempotent (a rewrite can produce new redexes), you need iteration — and the lattice structure guarantees convergence only if the iteration is ascending (each rewrite moves the cell up in the lattice).

Each summand is a "primitive" relation type. If you find a transformation that doesn't fit into any of the four existing sub-rings, it must be a new summand — a new primitive relation type.

**Coercion**: a non-surjective, non-injective map. Neither order-preserving (like subtyping) nor order-reversing (like duality). ## The PPN Filtration and Associated Graded Module

Your colleague's observation that the PPN's four domains form a filtration of the parse module is structurally important. Let me develop it.

A **filtration** of a module M is a sequence of submodules:

M₀ ⊆ M₁ ⊆ ... ⊆ Mₙ = M

Each level includes everything from the previous level plus new structure. The **associated graded module** is:

gr(M) = ⊕ᵢ (Mᵢ / Mᵢ₋₁)

Each graded piece Mᵢ / Mᵢ₋₁ captures "what level i adds beyond level i-1." The graded module decomposes the total module into independent layers.

For PPN:

```
M₀ = Token lattice (characters → tokens)
M₁ = Surface lattice (tokens → bracketed structure + whitespace significance)
M₂ = Core lattice (surface → fully elaborated AST)
```

With the Demand lattice branching:

```
M_D = Demand lattice (what the type checker needs from parsing)
```

The graded pieces:

- gr₀ = M₀ = Token (lexing)
- gr₁ = M₁ / M₀ = structural grouping (what surface adds beyond tokens)
- gr₂ = M₂ / M₁ = type annotations + macro expansion (what core adds beyond surface)
- gr_D = M_D ∩ M₂ = elaboration requirements (what demand adds to core)

### Why the Filtration Matters for Collapsing Your Pipeline

Your current 14-stage pipeline is a sequence of transformations:

```
source → lex → parse → desugar → ... → core AST → elaborate → typed AST
```

Each stage is a function from one representation to the next. The filtration perspective says: these 14 stages are **not** independent — they're computing successive approximations of the same module element (the fully parsed, elaborated program).

The propagator network approach to PPN says: represent the program as a single network of cells. The cells hold lattice values at different levels of the filtration. Propagators move information between levels (and within levels). Run to fixpoint. The fixpoint is the fully parsed, elaborated program.

The key advantage: information flows **bidirectionally** between levels. In a pipeline, information flows only forward (tokens → surface → core). In the propagator network, later stages can constrain earlier stages. If the type checker (core level) discovers that an expression must have a certain type, this information flows back to the surface level, potentially disambiguating a parse that was ambiguous at the token level.

This is where the module theory pays off. The filtration structure tells you:

**Each level can be computed independently to fixpoint, then the results are joined.** This is because the graded pieces are independent (the associated graded module is a direct sum). Level 0 (lexing) reaches fixpoint independently of level 1 (surface parsing). Level 1 reaches fixpoint using level 0's results. And so on. But the propagator network allows all levels to propagate simultaneously, with cross-level propagators handling the information flow.

**The Galois bridges between levels are module homomorphisms.** The bridge from Token to Surface is not arbitrary — it preserves the module structure. This means the bridge is determined by its action on generators (a finite number of cases), not on all elements. You can verify the bridge by checking it on generators and relying on the module homomorphism property to guarantee correctness on all inputs.

**The filtration is refinable.** If you find that one of your 14 stages is "too coarse" (it conflates things that should be separate), you can refine the filtration by inserting a new level. The module theory tells you where: compute the graded piece for the coarse level, and if it decomposes further, the decomposition tells you what the new levels should be.

## Residuation as Narrowing: The Transformative Insight

Your colleague's observation about residuation deserves special emphasis because it could change how your logic engine fundamentally operates.

Currently, narrowing works by enumeration: given `f(?) = v`, try each constructor C, check if `f(C(...))` can reduce to `v`. This is search — it explores the space of constructors.

Residuation says: given `f(?) = v`, compute `f \ v` directly. The residual `f \ v` is the greatest element `x` such that `f(x) ≤ v`. On a finite lattice with computable meet, this is a single lattice operation.

But there's a subtlety that your colleague flags correctly: this works **if the propagator algebra is residuated.** Let me examine whether yours is.

### Is Your Propagator Algebra Residuated?
