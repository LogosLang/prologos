## Research conversation: concurrency primitives for the LLVM substrate (2026-05-02)

Long research dialogue grounding what concurrency primitives the Zig
kernel needs to reach competitive parallel BSP, given the architectural

   persistent worker pool with futex park (not thread-fork-per-round);
   sequence Sprint D before PUs land if possible. Collaborator has
   already gone PU-first per user note.
5. **PU vs scheduler interplay** — concrete question from collaborator:
   should each PU have its own scheduler? Resolved: PU is a namespace
   of state, scheduler is execution machinery. One worker pool

   Worker-binding-per-round is the load-bearing contract.

### Key things that crystallized in the note

- **EBR is correct here precisely because BSP gives explicit phase
  boundaries.** The "blocking" pathology of EBR (a stalled reader
  holding back reclamation) cannot occur because no reader survives
  a round boundary. The same property that makes EBR risky in general
  workloads makes it strictly better than hazard pointers for ours.
- **Lê et al. 2013 has a known integer-overflow bug** that Chase-Lev
  2005 doesn't have. Crossbeam-deque (Rust) and ConcurrentDeque (C++17)
  carry the corrected version. Port from those, not the 2013 PDF.
- **Mimalloc v3 changed its threading model** from v2 (no more
  thread-local segments). FFI integration via `@cImport` is direct;
  `mi_heap_new()` per worker for arena-style allocation.
- **TigerBeetle's deterministic simulation testing posture transfers
  to `.pnet` round-trip verification** even though their workload
  (single-threaded financial OLTP) differs from ours.
- **The Sprint D MVP I'd actually recommend** (named in §10 Q6):
  Chase-Lev deque + persistent worker pool + futex park + per-(worker,
  PU=root) write logs + cell-partitioned merge + sense-reversing
  barrier. ~800 LOC of new Zig. EBR / comptime per-tag batches /
  `pu_run_parallel` follow as Sprint D+1/D+2 gated on measurement.
- **Three calibration measurements on the current sequential prototype**
  (~2 hours work) answer most open questions about which primitives
  matter at our actual scale: per-round wall time, tag distribution,
  false-sharing microbenchmark.
