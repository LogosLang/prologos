# SH (Self-Hosting) — Series Master Tracking

**Created**: 2026-04-30
**Status**: Stage 0/1 — research synthesis. No implementation tracks active yet; series exists to host upcoming work and capture the trajectory.
**Thesis**: Prologos self-hosts in stages, not all at once. Substrate retirement (runtime Racket → native via LLVM) and compiler retirement (Racket-hosted compiler → Prologos-hosted compiler) are distinct retirement timelines that can be pursued independently. The architectural endpoint is a Prologos compiler running as a propagator-network value, compiled to native code via LLVM, with no host-language dependency at runtime or build time.

**Key insight (2026-04-30)**: Self-hosting in our setting is a *two-axis* retirement problem, not one-axis. **Axis 1**: retire Racket as a *runtime* dependency (the substrate primitives — cells, propagators, BSP, CHAMP — get implemented in LLVM-compiled native code). **Axis 2**: retire Racket as a *compile-time* dependency (the compiler itself gets ported to Prologos). The two axes are independent; most languages retire axis 1 well before axis 2. Conflating them ("we'll be self-hosted when the compiler is in Prologos") understates how much can be done on axis 1 first. The `.pnet` serialization format is the linchpin — when it round-trips propagator structure as a value, it becomes the deployment artifact format and stage A.5 unlocks.

**Cross-series connections**: PPN Track 4 (compiler IS the network) is the architectural prerequisite — the compiler must be a propagator-network program before "the network compiles to LLVM" makes sense. PReductions / Track 9 (reduction as propagators with tropical/quantale cost + e-graph rewriting) is a *cross-series dependency*: the algorithmic perf wins it delivers are what makes self-hosted Prologos competitive at runtime. FFI Series provides the stage-A.5 → stage-B substrate-call boundary. BSP-LE's parallel scheduler is what the native substrate must reimplement. SRE form registry and UCS algebraic-class dispatch become runtime concerns once compiled programs use them.

## Two-Axis Retirement Picture

The series organizes around two independent retirement axes (per Key Insight above):

### Axis 1: Runtime Racket retirement

**Stage A (current)** → **Stage A.5 (target)**: Substrate primitives implemented natively. Deployed Prologos programs run as native binaries. No Racket required at user runtime.

Tracks: 1 (`.pnet` network-as-value), 2 (Low-PNet IR), 3 (PoC), 4 (production substrate), 5 (erasure), 6 (runtime services), 7 (FFI inversion), 8 (WASM).

### Axis 2: Compile-time Racket retirement

**Stage A.5** → **Stage B**: Compiler ported to Prologos, running on already-native substrate. Prologos developers no longer need Racket.

Tracks: 9 (compiler-in-Prologos), with 5 (erasure) as cross-cutting prerequisite.

**Stage B → Stage C**: Bootstrap verification, Diverse Double Compilation, reproducible builds. Trust trusting trust closure.

Tracks: 10 (bootstrap verification).

The two axes can run in parallel after Track 4 lands. Stage A.5 alone (axis 1 complete) produces a Prologos that's deployable without Racket while still being developed in Racket. That's a perfectly valid and shippable intermediate state — most languages live there indefinitely (GHC's runtime stopped depending on anything but C decades ago; the Haskell-in-Haskell port came much later).

## Open Questions

1. **Track 1 (`.pnet` network-as-value): what does propagator structure serialization look like?** Today merge functions are Racket closures — not directly serializable. Options: (a) merge functions become symbolic references registered in the SRE form registry, dispatched at load time; (b) propagator definitions are first-class data in the language (NTT-style declarations); (c) hybrid. Connects to NTT design; resolution likely waits on PPN Track 4 + NTT progress.

2. **Track 2 (Low-PNet IR): how many layers between propagator network and LLVM IR?** Direct lowering = simpler, less optimization opportunity. STG/Cmm two-layer (GHC) = more passes, more optimization. MLIR dialect-tower (n-layer, configurable) = most flexible, most engineering. Decision depends on Track 9 (PReduce) outputs — if super-optimization happens at the propagator-network layer, the lower IR can be thinner.

3. **Track 4 (production substrate): host language?** Rust (per `RESEARCH_RUST_LLVM_LANGUAGE_IMPLEMENTATION.md`), C++, or Prologos itself (chicken-and-egg with Track 9). Rust has the strongest ecosystem story (Inkwell for LLVM bindings, MMTk for GC, mature concurrency primitives). C++ is the second option. Prologos-itself bootstraps via Track 9 first — chicken-and-egg unless we cross-compile.

4. **Track 5 (type erasure): what is erasure-aware about propagator-network values?** QTT `m0` erasure is well-defined for terms. Propagator networks themselves have structure (cells, propagators) that may or may not survive erasure. A capability-typed channel: at runtime, is it a typed handle, an opaque pointer, or fully erased to a raw pointer? Idris 2's QTT erasure provides the cleanest precedent but doesn't have propagator networks.

5. **Track 6 (runtime services): how much do we provide vs depend on?** GC: BDW conservative is a few-thousand-line dependency, gets us up fast, has known performance ceilings. MMTk integration is more work, more competitive. Designing our own region-based GC with CHAMP-aware reclamation is the most ambitious and the most aligned with the persistent-data architecture. Concurrency: thread pool is maybe 200 lines; lock-free CHAMP operations + work-stealing for BSP is a research effort.

6. **Track 9 (compiler-in-Prologos): when is Prologos expressive enough?** Most language features needed for self-hosting are already shipped (dependent types, QTT, capabilities, sessions, FFI, macros). Outstanding: meta-programming maturity, type-class / trait infrastructure for generic programming, possibly module-system maturity. The list of "what's blocking the rewrite" is small and most items are PPN/PM completion-level, not new design.

7. **Stage A.5 vs Stage B ordering**: should we land *all* of axis 1 (Tracks 1-8) before starting axis 2 (Track 9)? Or interleave? Strict ordering simplifies but delays compiler-in-Prologos work that could start sooner. Interleaving introduces stage-confusion risk. The companion path note `2026-04-30_SELF_HOSTING_PATH_AND_BOOTSTRAP.md` discusses ordering trade-offs in §5.

8. **What to do about Racket performance worry today** while waiting for stage A.5? The contributor anxiety has a near-term answer (PReduce / Track 9 fixes the imperative reduction walk in Racket) and a long-term answer (stage A.5 makes the question moot). The near-term answer matters because it unblocks development without waiting on the long-term answer. Track 9 priority is correct.
