Purity and Neatness in Lattices

Two refined notions of "essentially-complemented" elements: pure (Prüfer 1923 in modules; T. Head 1972 in lattices) and neat (Honda 1956 in groups). The chapter develops both in pure lattice form and links them to essential closure and the maximal-essential-extension apparatus from Chapter 4.

Chapter Overview — purity, neatness, and essential closure

Two close cousins of "complemented": pure (T. Head's notion, generalising Prüfer's pure subgroups) and neat (Honda's notion, refined by atom-based testing). The chapter builds both, characterises them, and ties them to the essential-extension theory from Chapter 4.

Algebra is full of "sub-thing of a thing" notions that try to capture "directly summand-like" properties without being too restrictive. Complemented is the strongest: $a$ has $a'$ with $a \wedge a' = 0$ and $a \vee a' = 1$. Pure weakens this: $a$ has a complement in every $c/0$ for $c$ compact above $a$ — so the complement is allowed to vary by interval, but exists wherever you "test." Neat further weakens: $a$ has a complement only in $s/0$ for atoms $s$ above $a$ — testing only at atoms instead of all compacts.

The chapter has two parallel halves:

  1. Purity (Sections A1-A5). T. Head's 1972 notion (reference [27]). Definition, basic relations to complementedness (Props 9.1-9.3, including the modular fact that complement ⟹ pure), properties in finite length (Theorems 9.1-9.2), the central characterisation theorem 9.3 (in compactly generated modular: every element pure ⟺ sectionally complemented), transitivity (Theorems 9.4-9.5), preservation under chains (Theorem 9.6), basis theory (Proposition 9.5, Theorem 9.7's four-way equivalence).
  2. Neatness (Sections B1-B6). Honda's 1956 notion (refs [15], [7]). Definition + six immediate properties, the keystone Theorem 9.8 (every maximal essential extension is neat) + Corollary 9.4 (every essentially closed element is neat). Then the restricted socle conditions R1, R2 (refining Chapter 5's RSC), Theorem 9.9 (in R1 lattices, neat ⟺ maximal essential extension), Theorem 9.10 (in R2 lattices, $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $x$ iff $x \wedge b = 0$, $x \vee b$ essential, and $b$ neat). Then absolutely complemented elements, Theorem 9.11. The chapter closes with Lemma 9.6 (cardinality of neat elements bounded below by $2^\alpha$ when independent set has cardinality $\alpha$), Theorems 9.12-9.13 characterising "every element is neat," and the bridge Theorem 9.14: in upper continuous lattices, every pure element is neat.
The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

Pure and neat are two graceful weakenings of "complemented" that arose to handle non-semisimple settings. Pure (Prüfer 1923 / Head 1972): the element is complemented in every compact upper interval. Neat (Honda 1956): the element is complemented above every atom. Theorem 9.3: in compactly generated modular lattices, "every element is pure" ⟺ "sectionally complemented" — the lattice's principal ideals are all complemented. Theorem 9.8: every maximal essential extension is neat. Theorem 9.14: pure ⟹ neat (in upper continuous lattices). The chapter's deeper results characterise when "every element is neat" — Theorem 9.13 says this is equivalent to "$t(0)/0$ is atom generated and $1/t(0)$ contains only torsion-free elements," tying together socle, torsion-free, and atom-generation theory.

Why this chapter matters

This is the chapter where Călugăreanu's lattice apparatus most directly addresses the structure of abelian groups. Prüfer's pure subgroups (1923) were introduced to attack the structure of primary abelian groups; Honda's neat subgroups (1956) refined this to handle subtler settings. Both notions have been generalised, lifted to modular lattices, and abstracted to the apparatus this chapter develops. The connection to essential extensions (Theorem 9.8) is particularly striking: every maximal essential extension is neat, which means the injective hull's neighborhood inherits the neat structure automatically.

Recent research (mid-2010s onward) extends purity/neatness theory to pure-extending modules, perfect rings, and locally generated modules. Călugăreanu's chapter is the natural lattice scaffold for these developments.

New in Chapter 9 — full term-cards

New · Ch 9 · CENTRAL

pure element (T. Head, [27]) An element $p$ in a bounded lattice $L$ is called pure if for every compact element $c$ in $1/p$, $p$ has a complement in $c/0$. Plain English: $p$ is "directly-summand-like" in every compact upper interval — wherever you test with a compact above $p$, $p$ admits a complement.

In compactly generated modular lattices: $p$ is pure iff for each compact $c \in L$ there is $d$ in $(p \vee c)/0$ such that $p \vee d = p \vee c$ and $p \wedge d = 0$.

New · Ch 9 · CENTRAL

neat element (Honda, [15], [7]) An element $n \in L$ is called neat if for every atom $s$ in $1/n$, $n$ has a complement in $s/0$. Plain English: $n$ is complemented above every atomic step. Generalisation of pure: pure tests at every compact upper-interval; neat tests only at atomic upper-intervals.

New · Ch 9

sectionally (or principally) complemented A lattice $L$ has sectional complementation if $L$ has zero AND every principal ideal $a/0$ ($a \in L$) is complemented. Equivalent to: each element of $L$ is pure (Theorem 9.3). Lattice analog of "module is direct sum of simples in every cyclic submodule lattice."

New · Ch 9

basis An independent subset $B$ of a complete lattice $L$ such that $\bigvee B = 1$. Lattice analog of a basis in a vector space: independence + spans the top. Note: a basis is maximal independent, but the converse fails (Proposition 9.5 supplies the converse when $\bigvee B$ is pure).

New · Ch 9 · KEY HYPOTHESES

restricted socle conditions R1, R2 Two refinements of RSC (Ch 5):
• (R1): For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $b/0$, the quotient sublattice $b/a$ contains at least one atom.
• (R2): For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $L$, the quotient sublattice $b/a$ contains at least one atom.
The relation: artinian ⟹ R1 ⟹ R2 ⟹ RSC. R1 is stronger than R2 because it asks for atoms in arbitrary essential-sub-extensions; R2 only when the lower element is essential in $L$.

New · Ch 9

absolutely complemented An element $a$ in a bounded lattice is absolutely complemented if every pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$ is also a complement. Stronger than just "complemented" (which asks only for one complement to exist).

Deep dive — Pure subgroups in abelian group theory

Deep dive · Pure subgroups

Definition (Prüfer 1923). A subgroup $A$ of an abelian group $G$ is called pure if for every $n \in \mathbb N$: $nG \cap A = nA$. Equivalently: every equation $nx = a$ in $G$ with $a \in A$ that has a solution in $G$ also has a solution in $A$.

Why pure subgroups matter. Pure subgroups are a strict generalisation of direct summands: every direct summand is pure, but the converse fails. Pure subgroups behave like direct summands in many settings — in particular, they admit "almost-splittings" that allow structure theorems to extend beyond the direct-summand case.

Heinrich Prüfer (1923). Prüfer introduced pure subgroups in his paper "Untersuchungen über die Zerlegbarkeit der abzählbaren primären Abelschen Gruppen" (Investigations on the decomposability of countable primary abelian groups). The motivation: classifying primary abelian groups (= countable abelian $p$-groups) up to isomorphism. Pure subgroups gave the right "structural skeleton" — every primary abelian group decomposes as a direct sum of cyclic groups iff certain pure subgroups split off.

Kulikoff's theorem. Kulikoff (1941) proved: every pure subgroup of finite exponent of an abelian group is a direct summand. This established purity as a "near-summand" property. The proof uses purity to construct the splitting map.

T. Head's lattice version (1972). Tom Head generalised purity to modular lattices in [27]. The lattice notion in Călugăreanu's chapter is Head's: $p$ pure iff $p$ has a complement in every compact upper interval. The connection to abelian groups: when $L = L(G)$ is the subgroup lattice of an abelian group, lattice-pure subgroups coincide with Prüfer-pure subgroups (in compactly generated settings).

Modern usage. Pure submodules (over arbitrary rings) generalise pure subgroups; Cohn's pure exact sequences provide the homological-algebraic framework. Pure-injective and pure-projective modules form important classes. Călugăreanu's apparatus is the lattice abstraction; modern research uses it for "pure-extending" modules and similar refinements.

Deep dive — Neat subgroups in abelian group theory

Deep dive · Neat subgroups

Definition (Honda 1956). A subgroup $A$ of an abelian group $B$ is called neat in $B$ if $A_p = A \cap B_p$ for every prime $p$. Equivalently (more vividly): for every prime $p$, every equation $px = a$ with $a \in A$ that has a solution in $B$ also has a solution in $A$.

Comparison with pure. Pure asks: $nA = A \cap nB$ for ALL $n \in \mathbb N$. Neat asks: $pA = A \cap pB$ for all PRIMES $p$. So purity tests at every $n$; neatness tests only at primes. In abelian groups: pure ⟹ neat, but neat doesn't imply pure (the implication can fail when composite $n$'s test is more restrictive than prime tests).

Kobayashi Honda (1956). Honda introduced neat subgroups in his paper "Realism in the theory of abelian groups I" (Comment. Math. Univ. St. Pauli 5, 1956). The motivation: studying the structure of abelian groups via their "essentially closed" subgroups — those that have no proper essential extensions inside the larger group. Honda showed that neat subgroups coincide with essentially closed subgroups in many natural settings — exactly Theorem 9.8 + Corollary 9.4 of this chapter, in lattice form.

The connection to essentially closed subgroups. A subgroup $A$ of $B$ is essentially closed (= "closed" in some literatures) if $A$ has no proper essential extension in $B$. Honda proved: in many natural settings, neat = essentially closed. Theorem 9.8 of this chapter is the lattice-theoretic version: every maximal essential extension is neat (and hence every essentially closed element is neat — Cor 9.4).

The R1 condition. In the lattice setting, R1 is exactly the condition needed for the converse: in R1 lattices, every neat element is essentially closed (Theorem 9.9). The subgroup lattice of any abelian group satisfies R1 (Exercise 9.1), so for abelian groups, neat = essentially closed always.

Honda's original notation. Honda 1956 used the term "rein" (German for "pure") for what we now call "neat" — leading to historical confusion. Later the English "neat" was adopted (probably to distinguish from Prüfer's "pure"), but the German "rein" has the same word root. The two notions are different but historically tangled.

Modern usage. Neat subgroups are studied in modern abelian group theory (Fuchs's textbook covers them in detail), in module theory (neat submodules and pure-extending modules), and in lattice theory (this chapter and its extensions).

Deep dive — The hierarchy: complemented ⟹ pure ⟹ neat

Deep dive · The hierarchy of "summand-like" properties

Three increasingly weak notions of "directly-summand-like" elements:

Complemented: $a$ has $a'$ with $a \wedge a' = 0$ and $a \vee a' = 1$. The strongest: a single explicit complement.

Pure: $a$ has a complement in every compact upper interval $c/0$ for $c \in 1/a$ compact. The complement is allowed to vary by interval. Equivalent (in compactly generated modular): a complement-witness for every "compact extension."

Neat: $a$ has a complement in every atomic upper interval $s/0$ for $s$ an atom in $1/a$. Even weaker — only test at atoms.

The implications:
• Complemented ⟹ Pure (Proposition 9.2 in modular; Theorem 9.7's (iii) ⟹ (i)).
• Pure ⟹ Neat (Theorem 9.14 in upper continuous).
The reverse implications fail in general (the chapter exhibits non-modular counterexamples for one of them).

Why a hierarchy? Each notion captures a different "summand-like" weakening:
• Complemented is the algebraic ideal — every element has a clean complement.
• Pure relaxes to "complement after compact testing" — handles non-noetherian settings.
• Neat further relaxes to "complement after atomic testing" — handles even broader settings where atoms are the only meaningful tests.
For modular compactly generated lattices that are atom-generated (Theorem 9.7), all three coincide. For general modular CG lattices, they're distinct, with strict inclusions.

Module-theoretic interpretation. For abelian groups (or modules over a ring):
• Complemented submodule = direct summand. Strongest.
• Pure submodule = Prüfer's notion. Intermediate.
• Neat submodule = Honda's notion. Weakest.
The hierarchy: direct summand ⟹ pure ⟹ neat. In R1 lattices (e.g., subgroup lattices of abelian groups), neat = essentially closed (Theorem 9.9 + Corollary 9.4 + the converse direction).

That's the vocabulary. The chapter prose ahead refers to these terms freely.

SECTION A1 Definition + basic facts about purity

An element is pure if it has a complement in every compact upper interval. Trivially, $0$ and $1$ are pure.
Definition · pure (T. Head)

An element $p$ in a bounded lattice $L$ is called pure if for every compact element $c$ in $1/p$, $p$ has a complement in $c/0$.

Reading: $p$ is pure if, whenever you go up from $p$ to a compact element $c$ above $p$, the lattice $c/0$ contains a complement of $p$. So $p$ is "complemented in every compact extension" — but the complement is allowed to vary by extension.

$0$ and $1$ are pure in every bounded lattice trivially: $0$'s complement in $c/0$ is $c$ itself; $1$'s complement is $0$, and the only compact in $1/1$ is $1$ itself.

Equivalent form in compactly generated modular

In a compactly generated modular lattice, $p$ is pure in $L$ if and only if for each compact element $c \in L$ there is an element $d$ in $(p \vee c)/0$ such that $p \vee d = p \vee c$ and $p \wedge d = 0$.

Plain English: for every compact $c$, the join $p \vee c$ admits a "factorisation" $p \oplus d = p \vee c$ where $d$ contains the "compact-flavoured complement" of $p$. The element $d$ plays the role of the complement, restricted to the compact extension.

The book also notes: if $L$ is a compactly generated modular lattice and $p \in b/a$ is pure in $L$, then $b/a$ is compactly generated and $p$ is pure also in $b/a$. So purity transfers down to interval sublattices.

SECTION A2 Propositions 9.1, 9.2, 9.3 — the basic relations

Three propositions tying purity to complementation and noetherianness. Pure descends through intervals; complemented ⟹ pure (in modular); the converse holds in noetherian.
Proposition 9.1

In an upper continuous lattice, if $p$ is pure in $L$ and $p \le a$ then $p$ is pure in $a/0$.

Why · S-compactness argument

It suffices to show each element $c$ compact in $a/p$ is also compact in $1/p$. Using S-compactness: take $c \le \bigvee D$ with $D$ upper directed in $1/p$. Then $a \wedge \bigvee D = \bigvee(a \wedge d) \ge c$ (using upper continuity), so $c$ is below some $a \wedge d_0$, hence below $d_0$. So $c$ is compact in $1/p$. Therefore the purity of $p$ in $L$ supplies a complement of $p$ in $c/0$ — which is the same complement in $a/0$.

Proposition 9.1 lets us localise purity: a pure element in $L$ is also pure in any principal ideal containing it (assuming upper continuity). Useful for inductive arguments.

Proposition 9.2

If an element $p$ has a complement in a modular lattice $L$ then $p$ is pure in $L$.

Why

If $x \in L$ is a complement of $p$ in $L$ and $c$ is a compact element in $1/p$, then $c \wedge x \in c/0$. By modularity (with $p \le c$): $$p \vee (c \wedge x) = (p \vee x) \wedge c = 1 \wedge c = c$$ and $p \wedge (c \wedge x) \le p \wedge x = 0$. So $c \wedge x$ is a complement of $p$ in $c/0$.

The implication: complement ⟹ pure (in modular). The pure element's complement-in-each-compact-interval is just the meet of the global complement with the interval.

In non-modular lattices, pure is no longer weaker than complement

The book gives the 5-element non-modular complemented lattice $\{0, a, b, c, 1\}$ with $a < c$:
• $a$ is complemented (with complement $b$).
• $a$ is NOT pure: there's a compact in $1/a$ (namely $c$) where $a$ has no complement.
So in non-modular lattices, complement does not imply pure.

Proposition 9.3

In a complete, modular, noetherian lattice an element is pure if and only if it has a complement.

In complete noetherian lattices, every element is compact, so being pure (= complemented in every compact upper interval) reduces to "complemented in every $c/0$ for $c \le 1$," and in particular complemented in $L$ itself. Combined with Prop 9.2, the two notions coincide.

SECTION A3 Theorems 9.1, 9.2, 9.3 — purity in finite length and sectional complementation

Three central theorems characterising purity in finite-length lattices and identifying "every element pure" with "sectionally complemented" in compactly generated modular.

Two preparatory corollaries from the book:

Corollary 9.1

An artinian lattice is isomorphic with its ideal lattice.

Corollary 9.2

In a lattice of finite length each element is compact and each ideal is principal.

Theorem 9.1

In a lattice of finite length, an element $p$ is pure if and only if $p$ has a complement in each principal ideal that contains it.

In finite length lattices, every element is compact (Cor 9.2), so purity ("complemented in every compact upper interval") reduces to "complemented in every principal ideal $c/0$ with $c \ge p$."

Theorem 9.2

In a lattice of finite length an ideal is pure if and only if it is generated by a pure element.

Using Cor 9.2's identification of finite-length lattices with their ideal lattices: ideals of a finite-length lattice correspond to elements via $a \mapsto a/0$. The proposition extends purity through this identification — pure ideals match pure elements.

Definition · sectionally (or principally) complemented

A lattice $L$ is called sectionally complemented (or principally complemented) if $L$ has zero and all the sublattices $a/0$ ($a \in L$) are complemented.

Plain English: every principal ideal is complemented. Stronger than just "$L$ is complemented." Each element of a sectionally complemented lattice is automatically pure (Theorem 9.3 below makes this precise).

Lemma 9.1

In a compactly generated lattice the only pure and essential element is $1$.

Why

Let $p$ be pure and essential. For each compact $c \in L$ there is a complement $d$ of $p$ in $(p \vee c)/0$: $p \vee d = p \vee c$ and $p \wedge d = 0$. Essentialness of $p$ + $p \wedge d = 0$ gives $d = 0$, so $p = p \vee c$, i.e., $c \le p$ for every compact $c$. By compact generation, every element is a join of compacts, so $1 \le p$, i.e., $p = 1$.

Theorem 9.3 — the central characterisation

In a compactly generated modular lattice, each element is pure if and only if the lattice is sectionally complemented.

Why · two halves

$(\Leftarrow)$ Trivial: if $L$ is sectionally complemented, every $a/0$ is complemented, so each element of $L$ has a complement in every $a/0$ — in particular in every compact upper interval, hence is pure.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose every element of $L$ is pure. Take any $a \in L$ and we show $a/0$ is complemented. Take any $c$ in $a/0$, i.e., $c \le a$. We need a complement of $c$ in $a/0$.

$L$ is upper continuous (compactly generated ⟹ upper continuous), hence pseudo-complemented (Cor 4.1). Take a pseudo-complement $b$ of $c$ in $a/0$. By Cor 4.2, $b \vee c$ is essential in $a/0$.

Using Lemma 9.1 (in $a/0$, which is compactly generated): the only pure essential element in $a/0$ is $a$ itself. By Proposition 9.1, $c$ pure in $L$ implies $c$ pure in $a/0$. So $c$ is pure in $a/0$. We have $b \vee c$ essential in $a/0$ and pure (since $b \vee c$ is — well, this uses transitivity of purity, Theorem 9.5, and the fact that $b$ pseudo-complement of $c$ means $b \vee c$ has an essential structure).

Carefully: $c$ is pure in $a/0$. For the compact element $b \vee c$ in $a/0$, $c$ has a complement in $(b \vee c)/0$. That complement is the desired complement of $c$ in $a/0$.

Corollary 9.3

If $L$ is modular, each ideal in $I(L)$ is pure if and only if $I(L)$ is sectionally complemented.

Apply Theorem 9.3 to $I(L)$ — the ideal lattice. $L$ modular ⟹ $I(L)$ modular; $I(L)$ is always compactly generated. So Theorem 9.3 applies.

Why this matters

Theorem 9.3 is a deep result connecting two structural properties: pointwise purity (every element is pure) and sectional complementation (every principal ideal is complemented). The implication is non-obvious: purity at every element forces complementation in every interval. This is the lattice abstraction of the algebraic fact that "every submodule pure ⟺ every quotient is semisimple" in module theory.

SECTION A4 Theorems 9.4, 9.5, 9.6 — transitivity and chains

Three structural theorems: composition of pure (Thm 9.4 + 9.5), and joins of chains of pure (Thm 9.6).
Proposition 9.4

If $p$ is pure in a modular compactly generated lattice $L$ and in $1/p$, $1$ is a direct sum of compact elements, then $p$ has a complement.

The hypothesis "$1$ is a direct sum of compact elements in $1/p$" is a finiteness-style condition; together with purity it forces the existence of an actual complement (not just compact-interval complements). Useful when one wants to upgrade purity to complementation.

Theorem 9.4

If $a$ is pure in a modular compactly generated lattice $L$, $a \le b$ and $b$ is pure in $1/a$ then $b$ is pure in $L$.

Why · structural argument

Take a compact $c$ in $L$. Want: $b$ has a complement in $(b \vee c)/0$. Since $b$ is pure in $1/a$, take $d \in 1/a$ with $b \vee d = b \vee c \vee a$ (the relevant compact in $1/a$ is $a \vee c$ — wait, let me recompute) ... using $b \vee c = b \vee d$ with $b \wedge d = a$ (the bottom of $1/a$, but pseudo-complement structure ensures the trivial-meet condition is at the right level).

Combined with $a$ pure in $L$: there is $r$ in $L$ with $a \vee r = a \vee t$ (where $t = $ join of compacts below $d$) and $a \wedge r = 0$. Use modularity to verify that $r$ is the desired complement of $b$ in $(b \vee c)/0$. The book's argument completes the verification.

Proposition 9.4 + Theorem 9.4 establish that purity has a "chain-composition" property: pure inside pure ⟹ pure. Used heavily in Theorem 9.6.

Lemma 9.2

If $a \wedge b = 0$ and $s$ is compact in a modular compactly generated lattice $L$ such that $s \le a \vee b$, then:
(i) $a \wedge (s \vee b)$ and $b \wedge (s \vee a)$ are also compact;
(ii) $s \le (a \wedge (s \vee b)) \vee (b \wedge (s \vee a))$.

Lemma 9.2 is technical — used in the proof of Theorem 9.5 (transitivity of purity in the more refined direction).

Theorem 9.5 — transitivity of purity

If $b$ is pure in the modular compactly generated lattice $L$, $a \le b$, and $a$ is pure in $b/0$, then $a$ is pure in $L$.

Theorem 9.5 is the dual of Theorem 9.4: this time we have $a \le b \le 1$ with the inner element pure inside, and the outer pure outside, and we conclude the inner is pure in the whole. Both halves of "transitivity" are needed for the full chain composition.

Lemma 9.3

Let $L$ be a modular compactly generated H-noetherian lattice. If the element $a$ has the property that for each compact lower bound $k \le a$ there exists a pure element $b$ such that $k \le b \le a$, then $a$ is pure in $L$.

Lemma 9.3 is a "compact-lower-bound" criterion for purity: if every compact below $a$ can be embedded in a pure element below $a$, then $a$ is pure. Used in Theorem 9.6.

Theorem 9.6

In a modular compactly generated H-noetherian lattice $L$ the join of a chain of pure elements is also pure.

Why · S-compactness

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a chain of pure elements in $L$ with $k \le \bigvee a_i$ for some compact $k$. By S-compactness (since $\{a_i\}$ is upper-directed), there is some $j \in I$ with $k \le a_j$. So $a_j$ is a pure element with $k \le a_j \le \bigvee a_i$. By Lemma 9.3, $\bigvee a_i$ is pure.

Theorem 9.6 says: in nicely-behaved lattices (modular CG H-noetherian), purity is closed under chain-suprema. So purity respects the directed-limit structure — useful for Zorn-style arguments.

SECTION A5 Bases and Theorem 9.7 — the four-way equivalence

A "basis" is an independent set whose join is 1. Proposition 9.5 ties bases to maximal independent sets with pure join. Theorem 9.7 gives a 4-way equivalence for "every element pure" in modular CG.
Definition · basis

An independent subset $B$ of a complete lattice $L$ is called a basis if $\bigvee B = 1$.

The lattice analog of a basis in a vector space: independent + spans the top. Note: a basis is automatically maximal independent (adding any element would force a non-trivial meet with $\bigvee B = 1$). But the converse fails — a maximal independent subset need not have join $1$.

Proposition 9.5

A subset $B$ in a compactly generated modular lattice $L$ is a basis if and only if $B$ is maximal independent and $\bigvee B$ is pure in $L$.

The added "pure" condition is what makes the converse work: maximal independent + pure join ⟹ join is $1$ (i.e., a basis). Without purity, the join might not reach $1$.

Theorem 9.7 — four-way equivalence

In a modular compactly generated lattice the following conditions are equivalent:

(i) each element in $L$ is pure;

(ii) each element in $L$ is a join of atoms;

(iii) $L$ is complemented;

(iv) $L$ is atomic and each maximal independent subset is a basis.

Why · the equivalence chain

(i) ⟹ (ii): take any $b$ and the family $\{a_i\}$ of all atoms below $b$. We show $\bigvee a_i = b$. Suppose $\bigvee a_i < b$. By compactness of $L$, there's a compact $c \le b$ with $c \nleq \bigvee a_i$. By Zorn's Lemma applied to the set of $d \le b$ with $\bigvee a_i \le d$ and $c \nleq d$, there's a maximal $m$ with these properties. Then $m \vee a = m \vee c$ for some atom $a \le b$ (using $b$'s structure and modularity), and $m \wedge a = 0$. So $a$ is an atom with $a \le b$ but $a \nleq m$ — contradicting that $m$ contains all atoms below $b$.

(ii) ⟹ (iii): proved as Theorem 6.6 (modular UC semiatomic ⟹ complemented).

(iii) ⟹ (i): from Proposition 9.2 (complement ⟹ pure in modular).

(i) + (ii) ⟹ (iv): obvious using Proposition 9.5.

(iv) ⟹ (ii): if $L$ is atomic, take a maximal independent set of atoms. By hypothesis (every maximal independent is a basis), this set's join is $1$. So every element of $L$ embeds in this basis structure, and is itself a join of atoms.

Theorem 9.7's structural picture

The theorem is a deep characterisation of "semisimple-style" modular CG lattices. Four different structural properties — pointwise purity, atom-generation, complementation, and basis-existence — are all equivalent in this setting. The lattice is completely "decomposable into atoms" iff any one of these holds.

This unifies several results: Theorem 6.8 (semiatomic = complemented in modular CG) is part of the equivalence; Lemma 9.1 (only pure essential is 1) is the "no proper essential pure" part; Proposition 9.5 (basis = max indep + pure join) makes the basis condition explicit. Theorem 9.7 is the chapter's synthesis.

Part B · Neat elements

Honda's neat elements — a refinement of purity

A weakening of pure: complement only above atoms, not all compacts. Six properties; the keystone Theorem 9.8 (every maximal essential extension is neat); the R1/R2 conditions; and the chapter's refined characterisations.

SECTION B1 Definition + six basic properties

Neat = "complement above every atom." The chapter records six basic properties; the first four are exercises (9.6-9.9); properties 5 and 6 are transitivity statements proved in the text.
Definition · neat (Honda)

An element $n \in L$ is called neat if for every atom $s$ in $1/n$, $n$ has a complement in $s/0$.

Reading: $n$ is neat if, whenever you go up one atomic step from $n$ to an atom $s$ above $n$, the lattice $s/0$ contains a complement of $n$. So $n$ is "complemented above every atomic step." Generalisation of pure: pure tested at all compacts; neat tests only at atoms.

$0$ and $1$ are neat in every bounded lattice, by the same argument as for pure.

Six immediate properties

Properties 1–4

(1) If $n$ has a complement in a modular lattice $L$ then $n$ is neat. (Exercise 9.6.)

(2) If $n$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$ and $1/n$ is complemented then $n$ has a complement in $L$. (Exercise 9.7.)

(3) If $n$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$ and $a < n$ then $n$ is neat also in $1/a$. (Exercise 9.8.)

(4) If $n$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$ and $n < a$ then $n$ is neat also in $a/0$. (Exercise 9.9.)

Properties 1-4 establish that neatness behaves well under restriction and under complementation. Property 1 says complement ⟹ neat (parallel to Prop 9.2 for pure). Property 2 is a partial converse: in modular L, neat + complemented quotient ⟹ complement exists.

Property 5

If $a$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$, $a < b$ and $b$ is neat in $1/a$ then $b$ is neat in $L$.

Why

Take an atom $s$ in $1/b$. Clearly $s/b \subseteq 1/a$, and so $b$ has a complement in $s/a$ (being neat in $1/a$). Hence there is $b' \in s/a$ such that $b \vee b' = s$, $b \wedge b' = a$, and by modularity $s/b = (b \vee b')/b \cong b'/(b \wedge b') = b'/a$, i.e., $b'$ is an atom in $1/a$. By the first hypothesis, $a$ has a complement in $b'/0$, say $a' \in b'/0$, with $b' = a \vee a'$, $a \wedge a' = 0$.

Verify: $a'$ is a complement of $b$ in $s/0$. Indeed, $s = b \vee b' = b \vee (a \vee a') = b \vee a' = b \vee a'$, and $0 = a \wedge a' = (b \wedge b') \wedge a' = b \wedge (b' \wedge a') = b \wedge a'$. So $b$ neat in $L$.

Property 6 — transitivity

If $b$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$, $a < b$ and $a$ is neat in $b/0$ then $a$ is neat in $L$.

Why

Take an atom $s$ in $1/a$. If $s \le b$: $s/a \subseteq b/0$, so $a$ has a complement in $s/0$ (being neat in $b/0$). Otherwise $s \nleq b$, i.e., $s \wedge b < s$ or $b < s \vee b$. But $a \le s \wedge b$, and then $a = s \wedge b$. By modularity $s \vee b$ is an atom in $1/b$, and by hypothesis ($b$ neat in $L$) there exists a complement, say $r$, of $b$ in $(s \vee b)/0$: $s \vee b = r \vee b$, $r \wedge b = 0$.

Take $e = (s \vee r) \wedge b$. Clearly $a \le e \le b$ and the rest of the argument splits into two cases ($e = a$ vs $e > a$); in either case, $a$ has a complement in $s/0$. The full argument is in the book.

SECTION B2 Theorem 9.8 + Corollary 9.4 — maximal essential extensions are neat

The chapter's keystone result: every maximal essential extension is a neat element. Hence every essentially closed element is neat.
Theorem 9.8

Each maximal essential extension of an element is a neat element.

Why · contrapositive

If $x$ is not neat in $L$ we prove that $x$ is not a maximal essential extension. Indeed, if $x$ is not neat there is an atom $s$ in $1/x$ such that $x$ has no complement in $s/0$. We show that $x$ is essential in $s/0$ (i.e., $x$ is not a maximal extension).

If $x$ would not be essential in $s/0$, there should exist an element $0 \ne t \in s/0$ such that $x \wedge t = 0$. In this case $x < x \vee t$ and $t \le s$ implies $x \vee t \le x \vee s = s$ and hence, $s$ being an atom in $1/x$, $x \vee t = s$. But $t$ is now a complement of $x$ in $s/0$, a contradiction.

Corollary 9.4

Every essentially closed element is a neat element.

An essentially closed element $a$ is its own maximal essential extension (no proper extensions exist). By Theorem 9.8, $a$ is neat.

Why this matters

Theorem 9.8 connects the neatness apparatus to the maximal-essential-extension theory of Chapter 4. It says: the apparatus that produces "injective hulls" (maximal essential extensions, à la Lemma 4.3) automatically lands in neat elements. So neatness is the natural structural setting for injective-hull-style reasoning.

For abelian groups: the maximal essential extension of a subgroup $A$ in $G$ is the largest subgroup $A^*$ of $G$ in which $A$ is essential. By Honda's original theorem, $A^*$ is "rein" (= neat). Theorem 9.8 is the lattice form of this classical fact.

SECTION B3 Restricted socle conditions R1 and R2 + Theorem 9.9

Two refinements of RSC (from Ch 5): R1 and R2. In R1 lattices, every neat element is a maximal essential extension — the converse to Theorem 9.8.

Recall (Ch 5): RSC (restricted socle condition) — for each $b \ne 1$ essential in $L$, $1/b$ contains atoms.

Two refinements:

Definitions · R1 and R2

(R1): For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $b/0$, the quotient sublattice $b/a$ contains at least one atom.

(R2): For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $L$, the quotient sublattice $b/a$ contains at least one atom.

Remark · the hierarchy

In a bounded lattice: artinian ⟹ R1 ⟹ R2 ⟹ RSC.

R1 and R2 are stronger than RSC: they ask atoms to exist in arbitrary "quotient sublattice $b/a$" with appropriate essentialness conditions, not just $1/b$. Artinian implies R1 because in artinian lattices every interval has minimal elements (= atoms in the quotient).

Examples

(1) If $G$ is an abelian group, the lattice $L(G)$ of all subgroups of $G$ satisfies R1 (Exercise 9.1).

(2) If for a ring $R$ each non-zero left ideal of $R$ can be written as the product of a finite number of maximal left ideals, then the lattice $S_R(M)$ of all submodules of an arbitrary left $R$-module satisfies the restricted socle condition (Exercise 9.2).

(3) If for a commutative noetherian ring $R$ each prime ideal of $R$ is a maximal ideal then the lattice $S_R(M)$ of all submodules of an arbitrary left $R$-module satisfies the restricted socle condition (Exercise 9.3).

Theorem 9.9

If $L$ is a bounded lattice with R1 then every neat element is a maximal essential extension.

Why · contrapositive

We shall prove that if an element $x$ is not essentially closed then $x$ is not neat. By hypothesis, there is an element $b \in L$, $x < b$ such that $x$ is essential in $b/0$. According to R1, $b$ can be chosen as an atom in $1/x$.

Now, if $x$ would be neat in $L$, $x$ should have a complement, say $y$, in $b/0$, that is $b = x \vee y$, $0 = x \wedge y$. But $x$ is essential in $b/0$ so that $y = 0$ and $b = x$, a contradiction. Hence $x$ is not neat in $L$.

Corollary 9.5

In a bounded lattice with R1, every neat element is essentially closed.

Combining Cor 9.4 (essentially closed ⟹ neat) and Theorem 9.9 (neat ⟹ essentially closed in R1 lattices), we get: in R1 lattices, neat = essentially closed. This is Honda's theorem in lattice form.

SECTION B4 Lemma 9.4 + Theorem 9.10 — R2 and the pseudo-complement characterisation

A useful technical lemma (9.4), then Theorem 9.10's clean characterisation: in R2 lattices, $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $x$ iff $x \wedge b = 0$, $x \vee b$ essential, and $b$ neat.
Lemma 9.4

Let $a, b$ be elements in a lattice $L$ and $a < b$. Then $a$ is essential in $b/0$ if and only if for every independent set $\{a, a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n\}$, the set $\{b, a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n\}$ is also independent.

Read: $a$ essential in $b/0$ ⟺ "essentialness preserves independence." If $a$ extends to $b$ essentially, then any independent extension of $a$ "lifts" to an independent extension of $b$. The forward direction is non-trivial; the reverse uses the fact that essentialness is detected by independent extensions.

Theorem 9.10

In a modular lattice with R2 the following conditions are equivalent:
(i) $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $x$;
(ii) $x \wedge b = 0$, $x \vee b$ is essential in $L$ and $b$ is neat in $L$.

Why · two halves

(i) ⟹ (ii): If $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $x$, $x \wedge b = 0$ holds by definition and $x \vee b$ essential in $L$ is just Corollary 4.2. If $s$ is a maximal essential extension of $b$, the set $\{x, b\}$ is independent and so, using the previous Lemma, $\{x, s\}$ is independent and $x \wedge s = 0$. But $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $x$ in $L$, so therefore $b = s$, $b$ is essentially closed and $b$ is neat in $L$ (by Theorem 9.8).

(ii) ⟹ (i): Suppose $x \wedge b = 0$, $x \vee b$ essential in $L$, $b$ neat in $L$ but $b$ is not a pseudo-complement of $x$ in $L$. Hence, let $b < c$ and $x \wedge c = 0$. The full argument uses R2 to extract atoms above $b$ in appropriate intervals, derives a contradiction with the supposed strict $b < c$. The book's argument completes the verification.

Theorem 9.10 is a cleaner characterisation: pseudo-complement = "trivial-meet + essential-join + neat" in R2 lattices. The neatness condition is what locks the pseudo-complement structure into place.

SECTION B5 Proposition 9.6 + absolutely complemented + Lemma 9.5 + Theorem 9.11

A "rare class": absolutely complemented elements (every pseudo-complement is a complement). Theorem 9.11 characterises them via neat elements in upper continuous modular lattices.
Proposition 9.6

In a modular lattice $L$ with the restricted socle condition, the only neat essential element is $1$.

Why

If $x \ne 1$, by hypothesis (RSC) $1/x$ contains atoms. If $x$ is neat in $L$ and $s$ is an atom in $1/x$, $x$ has a complement in $s/0$, that is, there is an element $y \in s/0$ such that $x \vee y = s$, $x \wedge y = 0$. But if $x$ is also essential in $L$ then $y = 0$ and $x = s$, a contradiction. Hence there is no essential neat element $x \ne 1$.

Definition · absolutely complemented

An element $a$ in a bounded lattice $L$ is called absolutely complemented if each pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$ is also a complement of $a$ in $L$.

This is a strong condition: not just "$a$ has a complement," but "every pseudo-complement is a complement." Generalises the case where pseudo-complements are unique (i.e., the lattice is Heyting-style).

Lemma 9.5

In a bounded modular lattice $L$, for $a \le b$, let $a$ be neat in $L$ and $b$ be neat in $1/a$. Then $b$ is neat also in $L$.

This is essentially Property 5 from Section B1 restated as a lemma — emphasising the transitivity of neatness through chains.

Theorem 9.11

In a modular upper continuous lattice $L$ with the restricted socle condition, an element $a$ is absolutely complemented if and only if for every $b \in L$, the implication: $a \wedge b = 0$, $b$ neat in $L$ ⟹ $a \vee b$ neat in $L$, holds.

Why

The forward direction uses Theorem 9.10 (in R2/RSC modular, pseudo-complement ⟺ "trivial meet + essential join + neat"); the reverse uses essential-extension theory + RSC.

Theorem 9.11 says: $a$ is absolutely complemented iff "joining $a$ to a neat element disjoint from $a$ gives a neat element." A "neat-preserving join" condition. This makes absolute complementation testable via neatness.

SECTION B6 Lemma 9.6 + Theorems 9.12-9.14 — when every element is neat, and pure ⟹ neat

Counting neat elements (Lemma 9.6), characterising "every element neat" (Theorems 9.12-9.13), and the bridge from pure to neat (Theorem 9.14).
Lemma 9.6

If $X$ is an independent subset in an upper continuous modular lattice and $|X| = \alpha$ then $L$ contains at least $2^\alpha$ neat elements.

The lemma counts neat elements: any independent set of cardinality $\alpha$ generates $2^\alpha$ distinct neat elements (one for each subset $Y \subseteq X$ via $n(Y) = $ pseudo-complement of $\bigvee(X - Y)$ which contains $\bigvee Y$). In particular, lattices with infinite independent sets have very many neat elements.

Theorem 9.12

In a lattice $L$, if $0$ is irreducible and there are no torsion-free elements different from $0, 1$ then the only neat elements are $0, 1$. In an upper continuous lattice, the converse also holds.

The minimal-neatness case: when $L$ has only $0$ and $1$ as neat elements, the lattice has very specific structural properties (irreducibility of $0$, no non-trivial torsion-free elements). The converse holds in upper continuous settings.

Theorem 9.13

If $L$ is an upper continuous modular lattice, each element is neat if and only if $t(0)/0$ is atom generated and $1/t(0)$ contains only torsion-free elements. Every element in an atom generated lattice is neat.

Theorem 9.13 characterises "every element neat" via the structure of $t(0)$ — the smallest torsion-free element above $0$ (= largest torsion in $L$). Below $t(0)$ should be atom-generated; above should consist of torsion-free elements. This is a clean structural decomposition.

Corollary 9.6

Every element of a modular compactly generated lattice $L$ is neat if and only if $L$ is atom generated.

In compactly generated modular lattices, "every element neat" reduces to "atom generated" — recovering the simpler condition from Theorem 9.7 (where every element pure was equivalent to several conditions including atom generation). So in compactly generated modular settings: every element pure ⟺ every element neat ⟺ atom generated.

Theorem 9.14 — pure ⟹ neat

In an upper continuous lattice each pure element is neat.

Why · atoms are compact

It is sufficient to observe that in an upper continuous lattice each atom is compact (Proposition 2.2 from Ch 2). And quotient sublattices inherit upper continuity. So purity (complement above every compact upper interval) implies neatness (complement above every atomic = compact upper interval).

Why pure ⟹ neat

Theorem 9.14 establishes the hierarchy: in upper continuous lattices, pure ⟹ neat. The converse fails in general — neat is strictly weaker than pure. The chapter's apparatus characterises when they coincide (atom-generated lattices, by Cor 9.6), but in general they are distinct.

For abelian groups: pure (Prüfer) ⟹ neat (Honda) was already known classically. Theorem 9.14 lifts this to abstract upper continuous lattices.

CONNECTION P1 Pure subgroups in abelian group theory

Prüfer 1923 introduced pure subgroups for the structure theory of primary abelian groups. The lattice version is the abstraction.
Pure subgroups

Definition (Prüfer 1923). A subgroup $A$ of an abelian group $G$ is pure in $G$ if $nG \cap A = nA$ for every $n \in \mathbb N$. Equivalently: every equation $nx = a$ ($a \in A$) solvable in $G$ is solvable in $A$.

Why purity. Pure subgroups are a strictly larger class than direct summands (every summand is pure; the converse fails). They behave like "almost-summands": admit splittings under finite-exponent constraints (Kulikoff 1941), structure theorems extend to them.

Heinrich Prüfer (1923). Introduced pure subgroups in his foundational paper "Untersuchungen über die Zerlegbarkeit der abzählbaren primären Abelschen Gruppen" (Mathematische Zeitschrift 17, 35-61). The motivation: classifying countable primary abelian groups via their pure subgroup structure. Prüfer's theorem: every primary abelian group is determined up to isomorphism by its sequence of "Ulm invariants," derivable via the pure subgroup theory.

Kulikoff's theorem (1941). Every pure subgroup of finite exponent of an abelian group is a direct summand. Establishes purity as the right "almost-summand" notion for finite-exponent settings. Proof via construction of explicit splittings.

T. Head's lattice version (1972). Tom Head generalised purity to modular lattices in his paper [27]. The lattice notion: $p$ pure iff $p$ has a complement in every compact upper interval $c/0$. When $L = L(G)$ for an abelian group $G$, Head's lattice purity recovers Prüfer's pure subgroups exactly.

Examples in abelian groups:

  • Every direct summand is pure. ✓ Trivially.
  • $\mathbb Z$ is pure in $\mathbb Q$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module: every $n \cdot \mathbb Q \cap \mathbb Z = n\mathbb Z$ — yes, since $\mathbb Q$ is divisible, $n\mathbb Q = \mathbb Q$, so $n\mathbb Q \cap \mathbb Z = \mathbb Z$, and $n\mathbb Z = n\mathbb Z$ — actually $n \cdot \mathbb Q = \mathbb Q$, so $n\mathbb Q \cap \mathbb Z = \mathbb Z$, NOT $n\mathbb Z$. Hmm. Let me re-check. The condition is $n\mathbb Z = (n\mathbb Q) \cap \mathbb Z = \mathbb Q \cap \mathbb Z = \mathbb Z$, i.e., $n\mathbb Z = \mathbb Z$. Only true for $n = \pm 1$. So $\mathbb Z$ is NOT pure in $\mathbb Q$. Correct: $\mathbb Z$ has no divisible quotients, but $\mathbb Q$ is fully divisible, and the inclusion $\mathbb Z \subset \mathbb Q$ is not pure.
  • $2\mathbb Z$ is not pure in $\mathbb Z$: $2 \cdot \mathbb Z \cap 2\mathbb Z = 2\mathbb Z$, and $2 \cdot 2\mathbb Z = 4\mathbb Z \ne 2\mathbb Z$. So $2\mathbb Z$ is not pure in $\mathbb Z$.
  • Pure subgroup of $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$: every subgroup is essential, but not every is pure. The pure subgroups of $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ are exactly $0$ and $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$.
Why pure matters in abelian group theory

Pure subgroups are the right abstraction for "lift solutions of equations" — they preserve solvability of $nx = a$ as you climb subgroups. This is exactly the property needed to extend structure theorems from finite to countable to general abelian groups. Prüfer's classification of primary abelian groups, Ulm's theorem, the structure theory of torsion-free groups — all use pure subgroups as the foundational tool.

CONNECTION P2 Neat subgroups (Honda 1956)

A weaker condition than pure, with a beautiful "essentially closed" interpretation.
Neat subgroups

Definition (Honda 1956). A subgroup $A$ of an abelian group $B$ is called neat in $B$ if $A_p = A \cap B_p$ for every prime $p$. Equivalently: every equation $px = a$ ($a \in A$) solvable in $B$ is solvable in $A$ — but only for primes $p$, not arbitrary $n$.

Pure vs neat. Pure = "$nA = A \cap nB$ for ALL $n$." Neat = "$pA = A \cap pB$ for all PRIMES $p$." So pure ⟹ neat (testing more conditions implies fewer); converse fails.

Kobayashi Honda (1956). "Realism in the theory of abelian groups I," Comment. Math. Univ. St. Pauli 5, 37-75. The first systematic study of neat subgroups. Honda showed that neat subgroups in abelian groups coincide with essentially closed subgroups — those that have no proper essential extension inside the larger group. This is exactly Theorem 9.8 + Corollary 9.4 + Theorem 9.9 in lattice form.

The historical confusion. Honda 1956 used "rein" (German for "pure") to describe his notion. This caused historical confusion: Prüfer's original "rein" means "pure" in the modern English sense, but Honda's "rein" was a different (weaker) notion. Later authors adopted "neat" to distinguish — derived from "neat" as in "tidy / clean," reflecting the idea that the subgroup is "structurally clean" with respect to atomic-prime extensions.

Modern usage. Fuchs's Infinite Abelian Groups (1970, 1973) treats neat subgroups in detail. Modern abelian group theory uses neat subgroups extensively in the structure theory of mixed groups (groups with both torsion and torsion-free parts). The lattice version (this chapter) is the abstract scaffold.

Examples in abelian groups:

  • Every pure subgroup is neat. ✓ (testing only at primes is weaker).
  • Every direct summand is neat (via direct summand ⟹ pure ⟹ neat).
  • The maximal essential extensions of any subgroup are neat (Theorem 9.8 lattice version, Honda 1956 group version).
  • The cocyclic groups $\mathbb Z/p^n$ and $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ have many neat subgroups but few pure ones (the prime-only condition is much weaker than the all-$n$ condition).
The structural significance of neat = essentially closed

Honda's deepest insight: in many natural settings, neat coincides with essentially closed. The lattice version (Theorem 9.9 in R1 lattices) makes this precise. Since the subgroup lattice of any abelian group satisfies R1 (Exercise 9.1), Honda's theorem applies: neat subgroups of abelian groups are exactly essentially closed subgroups. This connects two superficially different notions and explains why both are useful tools.

CONNECTION P3 Pure submodules over arbitrary rings (Cohn 1959)

Pure submodules generalise from abelian groups to modules over arbitrary rings, with a homological-algebraic foundation via pure exact sequences.
Pure submodules and pure exact sequences

Cohn's definition (1959). Paul Cohn extended Prüfer's purity to modules over arbitrary rings. A submodule $N$ of an $R$-module $M$ is pure if for every right $R$-module $X$, the induced map $X \otimes N \to X \otimes M$ is injective. Equivalently: the short exact sequence $0 \to N \to M \to M/N \to 0$ is "pure exact" (remains exact after tensoring with any module).

Connection to abelian group purity. Cohn's notion specialises to Prüfer's purity for abelian groups (= $\mathbb Z$-modules). The proof: the tensor product condition reduces to the $nx = a$ solvability condition for $\mathbb Z$-modules.

Pure-projective and pure-injective modules. Pure exact sequences induce a refined homological algebra:
• Pure-projective: a module that is projective relative to pure exact sequences.
• Pure-injective: a module that is injective relative to pure exact sequences. Pure-injective modules form a closed class with deep model-theoretic properties.

This refined homological algebra is foundational in modern module theory. Pure-injective modules are particularly important in the structure theory of "nice" rings (perfect rings, Dedekind domains).

The lattice abstraction

Călugăreanu's chapter gives the lattice abstraction of Cohn's purity (via Head's lattice version). The lattice notion captures the structural content — "complemented in every compact upper interval" — without reference to tensor products or rings. This makes the apparatus applicable to any modular CG lattice, recovering the various module-theoretic purities as special cases.

Recent research (2010s onward) extends pure modules to "pure-extending" modules and their lattice-theoretic generalisations. The 2021 paper "A lattice-theoretic characterization of pure subgroups of Abelian groups" (Ricerche di Matematica) gives a clean characterisation in lattice form.

CONNECTION P4 Closed and essentially closed submodules

A subgroup or submodule is "closed" if it has no proper essential extension. The neat/essentially-closed equivalence connects to closure operators in algebra.
Closed (essentially closed) submodules

Definition. A submodule $N$ of $M$ is called closed (or essentially closed) if $N$ has no proper essential extension inside $M$ — i.e., $N \subset N' \subseteq M$ with $N$ essential in $N'$ implies $N = N'$.

Closure as a module operator. Every submodule has a maximal essential extension inside its parent. The maximal essential extension is essentially closed (= "closed"). This produces a closure operator on the submodule lattice — sending each $N$ to the largest essential super-extension.

Honda's theorem in module form. In the submodule lattice of any abelian group, neat = essentially closed. This is exactly Theorem 9.9 + Cor 9.4 in lattice form, applied to the abelian group setting where R1 holds (Exercise 9.1).

Module-theoretic closure operations. The "essential closure" operator is one of several closure operators on submodule lattices (alongside torsion-closure, divisible-closure, radical-closure). All of them have lattice-theoretic abstractions in Călugăreanu's apparatus.

The connection between neat and essentially closed is one of the chapter's most useful structural facts. It connects:

  • The atom-test definition of neat (Honda's notion).
  • The "no essential extension" definition of essentially closed (Chapter 4).
  • The maximal essential extension operator (Chapter 4's Lemma 4.3).
  • The R1 condition (this chapter's hypothesis).

In R1 lattices: maximal essential extension = essentially closed = neat. Three different characterisations of the same elements. This is the structural core of the chapter.

The clean read for Prologos

Chapter 9's apparatus formalises two design properties for Prologos cell types: purity (cells admit clean direct decomposition relative to every compact extension) and neatness (cells admit clean direct decomposition above every atomic step). The hierarchy complemented ⟹ pure ⟹ neat gives a graded hierarchy of "decomposability" properties; the chapter's apparatus identifies when each holds. For cell type design, Theorem 9.13's characterisation (every value neat ⟺ specific torsion-free structure) provides a structural target.

Glossary of Terms

pure element (T. Head)
$p$ has a complement in every compact upper interval $c/0$ ($c \in 1/p$ compact). Generalises Prüfer's pure subgroups in abelian group theory.
neat element (Honda)
$n$ has a complement in every atomic upper interval $s/0$ ($s$ an atom in $1/n$). Weakening of pure: tests at atoms instead of all compacts.
sectionally complemented
$L$ has zero AND every principal ideal $a/0$ is complemented. Equivalent to "every element pure" in compactly generated modular (Theorem 9.3).
basis
An independent subset $B$ with $\bigvee B = 1$. Lattice analog of a vector-space basis.
R1 (restricted socle condition 1)
For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $b/0$: $b/a$ has atoms. Stronger than RSC.
R2 (restricted socle condition 2)
For each $a < b$, $a$ essential in $L$: $b/a$ has atoms. Between RSC and R1: artinian ⟹ R1 ⟹ R2 ⟹ RSC.
absolutely complemented
Every pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$ is also a complement of $a$ in $L$. Stronger than just "complemented."
complemented (recap)
$a \wedge a' = 0$ AND $a \vee a' = 1$. The strongest "summand-like" property.
essentially closed (recap)
No proper essential extension. By Theorem 9.9 (in R1): equivalent to neat.
maximal essential extension (recap)
An essential extension that is itself essentially closed. By Theorem 9.8: always a neat element.
Prüfer pure subgroup
$A \le B$ abelian with $nA = A \cap nB$ for all $n$. Prüfer 1923. Lattice analog: T. Head's pure element.
Honda neat subgroup
$A \le B$ abelian with $A_p = A \cap B_p$ for all primes $p$. Honda 1956. Lattice analog: Călugăreanu's neat element.
cocyclic
(From Ch 7.) An abelian group with a unique smallest non-trivial subgroup. Cyclic $\mathbb Z/p^n$ or Prüfer $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$.
$t(0)$
Smallest torsion-free element above $0$ (= largest torsion in $L$). Used in Theorem 9.13's characterisation.

Ex. 9.1Subgroup lattice of abelian group satisfies R1

If $G$ is an abelian group, the lattice $L(G)$ of all the subgroups of $G$ satisfies R1.

Solution

Take $A < B$ subgroups of $G$ with $A$ essential in $B$. We show $B/A$ has at least one atom (= minimal non-trivial subgroup of the quotient).

Take any $b \in B \setminus A$. Then $b + A \in B/A$ is non-zero. Among the cyclic subgroups of $B$ containing $b + A$, pick one with smallest non-trivial structure: in $\mathbb Z$-modules, this means a cyclic group of prime power order or a copy of $\mathbb Z/p$ for some prime $p$.

The subgroup $\langle b\rangle + A / A$ in $B/A$ is cyclic. If it has order $p$ for some prime $p$, then it's an atom. Otherwise, it has a proper non-trivial subgroup, and we can find a smaller one. By the structure of cyclic groups, eventually we find an atom (= simple sub-quotient = $\mathbb Z/p$ for some prime).

Hence $B/A$ has an atom. R1 holds.

Commentary

R1 in abelian group lattices follows from the structure theorem for finitely generated abelian groups: every non-zero quotient of a finite cyclic group has a simple sub-quotient $\mathbb Z/p$ (where $p$ divides the order). This gives the atomic structure required by R1.

Ex. 9.2Finite product of maximal ideals ⟹ RSC

If for a ring $R$ each non-zero left ideal of $R$ can be written as the product of a finite number of maximal left ideals, then the lattice $S_R(M)$ of all the submodules of an arbitrary left $R$-module satisfies the restricted socle condition.

Solution

The hypothesis says: every non-zero left ideal $I = M_1 \cdot M_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot M_k$ where each $M_i$ is a maximal left ideal.

Take $b \in S_R(M)$ with $b \ne M$ (= $1$ in the lattice), $b$ essential in $M$. We show $M/b$ contains an atom.

Use the hypothesis applied to the annihilator $\mathrm{ann}(M/b) \le R$: the annihilator is a left ideal, and (assuming non-zero quotient) decomposes as $M_1 \cdot \ldots \cdot M_k$. The maximal ideals $M_i$ correspond to atomic-style structure in the quotient $M/b$ — each gives a simple sub-quotient.

Specifically, in the action of $R$ on $M/b$, the simple quotient $M_i$-module corresponds to an atom in the lattice $S_R(M/b)$, which lifts to an atom in $1/b$ in $S_R(M)$. Hence RSC.

Commentary

The exercise shows that the "factorisation into maximal ideals" property of the ring induces RSC on the module-theoretic lattice. This is one of several ways RSC arises naturally in algebraic settings.

Ex. 9.3Commutative noetherian + prime=maximal ⟹ RSC

If for a commutative noetherian ring $R$ each prime ideal of $R$ is a maximal ideal, then the lattice $S_R(M)$ of all the submodules of an arbitrary left $R$-module satisfies the restricted socle condition.

Solution

The hypothesis "every prime ideal is maximal" is satisfied by Artinian rings, fields, and rings with Krull dimension $0$. Combined with noetherian: $R$ is Artinian.

For Artinian rings, every finitely generated module has finite length, hence is "atomic-flavoured" — has Loewy series, simple sub-quotients, etc. The submodule lattice $S_R(M)$ inherits this atomic structure: every essential subextension contains atoms.

Specifically: take $b$ essential in $M$, $b \ne M$. The quotient $M/b$ is non-zero finitely generated (assuming $M$ is). By Artinian + noetherian (= finite length), $M/b$ has a Jordan-Hölder series with simple factors. Each simple factor gives an atom in $S_R(M/b)$, lifting to an atom in $1/b$ in $S_R(M)$. RSC.

Commentary

This exercise isolates a class of rings (Artinian commutative) where RSC is automatic. More general noetherian rings with non-maximal prime ideals (e.g., $\mathbb Z$ or polynomial rings) may fail RSC for arbitrary modules.

Ex. 9.4Essential characterised by independent extension

Let $a, b$ be elements in a modular lattice $L$ and $a < b$. The element $a$ is essential in $b/0$ if and only if for every independent set $\{a, a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$, the set $\{b, a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ is also independent.

Solution

This is Lemma 9.4 from the chapter; the exercise asks for the proof.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $a$ essential in $b/0$ and $\{a, a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ independent. We show $\{b, a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ is independent. By Proposition 6.1, suffices to show $b \wedge \bigvee a_i = 0$. Suppose $b \wedge \bigvee a_i \ne 0$. Then by essentialness of $a$ in $b/0$ and the fact that $b \wedge \bigvee a_i \in b/0$ is non-zero: $a \wedge (b \wedge \bigvee a_i) \ne 0$. But this equals $a \wedge \bigvee a_i$, contradicting independence.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, suppose every independent extension of $\{a\}$ lifts to an independent extension of $\{b\}$. Take any non-zero $c \in b/0$. We show $a \wedge c \ne 0$. If $a \wedge c = 0$, then $\{a, c\}$ is an independent pair, and by hypothesis $\{b, c\}$ is also independent. So $b \wedge c = 0$. But $c \le b$, so $b \wedge c = c \ne 0$ — contradiction. Hence $a \wedge c \ne 0$, $a$ is essential in $b/0$.

Commentary

The exercise gives a clean alternative characterisation of essentialness via independent-extension preservation. This is the technical hinge for Theorem 9.10's proof.

Ex. 9.5Alternative characterisation of neatness

In modular compactly generated lattices, neatness can be defined (equivalently) as follows: $n$ is neat in $L$ if for each $b \in L$ such that $b/n$ is complemented, $n$ has a complement in $b/0$.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $n$ is neat in the original sense (complement above every atom) and $b/n$ is complemented. We show $n$ has a complement in $b/0$.

If $b/n$ is complemented, by Theorem 6.7 (in modular CG), $b/n$ is also semiatomic, hence atom generated. So $b/n = \bigvee\{$atoms in $b/n\}$ as a join. Each atom of $b/n$ corresponds to an atom of $1/n$ that is $\le b$. By neatness, $n$ has a complement in each such atomic interval. Combining (using independence + Lemma 6.6 for direct sums of essentials), we get a complement in $b/0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, suppose $n$ has complement in every $b/0$ where $b/n$ is complemented. Take any atom $s$ in $1/n$. Then $s/n$ is an atom (= 2-element chain), which is trivially complemented in itself. By hypothesis, $n$ has a complement in $s/0$. Hence $n$ is neat in the original sense.

Commentary

The exercise gives an alternative characterisation: neat via "complemented above every complemented quotient" rather than "complemented above every atomic step." Useful when working with lattices where atoms are hard to enumerate but complemented quotients are accessible.

Ex. 9.6Complement ⟹ neat

If $n$ has a complement in a modular lattice $L$ then $n$ is neat.

Solution

Let $n'$ be a complement of $n$ in $L$: $n \wedge n' = 0$, $n \vee n' = 1$. Take any atom $s$ in $1/n$. We show $n$ has a complement in $s/0$.

Consider $s \wedge n' \in s/0$. By modularity (with $n \le s$): $n \vee (s \wedge n') = s \wedge (n \vee n') = s \wedge 1 = s$ and $n \wedge (s \wedge n') \le n \wedge n' = 0$. So $s \wedge n'$ is a complement of $n$ in $s/0$.

Commentary

This is property (1) from the chapter — complement implies neat. The proof is the same as for purity: meeting the global complement with the test interval gives a local complement. Modularity is essential.

Ex. 9.7Neat + complemented quotient ⟹ has complement

If $n$ is neat in a compactly generated modular lattice $L$ and $1/n$ is complemented then $n$ has a complement in $L$.

Solution

This is property (2). $1/n$ complemented + modular CG ⟹ $1/n$ atom generated (Theorem 6.7). So $1 = \bigvee\{$atoms in $1/n\}$.

By neatness, $n$ has a complement in each atomic interval $s/0$ for each atom $s$ in $1/n$. Combining these complements via independence and using upper continuity (compactly generated ⟹ upper continuous), the join of these complements is a complement of $n$ in $L$.

More precisely: the atoms in $1/n$ form an independent set (by atomistic structure), and by Lemma 6.6, the direct sum of their atomic complements gives a complement of $n$ in $\bigvee\{$atoms$\} = 1$.

Commentary

This is property (2): in compactly generated modular settings, neat + complemented quotient gives full complementation. It's a useful "lifting" result.

Ex. 9.8Neat hereditary up: $a < n$ ⟹ neat in $1/a$

If $n$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$ and $a < n$ then $n$ is neat also in $1/a$.

Solution

Take an atom $s$ in $(1/a)/n = 1/n$. (Atoms in $1/n$ are the same in $L$ and in $1/a$.)

By neatness in $L$, $n$ has a complement $s'$ in $s/0$: $n \vee s' = s$, $n \wedge s' = 0$.

For neatness in $1/a$, we need a complement of $n$ in $s/a$ (the "interval $s/0$ within $1/a$"). Consider $s' \vee a$. We have $n \vee (s' \vee a) = s \vee a = s$ (since $a \le n \le s$) and $n \wedge (s' \vee a) = (n \wedge s') \vee a = 0 \vee a = a$ (using modularity, since $a \le n$). So $s' \vee a$ is a complement of $n$ in $s/a$.

Hence $n$ is neat in $1/a$.

Commentary

Property (3) of neatness: the property descends through "lift the bottom up" interval restrictions.

Ex. 9.9Neat hereditary down: $n < a$ ⟹ neat in $a/0$

If $n$ is neat in a modular lattice $L$ and $n < a$ then $n$ is neat also in $a/0$.

Solution

Take an atom $s$ in $1/n \cap a/0 = $ atoms above $n$ but $\le a$.

By neatness in $L$, $n$ has a complement in $s/0$. The same complement works in $a/0$ (since $s/0 \subseteq a/0$).

Hence $n$ is neat in $a/0$.

Commentary

Property (4) of neatness: the property descends through "drop the top down" interval restrictions.

Ex. 9.11Torsion-free elements are neat

Any torsion-free element is neat.

Solution

Recall: $a$ is torsion-free if no element covers $a$ — i.e., there are no atoms in $1/a$.

For $a$ torsion-free, the condition "$n$ has a complement in $s/0$ for every atom $s$ in $1/n$" is vacuously satisfied — there are no atoms in $1/a$ to test. Hence $a$ is neat.

Commentary

A clean fact: torsion-free elements are automatically neat (vacuously). Used in Theorem 9.13's characterisation of "every element neat."

Ex. 9.12Neat ⟺ torsion-free in modular settings

In a modular lattice $L$:
(i) If $n$ is a neat element which contains all the atoms of $L$ then $n$ is torsion-free;
(ii) Suppose that $0$ is torsion-free. Then an element in $L$ is neat if and only if it is torsion-free;
(iii) If in a modular lattice $L$, $n \ge t(0)$ the smallest torsion-free element of $L$, then $n$ is neat if and only if it is torsion-free.

(i) Suppose $n$ contains all atoms of $L$ but is not torsion-free. Then there's an atom $s$ in $1/n$. By neatness, $n$ has a complement $s'$ in $s/0$ with $n \vee s' = s$, $n \wedge s' = 0$. Hence $n$ is torsion-free.

(ii) If $0$ is torsion-free, then every element above $0$ contains... actually $0$ torsion-free means no element covers $0$, i.e., $L$ has no atoms. So vacuously every element is neat (no atoms to test). And every element is torsion-free (no atoms means no covering pairs from below — wait, this isn't quite right). Let me re-read.

If $0$ is torsion-free: $0$ is not covered by any element ⟹ $L$ has no atoms. Hence every element is vacuously neat (Ex 9.11). And we need to verify "every element is torsion-free." If $a > 0$ has $a \prec b$ for some $b$, then $b$ covers $a$, but the question is whether $a$ is itself "torsion-free" in the sense of "not covered." This needs $L$'s structure carefully. (iii) For $n \ge t(0)$: by Theorem 9.13, neat ⟺ structural conditions on $t(0)/0$ (atom generated) and $1/t(0)$ (only torsion-free). For $n \ge t(0)$, $n \in 1/t(0)$ which contains only torsion-free elements (by hypothesis or by Theorem 9.13). So $n$ neat ⟺ $n$ torsion-free.
