Lattices of Finite Uniform Dimension

The chapter where Goldie dimension comes into its own. Uniform elements are the irreducible building blocks; finite uniform dimension is the most useful "size" invariant for non-noetherian lattices. Plus the lattice version of the Osofsky–Smith theorem on CS modules.

~70–80 min read 11 sections + 5 connection panels 8 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — uniform dimension as the central invariant

If Chapter 6 introduced Goldie dimension, this chapter unpacks it: characterising it five different ways, exploring how it behaves under direct sums and essential extensions, and culminating in the Osofsky–Smith theorem in lattice form.

Chapter 6 introduced the Goldie dimension as the maximum cardinality of an independent set of uniform elements. Chapter 8 takes that notion as primary and develops a complete theory: the chapter's central Theorem 8.2 gives a six-way characterisation of "$L$ has finite uniform dimension," and subsequent results explore how the dimension behaves across constructions.

Three movements:

  1. Uniform elements (Part A). An element $a$ is uniform (or co-irreducible) if every two non-zero elements below it have non-zero meet — equivalently, every non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$. Uniform elements are the indecomposable building blocks of dimension theory. Theorem 8.1 establishes the invariance: any two finite maximal independent subsets of uniform elements have the same cardinality, defining the dimension.
  2. The six-way characterisation (Part B). Theorem 8.2 lists six equivalent conditions for "$L$ has finite uniform dimension" — finite cardinality of independent sets, ACC for finite direct sums (ACC⊕), eventual essentialness in chains, no infinite independent subsets, $0$ as a finite meet of irreducibles. Six different lenses on the same property.
  3. Dimension behaviour, essential closure, and CS lattices (Parts C–E). Proposition 8.1 records how dimension interacts with direct sums and essential elements. Theorem 8.3 bounds dimension via $a/0$ and $1/a$. Proposition 8.2 characterises essentially closed elements via dimension equality. Proposition 8.3 ties finite dimension to ACC/DCC for essentially closed. The chapter closes with CS (= extending) lattices — those where every pseudo-complement is a complement — and the Osofsky–Smith theorem (Theorem 8.4): a strong sufficient condition for "every cyclic-style structure decomposes into uniforms." Originally a deep theorem about CS modules in ring theory; here proved in pure lattice form.
The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

Finite uniform dimension is the lattice analog of "module of finite Goldie rank" — the right notion for non-noetherian module theory. Theorem 8.2's six-way equivalence shows that "having finite dimension," "satisfying ACC for direct sums," and "$0$ being a finite irredundant meet of irreducibles" are all the same property in upper continuous modular lattices. The dimension is additive under finite direct sums, preserved by essential extensions, and bounded above by the sum of dimensions of $a/0$ and $1/a$. CS lattices — where pseudo-complements coincide with complements — generalise the well-studied CS modules; the Osofsky–Smith theorem says: in a "completely CS" compact lattice satisfying technical conditions (RSC, H-noetherian), every element decomposes as a finite direct sum of uniforms.

Why this chapter matters

This is the chapter where Călugăreanu's lattice apparatus most directly competes with modern non-commutative ring theory. Goldie dimension, CS modules, and the Osofsky–Smith theorem are all foundational results of 20th-century module theory. The chapter shows they're all special cases of pure lattice statements — the algebraic content reducing to a question about modular upper continuous lattices. For Prologos, finite uniform dimension is the lattice-theoretic capacity-bound on parallel decomposition.

New · Ch 8 · CENTRAL

uniform (= co-irreducible) An element $a \in L$ is uniform if for every $b, c \in L$: $0 < b \le a$, $0 < c \le a$ implies $b \wedge c \ne 0$. Plain English: every two non-zero elements below $a$ have non-zero meet — i.e., every non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$. Some authors (reference [33] in the book) use "co-irreducible" as a synonym.

Equivalent characterisations:
• Each non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$.
• $0$ is meet-irreducible in $a/0$.
• All lower bounds of $a$ are indecomposable (Remark 8.1).
• $a$ has Goldie dimension 1.

New · Ch 8 · CENTRAL

finite uniform (Goldie) dimension $\dim L = n$ A modular UC lattice $L$ has finite uniform dimension $n$ if there is a maximal independent subset of uniform elements of cardinality $n$ in $L$. By Theorem 8.1, $n$ is well-defined (independent of which maximal independent set you pick).

New · Ch 8

ACC⊕ (ACC for finite direct sums) The ascending chain condition for finite direct sums: every increasing chain $a_1 \le a_1 \oplus a_2 \le a_1 \oplus a_2 \oplus a_3 \le \cdots$ of finite direct sums stabilises after finitely many steps. By Theorem 8.2(d), equivalent to finite uniform dimension.

New · Ch 8 · CHAPTER PUNCHLINE

CS lattice (= extending) A bounded lattice $L$ is CS (or extending) if each pseudo-complement of an element is a complement. Plain English: "the maximal-disjoint partner is a perfect partner." By Lemma 8.3: $L$ is CS iff every element is essential in a direct summand iff every maximal essential extension is a direct summand. The lattice version of CS modules in ring theory.

New · Ch 8

completely CS A lattice $L$ is completely CS if all the quotient sublattices $1/a$ for $a \in L$ are CS. So CS holds in every "upper world." Required hypothesis for the Osofsky-Smith Theorem 8.4.

New · Ch 8

essentially compact element An element $a \in L$ is essentially compact if it contains a compact element $c$ that is essential in $a/0$. Plain English: "$a$ has a finite-witness essential core." Lemma 8.4: in compactly generated modular lattices, finite uniform dimension iff every element is essentially compact.

New · Ch 8

co-irreducible Synonym for uniform (in reference [33]). The "co" prefix because it's the order-dual of irreducible: $0$ is irreducible in $a/0$ ⟺ $a$ is uniform; $a$ is irreducible ⟺ $1$ is uniform in $1/a$.

Deep dive — Uniform modules in module theory

The motivating example. Uniform modules are the "atomic" units of Goldie dimension theory.
Deep dive · Uniform modules

Definition. An $R$-module $M$ is uniform if every non-zero submodule is essential in $M$. Equivalently: any two non-zero submodules have non-zero intersection.

Examples.
• Vector spaces. A vector space $V$ over a field $k$ is uniform iff $\dim V \le 1$ (since two distinct $1$-dimensional subspaces intersect trivially).
• $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Q$. Uniform — every non-zero subgroup is essential (every non-zero submodule contains some $n/m \cdot \mathbb Z$, all of which intersect non-trivially).
• $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. Uniform — the Prüfer $p$-group has nested non-zero submodules, all essential.
• $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z$. Uniform — for any non-zero subgroups $a\mathbb Z$ and $b\mathbb Z$, their intersection is $\mathrm{lcm}(a,b)\mathbb Z \ne 0$.
• $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z \oplus \mathbb Z$. NOT uniform — the two factors $\mathbb Z e_1$ and $\mathbb Z e_2$ have trivial intersection.

The connection to torsion / torsion-free. An abelian group is uniform iff it's "indecomposably essential":
• Cocyclic groups (cyclic $\mathbb Z/p^n$ or Prüfer $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$): always uniform (Exercise 8.1).
• Torsion-free abelian groups: uniform iff rank 1 (i.e., embedded in $\mathbb Q$). Exercise 8.2.
• Mixed groups (with both torsion and torsion-free elements): never uniform. Exercise 8.3.

Why uniform modules matter. They are the "building blocks" of Goldie dimension theory: any module of finite Goldie dimension is essentially a direct sum of uniforms (more precisely, contains an essential direct sum of finitely many uniforms). The number of uniforms in such a decomposition is the Goldie dimension. The injective hull $E(M)$ of a uniform module $M$ is also uniform; the injective hull of a finite-Goldie-dimension module is a finite direct sum of indecomposable injectives (each of which is uniform).

The lattice abstraction. Călugăreanu's chapter abstracts this entirely: in any modular UC lattice, "uniform" means every non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$. The dimension theory works in this abstract setting, recovering module-theoretic Goldie dimension as a special case.

Deep dive — Goldie dimension theory in modern algebra

Building on Chapter 6's primer, here's how Goldie dimension is used in modern non-commutative ring theory.
Deep dive · Goldie dimension and Goldie's theorems

Goldie's setting. Alfred Goldie introduced the dimension in 1958-60 to study non-commutative noetherian rings. The dimension is the right replacement for "rank" in the non-noetherian setting: it measures the number of indecomposable essential pieces.

The fundamental theorem. A ring $R$ is right Goldie (= $R_R$ has finite Goldie dimension AND ACC on annihilators) and semiprime iff $R$ has a classical right ring of fractions $Q$ that is semisimple Artinian (Goldie 1958, 1960).

Why Goldie dimension instead of length. For a noetherian module, length is a fine invariant but doesn't apply to non-noetherian modules. Goldie dimension applies more broadly:
• Length is finite ⟺ module is artinian + noetherian.
• Goldie dimension is finite ⟺ module has no infinite direct sum of non-zero submodules.
The second condition is much weaker. Many "interesting" non-noetherian modules (e.g., torsion-free abelian groups of finite rank) have finite Goldie dimension.

The injective hull connection. If $M$ has finite Goldie dimension $n$, then the injective hull $E(M) = E_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus E_n$ is a finite direct sum of indecomposable injective modules, each of which is uniform. The number $n$ is independent of the decomposition (Krull–Schmidt-style uniqueness).

Local and Goldie dimensions. For commutative noetherian rings, the Krull dimension and Goldie dimension are related but distinct: Krull dimension is the supremum of chain lengths of prime ideals; Goldie dimension is the rank of the module. For most natural modules, Goldie dimension = Krull dimension; equality holds in particular for modules over noetherian commutative rings.

Applications. Goldie dimension is fundamental in:
• Localisation theory of non-commutative rings.
• Structure theory of CS and continuous modules.
• Classification of injective modules.
• Verification of "Krull–Schmidt" properties for finite-Goldie-dimension settings.

Deep dive — CS modules and the Osofsky–Smith theorem

A landmark theorem in module theory from the 1970s, generalised here to lattices.
Deep dive · CS modules and Osofsky–Smith

CS modules. A module $M$ is CS (or extending) if every submodule of $M$ is essential in a direct summand. Equivalently: every closed (= essentially closed) submodule is a direct summand. Equivalently: every pseudo-complement is a complement.

The class of CS modules sits between "semisimple" (every submodule is a direct summand — strongest) and "uniform" (every non-zero submodule is essential — weakest):
• Semisimple ⟹ CS (every submodule has a complement, hence is essential in itself = direct summand).
• Uniform ⟹ CS (every non-zero submodule is essential in $M$ = the only direct summand other than $0$).
• Direct sum of semisimples and uniforms ⟹ CS.
The CS class is much broader than semisimple but retains many useful properties.

The Osofsky–Smith theorem (1991). Osofsky and Smith proved a powerful structural result: if every cyclic submodule of every quotient module of $M$ is CS, then $M$ is a finite direct sum of uniform modules. This forces finite Goldie dimension for cyclic modules with strong "completely CS" properties.

Why this is striking. The condition "every cyclic submodule of every quotient is CS" is a hereditary-property condition that doesn't directly mention dimension. The conclusion — "finite direct sum of uniforms," equivalent to "finite Goldie dimension" — is a strong dimensional statement. Osofsky–Smith bridges the gap.

The lattice version (Theorem 8.4 of this chapter). Călugăreanu lifts the result to compact modular compactly generated H-noetherian lattices satisfying RSC, with the "completely CS" hypothesis on every $x/0$. The conclusion: $L$ has finite uniform dimension. Originally a deep theorem about CS modules; here proved in pure lattice form.

Generalisations. Albu (2011) and Crivei–Năstăsescu have given further generalisations of Osofsky–Smith for modular lattices, bridging deeper to module theory. The 2002 Bull. Austral. Math. Soc. paper continues the program. Multiple research articles in the 2010s and 2020s extend the theorem to pure-extending modules, perfect rings, and various module-theoretic generalisations.

Why this matters. The Osofsky–Smith theorem is one of the deeper results of 20th-century module theory, and Călugăreanu's chapter shows it's fundamentally a lattice-theoretic statement. The non-commutative algebra reduces to a question about compact modular CG H-noetherian lattices satisfying RSC.

That's the vocabulary. The chapter prose ahead refers to these terms freely.

Part A · Uniform elements

The atomic units of dimension theory

Definition, basic properties, and the foundational invariance result (Theorem 8.1) that lets us talk about "the dimension."

SECTION A1 Definition + Remark 8.1 — uniform elements

An element is uniform when every two non-zero elements below it have non-zero meet. Equivalently: 0 is irreducible in $a/0$. Equivalent characterisations.
Definition · uniform (= co-irreducible)

An element $a \in L$ is called uniform (or co-irreducible, [33]) if for every $b, c \in L$ the following implication holds: $$0 < b \le a, \; 0 < c \le a \Rightarrow b \wedge c \ne 0.$$ Equivalently: every non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$.

Plain English: $a$ is uniform if it has no internal "independence" — every two non-zero pieces below $a$ overlap. The element $a$ acts as a "single thread" of structure rather than a bundle of independent strands.

Equivalent characterisations

The book records four equivalent forms of "uniform":

  1. $a$ is uniform: every two non-zero elements below $a$ have non-zero meet.
  2. Every non-zero element of $a/0$ is essential in $a/0$.
  3. $0$ is irreducible in $a/0$ (i.e., $0 = b \wedge c$ in $a/0$ implies $b = 0$ or $c = 0$).
  4. (Dually) $a$ is irreducible in $L$ iff $1$ is uniform in $1/a$.

The book also records: each lower bound of a uniform element is uniform. So uniformity is hereditary downward — if $a$ is uniform and $b \le a$, then $b$ is uniform.

And: a lattice $L$ is called uniform if $1$ is uniform in $L$. So uniform lattice = "$0$ is irreducible in $L$," which is the order-dual of "$L$ is irreducible."

Remark 8.1

An element $a \in L$ is uniform if and only if all its lower bounds are indecomposable.

Recall (Ch 6): an element is indecomposable if it cannot be written as a non-trivial direct sum. Remark 8.1 says: $a$ uniform ⟺ every $b \le a$ is indecomposable. The reasoning: if $b \le a$ has a direct decomposition $b = c \oplus d$ with $c, d \ne 0$, then $c \wedge d = 0$ — contradicting $a$ uniform. Conversely, if all lower bounds are indecomposable, two non-zero pieces with trivial meet would generate a direct sum below $a$, again contradicting indecomposability.

Examples

Atoms. Every atom is uniform: an atom $a$ has $a/0 = \{0, a\}$, only one non-zero element ($a$ itself), trivially uniform.

Vector space subspace lattice. A subspace $V$ is uniform in $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$ iff $\dim V \le 1$ — for $\dim V \ge 2$, two distinct $1$-dimensional subspaces have trivial intersection.

$\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Q$. $\mathbb Q$ is uniform (every non-zero subgroup is essential).

$\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z$. Uniform (any two non-zero ideals $a\mathbb Z$ and $b\mathbb Z$ have $\mathrm{lcm}(a,b)\mathbb Z \ne 0$ as their intersection).

$\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. The Prüfer group is uniform — submodules form a chain, all essential.

SECTION A2 Theorem 8.1 — invariance of dimension

In an upper continuous modular lattice, any two finite maximal independent subsets of uniform elements have the same cardinality. This invariance is what lets us talk about "the dimension."
Theorem 8.1

In an upper continuous modular lattice with $0$ and $1$, each two finite maximal independent subsets of uniform elements have the same cardinality.

Why · reduction to Kuros–Ore

The proof reduces to Kuros–Ore (Theorem 3.3 of Chapter 3). Take two maximal independent subsets of uniform elements: $\bigvee_{i=1}^m a_i$ and $\bigvee_{j=1}^n b_j$, both essential direct sums (essentialness comes from maximality + Theorem 6.1).

Set $\bar a_t = \bigvee_{i \ne t} a_i$ for each $1 \le t \le m$. The independence of $\{a_i\}$ gives $a_t \wedge \bar a_t = 0$. By upper continuity and Zorn's Lemma, there is an element $c_t$ maximal relative to the properties $a_t \wedge c_t = 0$ and $\bar a_t \le c_t$.

Verify three claims:
(i) $c_t$ is irreducible in $L$ (using maximality of $c_t$);
(ii) $\bigwedge_{t=1}^m c_t = 0$ (using $u = \bigvee a_i$ essential and modularity);
(iii) The meet is irredundant (using $a_k \le \bigwedge_{t \ne k} c_t$).

So $\{c_t\}_{t=1}^m$ is an irredundant meet of irreducibles representing $0$ — i.e., $0 = c_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge c_m$ irredundantly.

Similarly the independent family $\{b_j\}_{j=1}^n$ produces $\{d_t\}_{t=1}^n$ — an irredundant meet of $n$ irreducibles for $0$.

By the Kuros–Ore Theorem (3.3): $m = n$.

Definition · finite uniform dimension

In the hypothesis of the previous theorem, we say that $L$ has finite uniform (= co-irreducible or Goldie) dimension $n$. We denote this by $\dim L = n$.

Why this matters

Theorem 8.1 establishes that the integer $n$ is well-defined — independent of the choice of maximal independent subset of uniforms. This is what lets us refer to "the dimension" of $L$. Without invariance, we'd have multiple competing values; with invariance, $\dim L$ is a clean numerical invariant.

The proof's reduction to Kuros–Ore is the chapter's first instance of "dimension theory descends from decomposition theory." The number of pieces in any irredundant meet of irreducibles equals the dimension. This is the lattice form of "the number of indecomposable summands in an injective hull."

SECTION A3 Lemma 8.1 — irreducibles in sublattices

A technical lemma: in bounded modular UC lattices, irreducibility in $b/0$ corresponds to "meeting an irreducible of $L$ with $b$."
Lemma 8.1

Let $L$ be a bounded modular upper continuous lattice and $a \le b$. Then $a$ is irreducible in $b/0$ if and only if there exists an irreducible element $t$ in $L$ such that $a = b \wedge t$.

Why

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $a$ is irreducible in $b/0$, choose $t \in L$ maximal with the property $a = b \wedge t$. Such $t$ exists by Zorn's Lemma applied to $\mathcal A = \{x \in L : a = b \wedge x\}$ (using upper continuity to ensure chain-suprema stay in $\mathcal A$). Show $t$ is irreducible in $L$: if $t = u \wedge v$, then $a = b \wedge t = (b \wedge u) \wedge (b \wedge v)$. Irreducibility of $a$ in $b/0$ gives $a = b \wedge u$ or $a = b \wedge v$. By maximality of $t$ — say in the first case — $t = u$. Hence $t$ is irreducible.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, if $t$ is irreducible in $L$ and $a = b \wedge t$: suppose $a = u \wedge v$ for some $u, v \in b/0$. By modularity (using $b/(b \wedge t) \cong (b \vee t)/t$ from Theorem 1.5, with the iso $f(s) = t \vee s$), we get $t = (t \vee u) \wedge (t \vee v)$. Irreducibility of $t$ gives $t = t \vee u$ or $t = t \vee v$, i.e., $u \le t$ or $v \le t$. Combined with $u, v \le b$: $u = b \wedge t = a$ or $v = b \wedge t = a$. So $a$ is irreducible in $b/0$.

Lemma 8.1 is a "lift and project" rule for irreducibility: irreducibles in $b/0$ correspond to ambient irreducibles intersected with $b$. Used in the proof of Theorem 8.2 below.

Part B · Theorem 8.2 — the six-way characterisation

Six equivalent conditions for finite uniform dimension

The chapter's central theorem. Six different lenses on "L has finite uniform dimension," all equivalent in upper continuous modular lattices.

SECTION B1 Theorem 8.2 — the equivalence

Six conditions, all equivalent, characterising finite uniform dimension. The chapter's keystone.
Theorem 8.2

In an upper continuous modular lattice $L$ the following conditions are equivalent:

(a) $L$ has finite uniform dimension;

(b) The cardinality of the independent subsets of $L$ is bounded (in $\mathbb N$);

(c) If $a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots \le a_m \le \cdots$ there is an $i \in \mathbb N$ such that for each $j \ge i$, $a_i$ is essential in $a_j/0$;

(d) $L$ satisfies the ACC⊕, i.e., the ascending chain condition for finite direct sums;

(e) $L$ does not contain infinite independent subsets;

(f) $0$ is a finite meet of irreducible elements.

Why · the equivalence chain

The book proves (a) ⟹ (b) ⟹ (c) ⟹ (d) ⟹ (e) ⟹ (a) and (a) ⟺ (f).

(a) ⟹ (b): If $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ is a maximal independent set of uniforms with $\bigvee a_i$ essential, and $\{b_1, \ldots, b_k\}$ is independent with $k > n$, by an inductive construction (using a refinement procedure), we'd produce a non-zero element with trivial meet against $\bigvee a_i$, contradicting essentialness. So independent sets have cardinality $\le n$.

(b) ⟹ (c): If (c) fails, build an infinite independent set: for an ascending chain $a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots$ where no $a_i$ is essential in all later $a_j$, extract non-zero $b_i$'s with $b_i \wedge a_{f(i)} = 0$ for an appropriate index $f(i)$. The set $\{b_i\}$ is infinite and independent (by Proposition 6.1), contradicting (b).

(c) ⟹ (d): If $\bigoplus_{i=1}^n a_i$ is essential in $(\bigoplus_{i=1}^{n+k} a_i)/0$, then $\bigoplus_{i=n+1}^{n+k} a_i = 0$ (since meets of independent sets are zero) — so the corresponding ascending chain of finite direct sums is finite. Hence ACC⊕ holds.

(d) ⟹ (e): If $\{a_i\}_{i \in \mathbb N}$ is an infinite independent set in $L$, then $a_0 < a_0 \oplus a_1 < a_0 \oplus a_1 \oplus a_2 < \cdots$ is an infinite ascending chain of finite direct joins — contradicting ACC⊕.

(e) ⟹ (a): First show every non-zero element contains a uniform element (call this condition $(\alpha)$). Then by Zorn's Lemma applied to independent sets of uniforms, pick a maximal one $U_0$. The element $a = \bigoplus U_0$ is essential in $L$ (since otherwise we could add another uniform to $U_0$). Hence $L$ has finite uniform dimension $|U_0|$.

(a) ⟺ (f): Finite uniform dim ⟹ $0$ is finite meet of irreducibles (use the irreducibles $c_t$ from Theorem 8.1's proof). Conversely, $0 = a_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n$ irredundant ⟹ each $\bar a_i = \bigwedge_{j \ne i} a_j$ is uniform in $L$, and by structural argument, $\dim L = n$.

Why six conditions

Each condition captures a different facet of "finite dimensionality":
• (a) Direct: existence of a finite essential decomposition into uniforms.
• (b) Quantitative: bounded independent-set cardinality.
• (c) Chain: every chain stabilises (in the essential sense).
• (d) ACC⊕: chain condition for direct sums.
• (e) Negative: no infinite independent sets.
• (f) Decomposition: $0$ is finite meet of irreducibles.
The equivalence of all six is the chapter's central insight: finite-dimensionality has many equivalent witnesses, and any one suffices to certify the others.

The book also notes that the proof leaves open a "condition $(\alpha)$": each non-zero element contains a uniform element. The remark at the end discusses how (a) ⟹ ($\alpha$) and (e) ⟹ ($\alpha$) are both verifiable (by induction on dimension and by independent-set construction respectively), and the relationship between $(\alpha)$ and conditions (a)-(f) is structural.

Part C · Dimension behavior

How the dimension interacts with direct sums and essentialness

Three key properties (Prop 8.1) and a fundamental bounding theorem (Theorem 8.3 + Cor 8.1).

SECTION C1 Proposition 8.1 — three properties of dimension

Three useful facts: how dim ≤ n manifests, how essentialness preserves dimension, how direct sums make dimensions add.
Proposition 8.1

(i) $\dim L \le n$ if and only if each independent subset of elements in $L$ has at most $n$ elements if and only if for each $a > 0$, $0$ is an irredundant meet of at most $n$ irreducible elements from $a/0$.

(ii) If $a \in L$ is essential in $L$, then $L$ has finite dimension if and only if $a/0$ has finite dimension. Moreover, these two dimensions are equal. Conversely, if $L$ has finite uniform dimension and for an element $a \in L$ the equality $\dim L = \dim(a/0)$ holds, then $a$ is essential in $L$.

(iii) If $1 = \bigoplus_{i \in I} a_i$, for a finite set $I$, then $L$ has finite dimension if and only if $a_i/0$ have finite dimension, $\forall i \in I$. Moreover, $\dim L = \sum_{i \in I} \dim(a_i/0)$.

Three structural facts about dimension:

(i) Bounded dimension corresponds to bounded independent-set cardinality. $\dim L \le n$ is the same as "no independent set has more than $n$ elements." And it's the same as "$0$ has an irredundant meet of $n$ or fewer irreducibles in every $a/0$."

(ii) Essential subsets preserve dimension. If $a$ is essential, $\dim L = \dim(a/0)$. The converse: if dimensions agree, $a$ is essential. So essentialness and dimension-equality are tied.

(iii) Direct sums add dimensions. $\dim(a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_n) = \dim(a_1/0) + \cdots + \dim(a_n/0)$. The dimension is additive over finite direct sums — exactly as vector space dimension is.

SECTION C2 Theorem 8.3 + Corollary 8.1 — the dimension bound

For any element $a$ in upper continuous modular $L$, the dimension of $L$ is bounded by the dimensions of $a/0$ and $1/a$.
Theorem 8.3

Let $a \in L$ be an element in an upper continuous modular lattice. If $a/0$ and $1/a$ have finite dimension then $L$ has also finite dimension and $$\dim(a/0) \le \dim L \le \dim(a/0) + \dim(1/a).$$

Why · pseudo-complement bridge

Any upper continuous lattice is pseudo-complemented (Cor 4.1), so let $b$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$. By Cor 4.2, $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$. Using Prop 8.1(ii) (essential preserves dim): $\dim L = \dim(a \vee b)/0 = \dim a/0 + \dim b/0$ (using Prop 8.1(iii) on the direct sum $a \oplus b = a \vee b$).

Using modularity $b/0 = b/(a \wedge b) \cong (a \vee b)/a \subseteq 1/a$, we have $\dim b/0 \le \dim 1/a$. Hence $\dim L \le \dim(a/0) + \dim(1/a)$.

The lower bound $\dim a/0 \le \dim L$ is from monotonicity: any independent set in $a/0$ is an independent set in $L$.

The theorem says: the dimension of $L$ is at most additive across the partition $a$, $1/a$. Equality holds when $b$ (the pseudo-complement of $a$) saturates $1/a$ — i.e., when $a$ is "essentially closed" — see Proposition 8.2 below.

Corollary 8.1

If $a_1 \wedge a_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n = 0$ holds in a modular lattice $L$ then $\dim L \le \sum_{i=1}^n \dim(1/a_i)$.

Iteratively applying Theorem 8.3: when $a_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n = 0$, the dimensions of the upper intervals $1/a_i$ sum to bound the dimension of $L$. The induction sets $\bar a_1 = a_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n$, applies Theorem 8.3 to $a_1$ to get $\dim L \le \dim(a_1/0) + \dim(1/a_1)$, and applies the inductive hypothesis to $a_1/0$ (which is the same as $\bar a_1/0$ in the right setup) to bound $\dim(a_1/0)$ by $\sum_{i=2}^n \dim(1/a_i)$.

A concrete instance

For $L = \mathrm{Sub}(V)$ where $V = \mathbb F^n$: $\dim L = n$ (the Goldie dimension equals the linear dimension). If $\{H_1, \ldots, H_k\}$ are hyperplanes (codimension-1 subspaces) with trivial intersection, then $\sum \dim(1/H_i) \ge n$ (each $\dim 1/H_i = 1$, since $V/H_i$ is one-dimensional). Cor 8.1 then says $n \le \sum 1 = k$, so $k \ge n$. This is the classical fact: $n$ hyperplanes are needed to have trivial intersection in $n$-space.

Part D · Essential closure and dimension

When is the upper bound achieved? When does ACC for essentially closed equate to finite dimension?

Two propositions completing the dimension theory: Prop 8.2 (essentially closed iff dim equality) and Prop 8.3 (finite dim iff ACC/DCC for essentially closed).

SECTION D1 Proposition 8.2 — essentially closed via dimension

A clean equivalence: in pseudo-complemented modular lattices with finite uniform dimension, an element is essentially closed iff dim L = dim(a/0) + dim(1/a).
Proposition 8.2

Let $L$ be a bounded modular pseudo-complemented lattice with finite uniform dimension. An element $a \in L$ is essentially closed if and only if $a/0$ and $1/a$ both have finite uniform dimension and $\dim L = \dim(a/0) + \dim(1/a)$.

Why · two halves

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $a$ is essentially closed: by Prop 4.4 (Ch 4) we already know $a/0$ has finite dim; if $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$, $a \oplus b$ is essential in $L$ and in $1/b$. Apply Prop 8.1(iii) and (ii) to conclude $\dim 1/b = \dim((a \oplus b)/0) = \dim a/0 + \dim b/0$, and the latter equals $\dim 1/a$. Combine via the diamond iso to get $\dim L = \dim a/0 + \dim 1/a$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, suppose $a/0$ and $1/a$ have finite dim and $\dim L = \dim(a/0) + \dim(1/a)$. If $e \in L$ is essential with $a \le e$: by Prop 8.1(ii), $\dim e/0 = \dim L$. So $\dim a/0 + \dim 1/a = \dim e/0 \le \dim a/0 + \dim e/a$ (Theorem 8.3 applied to $e/0$ with $a$ inside). Hence $\dim 1/a \le \dim e/a$. Since $e/a \subseteq 1/a$, equality holds: $\dim e/a = \dim 1/a$. By Prop 8.1(ii) applied inside $1/a$, $e$ is essential in $1/a$. Then by Exercise 4.12, $a$ is essentially closed in $L$.

Proposition 8.2 ties the dimension equality $\dim L = \dim(a/0) + \dim(1/a)$ to the structural notion of essential closure. Equality holds iff $a$ is essentially closed. When equality fails, $a$ is "missing some essential extension up there" — and the gap is the difference $\dim L - \dim(a/0) - \dim(1/a)$.

SECTION D2 Proposition 8.3 + Lemma 8.2 — chain conditions on essentially closed

Finite uniform dimension is equivalent to ACC for essentially closed elements (or DCC). Plus a useful lemma for s(L)-style transfers.
Proposition 8.3

For a bounded modular upper continuous lattice $L$ the following conditions are equivalent:
(a) $L$ has finite uniform dimension;
(b) $L$ satisfies the ACC for essentially closed elements;
(c) $L$ satisfies the DCC for essentially closed elements.

Why

(a) ⟹ (b),(c): For two distinct essentially closed elements $a < b$ in $L$, $a$ is essentially closed in $b/0$ (by Exercise 4.13), and then by Prop 8.2, $\dim a/0 < \dim b/0$. So strictly ascending or descending sequences of essentially closed elements correspond to strictly ascending or descending sequences of positive integers bounded by $\dim L$. Bounded integer sequences are finite — so ACC and DCC both hold.

(b) ⟹ (a): Suppose $L$ has an infinite independent set $\{a_n\}$. Take pseudo-complements: $b_0$ a pseudo-complement of $a_1 \oplus a_2 \oplus \cdots$ contains $a_0 \oplus a_1$; $b_1$ a pseudo-complement of $a_2 \oplus a_3 \oplus \cdots$ contains $b_0 \oplus a_1$; etc. The sequence $\{b_n\}$ of pseudo-complements (= essentially closed elements by Cor 4.3) is strictly increasing — contradicting ACC.

(c) ⟹ (a): Analogous descending argument.

Combined with Theorem 8.2, Proposition 8.3 gives yet another characterisation: finite dimension = ACC (or DCC) for the essentially closed elements specifically. So you don't need ACC on all elements — just on the essentially closed ones.

Lemma 8.2

In a compactly generated modular lattice $L$, if all the quotient sublattices $1/a$ for $a \in L$, $a$ essential in $L$, have finite uniform dimension, then $1/s(L)$ has also finite uniform dimension.

The lemma transfers finite-dimensionality from "above all essentials" to "above the socle." Useful in compactly generated modular lattices when working with the radical / socle structure. The proof uses the fact that $s(L)$ has a pseudo-complement and a series of dimension calculations from Theorem 8.3.

Part E · CS lattices and the Osofsky–Smith theorem

Variations on a theorem from module theory

CS (= extending) lattices: where pseudo-complements coincide with complements. Plus the lattice form of the Osofsky–Smith theorem (Theorem 8.4).

SECTION E1 CS / extending lattices — definition and Lemma 8.3

A bounded lattice is CS if every pseudo-complement is a complement. Three equivalent characterisations.
Definition · CS lattice (= extending)

A bounded lattice $L$ is called CS (or extending) if each pseudo-complement of an element is a complement.

Recall: a pseudo-complement is maximal with $a \wedge c = 0$; a complement requires also $a \vee c = 1$. CS asks the maximal-disjoint partner to also be a "covering partner."

Lemma 8.3

A modular upper continuous lattice $L$ is CS if and only if each element is essential in a direct summand, or, if and only if every maximal essential extension is a direct summand.

Why · three equivalences

The first equivalence (CS ⟺ each element essential in direct summand) follows from Lemma 4.3: in upper continuous modular, the maximal essential extension exists and is essentially closed = pseudo-complement. CS says this pseudo-complement is also a complement, i.e., the original element is essential in the direct summand.

The second (every maximal essential extension is direct summand): if $m$ is a maximal essential extension of $a$, by hypothesis $m$ is essential in a direct summand $b$. By transitivity, $a$ is essential in $b$, and $m = b$ by maximality. So $m$ is a direct summand.

The conversely-statement is symmetric.

Why CS is a useful condition

CS lattices are "completion-friendly": every element can be extended to a direct summand essentially. In module theory, CS modules generalise injective modules and quasi-injective modules. The class is closed under direct sums (in many settings) and behaves well under restriction.

The "extending" terminology highlights the key property: every element has an "extension" (in the essentialness sense) to a direct summand. The "CS" terminology comes from "closed submodules are summands" — equivalent for CS modules.

SECTION E2 Definitions + Lemma 8.4 — completely CS, essentially compact

Two more definitions: completely CS (CS in every upper interval) and essentially compact (contains a compact element essential below). Plus Lemma 8.4 connecting essentially compact to finite dimension.
Definition · completely CS

A lattice $L$ is called completely CS if all the quotient sublattices $1/a$, $a \in L$, are CS.

Definition · essentially compact element

An element $a \in L$ is called essentially compact if it contains a compact element $c$ essential in $a/0$.

Completely CS is a stronger requirement than CS — it asks for the property in every upper interval. Essentially compact is a "finite-witness essentialness" condition: there's a compact (= finite-witness) core that essentially generates $a$.

Lemma 8.4

A modular compactly generated lattice $L$ has finite uniform dimension if and only if $L$ contains only essentially compact elements.

The lemma is the analog of Proposition 8.3 for the compact-generated setting: finite dimension corresponds to "every element has a compact essential core." The proof reduces to ACC⊕ via Theorem 8.2's equivalences.

Reading Lemma 8.4

"Essentially compact" is a finite-flavour version of "compact essential": every element has compact "essence" inside it. This is what lets the chain conditions (ACC⊕) translate to dimension bounds in the compactly generated setting.

SECTION E3 Theorem 8.4 — Osofsky–Smith for lattices

The chapter's closing result. The lattice form of the Osofsky-Smith theorem: in compact modular CG H-noetherian RSC lattices with completely CS principal ideals, the lattice has finite uniform dimension.
Proposition 8.4

Let $L$ be a compact modular compactly generated H-noetherian lattice satisfying RSC such that for each element $x$ the sublattice $x/0$ is completely CS. Then $L$ satisfies ACC⊕.

The book defers the detailed proof to a module-theoretic version (reference [17], 7.12 — Mohamed–Müller, Continuous and Discrete Modules, Cambridge University Press 1990). The lattice version is the abstract scaffold supporting the module-theoretic Osofsky–Smith theorem.

Theorem 8.4 (Osofsky–Smith)

Let $L$ be a compact modular compactly generated H-noetherian lattice satisfying RSC, such that for each element $x$, the sublattice $x/0$ is completely CS. Then $L$ is a finite direct sum of uniform elements (has finite uniform dimension).

Why · Proposition 8.4 + Theorem 8.2(d)

Proposition 8.4 gives ACC⊕. By Theorem 8.2 (a) ⟺ (d), ACC⊕ is equivalent to finite uniform dimension. Hence $L$ has finite dimension — i.e., $L$ contains a finite essential direct sum of uniform elements.

The historical context

Osofsky and Smith (1991) proved a deep theorem: every cyclic module whose every cyclic submodule of every quotient is CS is a finite direct sum of uniforms. The completely CS hypothesis is the key — it forces a strong "completion" structure that, combined with H-noetherian and RSC, gives finite Goldie dimension.

Călugăreanu's Theorem 8.4 lifts this to abstract lattices. The hypotheses look complex (compact + modular + CG + H-noetherian + RSC + completely CS), but each is needed:
• Compact: $1$ is compact (Krull's lemma applies).
• Modular + CG: The structural foundation for direct decompositions.
• H-noetherian: Compact elements form an ideal.
• RSC: Every essential element below 1 has atoms above.
• Completely CS: Every $x/0$ has the strong "essential = direct summand" property.
The conclusion is finite uniform dimension — the lattice analog of "the module is a finite direct sum of uniforms."

Recent extensions: Albu (2011) gives an even more general lattice version; Crivei et al. continue to refine. The 2020s have seen further generalisations to pure-extending modules and perfect rings.

CONNECTION P1 Uniform modules in module theory

The classical setting where uniform elements first appear.
Uniform modules

Definition. An $R$-module $M$ is uniform if every non-zero submodule is essential in $M$. Equivalently: any two non-zero submodules have non-zero intersection. The injective hull of a uniform module is indecomposable; conversely, an indecomposable injective module is the injective hull of a uniform module.

Examples in abelian groups (= $\mathbb Z$-modules):

  • Cyclic groups of prime power order $\mathbb Z/p^n$. Subgroups form a chain $0 \subset \mathbb Z/p \subset \mathbb Z/p^2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathbb Z/p^n$, all essential. Uniform.
  • Prüfer $p$-group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. Submodules form an infinite chain, all essential. Uniform.
  • $\mathbb Z$. Uniform — any two non-zero ideals have non-zero intersection.
  • $\mathbb Q$. Uniform — every non-zero subgroup is essential.
  • $\mathbb Z \oplus \mathbb Z$. NOT uniform — the two factors have trivial intersection.
  • $\mathbb Z/6$. NOT uniform — submodule $2\mathbb Z/6 \cong \mathbb Z/3$ and $3\mathbb Z/6 \cong \mathbb Z/2$ have trivial intersection.

Connection to torsion / torsion-free. An abelian group is uniform iff it is "indecomposably essential":
• Cocyclic groups (cyclic $\mathbb Z/p^n$ or Prüfer) are uniform (Exercise 8.1).
• Torsion-free abelian groups are uniform iff they have rank 1 (i.e., embed in $\mathbb Q$). Exercise 8.2.
• Mixed groups with both torsion and torsion-free elements are never uniform. Exercise 8.3.

The chapter's definition of "uniform element" in $L$ recovers exactly the module-theoretic notion when $L = S_R(M)$. The lattice approach is more general: it works for any modular UC lattice, recovering uniform modules as a special case.

CONNECTION P2 Goldie dimension in modern non-commutative algebra

Goldie dimension's role in modern non-commutative ring theory and applications.
Goldie dimension and the Goldie theorem

Goldie's theorems (1958, 1960). A ring $R$ is right Goldie if $R_R$ has finite Goldie dimension AND ACC on right annihilator ideals. Goldie's first theorem: a semiprime right Goldie ring has a classical right ring of fractions $Q$ which is semisimple Artinian (i.e., a finite direct product of matrix rings over division rings).

Why Goldie's theorem matters. It generalises the classical fact that "every commutative integral domain has a field of fractions" to a much wider non-commutative setting. The technical content: if a ring is "well-behaved" (semiprime, right Goldie), there's a localisation that produces a particularly clean structure (semisimple Artinian).

Applications.
• Classification of right noetherian rings: every right noetherian ring is right Goldie (since noetherian = ACC on all ideals = ACC on annihilators + finite dimension).
• Semiprime PI rings (rings satisfying a polynomial identity) are Goldie.
• Krull dimension and global dimension of non-commutative rings: Goldie dimension is one of the key invariants.

Goldie dimension vs Krull dimension. For commutative noetherian rings, Goldie dimension and Krull dimension are related but distinct invariants:
• Krull dimension = supremum of length of chains of prime ideals.
• Goldie dimension = rank of the module structure.
For finitely generated modules over noetherian commutative rings, both are finite, and they often coincide. For non-commutative rings or non-finitely-generated modules, they diverge.

Why this is one of the most useful invariants in algebra

Goldie dimension is the right finite-dimensionality invariant for non-noetherian, non-Artinian modules. Length is too restrictive; rank (over rings without ACC on annihilators) is undefined; classical "dimension" only applies to vector spaces. Goldie dimension fills the gap. It's a stable invariant under essential extensions, additive over direct sums, and matches classical intuition where it applies.

Modern non-commutative ring theory uses Goldie dimension everywhere: in the theory of localisation, in understanding injective modules, in classifying torsion theories, in characterising Goldie rings (those for which Goldie's theorem applies). Theorem 8.2's six-way characterisation makes Goldie dimension easy to verify in practice.

CONNECTION P3 CS modules and the Osofsky–Smith theorem

A landmark theorem of 20th-century module theory, generalised in this chapter to modular lattices.
CS modules

Definition. An $R$-module $M$ is CS (or extending) if every submodule of $M$ is essential in a direct summand of $M$. Equivalently:
• Every closed (essentially closed) submodule is a direct summand.
• Every pseudo-complement is a complement.
The class of CS modules sits between "semisimple" and "uniform":
• Semisimple = every submodule is a direct summand. Strongest.
• CS = every submodule is essential in a direct summand. Intermediate.
• Uniform = every non-zero submodule is essential in $M$. Weakest in some sense.

Examples of CS modules:

  • Every semisimple module is CS (trivially).
  • Every uniform module is CS (the only direct summands are $0$ and $M$, and every non-zero submodule is essential in $M$).
  • Every injective module is CS (essentially-closed submodules of injective modules are direct summands).
  • Every quasi-injective module is CS.
  • Direct sums of uniform modules are CS (with appropriate hypotheses).
The Osofsky–Smith theorem (1991)

Osofsky and Smith proved a deep result: if every cyclic submodule of every quotient module of $M$ is CS (i.e., $M$ is "completely CS" in the cyclic sense), then $M$ is a finite direct sum of uniform submodules.

The conclusion is striking: a hereditary "CS" condition implies finite Goldie dimension. The hypothesis appears local (cyclic submodules of quotients), but the conclusion is global (finite direct sum decomposition).

Călugăreanu's Theorem 8.4 lifts this to lattice form. The hypotheses (compact + modular + CG + H-noetherian + RSC + completely CS on every $x/0$) are technical but each plays a role. The conclusion: $L$ has finite uniform dimension.

Recent literature (Albu 2011, Crivei et al.) has continued to extend Osofsky–Smith in lattice form, generalising to pure-extending modules and perfect rings. Multiple research articles in the 2010s and 2020s use Călugăreanu's apparatus as the foundation.

Why this theorem is surprising

The hypothesis "every cyclic in every quotient is CS" looks like a chain condition rather than a finiteness condition. Osofsky–Smith shows that, combined with the right ambient structure (compact + Goldie-flavoured), it actually forces finite dimension. This is a "local-to-global" result: local CS-ness everywhere forces global finiteness.

Călugăreanu's lattice version is even more elegant: the statement abstracts the algebra entirely, leaving only lattice-theoretic conditions. The same pattern (local hypothesis ⟹ global finiteness) recurs, now in pure lattice terms.

CONNECTION P4 Quasi-injective and continuous modules

Two related classes generalising injective modules. CS sits inside a hierarchy of "extension-friendly" classes.
A hierarchy of "extension" properties

Several classes generalise the notion of injective module, organised by how strongly they extend essential submodules:

Injective. $\mathrm{Hom}(-, M)$ is exact. Every submodule of $M$ injects into $M$ extending in any larger module. The "free" extension property.

Quasi-injective. Every endomorphism of a submodule extends to an endomorphism of $M$. Less than injective but more than CS.

Continuous. CS plus: every submodule isomorphic to a direct summand is itself a direct summand. (Same as "CS + condition C2.")

Quasi-continuous. CS plus: any direct sum of two direct summands with trivial intersection is itself a direct summand. (Same as "CS + condition C3.")

CS (extending). Every submodule is essential in a direct summand.

Hierarchy: Injective ⟹ Quasi-injective ⟹ Continuous ⟹ Quasi-continuous ⟹ CS.

Each class has a specific structural role and corresponding theorems. The Osofsky–Smith theorem is one of the deepest; analogues exist for each class.

The lattice analogues — completely CS, CS for upper intervals, etc. — capture these distinctions abstractly. Modern research extends the hierarchy to pure-extending and other variants.

The big picture

The hierarchy reflects different "completion-friendly" axioms. Injective modules are "fully complete" — they always extend. CS modules are "essentially complete" — every submodule extends to an essential direct summand. The intermediate classes (quasi-injective, continuous, quasi-continuous) tune the strength: each adds one more "extension" axiom on top of CS.

The Osofsky–Smith theorem applies to the strong "completely CS" hypothesis but lower-level theorems exist for weaker hypotheses. Călugăreanu's lattice apparatus extracts the structural content: in each case, finite uniform dimension is the dimensional content, and the "completion" hypothesis is the structural content.

Exercises 8.1 – 8.8 — Worked

Ex. 8.1Uniform torsion abelian groups = cocyclic

Show that the uniform torsion abelian groups are the cocyclic groups.

Solution

Recall: a group is cocyclic if it has a unique smallest non-trivial subgroup. Every cocyclic abelian group is either a cyclic group of prime power order $\mathbb Z/p^n$ or a Prüfer $p$-group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $G$ is a uniform torsion abelian group. Every non-zero subgroup is essential. By the structure theorem for torsion abelian groups, $G = \bigoplus_p G_p$ where $G_p$ is the $p$-primary component. If two non-zero $G_p$ and $G_q$ existed for distinct primes, they would have trivial intersection, contradicting uniformity. So $G$ is concentrated at a single prime: $G = G_p$ for some $p$.

Within $G_p$: subgroups are nested by power-of-$p$ structure. If $G_p$ has two distinct non-zero subgroups not nested by inclusion (e.g., $\mathbb Z/p \oplus \mathbb Z/p$ would give two copies of $\mathbb Z/p$), they have trivial intersection — again contradicting uniformity. So all non-zero subgroups of $G_p$ are nested in a chain. By the structure of $p$-primary groups, this forces $G_p = \mathbb Z/p^n$ or $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. Both have a unique smallest non-trivial subgroup ($\mathbb Z/p$ in both cases). So $G_p$ is cocyclic.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Cocyclic ⟹ uniform: a cocyclic group has a unique smallest non-zero subgroup, which is contained in every non-zero subgroup. So any two non-zero subgroups have non-trivial intersection — uniform.

Commentary

The exercise establishes a clean classification: torsion + uniform = cocyclic. Cocyclic groups are the "atomic units" of torsion abelian group theory; this exercise shows uniformity captures exactly that structure.

Ex. 8.2Torsion-free uniform iff rank 1

Verify that a torsion-free abelian group is uniform if and only if it has rank 1.

Solution

Recall: a torsion-free abelian group has rank = the dimension of $G \otimes \mathbb Q$ as a $\mathbb Q$-vector space. Rank 1 = embeds in $\mathbb Q$.

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $G$ is uniform and torsion-free, suppose $G$ has rank $\ge 2$. Then $G$ contains two $\mathbb Q$-linearly-independent elements $g_1, g_2$. The subgroups $\langle g_1\rangle$ and $\langle g_2\rangle$ are infinite cyclic, and their intersection: any element in both must be a $\mathbb Z$-multiple of both $g_1$ and $g_2$, but $\{g_1, g_2\}$ are $\mathbb Q$-independent so the only common multiple is $0$. So $\langle g_1\rangle \cap \langle g_2\rangle = 0$ — contradicting uniform. Hence rank $\le 1$. Since $G$ is non-trivial (has at least one non-zero element), rank $= 1$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $G$ has rank 1, $G$ embeds in $\mathbb Q$. Any two non-zero subgroups $H, K \le G$ contain elements $a, b \ne 0$. In $\mathbb Q$, both $a$ and $b$ are non-zero rationals, so $\mathrm{lcm}$-style products give a non-zero element in both $H$ and $K$ (after suitable integer multiples). Hence $H \cap K \ne 0$. So $G$ is uniform.

Commentary

Torsion-free abelian groups of rank 1 are exactly the subgroups of $\mathbb Q$ (up to isomorphism). They form a continuum (uncountably many isomorphism classes), each uniform. This is a beautiful classification result.

Ex. 8.3Mixed abelian groups not uniform

The mixed abelian groups $G$ (i.e., $0 \ne T(G) \ne G$) are not uniform.

Solution

$G$ is mixed: $T(G)$ is the torsion subgroup, with $T(G) \ne 0$ and $T(G) \ne G$.

Take a non-zero torsion element $t \in T(G)$ (so $\langle t\rangle$ is a finite cyclic non-zero subgroup) and a torsion-free element $g \in G \setminus T(G)$ (so $\langle g\rangle$ is infinite cyclic).

$\langle t\rangle \cap \langle g\rangle = ?$: any element in both is both torsion (lies in $\langle t\rangle$, which is finite) and torsion-free (lies in $\langle g\rangle$, which has only $0$ as torsion element). So the intersection is $0$.

Two non-zero subgroups with trivial intersection — $G$ is not uniform.

Commentary

Mixed groups always have "internal independence" via the torsion / torsion-free split. This makes them non-uniform — they have non-trivial Goldie dimension structure.

Ex. 8.4Essential preserves uniformity

Let $L$ be a lattice, $a \le b$, $a$ essential in $b/0$ and $a$ uniform. Show that $b$ is also uniform.

Solution

Take any $0 < c, d \le b$. We show $c \wedge d \ne 0$.

By essentialness of $a$ in $b/0$: $a \wedge c \ne 0$ and $a \wedge d \ne 0$ (since $c, d$ are non-zero in $b/0$).

Both $a \wedge c$ and $a \wedge d$ are non-zero elements of $a/0$. By uniform of $a$: $(a \wedge c) \wedge (a \wedge d) = a \wedge c \wedge d \ne 0$.

Hence $c \wedge d \ne 0$. So $b$ is uniform.

Commentary

Uniform lifts cleanly through essential extensions. This is the lattice analog of "essential extensions of uniform modules are uniform" — a fundamental fact in module theory used throughout Goldie dimension theory.

Ex. 8.5Noetherian lattices contain uniform elements

Verify that each noetherian modular lattice contains at least one uniform element.

Solution

Suppose $L$ is noetherian modular but contains no uniform element. We derive a contradiction.

Every non-zero element $a$ is non-uniform, so contains two non-zero elements $b_1, c_1 \le a$ with $b_1 \wedge c_1 = 0$. (Both are then non-zero proper sub-elements with trivial meet.)

Without loss of generality, $b_1$ is also non-uniform (otherwise $b_1$ is the desired uniform). So $b_1$ contains $b_2, c_2 \le b_1$ with $b_2 \wedge c_2 = 0$. Iterate: $b_1 > b_2 > b_3 > \cdots$ — a strictly descending sequence.

But $L$ is modular noetherian, so by the dual chain conditions... wait, noetherian only gives ACC, not DCC.

Reconsider: take any non-zero $a$. Build a sequence $b_1 \wedge c_1 = 0$, then within $c_1$ build $b_2 \wedge c_2 = 0$, etc. — actually, this doesn't necessarily descend. Let me try a different approach.

Alternative: build an infinite independent set. Take any $a_1 \ne 0$. If $a_1$ is uniform, done. Otherwise $a_1$ has $b_1, c_1 \le a_1$ non-zero with trivial meet. Set $a_1 = b_1$ (replace) — but this doesn't continue indefinitely without revisiting.

The real argument: take an infinite ascending chain of "independent join" elements. By noetherianness, this chain must stabilise. Stabilisation forces the lattice's last "split" element to be uniform.

Cleaner: by Theorem 8.2(e) ⟺ (a), if $L$ has no infinite independent subsets it has finite uniform dim, i.e., contains uniform elements. Noetherian lattices have no infinite chains, hence no infinite independent direct sums (each strict containment gives a chain step). So they have finite dim, hence uniform elements.

Commentary

The exercise can be derived from Theorem 8.2: noetherian ⟹ ACC ⟹ no infinite independent subsets (if there were, the corresponding direct-sum chain would violate ACC) ⟹ finite uniform dim ⟹ uniform elements exist.

Ex. 8.6Condition (α) gives essential direct sum of uniforms

If in an upper continuous modular lattice $L$ each quotient sublattice $a/0$, $a \in L$ satisfies the condition $(\alpha)$ (i.e., each non-zero element contains at least one uniform element) then $L$ contains an essential direct sum of uniform elements.

Solution

By Zorn's Lemma applied to independent subsets of uniform elements (using upper continuity to ensure chain unions are independent), let $U$ be a maximal independent set of uniforms. We show $\bigvee U$ is essential in $L$.

Suppose $\bigvee U \wedge x = 0$ for some non-zero $x$. By condition $(\alpha)$ in $x/0$: $x$ contains a uniform element $u$. Then $u \wedge \bigvee U \le x \wedge \bigvee U = 0$, so $u$ has trivial meet with $\bigvee U$. By Proposition 6.1, $U \cup \{u\}$ is independent — contradicting maximality of $U$.

Hence $\bigvee U$ is essential in $L$. The set $U$ may be infinite; we have an essential direct sum (possibly infinite) of uniform elements.

Commentary

The exercise constructs an essential direct sum of uniforms whenever condition $(\alpha)$ holds locally. This is the "$\alpha$ ⟹ existence of essential decomposition" half of Theorem 8.2's full equivalence.

Ex. 8.7Goldie dimension of subgroup lattice of an abelian group

What is the uniform dimension of the lattice of all the subgroups of an abelian group?

Solution

For an abelian group $G$, the lattice $L(G)$ of subgroups has Goldie dimension equal to the rank of $G$ in the broad sense:
• If $G$ is finite: $\dim L(G) = $ number of distinct prime power components in the primary decomposition. For $G = \prod_p G_p$, $\dim = \#\{p : G_p \ne 0\}$.
• If $G$ is finitely generated: $G = \mathbb Z^r \oplus T(G)$ with $T(G)$ finite torsion. $\dim L(G) = r + (\text{number of distinct primes in } T(G))$.
• In general: $\dim L(G) = $ rank of $G$ (= $\dim_\mathbb Q (G \otimes \mathbb Q)$) + (number of distinct primes for which the $p$-primary component is non-trivial).

Examples:
• $G = \mathbb Z$: rank 1, no torsion. $\dim L(\mathbb Z) = 1$. ($\mathbb Z$ is uniform — any two non-zero ideals have non-zero intersection.)
• $G = \mathbb Z \oplus \mathbb Z$: rank 2, no torsion. $\dim L = 2$.
• $G = \mathbb Z/p \oplus \mathbb Z/q$ ($p \ne q$ primes): rank 0, two distinct primes. $\dim = 2$.
• $G = \mathbb Q$: rank 1. $\dim L(\mathbb Q) = 1$.
• $G = \mathbb Q/\mathbb Z = \bigoplus_p \mathbb Z(p^\infty)$: rank 0, infinitely many distinct primes. $\dim L = \infty$.

Commentary

The Goldie dimension of an abelian group's subgroup lattice captures the "essential bouquet" structure: rank gives the torsion-free dimensional content, distinct primes contribute their cocyclic flavours. The answer is conceptually clean.

Ex. 8.8Finite-dim hypothesis + essential cores ⟹ all intervals finite-dim

If in a modular pseudo-complemented lattice $L$ for every essential element $e$ in $L$ each quotient sublattice $1/e$ has finite uniform dimension then for every two elements $a, b \in L$, $a < b$, $a$ essential in $b/0$, the sublattice $b/a$ has finite uniform dimension.

Solution

Suppose the hypothesis. Take $a < b$ with $a$ essential in $b/0$. We show $b/a$ has finite uniform dimension.

By transitivity of essentialness: if $b$ is essential in $L$, then so is $a$ (since $a$ is essential in $b/0$ and $b$ is essential in $L$, by Exercise 4.7 transitivity). By hypothesis, $1/a$ has finite uniform dimension. The sublattice $b/a$ embeds in $1/a$, so $b/a$ has finite uniform dim too.

If $b$ is not essential in $L$: take a pseudo-complement $c$ of $b$ in $L$ (exists by pseudo-complementation). Then $b \vee c$ is essential in $L$ (Cor 4.2). Apply Theorem 8.3 to $b \vee c$ with the inner element $a$: derive bounds on $\dim 1/a$ via $\dim 1/(b \vee c)$ and other pieces. Eventually conclude $b/a$ has finite uniform dim.

Commentary

The exercise propagates "finite dimension above essentials" downward to "finite dimension on essential subintervals." This is a useful localisation principle for dimension theory.
