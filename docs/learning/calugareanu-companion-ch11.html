Co-compact Lattices

The order-dual of compact: every meet that lands at $0$ has a finite sub-meet that already lands at $0$. The lattice abstraction of finitely cogenerated modules. Theorem 11.2 — the keystone — characterises co-compact lattices via "compact essential socle."

~50–60 min read 8 sections + 5 connection panels 6 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — the order-dual of compactness

A complete lattice is co-compact when arbitrary trivial meets are witnessed by finite trivial meets — the dual of "arbitrary cover witnessed by finite subcover." Theorem 11.2: in compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact ⟺ socle is compact and essential.

Chapter 2 introduced compact lattice elements: $c \le \bigvee X$ implies $c \le \bigvee F$ for some finite $F \subseteq X$. The compact lattice is the case $c = 1$ — the entire lattice can be "covered" by a finite sub-cover. Chapter 11 develops the order-dual: a lattice is co-compact if every "trivial meet" $\bigwedge X = 0$ is witnessed by a finite trivial meet $\bigwedge F = 0$ for some $F \subseteq X$.

In module theory, the lattice $S_R(M)$ is co-compact iff the module $M$ is finitely cogenerated — a notion introduced in the 1960s as the order-dual of finitely generated. Just as compact lattices generalise finitely generated modules, co-compact lattices generalise finitely cogenerated modules. The connection runs through Theorem 11.2 (the chapter's keystone): in compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact lattices coincide with those whose socle $s(L)$ is compact and essential — exactly the lattice form of "$M$ finitely cogenerated iff $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is essential and finitely generated."

Four movements in the chapter:

  1. Definition + basic properties (Part A). Co-compact = "trivial meets are finite-witnessed." Artinian ⟹ co-compact (immediate). Co-compact propagates downward (Lemma 11.1). Essential extension preserves co-compactness (Lemma 11.2). Compactly generated co-compact lattices have the socle essential (Lemma 11.4).
  2. Artinian + direct sums (Part B). Proposition 11.2: $L$ artinian ⟺ every $1/a$ co-compact. Proposition 11.3: in modular inductive, $a/0$ + $1/a$ co-compact ⟹ $L$ co-compact. The book emphasises this is a "purely lattice proof," avoiding the injective hull (a non-lattice construction).
  3. $r(L) = 0$ + Theorem 11.1 (Part C). Proposition 11.4: in compactly generated co-compact with $r(L) = 0$, $L$ is semiatomic and compact. Theorem 11.1: in compactly generated semiatomic modular, "compact ⟺ co-compact ⟺ $1$ is finite direct sum of atoms" — a clean three-way equivalence.
  4. Theorem 11.2 + dual compact (Part D). The keystone: in compactly generated modular, co-compact ⟺ socle is compact and essential. Then a separate notion of "dual compact" element, with the surprising fact that "dual compact lattice" = "co-compact lattice" but the corresponding elements differ (Proposition 11.5: in Boolean lattices, co-compact = compact for elements).
The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

Co-compact lattices generalise finitely cogenerated modules — modules whose every "intersection zero" is finite-witnessed. Theorem 11.2 (the chapter's keystone) gives a clean structural characterisation: in compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact ⟺ socle is compact and essential. The duality with compact lattices is rich but imperfect: while "co-compact lattice" coincides with "dual compact lattice," the corresponding elements differ in general. Proposition 11.5: in Boolean lattices, this asymmetry collapses — every co-compact element is compact. The chapter is short but structurally important: it ties the apparatus of socle, essentialness, and compactness together for the dimension-theoretic results of Chapter 8.

Terminology trap: "co-compact" vs "coatomic"

Chapter 10 was about coatomic lattices (every $a \ne 1$ has a maximal element above it). Chapter 11 is about co-compact lattices (the order-dual of compact, finitely cogenerated). These are different concepts.

Coatomic = "atoms in every upper interval" (where "atom" of upper interval is a maximal element).

Co-compact = "trivial meets are finite-witnessed" = "$1$ is compact in $L^{\mathrm{op}}$."

The terminology overlap is unfortunate but standard. Read carefully which "co-" is intended.

New in Chapter 11 — full term-cards

New · Ch 11 · CENTRAL

co-compact lattice (= finitely cogenerated) A complete lattice $L$ is co-compact (or finitely cogenerated, in [34]) if for every subset $X$ of $L$ such that $\bigwedge X = 0$, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $\bigwedge F = 0$. Equivalently: $L$ is co-compact iff the dual lattice $L^{\mathrm{op}}$ is compact. The lattice abstraction of finitely cogenerated modules.

New · Ch 11

co-compact element An element $a \in L$ is called co-compact if the sublattice $a/0$ is co-compact. So co-compactness as a property of elements is "co-compactness of their principal ideal."

New · Ch 11 · WATCH

dual compact element An element $a \in L$ is called dual compact if for each subset $X \subseteq L$ such that $a \ge \bigwedge X$, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $a \ge \bigwedge F$. Plain English: "$a$ is order-dually compact" — finite witness for being above-meet.

WARNING: The notion of "dual compact lattice" coincides with "co-compact lattice" (the same property of $1$). But "dual compact element" is in general DIFFERENT from "co-compact element" — they're not the same notion at the element level. See Proposition 11.5.

New · Ch 11 · alternative name

finitely cogenerated Synonym for co-compact (Reference [34] = Stenström 1975, Rings of quotients). The module-theoretic origin: a module is finitely cogenerated iff every set of submodules with intersection zero has a finite sub-set with intersection zero. The dual of "finitely generated."

Deep dive — Order duality in lattice theory

Deep dive · The order-dual lattice $L^{\mathrm{op}}$

Definition. For any lattice $L$, the order-dual lattice $L^{\mathrm{op}}$ has the same underlying set as $L$ but with the order reversed: $a \le_{L^{\mathrm{op}}} b$ iff $b \le_L a$. Joins in $L$ become meets in $L^{\mathrm{op}}$, and vice versa. The bottom $0$ of $L$ becomes the top of $L^{\mathrm{op}}$.

Why dualise? Many lattice notions come in dual pairs:
• Compact ↔ Co-compact
• Atomic ↔ Coatomic (every $a \ne 1$ has a maximal element above)
• Atom ↔ Dual atom (= co-atom = maximal)
• Socle $s(L)$ ↔ Radical $r(L)$
• Essential ↔ Superfluous
• Pseudo-complement ↔ Supplement (Ch 12)
• Noetherian (ACC) ↔ Artinian (DCC)

The duality principle. Any theorem about lattices, when applied to $L^{\mathrm{op}}$, gives a "dual theorem" about lattices. So results about compact lattices automatically yield results about co-compact lattices via $L \leftrightarrow L^{\mathrm{op}}$.

Asymmetries to watch for. Most module-theoretic constructions break duality. The submodule lattice $S_R(M)$ has a natural top (= $M$) and bottom (= $0$), but the dual would correspond to "quotient submodules" — a structurally different notion. So while lattice-theoretic dualities are clean, their module-theoretic interpretations can require care.

The chapter's specific use. Chapter 11 uses order-duality to lift Chapter 2's compact-lattice apparatus to co-compact-lattice apparatus. Theorem 11.2 has a clean dual form (essentially: in compactly generated modular, co-compact ⟺ "the dual of finitely generated structure" holds). The chapter occasionally notes asymmetries (e.g., the dual compact vs co-compact element distinction).

Deep dive — Finitely cogenerated modules

Deep dive · Finitely cogenerated modules

Definition. A module $M$ is finitely cogenerated if for every family of submodules $\{M_i\}$ with $\bigcap M_i = 0$, there's a finite sub-family $\{M_{i_1}, \ldots, M_{i_n}\}$ with $\bigcap_{j=1}^n M_{i_j} = 0$. Plain English: "every intersection-zero family is finitely-witnessed."

Equivalent characterisations (the lattice form of Theorem 11.2):
• $M$ is finitely cogenerated iff $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is finitely generated AND essential in $M$.
• $M$ is finitely cogenerated iff $M$ has finite uniform dimension AND every uniform submodule has a simple submodule (= atomic socle in lattice terms).
• $M$ is finitely cogenerated iff $E(M)$ (the injective hull) is a finite direct sum of indecomposable injectives, each containing a simple submodule.

Why finitely cogenerated matters. The dual of finitely generated. Just as finitely generated modules over noetherian rings have nice structure (composition series, finite length, Krull-Schmidt), finitely cogenerated modules over artinian rings have parallel structure. In abelian group theory, finitely cogenerated = "finite or has socle a finite direct sum of cyclic groups of prime order."

Examples.
• Every Artinian module is finitely cogenerated.
• Every finite module is finitely cogenerated.
• $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ (the Prüfer $p$-group) is finitely cogenerated (socle = $\mathbb Z/p$ is finite and essential).
• $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module: not finitely cogenerated (socle = $0$ is not essential).
• $\mathbb Q$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module: not finitely cogenerated (socle = $0$).
• $\bigoplus_p \mathbb Z_p$ over distinct primes: not finitely cogenerated (Exercise 11.2).

Onodera's theorem (1973). "Linearly compact modules and cogenerators II" (Hokkaido Math. J.): finitely cogenerated modules are exactly the linearly compact modules with essential socle. This connects to topological lattice theory and linearly compact rings.

The lattice version (Călugăreanu's chapter). Theorem 11.2 abstracts the module-theoretic characterisation: in compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact ⟺ socle compact + essential. The lattice form generalises beyond modules to any modular CG lattice.

Deep dive — Theorem 11.2 and dimension theory

Deep dive · Theorem 11.2 in context

Theorem 11.2 (the chapter's keystone): in a compactly generated modular lattice, co-compact ⟺ the socle $s(L)$ is compact and essential in $L$.

Why this is striking. Two superficially unrelated conditions — "every trivial meet is finite-witnessed" and "the socle is compact and essential" — turn out to be equivalent. The connection runs through the structural theory of socles and essentialness developed in Chapters 4-5.

The forward direction. Co-compact ⟹ socle compact (Theorem 11.1, in semiatomic context — when applied to $s(L)/0$) and essential (Lemma 11.4). The forward proof uses Lemma 11.4's key insight: in compactly generated co-compact, the socle is essential because the meet of all essential elements is $0$ (since these elements are essential), and co-compactness gives a finite-witness — but their finite meet is then essential too, forcing the meet to be the socle itself.

The reverse direction. Compact essential socle ⟹ co-compact uses Lemma 11.2 (essential extension preserves co-compactness): if $s(L)$ is essential in $L$ and $s(L)/0$ is co-compact (which follows from $s(L)$ compact + semiatomic), then $L$ is co-compact.

The Goldie connection. Theorem 11.2 implies: every co-compact lattice has finite Goldie dimension (Chapter 8). Reason: compact essential socle ⟹ finite essential decomposition into atoms, which IS a finite uniform-dimension witness. So co-compact is a strong dimensional condition.

Modern applications. Finitely cogenerated modules are used heavily in:
• Cogenerator theory: a module $C$ is a cogenerator if every module embeds in a product of copies of $C$. Finitely cogenerated modules and their duals have rich cogenerator structure.
• Morita duality: rings $R$ and $S$ are Morita-dual iff there's a bimodule giving a duality between $\mathrm{mod-R}$ and $S\mathrm{-mod}$ that exchanges finitely generated and finitely cogenerated.
• Coalgebra theory: cogenerator-type properties dualise generator properties.

That's the vocabulary. The chapter prose ahead refers to these terms freely.

Part A · Co-compact basics

Definitions, basic propagation, and the socle connection

Definition, the immediate "artinian ⟹ co-compact" remark, hereditary properties (Lemmas 11.1–11.4), the RSC consequence (Prop 11.1).

SECTION A1 Definition + Lemmas 11.1, 11.2 — basic propagation

Co-compact = trivial meets are finite-witnessed. Hereditary downward (Lemma 11.1) and preserved under essential extensions (Lemma 11.2).
Definition · co-compact (= finitely cogenerated)

A complete lattice $L$ is called co-compact (or finitely cogenerated, in [34]) if for every subset $X$ of $L$ such that $\bigwedge X = 0$, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $\bigwedge F = 0$. Obviously, $L$ is co-compact if and only if the dual $L^{\mathrm{op}}$ is compact. An element $a \in L$ is called co-compact if the sublattice $a/0$ is co-compact.

Plain English: $L$ is co-compact when every "intersection-zero family" is witnessed by a finite sub-family. This is the dual of "every cover-of-1 is finite-witnessed" (= compact).

The chapter recalls Proposition 1.3 from Chapter 1: $L$ is artinian iff for each subset $A$ there's a finite $F \subseteq A$ with $\bigwedge F = \bigwedge A$. As an immediate consequence:

Remark

Every artinian lattice is co-compact.

Reason: artinian gives finite witness for arbitrary meets (any subset $A$ has finite $F$ with $\bigwedge F = \bigwedge A$), so in particular the case $\bigwedge A = 0$ has finite witness — exactly the co-compact condition.

Lemma 11.1

If $L$ is a co-compact lattice, for each $0 \ne a \in L$ the sublattice $a/0$ is also co-compact.

The converse trivially holds if $L$ has $1$: take $a = 1$ and $a/0 = L$.

Lemma 11.2

Let $a$ be an essential element of a lattice $L$. If $a/0$ is co-compact then $L$ is also co-compact.

Why

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a family of non-zero elements of $L$ such that $\bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i = 0$. The element $a$ being essential in $L$, we have $a \wedge a_i \ne 0$ for each $i$, and $\bigwedge_{i \in I}(a \wedge a_i) = a \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i = a \wedge 0 = 0$.

The sublattice $a/0$ being co-compact, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq I$ such that $\bigwedge_{i \in F}(a \wedge a_i) = a \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in F} a_i = 0$.

Finally, $a$ being essential, $\bigwedge_{i \in F} a_i = 0$. Hence $L$ is co-compact.

Lemma 11.2 is the dual of "compact extension preserves compact": essential extension preserves co-compact. The proof uses essentialness exactly to push trivial-meets up from $a/0$ to $L$.

SECTION A2 Lemmas 11.3, 11.4 + Proposition 11.1 — connecting to the socle

Co-compact lattices have $r(L) = 0$ (in semiatomic), socle is essential (Lemma 11.4). Combined with RSC: co-compact CG ⟹ torsion (Prop 11.1).
Lemma 11.3

In a compactly generated modular semiatomic lattice the radical $r(L) = 0$.

(This is a dimensional consequence of semiatomicity in modular CG, recapped here for use in the chapter's main results.)

Lemma 11.4 · the socle-essential lemma

In a compactly generated co-compact lattice $L$ the socle $s(L)$ is essential in $L$.

Why

Let $a \in L$ be such that $s(L) \wedge a = 0$ or, equivalently, $s(a/0) = 0$. The sublattice $a/0$ being compactly generated, the socle is also the join of all the essential elements of $a/0$. So, being co-compact, $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in F} e_i$ for a finite family of essential elements $\{e_i\}_{i \in F}$ of $a/0$. Hence $0$ is essential in $a/0$, and so $a = 0$.

Plain English: in compactly generated co-compact lattices, the socle is "everywhere" — every non-zero element has a non-trivial atomic core. This is half of Theorem 11.2 (the keystone): co-compact ⟹ socle essential.

Remark 11.1

In every atomic lattice, the socle is essential (see Proposition 5.1). If the lattice $L$ is inductive then the converse is also true.

Reading: atomic ⟹ socle essential always; in inductive lattices, socle essential ⟹ atomic. So in inductive lattices, "atomic" and "socle essential" are equivalent.

Combining this with Lemma 11.4 and the inductive case (compactly generated ⟹ inductive): compactly generated co-compact lattices are atomic.

Proposition 11.1

Compactly generated co-compact lattices which satisfy the condition (RSC) are torsion lattices.

Why

Immediate by Proposition 5.9: in modular atomic lattices, RSC + atomic ⟹ torsion. We've established (CG co-compact ⟹ atomic) above, and RSC is given.

Proposition 11.1 connects co-compactness back to Chapter 5's torsion theory. It says: in well-behaved settings (CG + RSC), co-compact lattices are torsion lattices. Combined with Theorem 8.4 (Osofsky-Smith style results), this gives strong dimensional consequences.

Part B · Artinian + direct sums

Two structural propositions

Proposition 11.2's clean characterisation of artinian via co-compact upper intervals; Proposition 11.3's "purely lattice" proof of co-compact lifting.

SECTION B1 Proposition 11.2 — artinian via co-compact

A clean characterisation: L is artinian iff every upper interval $1/a$ is co-compact.
Proposition 11.2

A lattice $L$ is artinian if and only if for every $a \ne 1$ the sublattice $1/a$ is co-compact.

Why · two halves

$(\Rightarrow)$ Each sublattice of an artinian lattice is clearly artinian and so, by the Remark 11.1, is co-compact.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, let $\ldots \le a_n \le \ldots \le a_2 \le a_1$ be an ascending chain of elements in $L$ (we're verifying ACC for the dual, which is artinian for $L$). Wait — we need DCC. Let me re-read.

Actually: artinian = DCC. So suppose $a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \ldots$ is a descending chain. If $a = \bigwedge_{n \in \mathbb N} a_n$ then $\{a_n\}_{n \in \mathbb N}$ is a subset of $1/a$ (each $a_n \ge a$). The sublattice $1/a$ being co-compact, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq \mathbb N$ such that $a = \bigwedge_{n \in F} a_n$. Hence $a = a_m$ where $m = \max(F)$, and $a_{m+\ell} = a_m$ for each $\ell \in \mathbb N$, so the chain is finite and $L$ is artinian.

Proposition 11.2 makes the artinian/co-compact connection precise: artinian = "every upper interval is co-compact." The only-1 case ($a = 0$, $1/a = L$) gives "$L$ co-compact" as a special case, but artinian is the global condition that this holds in every upper world.

SECTION B2 Proposition 11.3 + Corollary 11.1 — direct sums of co-compact

In modular inductive lattices, $a/0$ + $1/a$ co-compact ⟹ L co-compact. The "purely lattice proof" avoids injective hull.
Proposition 11.3

If for an element $a$ of a modular inductive lattice $L$ the sublattices $a/0$ and $1/a$ are co-compact then the lattice $L$ is also co-compact.

Why · purely lattice argument

If $a = 0$ nothing remains to be proved. If $a \ne 0$, let $\{b_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a subset of $L$ with $\bigwedge_{i \in I} b_i = 0$. Then $\bigwedge_{i \in I}(a \wedge b_i) = a \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in I} b_i = a \wedge 0 = 0$ in $a/0$.

By co-compactness of $a/0$, there is a finite subset $F \subseteq I$ such that $\bigwedge_{i \in F}(a \wedge b_i) = a \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i = 0$. If $\bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i = 0$ (e.g., if $a$ is essential in $L$), the proof is complete.

If $\bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i \ne 0$, then let $c$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ which contains $\bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i$. We have $\bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i \in c/0 = c/(a \wedge c) \cong (a \vee c)/a \subseteq 1/a$ (the iso is given by modularity). The sublattice $1/a$ being co-compact, $(a \vee c)/a$ and hence $c/0$ are also co-compact.

But $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in F}(c \wedge b_i)$ in $c/0$, and there's a finite subset $G \subseteq I$ such that $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in G}(c \wedge b_i) = c \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in G} b_i$. Now, for $b = \bigwedge_{i \in F \cup G} b_i$ we have $b \le \bigwedge_{i \in F} b_i \le c$ and $c \wedge b \le c \wedge \bigwedge_{i \in G} b_i = 0$, so $b = 0$, and we have the required finite subset.

The book emphasises: this is a purely lattice proof which avoids the injective hull, a non-lattice notion (see [41]). Many module-theoretic proofs of analogous statements use the injective hull as a tool, but the lattice apparatus we've built up (essentialness, pseudo-complement, modular iso theorem) is enough.

Corollary 11.1

A finite direct sum of co-compact elements in an inductive modular lattice is co-compact.

Why · induction on number of summands

If $a/0, b/0$ are co-compact and $a \oplus b = 1$ ($b$ is a complement of $a$) then by modularity $b/0 \cong b/(a \wedge b) \cong (a \vee b)/a = 1/a$, and we use Proposition 11.3. The rest is done by induction.

Useful in practice: when constructing co-compact lattices, you can build them as finite direct sums of smaller co-compact pieces. This dualises Proposition 2.1 (finite joins of compacts are compact).

Part C · $r(L) = 0$ + Theorem 11.1

The radical-zero corner of co-compact theory

When the radical vanishes, co-compact lattices have a very clean structure (Prop 11.4). Theorem 11.1 gives the keystone three-way equivalence in CG semiatomic modular settings.

SECTION C1 Proposition 11.4 + Corollary 11.2

CG co-compact + r(L)=0 ⟹ semiatomic + compact. The strongest immediate consequence of Lemma 11.4 + radical theory.
Proposition 11.4

Let $L$ be a compactly generated co-compact lattice with the radical $r(L) = 0$. Then $L$ is semiatomic and compact.

Why · two parts

Semiatomic: From Lemma 11.4 we already know that $L$ is atomic. The lattice $L$ being compactly generated, the radical is also the union of all the superfluous elements (Theorem 7.1). Hence the condition $r(L) = 0$ implies that the only superfluous element of $L$ is $0$. Equivalently, for each $0 \ne a \in L$ there is an $x \ne 1$ such that $a \vee x = 1$. In particular, each atom has a complement (maximal if $L$ is also modular).

If $s(L) \ne 1$ then let $x \ne 1$ be such that $s(L) \vee x = 1$ ($L \ne 0$ atomic implies $s(L) \ne 0$). One obtains an atom which would not be contained in $s(L)$ — a contradiction. Hence $L$ is semiatomic.

Compact: $L$ being co-compact, the radical $r(L)$ is the intersection of all the maximal elements, and there's a finite subset, say with $n$ maximal elements, which has zero intersection. Compactness of $L$ follows by induction on $n$: each subset $X$ of $L$ with the join equal to $1$ has a finite subset which has also the join equal to $1$.

Corollary 11.2

Let $L$ be a compactly generated co-compact modular lattice with the radical $r(L) = 0$. Then $L$ is semiatomic.

(Direct application of Proposition 11.4 + the structural results from Chapters 6 and 10.)

SECTION C2 Theorem 11.1 — three-way equivalence

In CG semiatomic modular: compact ⟺ co-compact ⟺ 1 is finite direct sum of atoms.
Theorem 11.1

Let $L$ be a compactly generated semiatomic and modular lattice. Then the following conditions are equivalent:

(a) $L$ is compact;

(b) $L$ is co-compact;

(c) $1$ is a finite direct sum of atoms.

Why · the three-way chain

(a) ⟹ (c): $L$ being semiatomic and inductive (semiatomic CG ⟹ inductive), we have $1 = \bigoplus_{i \in I} s_i$ with $s_i$ atoms (Theorem 6.2). $\{s_i\}_{i \in I}$ has the join equal to $1$, a compact element, so a finite subset $F \subseteq I$ exists such that $1 = \bigoplus_{i \in F} s_i$.

(c) ⟹ (b): Suppose $1 = \bigoplus_{i=1}^n s_i$. We prove that if $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i$ there's a finite subset $F$ of $I$ such that $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in F} a_i$, by induction on $n$. If $n = 1$, the assertion is obvious. Assume the assertion holds for at most $n - 1$ atoms.

First, observe there's $k \in I$ with $a_k \wedge s_n = 0$. Indeed, otherwise $a_i \wedge s_n = s_n$ for every $i \in I$ or $s_n \le \bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i$, contradiction. The element $a_k$ is also a direct sum of at most $n - 1$ atoms (modularity is needed for the use of the Jordan-Hölder theorem). By induction, a finite subset of $\{a_i \wedge a_k\}_{i \in I}$ has intersection $0$. Hence $L$ is co-compact.

(b) ⟹ (a): follows from Lemma 11.3 (which assures $r(L) = 0$ in semiatomic) and Proposition 11.4.

Why this matters

Theorem 11.1 says: in compactly generated semiatomic modular lattices, "compact" and "co-compact" coincide — both are equivalent to "1 is a finite direct sum of atoms." This is a striking equivalence: two superficially different finiteness conditions (compact = "1 has finite-witness from below"; co-compact = "trivial meet has finite-witness") collapse in this setting.

The collapse is enabled by semiatomicity: when $L$ is built from atoms, both finiteness conditions reduce to "finitely many atoms suffice."

Remark

The implication (c) ⟹ (a) follows easily: in an upper continuous lattice every atom is compact and finite unions of compact elements are compact.

Part D · Theorem 11.2 + dual compact

The chapter's keystone, plus an order-dual subtlety

Theorem 11.2 — co-compact ⟺ socle compact and essential. Then dual compact elements (which differ from co-compact at the element level) and Proposition 11.5 (in Boolean lattices, they coincide).

SECTION D1 Theorem 11.2 — the chapter's keystone

In compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact ⟺ socle is compact and essential.
Theorem 11.2 — the keystone

Let $L$ be a compactly generated modular lattice. Then $L$ is co-compact if and only if the socle $s(L)$ is compact and essential in $L$.

Why · two halves

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $L$ is co-compact and $a \ne 0$ then clearly $a/0$ is also co-compact. Hence the sublattice $s(L)/0$ is co-compact and semiatomic. By Theorem 11.1, $a/0$ is also compact, i.e., $s(L)$ is compact in $L$. The essentialness follows from Lemma 11.4.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, if $s(L)$ is compact then $s(L)/0$ is semiatomic and compact, hence co-compact (again by the above theorem). The socle $s(L)$ being also essential in $L$, $L$ is co-compact by Lemma 11.2.

Why this is the chapter's keystone

Theorem 11.2 gives a clean structural test for co-compactness in compactly generated modular lattices: check the socle. If the socle is compact (= finitely generated as a sub-lattice / module) and essential (touches every non-zero element), then $L$ is co-compact. The essentialness ensures the socle "sees" everything; compactness ensures the socle is itself "small enough" to allow finite-witness arguments.

For modules: $M$ is finitely cogenerated ⟺ $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is finitely generated and essential. This is the classical module-theoretic characterisation, recovered as a special case of Theorem 11.2.

Remark · alternative form

From [34] (Stenström): the conditions in the previous Theorem are also equivalent with $s(L)$ essential in $L$ and $s(L)/0$ co-compact.

So the third equivalent: "$s(L)$ essential AND $s(L)/0$ co-compact." This is sometimes more convenient when the compactness of the socle isn't directly checkable but co-compactness of $s(L)/0$ is.

Connection to dimension theory

Theorem 11.2 has a strong dimensional consequence: every co-compact lattice (in CG modular) has finite uniform dimension. Reason: compact essential socle ⟹ finite essential decomposition into atoms (each atom is a uniform element by Theorem 8.1 / 8.2). The number of atoms in this decomposition is the Goldie dimension. So co-compact ⟹ finite Goldie dim.

This recovers the classical fact: finitely cogenerated modules have finite uniform dimension. The lattice version makes this transparent.

SECTION D2 Dual compact elements + Proposition 11.5

A separate notion: dual compact elements. The lattice notion coincides with co-compact, but the elements differ. In Boolean lattices, the asymmetry collapses.

The chapter notes a subtlety: there's a separate "dual" notion that one might define for elements.

Definition · dual compact element

An element $a \in L$ is called dual compact if for each subset $X \subseteq L$ such that $a \ge \bigwedge X$ there is a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $a \ge \bigwedge F$.

Plain English: "dual compact" means "finite witness for above-meet" — symmetric to compact's "finite witness for below-join."

Remark · lattices coincide, elements differ

The corresponding notion of dual compact lattice is the same as the co-compact lattices we already have defined (and used) above. Indeed, a lattice $L$ is called dual compact if for each $X \subseteq L$ such that $0 \ge \bigwedge X$ we have a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $0 \ge \bigwedge F$. Hence the dual compact lattices are exactly the co-compact lattices.

However, in general there is no relation between co-compact elements and dual compact elements! In fact, co-compact elements are more likely to be related to compact elements (see Exercises).

The asymmetry: at the lattice level, dual compact = co-compact (both are "$\bigwedge X = 0$ ⟹ finite $\bigwedge F = 0$"). But at the element level, "co-compact element" = "$a/0$ is co-compact" (a property of the principal ideal) is generally different from "dual compact element" (a property of $a$ relative to subsets of $L$).

Proposition 11.5

In a complete Boolean lattice, each co-compact element is compact.

Why · de Morgan

Notice that complete Boolean lattices are infinitely distributive, uniquely (relatively) complemented and the generalized de Morgan formulas hold. Denote by $a'$ the complement of $a$ in $L$.

Let $a \in L$ be a co-compact element of $L$ and let $a \le \bigvee X$ for a subset $X \subseteq L$. Then $a = a \wedge \bigvee X = \bigvee_{x \in X}(a \wedge x)$, and considering only complements relative to the sublattice $a/0$, we derive $0 = \bigwedge_{x \in X}(a \wedge x)'$.

Hence there exists a finite subset $F \subseteq X$ such that $0 = \bigwedge_{x \in F}(a \wedge x)'$ and, complementing once again, $a = \bigvee_{x \in F}(a \wedge x)$ or $a \le \bigvee F$.

Proposition 11.5: in Boolean lattices, the asymmetry collapses — co-compact elements are compact. This is because Boolean lattices have de Morgan duality, which turns the co-compact condition into the compact condition via complementation.

Why this matters

The proposition isolates a setting (Boolean lattices) where the order-dual machinery is fully symmetric. For non-Boolean lattices, the asymmetry persists, and the chapter's notation distinguishes "co-compact element" (= $a/0$ co-compact) from "dual compact element" (= finite witness above) — they're genuinely different.

In abelian group lattices: $L(G)$ is rarely Boolean (it's modular and complemented for elementary abelian groups, but in general non-distributive). So the asymmetry is real for typical algebra examples.

CONNECTION P1 Finitely cogenerated modules

The module-theoretic origin. Finitely cogenerated modules satisfy "every intersection-zero family is finite-witnessed."
Finitely cogenerated modules

Definition. A module $M$ is finitely cogenerated if for every family of submodules $\{M_i\}_{i \in I}$ with $\bigcap_i M_i = 0$, there's a finite sub-family $\{M_{i_1}, \ldots, M_{i_n}\}$ with $\bigcap_{j=1}^n M_{i_j} = 0$.

Equivalently: $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is finitely generated AND essential in $M$. (This is the module-theoretic version of Theorem 11.2 in this chapter.)

Examples:

  • Every Artinian module is finitely cogenerated. (Artinian ⟹ co-compact via Remark 11.1.)
  • Every finite module is finitely cogenerated.
  • $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$: socle $= \mathbb Z/p$, finitely generated AND essential. Finitely cogenerated.
  • $\mathbb Z$: socle $= 0$. Not finitely cogenerated.
  • $\mathbb Q$: socle $= 0$. Not finitely cogenerated.
  • $\bigoplus_{p \in \mathbb P} \mathbb Z_p$: socle is the entire module, but it's not finitely generated. Not finitely cogenerated (Exercise 11.2).
Why finitely cogenerated matters

The dual of finitely generated. In abelian group theory, finitely cogenerated abelian groups are exactly the groups $G$ such that $\mathrm{soc}(G) = T(G)[p]$ for finitely many $p$ is finite and essential. They include all finite abelian groups and the cocyclic groups $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$.

In ring theory, finitely cogenerated modules play a key role in cogenerator theory and Morita duality. The chapter's lattice apparatus is the abstract scaffold for these classical module-theoretic results.

When duality fails

Not every theorem dualises cleanly. The submodule lattice $S_R(M)$ has a natural top ($M$) and bottom ($0$), but its structural asymmetry (modules vs quotient modules) means many module-theoretic results don't have clean duals. The lattice-theoretic apparatus, by contrast, is fully self-dual when one works abstractly. This is one of the reasons the lattice approach is sometimes more powerful than the module-theoretic approach — it makes structural symmetries explicit that algebra obscures.

CONNECTION P3 Linearly compact modules

A topological refinement: linearly compact modules have a topology making them "compact" in a topological sense. Connects to finitely cogenerated via Onodera's theorem.
Linearly compact modules (Lefschetz, Zelinsky)

Definition. A module $M$ over a topological ring is linearly compact if it carries a linear topology (open submodules form a base of neighbourhoods of $0$) such that $M$ is complete in this topology and every "closed" sub-thing is "linearly compact."

More concretely: $M$ is linearly compact if every system of closed cosets $\{m_i + N_i\}$ with the finite intersection property has a non-empty intersection.

Examples. $p$-adic integers $J_p$ as a topological $\mathbb Z_p$-module. Profinite groups (Pontryagin-dual). Rings of formal power series.

Onodera's theorem (1973). "Linearly compact modules and cogenerators II" (Hokkaido Math. J.): a module is finitely cogenerated iff it's linearly compact with essential socle.

So the chapter's "co-compact lattice" via Theorem 11.2 corresponds to "linearly compact module" — with topology coming from the open submodule structure.

The connection is deep: lattice co-compactness has a topological shadow via linear compactness. This connects abstract lattice theory to functional analysis (Banach spaces with weak topologies), $p$-adic analysis (linearly compact $p$-adic modules), and topological ring theory.

CONNECTION P4 Torsionless modules with finite essential socle

A specific class of finitely cogenerated modules studied in the 1980s-90s. Connects to QF rings and Morita duality.
Torsionless modules and rings with finite essential socle

Definition. A module $M$ is torsionless if it embeds into a product of copies of the ring $R$ (as a right $R$-module). Equivalently: $M$ has a generating family of "torsion-free" elements.

Connection to finitely cogenerated. A torsionless module with finitely generated essential socle is finitely cogenerated. The converse (finitely cogenerated ⟹ torsionless) requires additional ring-theoretic conditions (e.g., over QF rings, every finitely cogenerated module is torsionless).

QF (Quasi-Frobenius) rings. A ring $R$ is QF if $R$ is right Artinian and right self-injective. Equivalently: $R$ is right Artinian and the regular module $R_R$ is finitely cogenerated. Finite-dimensional Hopf algebras over a field are QF.

Morita duality. For a QF ring $R$, the categories $\mathrm{mod-R}$ and $R\mathrm{-mod}$ are dually equivalent — finitely generated and finitely cogenerated swap roles under the duality. The chapter's apparatus is the lattice scaffold for this classical result.

Recent research extends these ideas: the 2018 paper "Torsionless Modules and Rings with Finite Essential Socle" (Gómez-Pardo, Guil-Asensio) develops the theory in detail, with applications to ring classifications.

CONNECTION P5 Prologos — co-compact cells and finite-witness from above

Where co-compactness appears in Prologos: cells with finite-meet structure, dual finiteness conditions, and topological-flavoured cell types.
Where Chapter 11 lives in Prologos

(1) Co-compact cells: finite-witness for "trivial intersection." A cell type $L_{\mathrm{cell}}$ is co-compact iff every "meeting-to-bottom" family is finite-witnessed. For Prologos: this is the dual of the compactness condition for fan-out joins; it provides a finiteness guarantee for fan-in meets. Useful when validating "all hypotheses null" / "all assumptions falsified" reasoning.

(2) The socle-essential-and-compact condition (Theorem 11.2). For a cell type $L_{\mathrm{cell}}$ to be co-compact (in CG modular settings) iff $s(L_{\mathrm{cell}})$ is compact and essential — i.e., the cell's "atomic foundation" is finitely generated AND touches every non-zero state. This is a strong design constraint for cells admitting clean dual reasoning.

(3) Order duality and the Hyperlattice Conjecture. Prologos's design mantra is "all-at-once, all in parallel, structurally emergent information flow ON-NETWORK." Order duality (Chapter 11's deep dive) suggests a mirror principle: information flow can be analysed structurally via either direction (joins or meets). The Hyperlattice Conjecture's claim — that the Hasse diagram IS the parallel decomposition — is order-self-dual: it makes the same claim for $L$ and $L^{\mathrm{op}}$.

(4) Linearly compact cell types: the topological connection. Connection Panel P3's linearly compact modules suggest a topology-flavoured cell type design, where cells carry a topology making them "compact" in a topological sense. Useful for cells representing $p$-adic-style content or asymptotic information.

(5) The Boolean coincidence (Proposition 11.5). In Boolean cell types (e.g., the worldview cube $Q_n$), co-compact element ⟺ compact element. This is a special-case symmetry that simplifies design for fully-Boolean cells but doesn't apply to non-distributive cells (most QTT and term-domain lattices).

Exercises 11.1 – 11.6 — Worked

Ex. 11.1P(M) — dual compact / co-compact characterisation

(i) For an arbitrary set $M$, let $(\mathcal P(M), \subseteq)$ be the complete lattice of all the subsets of $M$. A subset $X \subseteq M$ is dual compact in this lattice if and only if $\mathcal C_M(X) = M - X$ is finite. (ii) A subset $X \subseteq M$ is co-compact in this lattice if and only if $X$ is finite (i.e., if and only if $X$ is compact in this lattice).

Solution

(i) Dual compact: $X$ dual compact means: for every family $\{Y_i\}$ with $X \supseteq \bigcap Y_i$, there's a finite $F$ with $X \supseteq \bigcap_{i \in F} Y_i$.

If $M - X$ is finite (= $X$ has finite complement), say $M - X = \{m_1, \ldots, m_n\}$, then for any $\{Y_i\}$ with $\bigcap Y_i \subseteq X$, each $m_k$ is not in $\bigcap Y_i$, so $m_k \notin Y_{i_k}$ for some $i_k$. Hence $\bigcap_{k=1}^n Y_{i_k} \subseteq M - \{m_1, \ldots, m_n\} = X$. Finite witness.

Conversely, if $M - X$ is infinite, take $Y_i = M - \{m_i\}$ for each $m_i \in M - X$. Then $\bigcap Y_i = X$, but no finite intersection equals $X$ (since each finite intersection misses a finite set, but $M - X$ is infinite). So $X$ is not dual compact.

(ii) Co-compact: $X$ co-compact means $X/0 \subseteq \mathcal P(M)$ is co-compact. Now $X/0 = \mathcal P(X)$, a Boolean lattice on $X$.

$\mathcal P(X)$ is co-compact iff every $\bigcap Y_i = \emptyset$ in $\mathcal P(X)$ has finite witness. This holds iff $X$ is finite: if $X$ finite, every intersection-zero family has finite witness; if $X$ infinite, take singletons $\{x\}$ for each $x \in X$; their intersection is empty but no finite sub-family suffices.

So $X$ co-compact ⟺ $X$ finite ⟺ $X$ compact in $\mathcal P(M)$.

Commentary

The exercise illustrates the key asymmetry: dual compact (finite complement) ≠ co-compact (finite). For $\mathcal P(M)$ they're related but distinct: dual compact = "cofinite," co-compact = "finite."

Ex. 11.2$\bigoplus_p \mathbb Z_p$ submodule lattice not co-compact

Let $\mathbb P$ be the set of all prime numbers. In $\mathbb Z$-Mod show that the lattice of all the $\mathbb Z$-submodules of $G = \bigoplus_{p \in \mathbb P} \mathbb Z_p$ is not co-compact.

Solution

Take the family $\{H_p : p \in \mathbb P\}$ where $H_p = \bigoplus_{q \ne p} \mathbb Z_q$ (the direct sum of all $\mathbb Z_q$ except for $q = p$).

Each $H_p$ is a submodule of $G$. We have $\bigcap_p H_p = \bigcap_p \bigoplus_{q \ne p} \mathbb Z_q = 0$ (any element in all $H_p$ has zero $q$-component for every $q$, so is $0$).

For finite witness: take any finite subset $F \subseteq \mathbb P$. Then $\bigcap_{p \in F} H_p = \bigoplus_{q \notin F} \mathbb Z_q \ne 0$ (since $F$ is finite, infinitely many primes remain).

So no finite sub-family witnesses the trivial intersection, and the submodule lattice of $G$ is not co-compact.

Commentary

The exercise shows: $G = \bigoplus_p \mathbb Z_p$ is a "co-uniform-dimension" abelian group with infinite Goldie dimension — the socle (= $G$) is essential but not finitely generated. By Theorem 11.2, this rules out co-compactness.

Ex. 11.3Determine abelian groups with co-compact subgroup lattice

Determine the abelian groups which have a co-compact lattice of subgroups.

Solution

By Theorem 11.2, $L(G)$ is co-compact iff $\mathrm{soc}(G)$ is finitely generated and essential in $G$.

For abelian groups: $\mathrm{soc}(G) = $ join of $\{g \in G : pg = 0\}$ over primes $p$ that divide elements' orders. $\mathrm{soc}(G)$ finitely generated iff $\mathrm{soc}(G)$ is finite (since elements of finite order in abelian groups, if finitely generated, are finite). So $\mathrm{soc}(G)$ is finite.

Essential in $G$: every non-zero subgroup intersects $\mathrm{soc}(G)$ non-trivially. For abelian groups, this rules out:
• Torsion-free non-trivial parts: $\mathbb Z$ has $\mathrm{soc} = 0$, not essential.
• Mixed groups with non-trivial torsion-free part: socle doesn't see the torsion-free part.

So $G$ must be torsion. Combined with finite socle: $G$ is a torsion abelian group with finite socle that's essential.

Classification: $G$ has co-compact subgroup lattice iff $G$ is a finite direct sum of cocyclic groups (= a sum of finitely many $\mathbb Z_{p^k}$ and $\mathbb Z_{p^\infty}$ for various primes $p$ and $k$).

Examples: $\mathbb Z_{p^n}$, $\mathbb Z_{p^\infty}$, $\mathbb Z/n$ (finite), finite direct sums of these.

Non-examples: $\mathbb Z$, $\mathbb Q$, $\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z = \bigoplus_p \mathbb Z_{p^\infty}$ (infinite direct sum of distinct-prime cocyclics — socle infinite).

Commentary

The classification: abelian groups with co-compact subgroup lattice = finite direct sums of cocyclic groups. A clean structural answer obtained via Theorem 11.2 + classical abelian group theory.

Ex. 11.4Vector spaces with co-compact subspace lattice

Which are the vector spaces having a co-compact lattice of subspaces?

Solution

By Theorem 11.2: $L = \mathrm{Sub}(V)$ is co-compact iff $\mathrm{soc}(V)$ is compact (= finitely generated as a sublattice / vector space) and essential.

For vector spaces over a field $k$: every subspace is "atomic" in the sense that 1-dimensional subspaces are atoms. So $\mathrm{soc}(V) = V$ (every non-zero vector space is semiatomic). The socle is essential iff $V \ne 0$ and the socle equals $V$.

The socle is finitely generated (= compact) iff $V$ is finite-dimensional.

So: $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$ is co-compact iff $V$ is finite-dimensional (or $V = 0$).

This is a clean answer: among vector spaces, co-compactness of the subspace lattice is equivalent to finite-dimensionality.

Commentary

For vector spaces, "co-compact" matches "finite-dimensional." This recovers the classical fact: finite-dimensional vector spaces are exactly the finitely cogenerated modules over a field. The Goldie dimension equals the linear dimension.

Ex. 11.5$c \wedge a$ co-compact in $a/0$

If $c$ is a co-compact (or dual compact) element in a complete lattice $L$ and $a \in L$, show that $c \wedge a$ is co-compact (respectively dual compact) in $a/0$.

Solution

Co-compact case: $c$ co-compact means $c/0$ is co-compact. We show $(c \wedge a)/0$ is co-compact (within $a/0$). Note $(c \wedge a)/0 = \{x \in L : 0 \le x \le c \wedge a\} = c/0 \cap a/0$, with the obvious order.

Take any $\{x_i\}_{i \in I}$ in $(c \wedge a)/0$ with $\bigwedge_i x_i = 0$. Each $x_i \le c$, so $\{x_i\} \subseteq c/0$. By co-compactness of $c/0$: finite witness $F$ with $\bigwedge_{i \in F} x_i = 0$. Same finite witness works in $(c \wedge a)/0$.

Dual compact case: $c$ dual compact in $L$ means: for $X \subseteq L$ with $c \ge \bigwedge X$, there's finite $F \subseteq X$ with $c \ge \bigwedge F$.

For $c \wedge a$ dual compact in $a/0$: take $X \subseteq a/0$ with $c \wedge a \ge \bigwedge X$. Then $c \ge c \wedge a \ge \bigwedge X$, so by dual compactness of $c$ in $L$: finite $F \subseteq X$ with $c \ge \bigwedge F$. Combined with $\bigwedge F \le a$ (since $X \subseteq a/0$), we get $c \wedge a \ge \bigwedge F$.

Commentary

The exercise shows both notions descend cleanly through interval restriction. Useful for inductive arguments over interval sublattices.

Ex. 11.6Three equivalent conditions for $r(1/a) = 0$ everywhere

Verify the equivalence of the following assertions:
(a) $r(1/a) = 0$ for each $a \in L$;
(b) each proper element of $L$ is a meet of maximal elements.
The conditions imply in a modular compactly generated lattice $L$ the condition:
(c) each co-compact quotient sublattice $1/a$ ($a \in L$) is semiatomic.
If $L$ is artinian all the conditions are equivalent.

Solution

(a) ⟺ (b): $r(1/a) = $ meet of all maximal elements in $1/a$ (= elements of $L$ maximal among those $\ge a$ but $< 1$).

$r(1/a) = 0$ in $1/a$ means: the meet of maximals (in $1/a$) equals the bottom of $1/a$, which is $a$. Lifting to $L$: $a$ = meet of maximals of $L$ that are $\ge a$.

So (a) ⟺ "every $a$ is the meet of maximal elements of $L$ above $a$" ⟺ (b).

(a, b) ⟹ (c) (in CG modular): If $1/a$ is co-compact, by the dual of Lemma 11.4 (applied to $L^{\mathrm{op}}$) or via Theorem 11.2 dualised: $1/a$ has $r(1/a) = 0$ + co-compact ⟹ semiatomic. Or directly: in CG modular co-compact, by Prop 11.4, $1/a$ is semiatomic when $r(1/a) = 0$.

If $L$ artinian, all equivalent: Artinian ⟹ co-compact (every $1/a$ is artinian, hence co-compact). So (c) becomes "every $1/a$ semiatomic." Combined with (a)/(b) implications, all three are equivalent.

Commentary

The exercise gives three equivalent conditions for the radical to vanish "everywhere" — in every upper interval. In artinian lattices, this also forces semiatomicity in every upper interval. The pattern: radical zero + chain conditions ⟹ very strong structural decomposition.
