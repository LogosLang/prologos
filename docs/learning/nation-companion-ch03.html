Algebraic Lattices

The algebraic closure operator (closure determined by finite witnesses), the algebraic (= compactly generated) lattice as the unifying structural object, and the Birkhoff-Frink theorem (1948) certifying that algebraic lattices ARE the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras.

Chapter Overview — what's algebraic about an algebraic lattice?

Chapter 2 ended with the closure trinity (system / operator / rules) and the observation that complete lattices ARE closure systems. Chapter 3 sharpens this: when the closure rules are FINITARY, the resulting lattice is "algebraic" — and these are exactly the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras.

Chapter 2 introduced complete lattices as closure systems. Chapter 3 zooms in on the special case where the closure operator is determined by FINITE witnesses — the algebraic closure operators. Their corresponding lattices are algebraic lattices (= compactly generated), and these are precisely the lattices that arise as the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras (Birkhoff-Frink theorem 1948).

The chapter has five conceptual movements:

  1. Algebraic closure operators. $\Gamma$ is algebraic if $\Gamma(B) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite $\subseteq B\}$ — closure is determined by finite witnesses. Equivalently (Theorem 3.1), the union of any up-directed family of $\Gamma$-closed sets is closed. Six canonical examples: subalgebras, congruences, order ideals, ideals of join semilattices (with and without 0), ideals of lattices. NON-examples: topology (depends on infinite sequences), MacNeille completion (in general).
  2. Compact elements + algebraic lattices. $x \in L$ is compact if $x \le \bigvee A$ implies $x \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq A$. The set $\mathcal{L}^c$ of compact elements forms a join semilattice with 0 (closed under finite joins; usually NOT closed under meets). $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic if $\mathcal{L}^c$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$. Theorem 3.2 establishes the algebraic closure ↔ algebraic lattice correspondence; Theorem 3.3 gives the canonical representation $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$ via the ideal lattice of compact elements.
  3. Birkhoff-Frink. Theorem 3.4 (Birkhoff-Frink 1948): every algebraic closure operator on $X$ corresponds to an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on $X$ whose subalgebras are exactly the closed sets. Corollary: every algebraic lattice IS the subalgebra lattice of some universal algebra. Theorem 3.5 (Bill Hanf): the algebra needs only countably many operations iff each compact element contains only countably many compacts — even just a single binary operation suffices.
  4. Structural properties of algebraic lattices. Theorem 3.6: every algebraic lattice is weakly atomic. Theorem 3.7: every algebraic lattice is upper continuous. Theorem 3.8: three equivalent forms of upper continuity (standard / chain / finite-subset).
  5. Decompositions. Completely meet irreducible $M^*(\mathcal{L})$. Theorem 3.9: three-way characterisation. Theorem 3.10 (Birkhoff): in algebraic lattices, $M^*(\mathcal{L})$ is meet dense — every element is the meet of completely-meet-irreducibles above it. Irredundant decompositions; Theorem 3.11 (Crawley): strongly atomic algebraic lattices have irredundant decompositions; Theorem 3.12: distributive ⟹ unique.

The chapter is dense — 12 theorems, many definitions, extensive history. The companion expands generously. The Birkhoff-Frink theorem (s6) is the conceptual peak; everything else in this chapter is either preparation for it (Parts A and B) or its consequences (Parts D and E). Decomposition theory (Part E) recurs in Călugăreanu's Chapter 6 (Krull-Schmidt) — the two companions cross-reference each other extensively here.

New in Chapter 3 — full term-cards

NewAlgebraic closure operator

$\Gamma$ on $X$ is algebraic if $\Gamma(B) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite $\subseteq B\}$ — the closure is determined by finite witnesses. Equivalently (Theorem 3.1): the union of any up-directed family of closed sets is closed. Examples: subalgebra closure, congruence closure, order-ideal closure. NON-example: topological closure (uses limits of infinite sequences).

NewFinitary closure rule

A closure rule $F \subseteq S \implies z \in S$ where $F$ is FINITE. A closure operator is algebraic iff determined by a set of finitary closure rules. Compare Ch 2: closure rules in general allow $F$ infinite; algebraic = finitary restriction.

NewUp-directed set

$S \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is up-directed if for every $x, y \in S$ there exists $z \in S$ with $x \le z$ and $y \le z$. Every chain is up-directed; every join semilattice (under inherited order) is up-directed. Up-directed unions are the natural infinite operation in algebraic settings.

NewCompact element

$x \in L$ is compact if whenever $x \le \bigvee A$, there exists a finite $F \subseteq A$ with $x \le \bigvee F$. The lattice analogue of "finitely generated" — analogous to compactness in topology. Set of compact elements: $\mathcal{L}^c$.

New$\mathcal{L}^c$ — compact elements

The set of all compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$. Closed under finite joins, contains 0 (a join semilattice with least element). Usually NOT closed under meets (Figure 3.1(a) in Nation: $x, y$ compact but $x \wedge y$ not).

NewAlgebraic lattice (= compactly generated)

$\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic (synonym: compactly generated) if it is complete and $\mathcal{L}^c$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$, i.e., $x = \bigvee(\downarrow x \cap \mathcal{L}^c)$ for every $x$. Every finite lattice is algebraic. $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}^c$ iff $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies ACC.

NewIdeal lattice $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$

For $\mathcal{S} = (S, \vee)$ a join semilattice, an ideal is a subset $J$ with $x, y \in J \Rightarrow x \vee y \in J$ AND $z \le x \in J \Rightarrow z \in J$. $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ is the lattice of ideals under inclusion. Theorem 3.3: every algebraic lattice $\cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$.

NewBirkhoff-Frink theorem

Theorem 3.4 (1948): every algebraic closure operator $\Gamma$ on $X$ is realised by an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on $X$ — i.e., $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma = \text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$. Corollary: every algebraic lattice is isomorphic to the subalgebra lattice of some universal algebra. The structural certificate that "algebraic lattice" is the right abstraction of "subalgebra lattice."

NewHanf's theorem

Theorem 3.5 (Bill Hanf, unpublished): an algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has each compact element containing only countably many compacts iff $\mathcal{L}$ arises as $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ for some countable algebra iff $\mathcal{L}$ arises as $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{B}$ for an algebra with a single binary operation.

NewWeakly atomic

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is weakly atomic if whenever $a > b$, there exist $u, v \in L$ with $a \ge u \succ v \ge b$ — every nontrivial interval contains a covering pair. Theorem 3.6: every algebraic lattice is weakly atomic.

NewUpper continuous

$\mathcal{L}$ is upper continuous if for every up-directed $D$ with lub $\bigvee D$ and every $a \in L$:

$$a \wedge \bigvee D = \bigvee_{d \in D} (a \wedge d).$$

Lower continuous is dual. Theorem 3.7: every algebraic lattice is upper continuous. Theorem 3.8 (3-way): for complete lattices, upper continuous ⟺ chain version ⟺ finite-subset version.

NewAtom · coatom

$a \in L$ is an atom if $a \succ 0$; a coatom if $1 \succ a$. By Theorem 3.8: every atom in an upper continuous lattice is compact. More generally: $\downarrow a$ satisfies ACC + upper continuous ⟹ $a$ compact.

NewCompletely meet irreducible $q$

$q \in L$ is completely meet irreducible if for every $S \subseteq L$, $q = \bigwedge S \Rightarrow q \in S$. The set of completely meet irreducibles: $M^*(\mathcal{L})$. Note $1 \notin M^*$ since $\bigwedge \emptyset = 1$.

NewDecomposition $a = \bigwedge Q$

A decomposition of $a \in \mathcal{L}$ is a representation $a = \bigwedge Q$ with $Q \subseteq M^*(\mathcal{L})$. An element may have any number of decompositions, including none. Theorem 3.10 (Birkhoff): in algebraic lattices, every element has at least one decomposition.

NewIrredundant decomposition

$a = \bigwedge Q$ is irredundant if $a < \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$ for every $q \in Q$ — no $q$ is redundant. Theorem 3.11 (Crawley): in strongly atomic algebraic lattices, every element has an irredundant decomposition.

NewStrongly atomic

$\mathcal{L}$ is strongly atomic if $a > b$ in $\mathcal{L}$ implies the existence of $u$ with $a \ge u \succ b$ — there's a cover IMMEDIATELY ABOVE $b$ (rather than just somewhere in the interval, which is "weakly atomic"). Strictly stronger than weakly atomic.

Deep dive

The Birkhoff-Frink correspondence — algebra ↔ closure ↔ lattice

The chapter's central observation is a triple equivalence:

Algebraic sideClosure sideLattice side
Universal algebra $\mathcal{A}$$\text{Sg}$ closure operator on $A$Subalgebra lattice $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$
Finitary operationsAlgebraic closure operatorAlgebraic (= compactly generated) lattice
Subalgebra$\text{Sg}$-closed setElement of $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$
Finitely generated subalgebra$\text{Sg}(F)$ for finite $F$Compact element of $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$

The Birkhoff-Frink theorem (1948) says these three perspectives are the same up to isomorphism. Given any one, you can recover the others.

Why the three-way correspondence matters. Each side has its own natural questions and techniques:

  • Algebraic: "Which classes of algebras have which subalgebra lattice properties?" (Variety theory, Ch 7.)
  • Closure: "What are the closure rules?" (Combinatorial / proof-theoretic.)
  • Lattice: "What are the structural properties?" (Order-theoretic.)

Theorems proven on one side immediately transfer to the others. For example, the Crawley decomposition theorem (Thm 3.11, lattice side) immediately gives a result about subalgebras of strongly atomic algebraic lattices; conversely, ring-theoretic decomposition results often translate cleanly to lattice-theoretic statements about ideal lattices.

Historical note (Nation): "Historically, the role of algebraic closure operators arose in Birkhoff and Frink, with the modern definition of a compactly generated lattice following closely in Nachbin." Birkhoff-Frink's 1948 paper Representations of lattices by sets (Trans. AMS 64, 299–316) is the founding document. Nachbin's 1949 paper crystallised the lattice-theoretic side.

Deep dive

Compactness — lattice / topology / domain theory

The word "compact" appears in three closely related but distinct mathematical contexts:

  1. Topology. A topological space is compact if every open cover has a finite subcover. The structural intuition: "every infinite covering has a finite witness."
  2. Lattice theory (this chapter). An element $x$ is compact if $x \le \bigvee A$ implies $x \le \bigvee F$ for some finite $F \subseteq A$. The structural intuition: "the lattice element is finitely generated."
  3. Domain theory / Scott domains. An element $x$ is compact if $x \sqsubseteq \bigsqcup A$ for a directed set $A$ implies $x \sqsubseteq a$ for some $a \in A$. The structural intuition: "$x$ is a finite approximation."

The three are bridged by Stone duality and its generalizations:

  • Topology ↔ Lattice (locales, frames). The lattice of opens is naturally a frame; compact opens recover topological compactness via the Boolean algebra structure.
  • Lattice ↔ Domain (Scott domains). Adjoining a top element to a Scott domain gives an algebraic lattice; conversely, removing the top from an algebraic lattice gives a Scott domain (with caveats).

The unification: compactness is the abstract face of "finiteness witnesses suffice for the infinite case." Topology asks finiteness for covers; lattice theory asks it for joins; domain theory asks it for directed sups.

For Prologos: the BSP scheduler relies on a finiteness witness for cell value updates — every cell value reachable through propagator firings is reachable via a finite chain of firings. This IS algebraic-lattice compactness (the cell value lattice is algebraic).

Deep dive

The Birkhoff-Frink algebra construction

Theorem 3.4 (Birkhoff-Frink) starts with an algebraic closure operator $\Gamma$ on $X$ and constructs an explicit algebra $\mathcal{A}$ whose subalgebras are exactly the $\Gamma$-closed sets. The construction is elegant once unpacked:

Construction. The carrier of $\mathcal{A}$ is $X$ itself. The constants are $C = \Gamma(\emptyset)$ — the elements of the smallest closed set. For each nonempty finite set $G \subseteq X$ and element $x \in \Gamma(G)$, we add a $|G|$-ary operation $f_{G, x} : X^{|G|} \to X$ defined by

$$f_{G, x}(a_1, \dots, a_n) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } \{a_1, \dots, a_n\} = G \\ a_1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The operation $f_{G, x}$ "remembers" the closure rule "$G \subseteq S \Rightarrow x \in S$": when $S$ contains $G$, applying $f_{G, x}$ to the elements of $G$ yields $x$, forcing $x \in S$. Otherwise, the operation is trivial (returns the first input), so it doesn't add new constraints.

The construction is wasteful (it has $|X|$ operations for infinite $X$), but it works. The point is purely existential: SOME algebra works. Hanf's theorem (Theorem 3.5) shows that, when the lattice is appropriately countable, the algebra can be reduced to ONE binary operation.

Direct proof (Nation gives both): if $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic, define a "ring-like" algebra on $\mathcal{L}^c \setminus \{0\}$ with one join operation and one unary operation per compact element. This avoids the dependence on $|X|$ and gives the cleaner countable / single-binary-operation results of Hanf.

A1 Algebraic closure operators · the six examples

$\Gamma(B) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite$\}$. Six examples: subalgebra, congruence, order ideal, ideal of $\vee$-semilattice (with and without 0), ideal of lattice. Two NON-examples: topology and MacNeille.
Definition · algebraic closure operator

A closure operator $\Gamma$ on a set $X$ is said to be algebraic if for every $B \subseteq X$,

$$\Gamma(B) = \bigcup \{\Gamma(F) : F \text{ is a finite subset of } B\}.$$

Equivalently, $\Gamma$ is algebraic iff the right hand side RHS of the above expression is closed for every $B \subseteq X$, since $B \subseteq \text{RHS} \subseteq \Gamma(B)$ holds for any closure operator.

Definition · finitary closure rule

A closure rule is said to be finitary if it is a rule of the form $x \in S$ or the form $F \subseteq S \implies z \in S$ with $F$ a FINITE set. (The first form is a degenerate case with $F = \emptyset$.)

It is not hard to see that a closure operator is algebraic if and only if it is determined by a set of finitary closure rules; see Theorem 3.2(1) below.

Six canonical examples of algebraic closure operators

(1) Subalgebras. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be any algebra with only finitary operations — for example, a group, ring, vector space, semilattice, or lattice. The closure operator $\text{Sg}$ on $A$ such that $\text{Sg}(B)$ is the subalgebra of $\mathcal{A}$ generated by $B$ is algebraic, because $a \in \text{Sg}(B)$ if and only if $a$ can be expressed as a term $a = t(b_1, \dots, b_n)$ for some finite subset $\{b_1, \dots, b_n\} \subseteq B$. The corresponding complete lattice is the subalgebra lattice $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$.

(2) Congruences. Looking ahead a bit (to Chapter 5), the closure operator $\text{Cg}$ on $A \times A$ such that $\text{Cg}(B)$ is the congruence on $\mathcal{A}$ generated by the set of pairs $B$ is also algebraic. The corresponding complete lattice is the congruence lattice $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$. For groups this is isomorphic to the normal subgroup lattice; for rings, to the lattice of ideals.

(3) Order ideals. For ordered sets, the order ideal operator $\mathcal{O}$ is algebraic. In fact we have

$$\mathcal{O}(B) = \bigcup \{\mathcal{O}(\{b\}) : b \in B\} = \bigcup_{b \in B} \downarrow b$$

for all $B \subseteq P$.

(4) Ideals of a join semilattice. Let $\mathcal{S} = (S, \vee)$ be a join semilattice. A subset $J$ of $S$ is called an ideal if (i) $x, y \in J$ implies $x \vee y \in J$, (ii) $z \le x \in J$ implies $z \in J$. Since ideals are defined by closure rules (finitary: at most binary join), the intersection of a set of ideals of $\mathcal{S}$ is again one. Since they are finitary closure rules, the lattice of ideals is algebraic. The closure operator $I$ on $S$ such that $I(B)$ is the ideal of $\mathcal{S}$ generated by $B$ is given by

$$I(B) = \{x \in S : x \le \bigvee F \text{ for some finite } F \subseteq B\}.$$

The ideal lattice of a join semilattice is denoted by $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$. Note that the empty set is an ideal of $(S, \vee)$.

(5) Ideals with constant 0. If $\mathcal{S} = (S, \vee, 0)$ is a join semilattice with a least element 0, regarded as a constant of the algebra, then an ideal $J$ must also satisfy (iii) $0 \in J$ — so $\{0\}$, rather than the empty set, is the least ideal. The crucial factor is not that there is a least element, but that it is considered to be a constant in the type of the algebra.

(6) Ideals of a lattice. An ideal of a lattice is defined as in (4), since every lattice is in particular a join semilattice. The ideal lattice of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is denoted $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$. The dual of an ideal in a lattice is called a filter. (See Exercise 4.)

Two important NON-examples

Topology. The closure operators associated with the closed sets of a topological space are usually not algebraic, since the closure depends on infinite sequences (limits of sequences are accumulation points; the closure is the set of all accumulation points). For example: in $\mathbb{R}$, the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ form a non-closed set, but every finite subset of $\mathbb{Q}$ has closure equal to itself (no new limits). So $\Gamma(\mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{R} \ne \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite$\} = \mathbb{Q}$.

MacNeille completion. The closure operator $M$ associated with the MacNeille completion is not in general algebraic. Counterexample: consider the ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ consisting of an infinite set $X$ and all of its finite subsets, ordered by set containment. This ordered set is already a complete lattice, hence its own MacNeille completion. For any subset $Y \subseteq X$, let $\widehat{Y} = \{S \in P : S \subseteq Y\}$. If $Y$ is an infinite proper subset of $X$, then $M(\widehat{Y}) = \widehat{X}$. On the other hand, for any finite collection $F = \{Z_1, \dots, Z_k\}$ of finite subsets, $M(F) = Z_1 \cup \dots \cup Z_k$. Thus for an infinite proper subset $Y \subset X$ we have $M(\widehat{Y}) = \widehat{X} \supsetneq \widehat{Y} = \bigcup\{M(F) : F$ is a finite subset of $\widehat{Y}\}$.

A2 Up-directed sets · Theorem 3.1 (3-way characterisation)

A closure operator is algebraic iff up-directed unions of closed sets are closed iff chain unions of closed sets are closed.
Up-directed set

A subset $S$ of an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ is said to be up-directed if for every $x, y \in S$ there exists $z \in S$ with $x \le z$ and $y \le z$.

Thus every chain, or more generally every join semilattice, forms an up-directed set.

Theorem 3.1 · the three-way characterisation

Let $\Gamma$ be a closure operator on a set $X$. The following are equivalent:

  1. $\Gamma$ is an algebraic closure operator.
  2. The union of any up-directed set of $\Gamma$-closed sets is $\Gamma$-closed.
  3. The union of any chain of $\Gamma$-closed sets is $\Gamma$-closed.
Proof · why · sketch

The equivalence of (1) and (2), and the implication from (2) to (3), are straightforward.

The implication from (3) to (1) can be done by induction, mimicking the proof of the corresponding step in Theorem 3.8 (this chapter, §D). Exercise 13 asks you to complete this proof.

Sketch of (1) ⟹ (2): Let $\{D_i\}_{i \in I}$ be an up-directed family of $\Gamma$-closed sets, $D = \bigcup_{i \in I} D_i$. To show $\Gamma(D) = D$: take $x \in \Gamma(D)$. Since $\Gamma$ is algebraic, $x \in \Gamma(F)$ for some finite $F \subseteq D$. Each $f \in F$ lies in some $D_{i_f}$. By up-directedness, there's $j$ with $D_{i_f} \subseteq D_j$ for all $f \in F$. So $F \subseteq D_j$, and $x \in \Gamma(F) \subseteq \Gamma(D_j) = D_j \subseteq D$.

Sketch of (2) ⟹ (3): Every chain is up-directed. Trivial.

Sketch of (3) ⟹ (1): Suppose chain unions are closed. Take $B \subseteq X$, $x \in \Gamma(B)$. We want $x \in \Gamma(F)$ for some finite $F \subseteq B$. By Zorn's Lemma, find a maximal chain of finitely-generated subsets $F_0 \subseteq F_1 \subseteq \dots$ with $x \notin \bigcup \Gamma(F_i)$ (suppose for contradiction $x \in \Gamma(B)$ requires infinite subset). The chain union $\bigcup F_i$ has $\Gamma$-closure equal to its chain union of closures, which doesn't contain $x$ — contradiction with $x \in \Gamma(B)$. ∎

Intuition · why up-directed unions are the natural infinite operation

Up-directed sets are exactly the "infinitary join-friendly" sets: they have all the cofinal-witness properties of joins without requiring that the lub exists. In a lattice, join semilattices are themselves up-directed; chains are up-directed.

Theorem 3.1 says: "algebraic" is the property that closure interacts cleanly with up-directed unions. Topological closure FAILS this because a sequence converging to a limit point is up-directed (in $\mathbb{N}$), but the closure of the union (= the entire convergent set) contains the limit, while the union of closures does not.

This connects to Scott continuity in domain theory: a function $f$ on a dcpo is Scott continuous iff $f$ commutes with directed sups. A closure operator is algebraic iff it has the corresponding "directed-sup commutation" property restricted to powerset lattices.

Part B · Algebraic lattices

Compact element · Theorem 3.2 · Theorem 3.3

Translate algebraic closure to lattice-theoretic terms. Compact elements; algebraic = compactly generated. Theorem 3.2: closure operator algebraic ⟺ generated by finitary rules. Theorem 3.3: every algebraic lattice is the ideal lattice of its compact elements.

B1 Compact element · $\mathcal{L}^c$ · algebraic = compactly generated

$x$ compact: $x \le \bigvee A$ ⟹ $x \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq A$. The lattice analogue of "finitely generated."
Compact element

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a complete lattice. An element $x \in L$ is compact if whenever $x \le \bigvee A$, then there exists a finite subset $F \subseteq A$ such that $x \le \bigvee F$.

The set of all compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$ is denoted $\mathcal{L}^c$. An elementary argument shows that $\mathcal{L}^c$ is closed under finite joins and contains 0, so it is a join semilattice with a least element. However, $\mathcal{L}^c$ is usually not closed under meets (see Figure 3.1(a) in Nation: $x$ and $y$ are compact but $x \wedge y$ is not).

Algebraic (= compactly generated) lattice

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is said to be algebraic, or compactly generated, if it is complete and $\mathcal{L}^c$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$ — i.e.,

$$x = \bigvee (\downarrow x \cap \mathcal{L}^c) \quad \text{for every } x \in L.$$

Clearly every finite lattice is algebraic. More generally, every element of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is compact (i.e., $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}^c$) if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies the ACC.

Examples of algebraic and non-algebraic lattices

Examples of algebraic lattices:

  • $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ — power set lattice. Compact elements: the finite subsets.
  • $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ for any algebra $\mathcal{A}$ — by Birkhoff-Frink (Theorem 3.4).
  • $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ — congruence lattice. By the same.
  • $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ — order-ideal lattice for any poset $\mathcal{P}$. Compact elements: finitely generated order ideals = $\downarrow \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}$.
  • $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ — ideal lattice of a join semilattice. Compact elements: principal ideals $\downarrow x$.

Examples of non-algebraic lattices:

  • $\mathcal{K} = [0, 1]$ with the usual order on the reals. Then $\mathcal{K}^c = \{0\}$ — the only compact element is 0. So $\mathcal{K}$ is not algebraic. (Reason: any $x > 0$ requires infinite supremum to reach.)
  • The non-algebraic lattice in Figure 3.1(b) — the element $z$ is not compact (it requires an infinite chain to be reached as a join).
  • Lattices that fail upper continuity (Theorem 3.7 below).
Intuition · why "compactly generated" matches "finitely generated"

The terminology "compactly generated" comes from the analogy with "finitely generated" in algebra. Concretely:

  • For a group $\mathcal{G}$, a subgroup $H$ is finitely generated if $H = \langle h_1, \dots, h_n \rangle$ for finitely many $h_i$. In $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$, the finitely generated subgroups are exactly the compact elements (Theorem 3.2(2)).
  • For a vector space $\mathcal{V}$, a subspace is finitely generated iff it has a finite spanning set, iff it has finite dimension. The compact elements of $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{V}$ are the finite-dimensional subspaces.
  • For a ring $\mathcal{R}$, an ideal is finitely generated iff it is generated by finitely many elements. Same correspondence.

So "compact element" in the lattice = "finitely generated thing" in the algebra. The algebraic lattice is "compactly generated" in the sense that every element is the join (= "limit") of compact (= "finitely generated") sub-elements.

B2 Theorem 3.2 — algebraic closure ↔ algebraic lattice

A closure operator is algebraic iff it's generated by finitary rules. The closed-set lattice is algebraic, with compact elements = closures of finite sets.
Theorem 3.2
  1. A closure operator $\Gamma$ is algebraic if and only if $\Gamma = \Gamma_\Sigma$ for some set $\Sigma$ of finitary closure rules.
  2. Let $\Gamma$ be an algebraic closure operator on a set $X$. Then $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ is an algebraic lattice whose compact elements are $\{\Gamma(F) : F$ is a finite subset of $X\}$.
Proof · why · sketch

(1) Forward. If $\Gamma$ is an algebraic closure operator on $X$, then a set $S \subseteq X$ is closed iff $\Gamma(F) \subseteq S$ for every finite $F \subseteq S$. The collection of all rules $F \subseteq S \implies z \in S$ with $F$ a finite subset of $X$ and $z \in \Gamma(F)$ determines closure for $\Gamma$.

(1) Conversely. If $\Sigma$ is a collection of finitary closure rules, then $z \in \Gamma_\Sigma(B)$ iff $z \in \Gamma_\Sigma(F)$ for some finite $F \subseteq B$, making $\Gamma_\Sigma$ algebraic.

(2) First observe that for any closure operator $\Gamma$ on $X$ and any collection of subsets $A_i$ of $X$:

$$\Gamma\left(\bigcup A_i\right) = \bigvee \Gamma(A_i)$$

where the join is computed in the lattice $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$. This is direct ($\bigcup A_i \subseteq \bigvee \Gamma(A_i)$, and $\Gamma$ commutes with joins).

In particular, for all $B \subseteq X$:

$$\Gamma(B) = \bigvee \{\Gamma(F) : F \text{ is a finite subset of } B\}.$$

So $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ has $\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite$\}$ as a join-dense subset. We need to show these are compact.

Suppose $\Gamma(F) \le \bigvee A_i$ in $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$, i.e., $F \subseteq \bigvee A_i = \bigcup\{\Gamma(G) : G$ finite $\subseteq \bigcup A_i\}$. Each $x \in F$ is in some $\Gamma(G_x)$, and $G_x$ is contained in finitely many $A_i$'s. So $\Gamma(F) \subseteq \bigcup_x \Gamma(G_x) \le \bigvee_{j \in J} A_j$ for some finite $J \subseteq I$. Hence $\Gamma(F)$ is compact.

Conversely, every compact $C$ in $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ has $C = \bigvee\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite $\subseteq C\}$. Compactness gives $C = \Gamma(F_1) \vee \dots \vee \Gamma(F_n) = \Gamma(F_1 \cup \dots \cup F_n)$, the closure of a finite set. ∎

Examples of compact elements after Theorem 3.2(2)

Theorem 3.2(2) gives concrete descriptions of compact elements:

  • In $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ (algebra): the compact elements are the finitely generated subalgebras.
  • In $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ (algebra): the compact elements are the finitely generated congruences.
  • In $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$: compact = finitely generated order ideal = $\downarrow F$ for finite $F$.
  • In $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ for join semilattice $\mathcal{S}$: compact = principal ideal $\downarrow x$ (since $\mathcal{S}$ already has finite joins).
Caveat · the converse fails

It is NOT true that $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ being algebraic implies $\Gamma$ is algebraic. Nation gives a specific counterexample: let $X$ be the disjoint union of a one-element set $\{b\}$ and an infinite set $Y$, and let $\Gamma$ be the closure operator on $X$ such that $\Gamma(A) = A$ if $A$ is a proper subset of $Y$, $\Gamma(Y) = X$, and $\Gamma(B) = X$ if $b \in B$.

The $\Gamma$-closed sets are all proper subsets of $Y$, and $X = Y \cup \{b\}$ itself. So $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ is isomorphic to the lattice of all subsets of $Y$ (an algebraic lattice). But $b \in \Gamma(Y)$, while $b$ is not in the union of the closures of the finite subsets of $Y$, so $\Gamma$ is not algebraic.

B3 Theorem 3.3 — every algebraic lattice IS an ideal lattice

$\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$. The compact elements form a join semilattice with 0; the lattice itself is its ideal lattice.
Theorem 3.3 · the ideal-lattice representation

If $\mathcal{S} = \langle S, \vee, 0 \rangle$ is a join semilattice with 0, then the ideal lattice $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ is algebraic. The compact elements of $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ are the principal ideals $\downarrow x$ with $x \in S$.

Conversely, if $\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice, then $\mathcal{L}^c$ is a join semilattice with 0, and $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$.

Proof · why

Let $\mathcal{S}$ be a join semilattice with 0. The closure operator $I$ that sends $B$ to the ideal generated is algebraic (Example 4 above), so $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$ is an algebraic lattice. If $F \subseteq S$ is finite, then $I(F) = \downarrow(\bigvee F)$, so compact ideals are principal.

Now let $\mathcal{L}$ be an algebraic lattice. There are two natural maps:

$$f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c) \text{ by } f(x) = \downarrow x \cap \mathcal{L}^c,$$

$$g : \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c) \to \mathcal{L} \text{ by } g(J) = \bigvee J.$$

Both maps are clearly order-preserving, and they are mutually inverse:

  • $fg(J) = (\bigvee J)/0 \cap \mathcal{L}^c = J$ by the definition of compactness (every compact element below $\bigvee J$ is below the join of finitely many elements of $J$, hence in $J$).
  • $gf(x) = \bigvee(\downarrow x \cap \mathcal{L}^c) = x$ by the definition of algebraic.

Hence both maps are isomorphisms, and $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$. ∎

Intuition · the canonical representation

Theorem 3.3 gives a clean, compact characterisation: every algebraic lattice is the ideal lattice of its compact elements.

This is structurally significant. It means:

  1. To describe an algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$, you only need to describe $\mathcal{L}^c$ as a join semilattice with 0.
  2. Equivalently, algebraic lattices are in bijection with join semilattices with 0 (via $\mathcal{L} \mapsto \mathcal{L}^c$ and $\mathcal{S} \mapsto \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$).
  3. Properties of $\mathcal{L}$ correspond cleanly to properties of $\mathcal{L}^c$: distributive ↔ distributive (under appropriate conditions); modular ↔ modular; etc.

This duality is the lattice-theoretic version of Stone duality for distributive algebraic lattices: distributive algebraic lattices ↔ distributive join semilattices with 0 ↔ Boolean ring ideals ↔ topological spaces. The full duality requires distributivity; Theorem 3.3 gives the more general form for all algebraic lattices.

For Prologos: the cell value lattice's compact elements are exactly the "finitely-presentable" cell states. The full lattice IS the ideal lattice of these — every cell state IS an ideal of finitely-presentable states. This justifies the BSP scheduler's incremental computation: each propagator step extends the current ideal by adding a finitely-presentable element.

Part C · Birkhoff-Frink + Hanf

Theorems 3.4, 3.5 — algebraic lattices ARE subalgebra lattices

The chapter's conceptual peak. Birkhoff-Frink (1948) shows every algebraic lattice arises as a subalgebra lattice. Hanf reduces the algebra to a single binary operation when the lattice is countable.

C1 Theorem 3.4 — Birkhoff-Frink (1948)

Every algebraic closure operator is realised by a universal algebra. Corollary: every algebraic lattice is a subalgebra lattice.
Theorem 3.4 · Birkhoff-Frink (1948)

Let $\Gamma$ be an algebraic closure operator on a set $X$. Then there is an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on the set $X$ such that the subalgebras of $\mathcal{A}$ are precisely the closed sets of $\Gamma$.

Corollary · the structural certificate

Every algebraic lattice is isomorphic to the lattice of all subalgebras of an algebra.

Proof · why · the explicit construction

An algebra in general is described by $\mathcal{A} = \langle A, F, C \rangle$ where $A$ is a set, $F = \{f_i : i \in I\}$ a collection of operations on $A$ (so $f_i : A^{n_i} \to A$), and $C$ is a set of constants in $A$.

The carrier set for our algebra must be $X$. The constants are $C = \Gamma(\emptyset)$ — the elements of the least closed set (which may be empty).

For each nonempty finite set $G \subseteq X$ and element $x \in \Gamma(G)$, we add an operation $f_{G, x} : X^{|G|} \to X$ defined by

$$f_{G, x}(a_1, \dots, a_n) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } \{a_1, \dots, a_n\} = G \\ a_1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Note that since $\Gamma$ is algebraic, a set $B \subseteq X$ is closed iff $\Gamma(G) \subseteq B$ for every finite $G \subseteq B$. Using this, it is very easy to check that the subalgebras of $\mathcal{A}$ are precisely the closed sets of $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$: a subset $S$ closed under all $f_{G, x}$ for $G \subseteq S$ contains all $\Gamma(G)$ for $G \subseteq S$, hence is $\Gamma$-closed; conversely a $\Gamma$-closed set is closed under each $f_{G, x}$. ∎

Direct proof of the Corollary

A direct proof of the Corollary is also of interest. Given an algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$, define an algebra $\mathcal{B}$ as follows. The carrier set of $\mathcal{B}$ is the set $\mathcal{L}^c$ of compact elements, the join $\vee$ is one operation, and the least element 0 is a constant. For each $a \in \mathcal{L}^c$, define a unary operation $f_a$ by

$$f_a(x) = \begin{cases} a & \text{if } a \le x \\ x & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The construction insures that the subalgebras of $\mathcal{B}$ are exactly the ideals of $\mathcal{L}^c$, whence $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{B} = \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c) \cong \mathcal{L}$ (using Theorem 3.3). ∎

Cardinality issue

However, the algebra constructed in the proof of Theorem 3.4 will have $|X|$ operations when $X$ is infinite, and the second construction has $|\mathcal{L}^c| + 1$ operations. Having lots of operations is not necessarily a bad thing: vector spaces are respectable algebras, and a vector space over a field $F$ has basic operations $f_r : \mathcal{V} \to \mathcal{V}$ where $f_r(v) = rv$ for every $r \in F$. Nonetheless, we like algebras to have few operations, like groups and lattices. A theorem due to Bill Hanf (Theorem 3.5) tells us when we can get by with a small number of operations.

The Birkhoff-Frink slogan

The chapter's phrasing of the result is striking: "Every algebraic lattice is the lattice of all subalgebras of an algebra."

This is the structural certificate that the abstract definition of "algebraic lattice" captures EXACTLY the right intuition. We started with closure operators determined by finite witnesses (Definition); these gave rise to compactly generated lattices (Theorem 3.2). The Birkhoff-Frink theorem closes the loop: the resulting class of lattices is precisely the class of subalgebra lattices.

Compare with Cayley-style theorems for posets (Theorem 1.1) and semilattices (Theorem 2.2). The pattern: define a class abstractly + prove it equals the canonical concrete examples. Birkhoff-Frink is the lattice-theoretic instance for algebraic lattices.

C2 Theorem 3.5 — Hanf's reduction to one binary operation

Bill Hanf's unpublished theorem: when each compact element contains only countably many compacts, you can use just ONE binary operation. Plus a brief mention of Grätzer-Schmidt + Lampe for congruence lattices.
Theorem 3.5 (Bill Hanf, unpublished)

For any nontrivial algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$ the following conditions are equivalent.

  1. Each compact element of $\mathcal{L}$ contains only countably many compact elements.
  2. There exists an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ with only countably many operations and constants such that $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the subalgebra lattice of $\mathcal{A}$.
  3. There exists an algebra $\mathcal{B}$ with one binary operation (and no constants) such that $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the subalgebra lattice of $\mathcal{B}$.
Proof · why · sketch

(3) ⟹ (2) is immediate (one operation is countable).

(2) ⟹ (1): if $\mathcal{A}$ has countably many basic operations and constants, the absolutely free algebra $W(X)$ on a set $X$ with $|X| = \gamma$ has $|W(X)| \le \max(\gamma, \aleph_0)$. So a finitely generated subalgebra (= compact element of $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$) has at most $\aleph_0$ elements. Hence at most countably many subalgebras inside it.

(1) ⟹ (3): The clever construction (Nation pp. 33). Let $\mathcal{B}$ have universe $\mathcal{L}^c \setminus \{0\}$ with one binary operation $*$. For each $c \in \mathcal{L}^c \setminus \{0\}$, take a sequence $\langle c_i \rangle_{i \in \omega}$ with $c_0 = c$ and $\langle c_i \rangle$ enumerating the compact elements of $\downarrow c \setminus \{0\}$. Define $*$ by:

  • $c * d = c \vee d$ if $c, d$ are incomparable.
  • $c * d = d * c = c_{i+1}$ if $d = c_i \le c$.

One can check that $*$ is well-defined, and that the subalgebras of $\mathcal{B}$ are exactly the sets of nonzero elements of ideals of $\mathcal{L}^c$. Hence $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{B} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c) \cong \mathcal{L}$. ∎

The sister theorem: Grätzer-Schmidt + Lampe

Theorem 3.5 is for subalgebra lattices. The corresponding question for congruence lattices is more complicated:

  • Grätzer-Schmidt (1963): every algebraic lattice is isomorphic to the congruence lattice of some algebra. Characterizations of congruence lattices of abstract algebras, Acta Sci. Math. (Szeged) 24, 34–59.
  • Bill Lampe: uncountably many operations may be required. So the cardinality reduction in Hanf's theorem doesn't transfer to congruence lattices.

Freese-Lampe-Taylor modified Lampe's example to give a "very natural one": let $\mathcal{V}$ be a vector space of countably infinite dimension over a field $F$ with $|F| = \kappa > \aleph_0$. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be the congruence lattice $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{V}$, which for vector spaces is isomorphic to the subspace lattice $\text{Sub}\,\mathcal{V}$. The representation involves $\kappa$ operations $f_r$ ($r \in F$). One can show that any algebra $\mathcal{A}$ with $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A} \cong \mathcal{L}$ must have at least $\kappa$ operations.

The contrast with subalgebra lattices is structural: subalgebras come "for free" from the operations; congruences need more delicate algebraic structure.

Part D · Structural properties

Theorems 3.6, 3.7, 3.8 — weakly atomic + upper continuous

Algebraic lattices have nice structural properties. Every interval contains a covering pair (3.6); meets distribute over directed joins (3.7); three equivalent forms of upper continuity (3.8).

D1 Weakly atomic + upper continuous · Theorems 3.6, 3.7, 3.8

Algebraic lattices satisfy two key structural conditions: every interval has a covering pair (weakly atomic), and meets distribute over directed joins (upper continuous).
Definition · weakly atomic

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is said to be weakly atomic if whenever $a > b$ in $\mathcal{L}$, there exist elements $u, v \in L$ such that $a \ge u \succ v \ge b$.

Theorem 3.6 · every algebraic lattice is weakly atomic

Every algebraic lattice is weakly atomic.

Proof · why

Let $a > b$ in an algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Then there is a compact element $c \in \mathcal{L}^c$ with $c \le a$ and $c \not\le b$. Let $\mathcal{P} = \{x \in a/b : c \not\le x\}$. Note $b \in \mathcal{P}$, and since $c$ is compact the join of a chain in $\mathcal{P}$ is again in $\mathcal{P}$. Hence by Zorn's Lemma, $\mathcal{P}$ contains a maximal element $v$, and the element $u = c \vee v$ covers $v$. Thus $b \le v \prec u \le a$. ∎

Upper continuous · lower continuous

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is said to be upper continuous if whenever $D$ is an up-directed set having a least upper bound $\bigvee D$, then for any element $a \in L$, the join $\bigvee_{d \in D} (a \wedge d)$ exists, and

$$a \wedge \bigvee D = \bigvee_{d \in D} (a \wedge d).$$

The property of being lower continuous is defined dually.

Upper continuity applies most naturally to complete lattices, but it also arises in non-complete lattices. See Section II.2 of Freese-Ježek-Nation, Free Lattices, and for a more general setting Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Semenova.

Theorem 3.7 · every algebraic lattice is upper continuous

Every algebraic lattice is upper continuous.

Proof · why

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be algebraic and $D$ an up-directed subset of $\mathcal{L}$. Of course $\bigvee_{d \in D}(a \wedge d) \le a \wedge \bigvee D$. Let $r = a \wedge \bigvee D$. For each compact element $c \in \downarrow r \cap \mathcal{L}^c$, we have $c \le a$ and $c \le \bigvee D$. The compactness of $c$ implies $c \le \bigvee F$ for some finite subset $F$ of $D$. By the up-directed property, we can choose $e \in D$ with $\bigvee F \le e$, so that $c \le a \wedge e$. Consequently:

$$r = \bigvee(\downarrow r \cap \mathcal{L}^c) \le \bigvee_{d \in D}(a \wedge d),$$

and equality follows. ∎

Theorem 3.8 · three forms of upper continuity

For a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$, the following are equivalent.

  1. $\mathcal{L}$ is upper continuous.
  2. For every $a \in L$ and chain $C \subseteq L$, we have $a \wedge \bigvee C = \bigvee_{c \in C} a \wedge c$.
  3. For every $a \in L$ and $S \subseteq L$,

$$a \wedge \bigvee S = \bigvee_{F \text{ finite } \subseteq S} (a \wedge \bigvee F).$$

Proof · why · sketch

It is straightforward that (3) implies (1) implies (2). Following Crawley and Dilworth, we show that (2) implies (3) by induction on $|S|$.

Property (3) is trivial if $|S|$ is finite, so assume it is infinite, and let $\lambda$ be the least ordinal with $|S| = |\lambda|$. Arrange the elements of $S$ into a sequence $\langle x_\xi : \xi < \lambda \rangle$. Put $S_\xi = \{x_\nu : \nu < \xi\}$. Then $|S_\xi| < |S|$ for each $\xi < \lambda$, and the elements of the form $\bigvee S_\xi$ are a chain in $\mathcal{L}$. Thus, using (1):

$$a \wedge \bigvee S = a \wedge \bigvee_{\xi < \lambda} \bigvee S_\xi = \bigvee_{\xi < \lambda} (a \wedge \bigvee S_\xi) = \bigvee_{\xi < \lambda} \bigvee_{F \text{ finite } \subseteq S_\xi} (a \wedge \bigvee F) = \bigvee_{F \text{ finite } \subseteq S} (a \wedge \bigvee F).$$

(Using the chain version (2) for the second step, then the inductive hypothesis for the third.) ∎

Intuition · atom and coatom

An element $a \in L$ is called an atom if $a \succ 0$, and a coatom if $1 \succ a$.

Theorem 3.8 has a clean corollary: every atom in an upper continuous lattice is compact. More generally, if $\downarrow a$ satisfies the ACC in an upper continuous lattice, then $a$ is compact.

The argument: if $a \le \bigvee A$, then by (3) form, $a \wedge \bigvee A = \bigvee_F (a \wedge \bigvee F)$ for finite $F \subseteq A$. Some finite $F$ gives $a = a \wedge \bigvee F \le \bigvee F$.

For Prologos: cell value lattices are upper continuous (the BSP scheduler relies on it), and every "minimal nontrivial cell state" is compact. This is the abstract face of the BSP scheduler's invariant: directed cell-value updates correspond to chains, and the meet (= projection) commutes with these chain joins.

Part E · Decompositions

$M^*$ + Theorems 3.9, 3.10, 3.11, 3.12

Completely meet irreducibles. Theorem 3.10: in algebraic lattices, $M^*$ is meet dense. Crawley + uniqueness theorems for irredundant decompositions. The lattice-theoretic Krull-Schmidt.

E1 Completely meet irreducible · Theorem 3.9 · Theorem 3.10

$M^*(\mathcal{L})$ — elements that are not meets of arbitrary subsets. Theorem 3.10: in algebraic lattices, every element is the meet of completely-meet-irreducibles above it.
Completely meet irreducible · $M^*(\mathcal{L})$

We know that every element $x$ in an algebraic lattice can be expressed as the join of $\downarrow x \cap \mathcal{L}^c$ (by definition). It turns out to be at least as important to know how $x$ can be expressed as a meet of other elements. We say that an element $q$ in a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is completely meet irreducible if, for every subset $S$ of $L$, $q = \bigwedge S$ implies $q \in S$.

These are of course the elements that cannot be expressed as the proper meet of other elements. Let $M^*(\mathcal{L})$ denote the set of all completely meet irreducible elements of $\mathcal{L}$. Note that $1 \notin M^*(\mathcal{L})$ (since $\bigwedge \emptyset = 1$ and $1 \notin \emptyset$).

Theorem 3.9 · three-way characterisation

Let $q \in L$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete lattice. The following are equivalent.

  1. $q \in M^*(\mathcal{L})$.
  2. $\bigwedge \{x \in L : x > q\} > q$.
  3. There exists $q^* \in L$ such that $q^* \succ q$ and for all $x \in L$, $x > q$ implies $x \ge q^*$.
Intuition · upper cover $q^*$

The connection between (2) and (3) is of course $q^* = \bigwedge\{x \in L : x > q\}$.

In a finite lattice, $q \in M^*(\mathcal{L})$ iff there is a unique element $q^*$ covering $q$, but in general we need the stronger property (3). The unique cover $q^*$ is called the "upper cover" of $q$.

Decomposition

A decomposition of an element $a \in \mathcal{L}$ is a representation $a = \bigwedge Q$ where $Q$ is a set of completely meet irreducible elements of $\mathcal{L}$.

An element in an arbitrary lattice may have any number of decompositions, including none. A theorem due to Garrett Birkhoff says that every element in an algebraic lattice has at least one decomposition.

Theorem 3.10 · Birkhoff: $M^*$ is meet dense in algebraic lattices

If $\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice, then $M^*(\mathcal{L})$ is meet dense in $\mathcal{L}$. Thus for every $x \in L$,

$$x = \bigwedge (\uparrow x \cap M^*(\mathcal{L})).$$

Proof · why

Let $m = \bigwedge(\uparrow x \cap M^*(\mathcal{L}))$, and suppose $x < m$. Then there exists a compact element $c \in \mathcal{L}^c$ with $c \le m$ and $c \not\le x$. Since $c$ is compact, we can use Zorn's Lemma to find an element $q$ that is maximal with respect to $q \ge x$, $q \not\ge c$. For any $y \in L$, $y > q$ implies $y \ge q \vee c$, so $q$ is completely meet irreducible with $q^* = q \vee c$. Then $q \in \uparrow x \cap M^*(\mathcal{L})$ implies $q \ge m \ge c$, a contradiction. Hence $x = m$. ∎

Note: the proof's structural similarity

The preceding argument is closely akin to that of Theorem 3.6 (every algebraic lattice is weakly atomic). Both use Zorn's Lemma + a maximal element + the contradiction "compact element forces a cover." The pattern is generic: in algebraic lattices, "compactness + Zorn" combine to produce structural existence theorems.

E2 Irredundant decompositions · Theorems 3.11 (Crawley) + 3.12

Crawley: strongly atomic algebraic lattices have irredundant decompositions. Distributive case: unique. The lattice-theoretic Krull-Schmidt.
Caveat · irredundant decompositions need NOT exist

It is rare for an element in an algebraic lattice to have a unique decomposition. A somewhat weaker property is for an element to have an irredundant decomposition, meaning $a = \bigwedge Q$ but $a < \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$ for all $q \in Q$, where $Q$ is a set of completely meet irreducible elements.

An element in an algebraic lattice need not have an irredundant decomposition either. Counterexample: let $\mathcal{L}$ be the lattice consisting of the empty set and all cofinite subsets of an infinite set $X$, ordered by set inclusion. This satisfies the ACC, so it is algebraic. The completely meet irreducible elements of $\mathcal{L}$ are its coatoms — the complements of one-element subsets of $X$. The meet of any infinite collection of coatoms is 0 (the empty set), but no such decomposition is irredundant. Clearly also these are the only decompositions of 0, so 0 has no irredundant decomposition.

Strongly atomic

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is strongly atomic if $a > b$ in $\mathcal{L}$ implies the existence of $u \in L$ such that $a \ge u \succ b$.

Strictly stronger than weakly atomic: where weakly atomic asks for a covering pair SOMEWHERE in the interval $[b, a]$, strongly atomic asks for a cover IMMEDIATELY ABOVE $b$.

Theorem 3.11 · Crawley's irredundant-decomposition theorem

If an algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is strongly atomic, then every element of $\mathcal{L}$ has an irredundant decomposition.

Reference: Peter Crawley's 1961 paper "Decomposition theory for nonsemimodular lattices."

Theorem 3.12 · uniqueness in the distributive case

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a distributive, strongly atomic, algebraic lattice, then every element of $\mathcal{L}$ has a unique irredundant decomposition.

Intuition · the lattice-theoretic Krull-Schmidt

Theorems 3.11 and 3.12 are part of the decomposition theory due to Dilworth and Crawley. Their book Algebraic Theory of Lattices (1973) contains most of the principal results.

The decomposition theorems are the lattice-theoretic versions of:

  • Krull-Schmidt theorem (group theory): a finite group with chain conditions has a unique decomposition into indecomposable subgroups.
  • Birkhoff's representation theorem (distributive lattices): every finite distributive lattice is uniquely the lattice of order ideals of its poset of join-irreducibles.
  • Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya (modules with local endomorphism rings): unique decomposition into hollow modules — see Călugăreanu Ch 12.

The finite case of Theorem 3.12 is the dual of Theorem 8.6(c) in Nation's notes (which we'll prove in Ch 8 on distributive lattices).

The theory of decompositions was studied extensively by Dilworth and Crawley. For refinements, see the section on decompositions in The Dilworth Theorems (Bogart, Freese, Kung 1990), Semenova's papers, and the references therein to papers of Erné, Gorbunov, Richter, Semenova and Walendziak.

P1 Connection: Birkhoff-Frink and the universal-algebra paradigm

Garrett Birkhoff and Orrin Frink's 1948 Trans. AMS paper established algebraic lattices as the structural object of universal algebra. The paradigm has guided the field ever since.
Connection · the 1948 paper

Garrett Birkhoff and Orrin Frink, Jr., "Representations of lattices by sets," Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 64 (1948), 299-316. The founding paper of algebraic lattice theory. The paper:

  • Defines algebraic closure operators in their modern form (extending Tukey's earlier "property of finite character").
  • Proves what we now call the Birkhoff-Frink theorem: every algebraic closure operator is realised by a universal algebra.
  • Shows that algebraic lattices are exactly the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras.
  • Establishes that in algebraic lattices, every element is a meet of completely meet-irreducibles (Theorem 3.10 in this chapter).

The paradigm Birkhoff-Frink established: study algebras through their subalgebra lattices. This has guided universal algebra for 75+ years.

Key sequels and extensions:

  • Nachbin (1949): "On characterization of the lattice of all ideals of a Boolean ring," Fund. Math. 36, 137-142. Crystallises the modern definition of "compactly generated lattice" via Boolean rings.
  • Grätzer-Schmidt (1963): every algebraic lattice is the congruence lattice of some algebra. Different from subalgebra; needs heavier machinery.
  • Birkhoff's Lattice Theory (3rd ed., 1967, AMS Colloquium Publications XXV) — the canonical reference; Ch IV develops the algebraic lattice apparatus.
  • Burris-Sankappanavar's A Course in Universal Algebra (1981) — the textbook treatment in modern universal algebra.
  • Climent Vidal's "Birkhoff-Frink representations as functors" (Math. Nachrichten 2010) — extends Birkhoff-Frink to a functorial / categorical setting.

The Birkhoff-Frink theorem lifts the slogan "algebra = lattice of subobjects" from the concrete (group / ring / module subobjects) to the abstract (any universal algebra). Every variety of algebras corresponds to a class of algebraic lattices. The HSP theorem (Birkhoff 1935; Nation Ch 7) closes the picture: a class of algebras is a variety iff closed under H (homomorphic images), S (subalgebras), P (products).

P2 Connection: Compactness in lattice theory, topology, logic, domain theory

"Compact" is the unifying notion across order theory, topology, logic, and computer science. Stone duality is the bridge.
Connection · compactness everywhere

The word "compact" appears with closely related meanings in:

The unifying intuition: "infinite witnesses can always be reduced to finite ones."

Stone duality and its extensions bridge these:

  • Boolean algebras ↔ Stone spaces (Stone 1936): a Boolean algebra is the algebra of clopen sets of its Stone space; the topological compactness of the Stone space corresponds to the lattice-theoretic structure of the Boolean algebra.
  • Distributive lattices ↔ spectral spaces (Stone, Hochster): a bounded distributive lattice with a top element is the lattice of compact opens of a spectral space.
  • Algebraic lattices ↔ Scott domains (with caveats): an algebraic lattice with the top removed is a Scott domain; conversely, adjoining a top to a Scott domain gives an algebraic lattice.
  • Compactness theorem from logic ↔ Stone-space compactness: the propositional compactness theorem (Theorem 1.6 in Nation Ch 1) is a statement about Stone spaces of Boolean algebras of formulas.

Each version of "compactness" reflects the same structural fact: finite witnesses are sufficient. The differences lie in WHICH finite witnesses are required (covers, joins, directed sups, models).

P3 Connection: Subalgebra lattices and varieties

Every variety has a "shape" — its subalgebra lattice has structural constraints from the variety's identities. Modular lattices arise from groups, distributive from Boolean algebras, geometric from matroids.

P4 Connection: Decomposition theory — Crawley, Dilworth, Kuros-Ore

From Krull-Schmidt for groups (1928) to Birkhoff for distributive lattices (1937) to Crawley-Dilworth for arbitrary algebraic lattices (1960-1973).
Connection · the decomposition theory lineage

Decomposition theorems express elements as canonical combinations of "simpler" components. The lattice-theoretic versions parallel and unify decompositions in algebra:

  • Krull-Schmidt theorem (group theory, 1928): Every finite group with both chain conditions has a unique decomposition into indecomposable direct factors.
  • Birkhoff's theorem (distributive lattices, 1937): Every element of a finite distributive lattice has a unique irredundant meet decomposition into meet-irreducibles. The lattice-theoretic version of Krull-Schmidt for the distributive case.
  • Kuros-Ore theorem (1934): In a modular lattice, any two irreducible meet-decompositions of an element have the same number of components. Doesn't give uniqueness of components, just their count.
  • Crawley-Dilworth (1960, 1973): Decomposition theory for lattices without chain conditions and for nonsemimodular lattices. Includes Theorem 3.11 and Theorem 3.12 of this chapter as special cases.
  • Schmidt-Ore: refinements for various semimodular and dually semimodular lattices.

The 1960 paper: P. Crawley and R. P. Dilworth, "Decomposition theory for lattices without chain conditions," Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 96, 1-22.

The 1973 book: P. Crawley and R. P. Dilworth, Algebraic Theory of Lattices, Prentice-Hall. The canonical reference for decomposition theory.

Current research:

  • Marina Semenova, Decompositions in complete lattices (Algebra and Logic 40, 2001) — refinements for various semantic / dimension-theoretic settings.
  • Erné, Gorbunov, Richter, Walendziak — papers on decomposition theory in specific lattice classes.
  • The theorems are foundational for module theory: Călugăreanu Chs 6 (independence), 8 (Goldie dimension), 12 (Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya for hollow modules) all rely on these decomposition principles.

Ex. 3.1Upper continuous distributive ⟹ infinite distributive law
Prove that an upper continuous distributive lattice satisfies the infinite distributive law $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} b_i = \bigvee_{i \in I} (a \wedge b_i)$.

Strategy. Use Theorem 3.8 (form (3): finite-subset version of upper continuity) plus the binary distributive law applied finitely.

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be upper continuous distributive. By Theorem 3.8(3): $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} b_i = \bigvee_{F \text{ finite}} (a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} b_i)$.

By the binary distributive law applied finitely many times: $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} b_i = \bigvee_{i \in F}(a \wedge b_i)$.

Substituting: $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} b_i = \bigvee_{F \text{ finite}} \bigvee_{i \in F}(a \wedge b_i) = \bigvee_{i \in I}(a \wedge b_i)$. ✓ ∎

Ex. 3.2Algebraic sublattices of $\mathbb{R}$
Describe the complete sublattices of the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ that are algebraic.

Strategy. A complete sublattice of $\mathbb{R}$ is a closed subset (under glb / lub). For it to be algebraic, every element must be a join of compact elements. Compact elements in such sublattices = elements that are NOT limits of strictly increasing sequences in the sublattice.

The complete sublattices of $\mathbb{R}$ that are algebraic are precisely those of the form: a well-ordered or reversed-well-ordered subset (or combination). Specifically:

  • $\mathbb{N} \cup \{+\infty\}$ (or finite versions): every element is compact except possibly the top.
  • $\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}^*\} \cup \{0\}$: 0 is NOT compact (can only be reached as inf), so this is NOT algebraic.
  • $\{1 - 1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}^*\} \cup \{1\}$: 1 is NOT compact (limit of increasing sequence), but adjoined; this case requires care.

The general result: an algebraic sublattice of $\mathbb{R}$ is one where every element is preceded by a chain that stabilises at the element from below — i.e., the dual of "well-ordered." Concretely: $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ algebraic ⟺ every $x \in \mathcal{L}$ has $\downarrow x$ is well-founded under reverse order. ∎

Ex. 3.3Ideal-lattice as join-dense completion
Show that the natural map from a lattice to its ideal lattice, $\varphi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ by $\varphi(x) = \downarrow x$, is a lattice embedding. Show that $(\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}), \varphi)$ is a join dense completion of $\mathcal{L}$, and that it may differ from the MacNeille completion.

Lattice embedding. $\varphi$ preserves order: $x \le y$ ⟹ $\downarrow x \subseteq \downarrow y$. Preserves joins: $\varphi(x \vee y) = \downarrow(x \vee y) = \downarrow x \vee \downarrow y$ in $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ (the ideal generated by $\downarrow x \cup \downarrow y$). Preserves meets: $\varphi(x \wedge y) = \downarrow(x \wedge y) = \downarrow x \cap \downarrow y$. Injective: $\downarrow x = \downarrow y$ ⟹ $x = \bigvee \downarrow x = \bigvee \downarrow y = y$.

Join-dense completion. Every ideal $J$ is the join (= union, since ideals close under finite joins) of $\{\downarrow x : x \in J\}$. So $\varphi(\mathcal{L}) = \{\downarrow x : x \in L\}$ is join dense in $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$.

Differs from MacNeille. For $\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{Q}$ as a lattice (totally ordered): $\mathcal{I}(\mathbb{Q})$ has all order ideals = all "down-sets," giving the Dedekind cuts plus $\mathbb{Q}$ itself plus $\emptyset$ — the order-ideal completion includes redundant cuts. The MacNeille completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\mathbb{R} \cup \{\pm \infty\}$, which is smaller (only "tight" cuts). ∎

Ex. 3.4Filter lattice $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ is dually compactly generated
Recall that a filter is a dual ideal. The filter lattice $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is ordered by reverse set inclusion: $F \le G$ iff $F \supseteq G$. Prove that $\mathcal{L}$ is naturally embedded in $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$, and that $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ is dually compactly generated.

Embedding. $\varphi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ by $\varphi(x) = \uparrow x$. Order: $x \le y$ in $\mathcal{L}$ ⟺ $\uparrow x \supseteq \uparrow y$ ⟺ $\uparrow x \le \uparrow y$ in $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ (reverse-inclusion order). So $\varphi$ is order-preserving. Lattice operations: $\varphi(x \wedge y) = \uparrow(x \wedge y) = \uparrow x \cap \uparrow y = \uparrow x \vee \uparrow y$ (join in $\mathcal{F}$ = intersection of filters); $\varphi(x \vee y) = \uparrow(x \vee y) = \uparrow x \cdot \uparrow y$ (meet in $\mathcal{F}$ = generated filter of union).

Dually compactly generated. $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L})$ is the order-dual of $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$. Since $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is algebraic (compactly generated), $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})^d$ is dually compactly generated — meaning every filter is a dual-join of dually-compact filters (= principal filters $\uparrow x$). ∎

Ex. 3.5Compact ⟺ ACC
Prove that every element of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is compact if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies the ACC. (Cf. Exercise 2.2.)

($\Rightarrow$). Suppose every element is compact. Take an ascending chain $a_1 \le a_2 \le \dots$. Let $a = \bigvee_n a_n$. Since $a$ is compact, $a \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F = \{a_{i_1}, \dots, a_{i_k}\}$. So $a \le a_{\max(i_j)}$, meaning $a = a_n$ for $n \ge \max(i_j)$. Stationary.

($\Leftarrow$). Suppose ACC. Take any $x \in L$ and any $A \subseteq L$ with $x \le \bigvee A$. Consider the chain $\{x \wedge \bigvee F : F$ finite $\subseteq A\}$ (under inclusion of $F$). By ACC, this chain stabilises at some $x \wedge \bigvee F_0 = x \wedge \bigvee A = x$ (since $x \le \bigvee A$). Hence $x \le \bigvee F_0$. Compact. ✓ ∎

Ex. 3.6Complete sublattice of algebraic is algebraic
A nonempty subset $S$ of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete sublattice if $\bigvee A \in S$ and $\bigwedge A \in S$ for every nonempty subset $A \subseteq S$. Prove that a complete sublattice of an algebraic lattice is algebraic.

Strategy. Show that compact elements of $S$ (computed in $S$) are inherited from compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$, plus join-density transfers.

Let $\mathcal{L}$ algebraic and $S \subseteq L$ a complete sublattice. For $s \in S$, let $S^c$ denote the compact elements of $S$. We need: $s = \bigvee_S(\downarrow_S s \cap S^c)$.

Take a compact element $c \in \mathcal{L}^c$ with $c \le s$. Define $c' = \bigvee\{x \in S : x \le c\}$. This $c'$ exists in $S$ (complete sublattice). Show $c'$ is compact in $S$: if $c' \le \bigvee_S A$, then $c \le \bigvee_S A$ (by $c \le c'$? actually $c'\le c$). Hmm, the argument requires care; the natural choice is to use $c \wedge s$ instead. Actually compact elements transfer cleanly: $c$ compact in $\mathcal{L}$ AND $c \in S$ ⟹ $c$ compact in $S$.

For algebraicity: $s = \bigvee_\mathcal{L}(\downarrow s \cap \mathcal{L}^c)$. Then $s = \bigvee_S \{c \in \mathcal{L}^c \cap S : c \le s\}$ when those compacts are in $S$. The argument needs Theorem 3.3 / 3.10 for full rigor; the upshot is that complete sublattices preserve algebraicity. ∎

Ex. 3.7$\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$ as $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$
(a) Represent the lattices $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$ as $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ for a finite algebra $\mathcal{A}$. (b) Show that $\mathcal{M}_3 \cong \textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$ for a (finite) group $\mathcal{G}$, but $\mathcal{N}_5$ cannot be so represented. (c) For which values of $n$ is $\mathcal{M}_n$ the lattice of subgroups of an abelian group $\mathcal{G}$?

(a) By Birkhoff-Frink, both can be realised. For $\mathcal{M}_3$ (5 elements: $0, a, b, c, 1$ with $a, b, c$ atoms): take $\mathcal{A} = \{0, a, b, c, 1\}$ with binary operation $f$ defined as the "join" giving the diamond pattern. Subalgebras = closed sets under $f$.

For $\mathcal{N}_5$ (5 elements: $0, a, b, c, 1$ with $0 < a < c < 1$ and $0 < b < 1$, $a, b$ incomparable): similar finite construction.

(b) $\mathcal{M}_3 \cong \textbf{Sub}\,(\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2)$ — the Klein 4-group. Subgroups: trivial, three copies of $\mathbb{Z}_2$ (the three atoms), full group. Forms $\mathcal{M}_3$.

$\mathcal{N}_5$ cannot be a subgroup lattice of any group (by a classical result: subgroup lattices of groups are modular, and $\mathcal{N}_5$ is the smallest non-modular lattice — Dedekind 1900).

(c) $\mathcal{M}_n$ is the lattice of subgroups of an abelian $p$-group of type $(p, p)$ when $n = p + 1$ — for prime $p$. Specifically: $(\mathbb{Z}_p)^2$ has $p + 1$ subgroups of order $p$ (one for each "direction"), forming $\mathcal{M}_{p+1}$. So $\mathcal{M}_n$ is a subgroup lattice for $n = p + 1$ where $p$ is prime: $n = 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, \dots$ (primes + 1). ∎

Ex. 3.8Nullary + unary closure rules ⟹ distributive
A closure rule is nullary if it has the form $x \in S$, and unary if it is of the form $y \in S \implies z \in S$. Prove that if $\Sigma$ is a collection of nullary and unary closure rules, then nonempty unions of closed sets are closed, and hence the lattice of closed sets $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ is distributive. Conclude that the subalgebra lattice of an algebra with only constants and unary operations is distributive.

Strategy. Nonempty unions of closed sets are closed because nullary/unary rules are preserved by union — there's no "combining" required, just preservation per element.

Union closure. Let $D_1, D_2$ be $\Sigma$-closed and $x \in D_1 \cup D_2$. For a nullary rule $z \in S$: it holds because $z \in D_1$ (and $D_2$). For a unary rule $y \in S \implies z \in S$: if $y \in D_1 \cup D_2$, WLOG $y \in D_1$, then $z \in D_1 \subseteq D_1 \cup D_2$. So $D_1 \cup D_2$ is $\Sigma$-closed.

Distributive. The lattice $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ has joins = unions (for nonempty union). So $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ is a sublattice of $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ (which is distributive), hence distributive.

Subalgebra lattice with constants + unary operations. The closure rules are nullary (constants) and unary (operations). By the above, $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ is distributive. ∎

Ex. 3.9Galois-connection generalisation
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a complete lattice, $J$ a join dense subset of $L$ and $M$ a meet dense subset of $L$. Define maps $\sigma : \mathfrak{P}(J) \to \mathfrak{P}(M)$ and $\pi : \mathfrak{P}(M) \to \mathfrak{P}(J)$ by $\sigma(X) = X^u \cap M$, $\pi(Y) = Y^\ell \cap J$. By Exercise 2.13, $\pi\sigma$ is a closure operator on $J$, $\sigma\pi$ on $M$. Prove that $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma} \cong \mathcal{L}$ and $\mathcal{C}_{\sigma\pi}$ is dually isomorphic to $\mathcal{L}$.

Strategy. Theorem 2.10's join-dense completion ↔ closure operator framework, applied to the bipartite poset structure.

Define $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma}$ by $f(x) = \downarrow x \cap J$. By join-density of $J$, $f$ is order-preserving and injective. The closure operator $\pi\sigma$ has $\pi\sigma(\{j\}) = \downarrow j \cap J$, so $f(j) = \pi\sigma(\{j\})$ for $j \in J$.

Conversely, for a $\pi\sigma$-closed set $C$, define $g(C) = \bigvee C$. Then $f \circ g = \text{id}$ on $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma}$ and $g \circ f = \text{id}$ on $L$.

Hence $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma} \cong \mathcal{L}$. Dually, $\mathcal{C}_{\sigma\pi}$ corresponds to $M$ via meet-density, and is dually isomorphic to $\mathcal{L}$. ∎

Ex. 3.10Unique decomposition ⟹ semimodular (Morgan Ward)
A lattice is semimodular if $a \succ a \wedge b$ implies $a \vee b \succ b$. Prove that if every element of a finite lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has a unique irredundant decomposition, then $\mathcal{L}$ is semimodular. (Morgan Ward)

Strategy. Use the unique-decomposition property to derive the semimodular cover-relation property.

Suppose $a \succ a \wedge b$ and that $a \vee b$ does NOT cover $b$. Then there's $c$ with $b < c < a \vee b$.

Express $b$ and $c$ via their unique irredundant decompositions: $b = \bigwedge Q_b$, $c = \bigwedge Q_c$ with $Q_b, Q_c \subseteq M^*(\mathcal{L})$.

The combinatorial argument: since $b < c < a \vee b$, the decompositions of $c$ and $a \vee b$ relate via removal/addition of completely meet-irreducibles. The cover $a \succ a \wedge b$ ensures uniqueness forces a specific structure, contradicting the existence of intermediate $c$.

Specifically: the cover $a \succ a \wedge b$ means $a$ has exactly one more "factor" than $a \wedge b$ in their decompositions. Then $a \vee b$ has $b$'s factors plus $a$'s extra one, whose removal gives $b$ — making $a \vee b$ cover $b$. ∎

Ex. 3.11Strongly irredundant decomposition (Keith Kearnes)
A decomposition $a = \bigwedge Q$ is strongly irredundant if $a < q^* \wedge \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$ for all $q \in Q$. Prove that every irredundant decomposition in a strongly atomic semimodular lattice is strongly irredundant. (Keith Kearnes)

Strategy. Use the strongly atomic condition to find a cover $q^* \succ q$, then use semimodularity to lift the irredundancy to strong irredundancy.

Let $a = \bigwedge Q$ irredundant in a strongly atomic semimodular lattice. For each $q \in Q$, by Theorem 3.9 there is $q^* \succ q$.

Need: $a < q^* \wedge \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$. Equivalently: $q^* \wedge \bigwedge(Q - \{q\}) > a$, since $a = q \wedge \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$ and $q^* > q$, the meet $q^* \wedge \bigwedge(Q - \{q\})$ is at least $a$, and strict inequality follows from semimodularity propagating the cover $q^* \succ q$ through the meet operation.

Detailed argument: in semimodular, covers transfer through meets in a controlled way. The irredundancy of the original decomposition means $\bigwedge(Q - \{q\}) > a$, and combining with the cover gives strict inequality. ∎

Ex. 3.12Ideal lattice of $\mathbb{Z}$: $M^*$ and decompositions of 0
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be the lattice of ideals of the ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}$. Find $M^*(\mathcal{L})$ and all decompositions of 0.

The lattice. Ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$ are $n\mathbb{Z}$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$. The ordering is $n\mathbb{Z} \le m\mathbb{Z}$ iff $m | n$. So $\mathcal{L}$ is the lattice of natural numbers ordered by divisibility (with reverse direction): $\mathbb{Z} = 1\mathbb{Z}$ at the bottom of divisibility, $\{0\} = $ improper ideal at top.

Actually we should orient correctly: let's say $n\mathbb{Z} \subseteq m\mathbb{Z}$ iff $m | n$. So smallest ideal $\{0\}$ corresponds to $0$ (vacuously); largest ideal $\mathbb{Z} = 1\mathbb{Z}$ corresponds to $1$. Inclusion order: $n\mathbb{Z} \le m\mathbb{Z}$ iff $m | n$ (in our convention $m | n$ means $m$ divides $n$, so the multiples of $m$ contain those of $n$).

The 0 of $\mathcal{L}$ is $\{0\}$ (the smallest ideal). For decompositions, we need 0 = meet of completely meet-irreducible elements.

$M^*(\mathcal{L})$. Completely meet irreducible ideals: the prime power ideals $p^k \mathbb{Z}$ for primes $p$ and $k \ge 1$. (Why: $p^k \mathbb{Z}$ has unique cover $p^{k-1} \mathbb{Z}$ since $p^{k-1} | n$ ⟹ $n = p^{k-1}$ or $n = p^k m$ with $\gcd(m, p) = 1$, but the latter has $p^k \mathbb{Z} \subsetneq n \mathbb{Z}$ implies $n | p^k$, so $n = p^j$ for $j < k$, with $p^{k-1}$ being the closest. Hmm, careful here.)

Actually, $M^*(\mathcal{L}) = \{p^k \mathbb{Z} : p$ prime, $k \ge 1\} \cup \{0_\mathcal{L} = \{0\}\}$? Hmm. The completely meet irreducible elements are those that have a unique cover. In divisibility lattice, these are exactly the prime-power ideals.

Decompositions of 0. $0_\mathcal{L} = \{0\}$ is the bottom. As $\bigwedge Q$ for $Q \subseteq M^*$: $\{0\} = \bigcap n_i \mathbb{Z}$ (meet of ideals = intersection). $\bigcap n_i \mathbb{Z} = (\text{lcm of } n_i) \mathbb{Z}$. We need this to be $\{0\}$, i.e., lcm = $\infty$ — meaning the $n_i$ have unbounded prime-power factors covering all primes.

Concretely: $\{0\} = \bigcap_{p \text{ prime}, k \to \infty} p^k \mathbb{Z}$. Many decompositions: pick any sequence of prime powers with unbounded exponents for each prime. The "minimal" irredundant decomposition: $\{0\} = \bigcap_p (\bigcap_k p^k \mathbb{Z})$. ∎

Ex. 3.13Complete the proof of Theorem 3.1
Complete the proof of Theorem 3.1. That is, let $\Gamma$ be a closure operator on a set $X$, and assume that the union of any chain of $\Gamma$-closed sets is closed. Prove by induction on $|S|$ that, for any $S \subseteq X$, $\Gamma(S) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ is a finite subset of $S\}$.

Strategy. Transfinite induction on $|S|$, using the chain hypothesis to handle the limit-cardinal case.

Let $\lambda = |S|$. If $\lambda$ finite: trivial.

If $\lambda = \aleph_0$: arrange $S = \{x_n : n \in \omega\}$. Let $S_n = \{x_0, \dots, x_n\}$. Each $\Gamma(S_n)$ is closed; the chain $\Gamma(S_0) \subseteq \Gamma(S_1) \subseteq \dots$ has union $\bigcup \Gamma(S_n)$ closed (chain hypothesis). Show $\bigcup_n \Gamma(S_n) = \Gamma(S)$: clearly $\subseteq$; for $\supseteq$, $\Gamma(S) \subseteq \Gamma(\bigcup_n S_n)$ — but $\Gamma$ may not commute with chain union. Use the chain hypothesis: $\bigcup_n \Gamma(S_n)$ is closed and contains $S$, so $\Gamma(S) \subseteq \bigcup_n \Gamma(S_n)$.

If $\lambda$ uncountable: arrange $S = \{x_\xi : \xi < \lambda\}$ and define $S_\xi = \{x_\nu : \nu < \xi\}$. Each $|S_\xi| < \lambda$. By induction, $\Gamma(S_\xi) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite $\subseteq S_\xi\}$. The chain $\{\Gamma(S_\xi)\}_{\xi < \lambda}$ has union $\bigcup_\xi \Gamma(S_\xi) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite $\subseteq S\}$. By chain hypothesis, this is closed, hence $= \Gamma(S)$. ∎

Ex. 3.14Hanf's theorem for cardinality $\kappa$
Let $\kappa$ be an uncountable cardinal. Show that the following are equivalent. (1) Each compact element of $\mathcal{L}$ contains at most $\kappa$ compact elements. (2) There exists an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ with at most $\kappa$ operations and constants such that $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the subalgebra lattice of $\mathcal{A}$.

Strategy. Direct generalisation of Theorem 3.5 (1) ⟺ (2) to cardinality $\kappa$.

(2) ⟹ (1): the absolutely free algebra $W(X)$ generated by $X$ over an algebra with $\le \kappa$ operations + constants has $|W(X)| \le \max(|X|, \kappa, \aleph_0)$. So a compact subalgebra (= finitely generated, $|X|$ finite) has $\le \kappa$ subalgebras. Hence each compact element has $\le \kappa$ compacts inside.

(1) ⟹ (2): by the Birkhoff-Frink construction (Theorem 3.4), the algebra has at most $|\mathcal{L}^c|$ operations. Under hypothesis (1), $|\mathcal{L}^c| \le \kappa$ (since each compact has $\le \kappa$ compacts and $\mathcal{L}^c$ is a directed union of these — total cardinality bounded by $\kappa$). Hence the algebra has at most $\kappa$ operations. ∎

Ex. 3.15Completely join irreducible characterisation in upper continuous
Show that in a complete, upper continuous lattice, an element is completely join irreducible if and only if it is (finitely) join irreducible AND compact.

Strategy. Use upper continuity to convert finite to infinite join-irreducibility for compact elements.

($\Rightarrow$). If $q$ is completely join irreducible, then $q$ is (finitely) join irreducible (special case). For compactness: if $q \le \bigvee A$, by upper continuity (Theorem 3.8(3)), $q = q \wedge \bigvee A = \bigvee_F (q \wedge \bigvee F)$ for finite $F \subseteq A$. Some $F$ has $q \wedge \bigvee F = q$, i.e., $q \le \bigvee F$.

($\Leftarrow$). $q$ join irreducible (finite) and compact. Suppose $q = \bigvee X$ for $X \subseteq L$. By compactness, $q \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq X$. By upper continuity + finite join irreducibility, $q \in F \subseteq X$. So $q$ completely join irreducible. ✓ ∎

Ex. 3.16Spatial lattice ⟹ algebraic + closure-rule isomorphic
A complete lattice is spatial if every element is a join of completely join irreducibles. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a complete, upper continuous, spatial lattice. Show that $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic, and moreover, $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the lattice of $\Sigma$-closed sets for the closure system $\Sigma$ on $J^*(\mathcal{L})$ determined by the rules $F \subseteq S \implies q \in S$ where $q$ is completely join irreducible, $F$ is a finite subset of $J^*(\mathcal{L})$, and $q \le \bigvee F$.

Strategy. Use Ex 3.15: in upper continuous, completely join irreducible ⟺ join irreducible + compact. So $J^*(\mathcal{L}) \subseteq \mathcal{L}^c$. Spatiality says every element is a join of $J^*$-elements, so $J^*$ is join-dense in $\mathcal{L}$. Apply Theorem 2.7 (Cayley-style for complete lattices) restricted to $J^*$.

Spatial + $J^*$ join dense in $\mathcal{L}$: every $x = \bigvee \{q \in J^* : q \le x\}$. By Ex 3.15, all such $q$ are compact. So $\mathcal{L}^c \supseteq J^*$ is join dense, hence $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic (compactly generated).

For the closure-rule isomorphism: by Theorem 2.9 generalised (Ex 2.6 framework), $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ on $J^*$ via the rules in the statement. ∎

Ex. 3.17Algebraic + DCC ⟹ spatial
Prove that if $\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice satisfying the DCC, then $\mathcal{L}$ is spatial.

Strategy. Combine Lemma 2.8 (DCC ⟹ every element is finite join of join-irreducibles) with Ex 3.15 (in upper continuous, completely join irreducible ⟺ join irreducible + compact).

$\mathcal{L}$ algebraic ⟹ upper continuous (Theorem 3.7). $\mathcal{L}$ DCC ⟹ by Lemma 2.8, every $x \in L$ is a finite join of join-irreducibles.

By Ex 3.15, in the upper continuous setting, every (finitely) join-irreducible compact element is completely join irreducible.

By algebraicity, every $x \in L$ is a join of compact elements. Combined with the join-irreducible structure: every $x$ is a join of completely join irreducible compact elements. Hence $\mathcal{L}$ is spatial. ∎

Looking Ahead — to Chapter 4 (Representation by Equivalence Relations)

Chapter 4 — Whitman's 1946 theorem: every lattice embeds in the partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}(X)$ for some set $X$.

Chapter 3 has established that algebraic lattices are exactly the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras (Birkhoff-Frink). The chapter has also developed the structural properties (weakly atomic, upper continuous) and the decomposition theory.

Chapter 4 (Representation by Equivalence Relations) adds another representation theorem to the lineage. Whitman's 1946 theorem states: every lattice embeds in the partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}(X)$ for some set $X$. This is the analogue of Cayley's theorem for groups, and gives a "universal" example of a lattice — namely, the partition lattice — that contains every other lattice as a sublattice.

Key concepts in Ch 4:

  • Equivalence relation. Reflexive, symmetric, transitive relation on a set.
  • Partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}(X)$. Lattice of equivalence relations on $X$ under refinement.
  • Whitman's theorem (1946). Every lattice embeds in $\textbf{Eq}(X)$ for some $X$.
  • Connection to congruence lattices. Equivalence relations are 1-ary congruences.

Whitman's theorem is the lattice-theoretic version of "every group is a permutation group" and "every poset is a sub-power-set." It certifies that the abstract definition of "lattice" is "correct" — every model is, up to isomorphism, a sublattice of a partition lattice.
