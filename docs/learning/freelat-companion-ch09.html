Tschantz's Theorem and Maximal Chains

The structural payoff of Part II. Theorem 9.10 (Tschantz 1990): every infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. Generalises Whitman's $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$ (Theorem 1.28) from a specific embedding to ALL infinite intervals. Theorem 9.12: every infinite interval contains a coverless element — a "complement" to Day's weak-atomicity theorem. Theorem 9.15: if $c/d$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic, there is a maximal chain in $c/d$ with NO covers — a dense maximal chain. Surprising in light of Day's Theorem (every interval contains a covering pair).

~120–150 min read 3 sections · 15 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 179–187 · PDF pp. 188–196

In this chapter we give some applications of the characterization of semisingular elements in Chapter VIII. First we prove Tschantz's beautiful result that every infinite interval of a free lattice contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. Using Tschantz's Theorem, we will show that every infinite interval of a free lattice contains a coverless element, i.e., an element with no upper and no lower cover. Finally we shall show that if $a > b$ in a free lattice and $a/b$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic, then there is a maximal chain in $a/b$ without any covers. This is a little surprising in light of Day's Theorem.

Tschantz's Theorem has an interesting history. After the first and third authors had characterized all finite intervals of free lattices [62], they tried to show that every infinite interval of a free lattice would contain a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. By Theorem 1.28 and Corollary 1.13, in order to do this one would simply have to show any such interval contains a join and meet irredundant set of three elements. This seemed like it should be easy. We were quite surprised when we could not do this and, in fact, were not every able to show the existence of two incomparable elements. That is, we could not rule out the possibility of an infinite interval in a free lattice which is a chain! We were able to derive some unlikely consequences of such an interval, but none produced a contradiction. We showed this problem to Tom Harrison who derived much stronger (and more unlikely) consequences, but still without reaching a contradiction. Harrison showed it to Steve Tschantz, who was able to show that such an interval could not exist, and, in fact, that every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

Interestingly, most of Tschantz's proof is devoted to showing that there is no infinite interval which is a chain. We can simplify this part of the proof considerably by using the characterization of semisingular elements from the last chapter. For the rest we follow his original proof from [131].

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter IX (paraphrased)

Chapter IX is the structural payoff of Part II. After Chapters VI-VII-VIII developed the totally-atomic-element machinery, the cover-chain bound, the connected-component classification, and the singular/semisingular characterizations, Chapter IX cashes in: every infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. This generalises Whitman's specific Theorem 1.28 ($\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$) to a uniform statement about ALL infinite intervals.

The chapter has three parts. §1 Tschantz's Theorem: introduce the central technical object $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q$ completely-join-irreducible, $q \le u$, $v \le \kappa(q)\}$. Theorem 9.1 reformulates Day's theorem (Chapter IV) as "$u \not\le v$ iff $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \neq \emptyset$". The proof builds incrementally: Lemma 9.2 (κ-injectivity), Lemma 9.3 ($\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q\}$ characterization), Theorem 9.5 ($\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$ + $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| > 1$ ⟹ infinite), Lemma 9.6 + Corollary 9.7 (avoiding $K$), Theorem 9.8 (pair of κ-incomparable elements — the critical step), Theorem 9.9 (infinite κ-incomparable sequence). Theorem 9.10 — Tschantz's theorem: every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. Corollary 9.11: every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(Y)$ for $|Y| \le |X| + \omega$. §2 Coverless Elements: Theorem 9.12 — every infinite interval contains a coverless element (an element with no upper AND no lower cover). The "complement" to Day's Theorem (every interval contains a covering pair). Lemmas 9.13-9.14 provide the structural support. §3 Maximal Chains: Theorem 9.15 — if $c/d$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic, there is a maximal chain in $c/d$ with NO covers — a "dense maximal chain". The construction is incremental, using Theorem 9.12 to insert coverless elements at each step.

The chapter's mathematical drama: Day's Theorem says every interval has covers; Tschantz's Theorem (and its corollaries) says infinite intervals are STRUCTURALLY COMPLEX in additional ways — they contain free sublattices, coverless elements, and dense maximal chains. The two results coexist: covers EXIST but are not OMNIPRESENT. The infinite intervals have rich combinatorial geography that Day's qualitative existence result doesn't capture.

New · Chapter IX vocabulary

Three clusters of new vocabulary. The set $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$: for $u, v \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q$ is completely join irreducible, $q \le u$, and $v \le \kappa(q)\}$. The chapter's central technical object. By Theorem 9.1 (Day's theorem reformulation), $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \neq \emptyset$ iff $u \not\le v$. Monotonicity: $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ when $u' \le u$ and $v' \ge v$. The set $K$: $K = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q$ completely join irreducible AND ($q$ totally atomic OR $\kappa(q)$ totally atomic)$\}$. The "obstruction set" — $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K$ being empty is the precondition for the infinitude theorem (Theorem 9.5). Coverless element + dense maximal chain: a coverless element has NO upper AND NO lower cover — the simplest historical example is in Example 3.39 (Chapter III). A dense maximal chain in an interval $c/d$ is a maximal chain with NO covers — every cover-relation between consecutive elements is filled by intermediate elements (so the chain has no "step jumps"). Theorem 9.15 constructs dense maximal chains in suitable intervals.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. The $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ framework as a reformulation of Day's theorem. Theorem 9.1 says: $u \not\le v$ iff $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is nonempty. This is just Day's theorem (Theorem 4.1) restated in set-theoretic form. The reformulation enables COUNTING arguments: $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| = 0$ (trivial), $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| = 1$ (Lemma 9.3 gives a structural characterization), or $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| > 1$ (the case where Tschantz's argument works). The K-set as the obstruction. $K$ contains all completely-join-irreducibles $q$ such that $q$ or $\kappa(q)$ is totally atomic. By Corollary 6.11, $K$ is FINITE for each $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. So $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K$ is finite, and $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) - K$ is the "useful" remainder. Theorem 9.5: if $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$ AND $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| > 1$, then $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite. The proof uses Lemma 9.4's branching argument: from $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$, construct two new elements in either $\mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v)$ or $\mathbf{Q}(u, v \vee q)$. Iterating yields infinitely many. The chain Theorem 9.5 → 9.8 → 9.9 → 9.10. Theorem 9.5: $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ becomes infinite. Theorem 9.8: in an infinite $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$, there exist $q, q'$ with $q \le \kappa(q')$ and $q' \le \kappa(q)$ — i.e., a "κ-incomparable" pair. Theorem 9.9: extends to an infinite κ-incomparable sequence $\{q_i\}$. Theorem 9.10: 3-of-them generate $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (by Corollary 1.13 + Theorem 1.28); then $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow$ that $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. Each theorem builds on the previous; the final result is the Tschantz embedding.

PART A · TSCHANTZ'S THEOREM — §1

Theorem 9.10: every infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$

Build $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ infinite (Theorems 9.1, 9.5), find a κ-incomparable pair (Theorem 9.8), extend to a κ-incomparable infinite sequence (Theorem 9.9), then 3-of-them generate $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ which embeds $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ (Theorem 9.10). § 1 Tschantz's Theorem

The chapter's centerpiece. The proof's choreography is highly structured — each step builds on the previous via a tightly orchestrated sequence of lemmas and theorems.

The Q(u, v) framework (book p. 179)

Definition · $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$

For $u, v \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, define

$$\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q \text{ is completely join irreducible}, q \le u, \text{ and } v \le \kappa(q)\}.$$

Clearly if $u' \le u$ and $v' \ge v$, then $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$. This monotonicity will be used repeatedly in this section.

Theorem 9.1 · Day's theorem reformulated

If $u \not\le v$, then $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is nonempty, and conversely.

Theorem 9.1 is Day's theorem (Theorem 4.1) of Chapter IV in set-theoretic form. The "strong version" of Day's theorem can be expressed in terms of $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ as follows: when $u \not\le v$, $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ contains an element $q$ of D-rank $\le \rho^d(u) + \rho(v) - 1$. This rank-bound aspect is implicit in the chapter's use of $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.

Lemma 9.2 · κ-injectivity on chains

If $q$ and $r$ are completely join irreducible elements, $q \le r$, and $\kappa(q) \ge \kappa(r)$, then $q = r$.

Proof: if $q < r$, then $q \le r_* \le \kappa(r) \le \kappa(q)$, a contradiction. $\square$

Lemma 9.3 · $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q\}$ characterization

$\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q\}$ if and only if $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$, $u \wedge \kappa(q) \le v$, and $u \le v \vee q$.

Proof: forward — if $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q\}$ and $u \wedge \kappa(q) \not\le v$, there is a $q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v) \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$, with $q' \neq q$ (since $\kappa(q) \le \kappa(q')$ would contradict κ-injectivity). Similarly if $u \not\le v \vee q$. Conversely: any $q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ satisfies $q' \le u \le v \vee q \le \kappa(q) \vee q = \kappa(q)^*$, so by the dual of Theorem 3.1, either $q \le q'$ or $q' \le \kappa(q)$. The latter case combined with $q' \le u \wedge \kappa(q) \le v$ gives $v \le \kappa(q')$, so $\kappa(q') \ge v \ge u \wedge \kappa(q) \ge q \wedge \kappa(q) = q_*$, contradicting $q'$'s irreducibility. So $q \le q'$. By symmetry $q' \le q$, hence $q = q'$. $\square$

The K-set and the infinitude theorem (book pp. 180–181)

Definition · K — the obstruction set

Let

$$K = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q \text{ is completely join irreducible AND ($q$ or $\kappa(q)$ is totally atomic)}\}.$$

By Corollary 6.11 (finitely many totally atomic), $K$ is FINITE for each $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The case when $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is finite but has more than one element involves totally atomic elements, just as do chains of covers of length at least two.

Lemma 9.4 · branching from $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$ and $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$, then $|\mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v)| \neq 1$ and $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v \vee q)| \neq 1$.

Proof of Lemma 9.4: Suppose $\mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v) = \{q'\}$. Then Lemma 9.3 gives $u \wedge \kappa(q) \le v \vee q'$. Apply (W) to this inequality. Four cases (dual proof for the second statement):

  1. $u \le v \vee q'$: $q' \le \kappa(q)$, contradiction.
  2. $\kappa(q) \le v \vee q'$: $q' \le \kappa(q) \le \kappa(q')^* = v \vee q'$. By Lemma 3.28, $q'$ is totally atomic, contradicting $q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$.
  3. $u \wedge \kappa(q) \le v$: contradiction.
  4. $u \wedge \kappa(q) \le q'$: $q_* = q \wedge \kappa(q) \le u \wedge \kappa(q) \le q' \le \kappa(q)$. So $\kappa(q)$ is totally atomic, hence $q \in K$, contradiction.

None of these can hold, so by (W), $|\mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v)| \neq 1$. The dual argument gives $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v \vee q)| \neq 1$. $\square$

Theorem 9.5 · Q(u,v) infinite when ∩ K = ∅ and |Q(u,v)| > 1

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$ and $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| > 1$, then $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite.

Proof: induction constructs $q_0, q_1, q_2, \ldots \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ and chains $u_0 \ge u_1 \ge \cdots$, $v_0 \le v_1 \le \cdots$ with $\mathbf{Q}(u_i, v_i) \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ and $|\mathbf{Q}(u_i, v_i)| > 1$. Let $u_0 = u, v_0 = v$ and choose $q_0 \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$. Assume $u_i, v_i, q_i$ already chosen for $i < n$. Since $|\mathbf{Q}(u_{n-1}, v_{n-1})| > 1$, by Lemma 9.3 either $u_{n-1} \wedge \kappa(q_{n-1}) \not\le v_{n-1}$ or $u_{n-1} \not\le v_{n-1} \vee q_{n-1}$. In the former case let $u_n = u_{n-1} \wedge \kappa(q_{n-1})$ and $v_n = v_{n-1}$; in the latter let $u_n = u_{n-1}$ and $v_n = v_{n-1} \vee q_{n-1}$. Lemma 9.4: $|\mathbf{Q}(u_n, v_n)| \neq 1$. Pick $q_n \in \mathbf{Q}(u_n, v_n) \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$. The $q_i$'s are distinct: if $u_{i+1} = u_i \wedge \kappa(q_i)$, then $q_j \le u_{i+1} \le \kappa(q_i)$ for $j > i$, so $q_j \neq q_i$ (else $q_i \le \kappa(q_i)$ contradicting irreducibility). Similarly in the other case. $\square$

Avoiding K (book pp. 181–182)

Lemma 9.6 · avoiding totally atomic elements

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite and $S$ is a finite set of completely join irreducible elements in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, then there exist $u' \le u$ and $v' \ge v$ such that $\mathbf{Q}(u', v')$ is infinite and $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \cap S = \emptyset$. Moreover, $u'$ and $v'$ can be taken so that

$$u' = u \wedge \bigwedge \kappa(p_i) \quad \text{and} \quad v' = v \vee \bigvee r_i$$

for some (possibly empty) sequences $p_i, r_i \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ with $p_j \le \kappa(p_i)$ and $\kappa(r_j) \ge r_i$ whenever $i < j$.

Corollary 9.7 · stronger avoidance

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite, then there exist $u' \le u$ and $v' \ge v$ such that $\mathbf{Q}(u', v')$ is infinite, $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \cap K = \emptyset$, and for all $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u', v')$, $u' \not\le \kappa(q)^*$ and $v' \not\le \kappa(q)$.

The corollary specialises Lemma 9.6 to $S = $ specific structural-witness elements derived from $K$. The added conditions ($u' \not\le \kappa(q)^*$ and $v' \not\le \kappa(q)$) are exactly what's needed for Theorem 9.8's argument to go through.

The critical step: pair of κ-incomparable elements (book p. 182)

Theorem 9.8 · pair of κ-incomparable elements (THE critical step)

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite, then there exist $q, q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ such that

$$q \le \kappa(q') \quad \text{and} \quad q' \le \kappa(q).$$
Intuition · why this is "critical"

Theorem 9.8 produces a pair of "κ-incomparable" elements $q, q'$ — neither's κ-image dominates the other. This is the building block for Theorem 9.10's free-lattice embedding. The proof uses Theorem 8.6 (singular elements at top/bottom — Chapter VIII's main result) and Corollary 8.5 (the "useful test") to derive a contradiction from "no such pair exists". The argument's mechanics:

  1. By Corollary 9.7, assume $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \emptyset$ and the stronger conditions.
  2. Pick $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  3. If $u \wedge \kappa(q) \not\le v \vee q$, use Theorem 9.1 to get $q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u \wedge \kappa(q), v \vee q)$. Then $q' \le \kappa(q)$ (from $q' \le u \wedge \kappa(q)$) and $q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ with $v \vee q \le \kappa(q')$ giving $q \le \kappa(q')$. Done.
  4. If $u \wedge \kappa(q) \le v \vee q$, apply (W). The contradictions from Cases involve Theorems 8.6 and 8.5.

The proof's elegance: Chapter VIII's tools (Theorem 8.6 + Corollary 8.5) are exactly the right tools. The "useful test" of Corollary 8.5 directly forbids the configurations that would prevent the κ-incomparable pair from existing.

The infinite κ-incomparable sequence (book p. 183)

Theorem 9.9 · infinite κ-incomparable sequence

If $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite, then there is an infinite sequence $q_1, q_2, \ldots \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ such that $q_i \le \kappa(q_j)$ for all $i \neq j$.

Proof: using Corollary 9.7, choose $u_1 \le u$ and $v_1 \ge v$ so that $\mathbf{Q}(u_1, v_1)$ is infinite, disjoint from $K$, and contains no $q$ with $q_* \le v_1$ or $u_1 \le \kappa(q)^*$. We'll inductively construct the $q_i$'s and two auxiliary sequences determined from $u_1, v_1$, and the $q_i$'s by $u_{i+1} = u_i \wedge \kappa(q_i)$, $v_{i+1} = v_i \vee q_i$. By induction $\mathbf{Q}(u_n, v_n)$ is infinite. Theorem 9.8 gives $q_n$ and $q'$ with $q_n \le \kappa(q')$ and $q' \le \kappa(q_n)$. Set $q_{n+1} = q_n \wedge \kappa(q_n)$ — wait, the formal definition uses a more nuanced choice; the key fact is that the construction can be carried out so $\mathbf{Q}(u_{n+1}, v_{n+1})$ has more than one element, hence by Theorem 9.5 is infinite. Finally, by definition $q_i \le u_j \wedge \cdots \le \kappa(q_j)$ for $i > j$, and $q_j \le u_j \wedge \cdots \le \kappa(q_i)$ for $i > j$. So $q_i \le \kappa(q_j)$ for ALL $i \neq j$. $\square$

Theorem 9.10 — Tschantz's theorem (book pp. 183–184)

Theorem 9.10 · TSCHANTZ'S THEOREM

If $u/v$ is an infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, then $u/v$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

Proof of Tschantz's theorem

First we show that $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ is infinite. Since $u/v$ is infinite, for each $n > 0$ there is a chain $v = v_0 < v_1 < \cdots < v_n = u$ of length $n$ by Theorem 5.59 (meet-SD lattice without infinite chains is finite — applied to $u/v$). Clearly $\mathbf{Q}(v_{i+1}, v_i) \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ and $\mathbf{Q}(v_{i+1}, v_i) \cap \mathbf{Q}(v_{j+1}, v_j) = \emptyset$ for $i \neq j$ (since the elements are at incompatible cover-levels). Since each $\mathbf{Q}(v_{i+1}, v_i)$ must be nonempty by Theorem 9.1, this means $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| \ge n$. Since $n$ was arbitrary, $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ must be infinite.

By Theorem 9.9, there are $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_6 \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ such that $q_i \le \kappa(q_j)$ for $i \neq j$. For $\{i, j, k\} = \{1, 2, 3\}$ let $w_i = v \vee q_j \vee q_k \vee q_{i+3}$. Then $q_i \le w_j \wedge w_k$ and $w_i \le \kappa(q_i)$, so $w_j \wedge w_k \not\le w_i$. Moreover, $q_{i+3} \le w_i$ and $w_j \vee w_k \le \kappa(q_{i+3})$, so $w_i \not\le w_j \vee w_k$. Thus by Theorem 1.13, $\{w_1, w_2, w_3\}$ generate a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. So by Theorem 1.28 ($\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$), $u/v$ contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ as a sublattice. $\square$

Corollary 9.11 · Y can be larger

If $u/v$ is an infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ and $|Y| \le |X| + \omega$, then $u/v$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(Y)$.

Proof: this corollary follows from the last theorem if $X$ is finite. Otherwise the sublattice generated by

$$S = \{(x \wedge u) \vee v : x \in X - \mathrm{var}(u) - \mathrm{var}(v)\}$$

is isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(S)$ and is contained in $u/v$. Since $|S| = |X|$, the result follows. $\square$

Connection P1 · The Q(u, v) framework + Tschantz 1990

Steve Tschantz's 1990 paper "Infinite intervals in free lattices" (Order 6, 367–388) introduced the result that every infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(Y)$ for $|Y| \le |X| + \aleph_0$. The paper resolved a question Freese and Nation had been unable to settle: whether infinite intervals can be infinite chains. Tschantz showed they cannot — and not just that, they contain entire free lattices.

The Q(u, v) framework. Tschantz introduced $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ as the central technical object. The framework does several things at once: (a) reformulates Day's theorem in set-theoretic form (Theorem 9.1); (b) enables COUNTING arguments — $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| = 0, 1, $ or $> 1$; (c) supports MONOTONICITY arguments via $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \subseteq \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ for shrinking intervals; (d) provides the "obstruction set" $K$ — the finite set of completely-join-irreducibles whose presence prevents the infinitude theorem from kicking in.

The historical context. The Tschantz result emerged from a chain of failed attempts. Freese-Nation 1985 (Trans. AMS 288, 1–42) characterized FINITE intervals (Chapters III + VII §1-3 of the monograph). The natural follow-up: characterize INFINITE intervals. As the chapter scope explains: "We were quite surprised when we could not do this and, in fact, were not every able to show the existence of two incomparable elements." Tom Harrison (mathematician, otherwise unfamiliar — likely a student or colleague at Vanderbilt where Tschantz was) extended the consequences. Steve Tschantz finally cracked it.

The monograph's simplification. Tschantz's original 1990 paper was substantially longer; the monograph reduces the proof using Chapter VIII's Corollary 8.5 (the "useful test"). As the chapter says: "most of Tschantz's proof is devoted to showing that there is no infinite interval which is a chain. We can simplify this part of the proof considerably by using the characterization of semisingular elements from the last chapter." This is the "tool development → application" pattern: Chapter VIII's specialised tools simplify Chapter IX's deep theorem.

Connection P2 · The proof's structural choreography

The proof of Tschantz's theorem (Theorem 9.10) is one of the most intricate in the monograph. The structural choreography:

Step 1: Q(u, v) infinite. Theorem 9.5 + Theorem 5.59. From "$u/v$ infinite" derive "$\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ infinite" via the chain-of-length-$n$ construction.

Step 2: Avoid K. Lemma 9.6 + Corollary 9.7. Shrink $u, v$ to $u', v'$ so that $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \cap K = \emptyset$ and the stronger conditions hold. Uses the finiteness of $K$ (Corollary 6.11).

Step 3: κ-incomparable pair. Theorem 9.8. From the avoidance + infinitude conditions, produce $q, q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u', v')$ with $q \le \kappa(q')$ and $q' \le \kappa(q)$. Uses Theorem 8.6 + Corollary 8.5 to forbid the alternative configurations.

Step 4: Infinite κ-incomparable sequence. Theorem 9.9. Iterate Theorem 9.8 with shrinking $(u_i, v_i)$.

Step 5: 3-of-them generate $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. Take 6 elements $q_1, \ldots, q_6$ from the κ-incomparable sequence. Form 3 elements $w_1, w_2, w_3$ as specific joins. Verify by Corollary 1.13 that $\{w_1, w_2, w_3\}$ generates $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.

Step 6: $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$ via Theorem 1.28. Whitman's classical embedding. So $u/v$ contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ via the embedding in $\mathbf{FL}(3) \le u/v$.

The "useful test" pattern. Corollary 8.5 is invoked in Steps 3 (Theorem 9.8's proof) and indirectly in Steps 4-5. The test's structural constraint — for completely-join-irreducible $w$ with $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$, if $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$ then $u \in \{w_*, w\}$ or $u \succ w$ — is the structural rigidity that makes the construction tractable. Without it, the configurations that need to be ruled out would proliferate.

PART B · COVERLESS ELEMENTS — §2

Theorem 9.12: every infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a coverless element

A "complement" to Day's Theorem: every interval contains covers (Day) AND every infinite interval contains coverless elements (Tschantz applications). Both coexist. The proof uses Tschantz's theorem + Theorem 3.4 + Lemma 9.13.

§ 2 Coverless Elements

A coverless element has NO upper AND NO lower cover. The first historical instance was Example 3.39 (Chapter III — the Dean-style coverless construction). Theorem 9.12 shows there are MANY such elements: every infinite interval contains one.

In this section we use Tschantz's Theorem to prove a sort of complement to Day's Theorem. Recall that an element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is said to be coverless if it has no lower and no upper covers. In Example 3.39 we gave an example of a coverless element, but now we can show that there are many such elements.

Theorem 9.12 · every infinite interval contains a coverless element

Every infinite interval of a free lattice contains a coverless element.

Intuition · the proof strategy

Throughout this section $\sigma : \mathbf{FL}(X) \rightarrowtail \mathbf{FL}(Y)$ denotes a lattice embedding. The strategy: take an infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. By Tschantz's theorem (Theorem 9.10), there's an embedding $\sigma : \mathbf{FL}(\omega) \to u/v$. Construct a specific element $w \in \mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ with the property that $\sigma(w)$ is coverless in $u/v$. The element $w$ is built using Lemma 9.14 — a structural lemma about how lattice embeddings preserve canonical-form structure. The witness $w$ is

$$w = ((x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]) \vee (x_6 \wedge [(x_7 \wedge x_8) \vee (x_9 \wedge x_{10})])$$

where $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{10}$ are 11 distinct generators of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. The element has specific canonical-form structure that, under any lattice embedding, produces a coverless element in the target. Lemma 9.14 shows the canonical-form structure is preserved enough to force coverlessness.

Lemma 9.13 · embedding-preservation of canonical joinands

If $w_1$ is a canonical joinand of $w$ and $w_1 \notin X$, then $\sigma(w_1)$ is a canonical joinand of $\sigma(w)$.

The proof: let the canonical form of $w$ be given by (3) — $w = w_{11} \wedge \cdots \wedge w_{1k}$ canonically (with $w_1 \notin X$). Let $\sigma(w) = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_r$ canonically. Since $\sigma$ is a lattice embedding, $\sigma(w) = \sigma(w_1) \vee \cdots \vee \sigma(w_n)$, but $\sigma(w) > \sigma(w_2) \vee \cdots \vee \sigma(w_n)$. It follows that $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\} \ll \{\sigma(w_1), \ldots, \sigma(w_n)\}$ but $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\} \not\ll \{\sigma(w_2), \ldots, \sigma(w_n)\}$. Hence for some $i$ we have $u_i \le \sigma(w_{11}) \wedge \cdots \wedge \sigma(w_{1k}) = \sigma(w_1) = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_r$. For all $j$, $w_{1j} \not\le w$, so $\sigma(w_{1j}) \not\le \sigma(w)$ for all $j$. Hence by (W), $u_i \le \sigma(w_{1j}) \le u_j$ for some $j$. This forces $i = j$ and $u_i = \sigma(w_1)$. $\square$

Lemma 9.14 · joinand structure preserves complete-join-irreducibility

If $w \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, $w = w_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge w_n$ canonically with $n > 1$, and $w_1 = w_{11} \vee w_{12}$ canonically where $w_{1j} \notin X$ and $w_{1j} \not\le w$ for $j = 1, 2$, then $\sigma(w)$ is NOT completely join irreducible in $\mathbf{FL}(Y)$.

The proof: applying Lemma 9.13 to $w_1$, we see that both $\sigma(w_{11})$ and $\sigma(w_{12})$ are canonical joinands of $\sigma(w)$. Moreover, since $\sigma$ is a lattice embedding, $\sigma(w_{1j}) \not\le \sigma(w)$ for $j = 1, 2$. Now by Theorem 3.4, $\sigma(w)$ is not completely join irreducible. $\square$

Now we are ready to complete the proof of Theorem 9.12. Take an infinite interval in a free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. By Tschantz's Theorem (Theorem 9.10), there is an embedding $\sigma$ of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ into this interval. Let $w$ be given by

$$w = ((x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]) \vee (x_6 \wedge [(x_7 \wedge x_8) \vee (x_9 \wedge x_{10})]).$$

By the dual of Lemma 9.14 (with $w_1 = (x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]$) we have that $\sigma(w)$ has no upper cover. By the same lemma, neither of its joinands has a lower cover. Thus, by Theorem 3.5, $\sigma(w)$ has no lower cover. This proves Theorem 9.12. $\square$

Intuition · the witness element's structure

The witness $w$ has a specific canonical-form structure designed to force coverlessness:

  • $w$ is a join $w = (\text{LHS}) \vee (\text{RHS})$ — both operands are meets-of-joins-of-meets, structured to ensure: (a) $w$ has no upper cover (the dual of Lemma 9.14 forces the meet structure to be "extended", preventing meet-irreducibility, so no upper cover); (b) each of the canonical joinands LHS, RHS has no lower cover (Lemma 9.14 forces the join structure to be "extended", preventing complete-join-irreducibility, so no lower cover); (c) by Theorem 3.5, $w$ itself has no lower cover.
  • 11 generators used. The construction needs enough "degrees of freedom" to verify the lemma's hypotheses uniformly.

The pattern: COVERLESS ⟸ (no upper cover AND no lower cover via canonical-joinand structure). The lemmas show how lattice embeddings preserve this structure to a sufficient extent.

PART C · MAXIMAL CHAINS — §3

Theorem 9.15: $c/d$ neither atomic nor dually atomic ⟹ a dense maximal chain in $c/d$

A maximal chain WITH NO COVERS — every element except the endpoints is coverless. The construction uses Theorem 9.12 to insert coverless elements at each step. Surprising in light of Day's Theorem: covers are everywhere, but they don't have to participate in maximal chains.

§ 3 Maximal Chains

In intervals that are neither atomic nor dually atomic, maximal chains can be constructed entirely from coverless elements. The construction is a step-by-step refinement.

In this section we characterize those intervals in free lattices which contain a dense maximal chain, i.e., one with no covers. It follows from Theorem 3.5 that an element of a free lattice can have no more upper covers than the number of its canonical meetands. Thus it can have only finitely many upper (and lower) covers. This implies that if an interval of a free lattice is atomic, then every maximal chain in that interval must contain an atom and thus cannot be dense. Of course, if it is dually atomic it also cannot have a dense chain. The next theorem proves a converse to this.

Theorem 9.15 · dense maximal chain construction

Suppose that $c > d$ in a free lattice and that $c/d$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic. Then there is a maximal chain $C$ from $c$ to $d$ such that every element of $C - \{c, d\}$ is coverless.

Intuition · the construction

The proof builds the dense maximal chain $C$ step-by-step. The strategy:

  1. Enumerate covers. Let $a_i \succ b_i$ for $i > 0$ enumerate ALL covers in $c/d$.
  2. Build $C_i$ inductively. Start with $C_0 = \{c, d\}$. For each $i \ge 1$, extend $C_{i-1}$ to $C_i$ such that:
    • $C_i$ is finite.
    • Each element of $C_i - \{c, d\}$ is coverless.
    • $C_{i-1} \subseteq C_i$.
    • $C_i$ has an element incomparable with at least one of $a_i, b_i$ — guaranteeing the cover $a_i \succ b_i$ is "broken" in the maximal chain.
  3. Take the union. $C = \bigcup C_i$. Any maximal chain in $c/d$ containing $C$ has no cover (by construction, every cover is broken).

The key step: extend $C_{i-1}$ to $C_i$ that breaks the cover $a_i \succ b_i$. This uses Theorem 9.12 to find a coverless element in suitable infinite sub-intervals. The case analysis is intricate — Lemmas 7.22 + 7.6 + 7.32 are all invoked. Figures 9.1 and 9.2 diagram specific configurations encountered in the construction.

Proof outline (book pp. 185–187)

First we prove the theorem under the stronger hypothesis that $c/d$ has no atom and no coatom, then derive the full result from this.

Let $a_i \succ b_i$, $i > 0$, be an enumeration of all the covers in $c/d$. Let $C_0 = \{c, d\}$. Build chains $C_i$ with the four properties listed in the intuition box. Inductively: $C_{i-1}$ has been constructed. If $C_{i-1}$ has an element incomparable with $a_i$ or $b_i$, set $C_i = C_{i-1}$. Otherwise, $a_i, b_i$ are "in the chain" — there are elements $e \succ f$ in $C_{i-1}$ with $e \ge a_i \succ b_i \ge f$ and $C_{i-1}$ has no element strictly between $e$ and $f$.

Since the elements of $C_{i-1} - \{c, d\}$ are coverless, $a_i$ has no lower cover above $d$, and $b_i$ has no upper cover below $c$, we must have $e > a_i \succ b_i > f$.

First consider the case when $a_i$ is join reducible and $b$ is meet reducible. Let $q$ be the canonical joinand of $a_i$ which is not below $b_i$. Then $q$ is completely join irreducible and $\kappa(q) > b_i$. If $q \vee f < a_i$ and the interval $a_i/q \vee f$ is infinite, choose a coverless element $r$ in this interval by Theorem 9.12. Set $C_i = C_{i-1} \cup \{r\}$. Clearly $r$ is incomparable with $b_i$, since $q \le r$ and $q \not\le b_i$ implies $r \not\le b_i$, while $a > r$ and $a \succ b$ implies $r \not\ge b$.

If $q \vee f < a_i$ but $a_i/q \vee f$ is finite, Theorem 7.2 implies that any chain of covers containing $a_i$ can be of length at most two, since the hypothesis insures that $a_i$ is not in the connected component of 0 or 1. Detailed Whitman-condition manipulations + Lemma 7.22 + Theorem 7.6 + Theorem 7.32 yield the configuration in Figure 9.1, with $(q \vee f) \wedge \kappa(q) \prec q \vee f$. The construction inserts an intermediate element $v$ such that $C_i = C_{i-1} \cup \{v\}$ breaks the $a_i \succ b_i$ cover.

The other cases (one of $a_i, b_i$ is join/meet irreducible, or other branches) are handled similarly using the structural tools — Corollary 8.5, Theorem 9.12, Lemma 7.22 — to forbid configurations that would prevent the construction from proceeding.

Finally, for the FULL theorem (where $c/d$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic but might have atoms or coatoms): use the continuity of free lattices (Theorem 1.22). For an atomless $c/d$, find a descending chain $e_0 = e > e_1 > e_2 > \cdots > d$ with $\bigwedge e_n = d$. The previous case applies to each interval; combining gives the dense maximal chain. The continuity of free lattices ensures the limit construction works. $\square$

Connection P4 · Maximal chains and continuity

Theorem 9.15's construction relies on two complementary properties of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$:

(1) Continuity (Theorem 1.22). Free lattices are continuous: $a \wedge \bigvee D = \bigvee_{d \in D}(a \wedge d)$ for up-directed $D$. The continuity ensures that the inductive construction's limit is well-defined and behaves correctly under joins/meets.

(2) Day's Theorem (covers exist). Despite the "no cover in maximal chain" conclusion, Day's Theorem still applies: every nontrivial interval has a covering pair. The maximal chains constructed by Theorem 9.15 STEP AROUND the covers — they don't include them — but the covers are still there.

The structural duality. Day's Theorem says covers exist ABUNDANTLY (every interval has them). Theorem 9.15 says covers can be SYSTEMATICALLY AVOIDED in maximal chain construction. The two coexist because $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s Hasse diagram is not "dense enough" to force every maximal chain through covers — there's enough "lateral motion" via coverless elements to bypass covers.

Comparison to discrete lattices. In a finite lattice, every maximal chain is finite, and there's no notion of "dense maximal chain" — every chain has cover-relations between consecutive elements. In $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (specifically infinite intervals), maximal chains can be uncountable AND have no covers. This is the structural feature that distinguishes free lattices from finite ones: the infinite combinatorial richness allows for "chain-without-covers" patterns.

Theoretical impact. Theorem 9.15 was an unexpected result. It shows that the structural intuition "maximal chains follow covers" — true in finite lattices — fails in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The implication for general lattice theory: the relationship between cover relations and maximal chains is more subtle than in finite combinatorics.

Connection P3 · The Day-FN-Tschantz arc complete

Chapter IX completes a structural arc that began with Day 1979 and developed through Freese-Nation 1985 and beyond.

Day 1979: qualitative weak atomicity. Every nontrivial interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a covering pair (Theorem 2.84 of Chapter II §7).

Freese-Nation 1985 (Trans. AMS 288): rank-bounded weak atomicity (Chapter IV's Theorem 4.1). The covering pair has explicit complexity bounded by canonical-form depth of the interval endpoints.

Tschantz 1990 (Order 6, 367-388): Theorem 9.10 — every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. The structural strengthening: not just "has covers" but "has free sublattice".

Theorem 9.12 (this chapter): every infinite interval contains a coverless element. The structural complement: "has covers AND has coverless elements simultaneously".

Theorem 9.15 (this chapter): intervals neither atomic nor dually atomic have dense maximal chains. The structural extreme: "covers can be entirely avoided in maximal chain construction".

Together, these results paint a complete picture of the LOCAL-INFINITE structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$: covers exist (Day), are bounded in complexity (Freese-Nation), but the intervals around them are RICH (Tschantz), with coverless elements (9.12), and even with maximal-chain-bypassing (9.15). Free lattices are not "uniformly cover-y" — they have both rigid (covers) and flexible (coverless) structures coexisting.

Historical significance. The Tschantz result took TWELVE YEARS after Day's 1979 weak-atomicity theorem to emerge. Multiple researchers — Freese, Nation, Harrison — failed to crack it before Tschantz. The difficulty: showing infinite intervals contain INCOMPARABLE PAIRS, not just covers. Once Tschantz showed even ONE incomparable pair existed, Theorems 9.9-9.10 followed by extension. The bottleneck was the κ-incomparable pair (Theorem 9.8) — exactly where Chapter VIII's Corollary 8.5 simplifies the argument.

GLOSSARY Chapter IX vocabulary, alphabetical

All new terminology with brief definition. Page references to monograph book pages.
Coverless element
An element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with NO upper cover AND NO lower cover. Example 3.39 (Chapter III) gave the first historical example. Theorem 9.12: every infinite interval contains a coverless element.
Dense maximal chain
A maximal chain in an interval $c/d$ such that every element of the chain (other than the endpoints $c, d$) is coverless. Equivalently: a maximal chain with no cover-relation between any two consecutive elements. Theorem 9.15: such chains exist in intervals neither atomic nor dually atomic.

The set $K = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q$ is completely join irreducible AND ($q$ or $\kappa(q)$ is totally atomic)$\}$. Finite for each $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (by Corollary 6.11). κ-incomparable pair
A pair $q, q' \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ such that $q \le \kappa(q')$ AND $q' \le \kappa(q)$. The condition makes neither's κ-image dominate the other. Theorem 9.8: such pairs exist in infinite $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$. Theorem 9.9: extends to infinite κ-incomparable sequences.
Lattice embedding $\sigma$
A function $\sigma : \mathbf{FL}(X) \rightarrowtail \mathbf{FL}(Y)$ that preserves all lattice operations and is injective. The notation $\rightarrowtail$ specifically indicates an embedding (vs. mere homomorphism).
$\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$
For $u, v \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{q \in \mathbf{FL}(X) : q$ is completely join irreducible, $q \le u$, $v \le \kappa(q)\}$. The chapter's central technical object. By Theorem 9.1 (Day's theorem reformulation), $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \neq \emptyset$ iff $u \not\le v$.

COMPUTED EXAMPLES Worked instances of Chapter IX results

Five computed examples: $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ for a small case; identifying $K$-elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$; verifying Tschantz's theorem on a specific infinite interval; the coverless-witness $w$ from Theorem 9.12's proof; constructing a dense maximal chain conceptually.
Example IX.A$\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ for $u = x \vee y, v = x \wedge y$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$
Compute $\mathbf{Q}(x \vee y, x \wedge y)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ — a specific small case.

Setup. $u = x \vee y$, $v = x \wedge y$. Is $u \le v$? $u = x \vee y \ge x \ge x \wedge y = v$, so $u \ge v$, NOT $u \le v$ unless $u = v$. So $u \not\le v$, and by Theorem 9.1, $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \neq \emptyset$.

Find $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$. $q \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ means $q$ completely join irreducible, $q \le x \vee y$, and $x \wedge y \le \kappa(q)$.

Test specific candidates:

  • $q = x$. $x \le x \vee y$ ✓. $\kappa(x) = \overline{x} = y \vee z$. Is $x \wedge y \le y \vee z$? $x \wedge y \le y \le y \vee z$ ✓. So $q = x \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = y$. By symmetry, $y \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = z$. Is $z \le x \vee y$? Whitman case 4: $z$ generator, $x \vee y$ join — need $z \le x$ (no) or $z \le y$ (no). So $z \not\le x \vee y$. Hence $z \notin \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = x \wedge z$. Is $x \wedge z \le x \vee y$? Trivially yes ($x \wedge z \le x \le x \vee y$). $\kappa(x \wedge z) = \bigvee(X - \{x, z\}) = y$. Is $x \wedge y \le y$? Yes. So $q = x \wedge z \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = y \wedge z$. Similar — $y \wedge z \in \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = x \wedge y \wedge z$. $x \wedge y \wedge z \le x \vee y$ ✓. $\kappa(x \wedge y \wedge z) = \bigvee \emptyset = 0$. Is $x \wedge y \le 0$? No. So $x \wedge y \wedge z \notin \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $q = x \wedge (y \vee z)$ (Dean's coverless element). Not completely join irreducible (Example 3.36). Excluded.

So $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \supseteq \{x, y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z\}$. Are there more elements? The completely-join-irreducibles in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ are: generators ($x, y, z$), meets-of-generators (totally atomic — $x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z, x \wedge y \wedge z$), and certain σ/μ-derived elements. Check each:

  • $\sigma_z(x \wedge y) = (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z)$. $\kappa = z \vee \cdots$ (Example VI.D). Is $x \wedge y \le \kappa$? Yes (computed). Is $(x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) \le x \vee y$? Whitman case 6: meet of joins ≤ join. Need $(x \vee z) \le x \vee y$ or $(y \vee z) \le x \vee y$ or $\sigma \le x$ or $\sigma \le y$. The first: $z \le x \vee y$? No. So $\sigma \notin \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$.
  • $\mu_z(x \vee y) = (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$. NOT completely join irreducible (it's a join of two distinct elements). Excluded.

Final: $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) = \{x, y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z\}$ — 4 elements.

Implication. $|\mathbf{Q}(u, v)| = 4 > 1$, so the conditions for Theorem 9.5's "infinitude" potentially apply. But all 4 elements are in $K$ (each is totally atomic). So $\mathbf{Q}(u, v) \cap K = \mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ — the obstruction set absorbs the entire witness set. Theorem 9.5 doesn't kick in; $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ remains finite. This is the "small intervals have small witness sets" pattern.

Example IX.B$K$-elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$
Identify the elements of $K$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ — completely-join-irreducible elements such that the element OR its κ-image is totally atomic.

Setup. $K = \{q$ completely-join-irreducible : $q$ totally atomic OR $\kappa(q)$ totally atomic$\}$. By Example 6.2 (Chapter VI), the 17 totally-atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ are:

  • 3 generators: $x, y, z$.
  • 4 meets of generators: $x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z, x \wedge y \wedge z$.
  • 4 joins of generators: $x \vee y, x \vee z, y \vee z, x \vee y \vee z$.
  • 3 of form $\sigma_z(x \wedge y) = (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z)$ etc.
  • 3 of form $\mu_z(x \vee y) = (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$ etc.

Filter to completely-join-irreducible. Among the 17 totally-atomic elements:

  • Completely-join-irreducible: generators (3), meets of generators (4), and the $\sigma_z(x \wedge y)$ family (3) — 10 elements.
  • NOT completely-join-irreducible: joins of generators (4), the $\mu_z(x \vee y)$ family (3) — 7 elements (these are joins, not join-irreducible).

So 10 totally-atomic completely-join-irreducible elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.

$K$ itself. $K$ contains: (a) totally-atomic completely-join-irreducible elements (10 above), AND (b) elements $q$ where $\kappa(q)$ is totally atomic. By the κ-bijection, $\kappa(q)$ is totally-atomic completely-meet-irreducible iff $q$ is in the κ-preimage of those.

By Theorem 6.10 + duality, totally-atomic completely-meet-irreducibles are: 3 generators, 4 joins of generators, 3 of the $\mu_z(x \vee y)$ form — 10 elements.

So $K \supseteq \{q : q$ in the join side $\} \cup \{q : \kappa(q)$ in the meet side$\}$. Each side has 10 elements; via κ-bijection, the two sides ARE the same set (since κ pairs join-irreducibles with meet-irreducibles).

Hence $|K|$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ is around 10-20 elements (with κ-pair identification). The exact count requires care but is FINITE.

Implication. $K$ is a small finite set in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. For any pair $u, v$ with $u \not\le v$, the witness set $\mathbf{Q}(u, v)$ either lies entirely in $K$ (small case) or has elements outside $K$ (Theorem 9.5's infinitude can kick in). The "outside K" condition is what makes infinite intervals structurally rich.

Example IX.CAn infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — verify it contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$
Take an infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — for example, $1/x$ where $1 = x \vee y \vee z$. Outline how Tschantz's theorem applies.

Setup. Take $u = 1 = x \vee y \vee z$ and $v = x$. The interval $1/x = \{w \in \mathbf{FL}(3) : x \le w \le 1\}$.

Verify $1/x$ is infinite. Whitman's $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$ (Theorem 1.28) — the embedding takes $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ into specific elements of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. Most of the embedded elements are $\ge x$ (or can be transformed to be). So $1/x$ has uncountably many elements, certainly infinite.

Apply Theorem 9.10. By Tschantz, $1/x$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

The construction's outline. By the proof:

  1. $\mathbf{Q}(1, x)$ is infinite (by Theorem 5.59 + chain construction). The witness set is uncountable.
  2. By Corollary 9.7, find $u' \le 1$, $v' \ge x$ such that $\mathbf{Q}(u', v') \cap K = \emptyset$ and stronger conditions. The reduction to the obstruction-free witness set.
  3. By Theorem 9.9, find an infinite κ-incomparable sequence $q_1, q_2, \ldots \in \mathbf{Q}(u', v')$. These are completely-join-irreducible elements with $q_i \le \kappa(q_j)$ for $i \neq j$.
  4. Take $q_1, \ldots, q_6$ from the sequence. Form $w_1 = v' \vee q_2 \vee q_3 \vee q_4$, $w_2 = v' \vee q_1 \vee q_3 \vee q_5$, $w_3 = v' \vee q_1 \vee q_2 \vee q_6$.
  5. By Corollary 1.13, $\{w_1, w_2, w_3\}$ generates a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.
  6. By Theorem 1.28, this $\mathbf{FL}(3) \le 1/x$ contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

Hence $1/x$ contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. ✓

Implication. Even simple infinite intervals like $1/x$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — the "top half" of the lattice above a single generator — are structurally as complex as the entire $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. The structural richness is uniform across infinite intervals.

Example IX.DThe coverless witness $w$ from Theorem 9.12's proof
Examine the explicit coverless witness $w = ((x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]) \vee (x_6 \wedge [(x_7 \wedge x_8) \vee (x_9 \wedge x_{10})])$ from Theorem 9.12's proof.

Setup. $w \in \mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ uses 11 distinct generators $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{10}$.

The structure. $w$ is a join: $w = w_L \vee w_R$ where:

  • $w_L = (x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]$ — a meet of two parts: $(x_0 \vee x_1)$ and a join $[(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]$.
  • $w_R = x_6 \wedge [(x_7 \wedge x_8) \vee (x_9 \wedge x_{10})]$ — a meet of $x_6$ with a join.

Both $w_L, w_R$ are MEETS, so $w$ is a join of meets — meet-of-joins-of-meets at the top level.

Verify $w$ has no upper cover. By the dual of Lemma 9.14: take $w_1 = w_L$ (the canonical meetand of $w$, which is itself a meet). Apply the dual: $w_L = (x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge \cdots$ — both meetands are NOT in $X$ (the first is a join, the second is a join). Both are not below $w$ (canonical-form check). By the dual of Lemma 9.14 applied to $\sigma(w)$ for any embedding $\sigma$, $\sigma(w)$ is NOT completely meet irreducible. Hence $\sigma(w)$ has no upper cover.

Verify each canonical joinand has no lower cover. For $w_L$: $w_L = (x_0 \vee x_1) \wedge [(x_2 \wedge x_3) \vee (x_4 \wedge x_5)]$. The canonical joinands of $w_L$'s second meetand: $x_2 \wedge x_3$ and $x_4 \wedge x_5$. Both NOT in $X$, both not below $w_L$. By Lemma 9.14, $\sigma(w_L)$ is NOT completely join irreducible. Hence $\sigma(w_L)$ has no lower cover. Similarly $\sigma(w_R)$ has no lower cover.

Conclude $\sigma(w)$ has no lower cover. By Theorem 3.5 (Chapter III's cover bijection), the lower covers of a join element are in bijection with its completely-join-irreducible canonical joinands. Both $\sigma(w_L)$ and $\sigma(w_R)$ are NOT completely join irreducible. So $\sigma(w)$ has NO lower covers.

Combined: $\sigma(w)$ has neither upper nor lower covers — hence COVERLESS. ✓

Implication. The witness $w$ is a UNIVERSAL coverless template: under ANY lattice embedding $\sigma : \mathbf{FL}(\omega) \to \mathbf{FL}(X)$, $\sigma(w)$ is coverless in the target. The 11-generator structure provides enough "degrees of freedom" to verify the lemma's hypotheses uniformly. Combined with Tschantz's theorem (every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$), this gives Theorem 9.12: every infinite interval contains a coverless element.

Example IX.EA dense maximal chain construction outline
Outline the construction of a dense maximal chain in a specific infinite, atomless, dually-atomless interval of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.

Setup. Take an interval $c/d$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ that is neither atomic nor dually atomic. For concreteness: $c = $ some "deep" element, $d = $ a coverless predecessor several covers down. By Theorem 9.12, we can find such configurations within infinite sub-intervals.

The construction (Theorem 9.15 conceptual outline).

  1. Enumerate all covers in $c/d$ as $a_1 \succ b_1, a_2 \succ b_2, \ldots$ (countably many, since $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has countably many elements when $X$ is finite).
  2. Start with $C_0 = \{c, d\}$.
  3. Inductively build $C_i$ from $C_{i-1}$. At step $i$, check if some element of $C_{i-1}$ is incomparable with $a_i$ or $b_i$. If yes, $C_i = C_{i-1}$ (the cover $a_i \succ b_i$ is already "broken"). If no, the cover is "in the chain". Use Theorem 9.12 to find a coverless element $r$ in a suitable infinite sub-interval near $a_i, b_i$, and set $C_i = C_{i-1} \cup \{r\}$. Verify $r$ is incomparable with $b_i$.
  4. Take $C = \bigcup C_i$. Any maximal chain extension of $C$ is a dense maximal chain.

Properties of the resulting dense maximal chain.

  • Uncountable in general (since each cover-breaking step inserts a coverless element, and there are countably many covers, but the maximal chain extension can have uncountably many elements between).
  • NO covers (every element is coverless or one of the endpoints).
  • Maximal: cannot be extended without losing the "no covers" property.

Implication. The dense maximal chain demonstrates the "covers can be systematically avoided" structural feature. In an infinite atomless dually-atomless interval, you can traverse from one endpoint to the other via a continuous chain of coverless elements, NEVER passing through a cover-relation. Day's Theorem still says covers exist — the dense chain just doesn't include any of them.
