Totally Atomic Elements

The first chapter of Part II — fine structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Totally atomic = both lower atomic AND upper atomic. The chapter proves $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains only finitely many totally atomic elements (Corollary 6.11) — a deep and surprising structural fact. The exact count is given by an explicit formula involving Stirling numbers of the second kind: 17 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, 94 in $\mathbf{FL}(4)$, 667 in $\mathbf{FL}(5)$, 125,499,820 in $\mathbf{FL}(10)$. Plus an explicit canonical-form formula for $\kappa(w)$ when $w$ is totally atomic, and the role of totally atomic elements as universal "cover-witnesses" in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

~110–135 min read 3 sections · 33 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 135–149 · PDF pp. 144–158

Totally atomic elements play a surprisingly important role in determining the fine structure of the lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Therefore it behooves us to study them more carefully. In particular, in the first section of this chapter we will provide an explicit description of the totally atomic elements in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. As a consequence, we will see that for any finite set $X$, the number of totally atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is finite.

Repeatedly in the arguments in the next four chapters, we will encounter conditions on an element of a free lattice which force that element to be totally atomic. Some of these results are of independent interest, and these are included in Section 3. In subsequent chapters, more will follow.

Later we shall also need to know the canonical form of $\kappa(w)$ for a join irreducible totally atomic element $w$. The syntactic algorithm obtained in Chapter III can be, of course, applied to find $\kappa(w)$ in a form which is not canonical and then the general procedure, described in Chapter I, can be used to transform $\kappa(w)$ into canonical form. However, we need a more specific characterization.

Throughout this chapter, let $X$ be a finite set with at least three elements.

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter VI (paraphrased)

Chapter VI is the first chapter of Part II of the monograph (the "fine structure" half). After Chapters I–V's structural-classification arc, Chapter VI zooms into a specific class of elements within $\mathbf{FL}(X)$: totally atomic elements — those that are both lower atomic AND upper atomic. By Chapters III and V, this means they are completely join irreducible (have a lower cover $w_*$) AND completely meet irreducible (have an upper cover $w^*$), with additional structural conditions.

The chapter has three conceptual parts. Part A (§1): characterization. Theorem 6.1 gives a 4-fold equivalent for "totally atomic" specialised to $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Then the construction: $G$ is the smallest subset of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ containing $X$ and closed under the operations $\sigma_x(w) = x \vee w$, $\mu_x(w) = x \wedge w$ for generators $x$ NOT occurring in the canonical representation of $w$. Theorem 6.10: $G$ equals the totally atomic elements. Corollary 6.11 gives the explicit count via Stirling numbers. Part B (§2): canonical form of $\kappa(w)$ for totally atomic $w$. Theorem 3.32 of Chapter III gives a formula for $\kappa(w)$, but not in canonical form. Theorem 6.21 gives the canonical form explicitly. Part C (§3): the structural role. Theorem 6.24: a cover-witness in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is automatically totally atomic. Theorems 6.26–6.30: structural consequences for κ-images, lower-cover existence, completely-meet-irreducibility. Theorem 6.31: a technical "no v" theorem — used in Chapter VIII for semisingular elements.

The chapter's central structural fact (Corollary 6.11): $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains only finitely many totally atomic elements. This is striking. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is infinite (Birkhoff 1935 for $|X| \ge 3$), uncountable in many senses, but the totally-atomic elements are RARE — they form a finite subset whose size grows polynomially in $|X|$ but is dwarfed by the lattice's overall size. The finite count is what makes covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ "controllable": every cover-witness must be totally atomic, and there are only finitely many totally atomic elements to consider.

Five clusters of new vocabulary. Totally atomic: an element $a$ is totally atomic if it is BOTH lower atomic AND upper atomic. By the definitions in Chapter III: $a$ is lower atomic if for every $u < a$ there exists $v$ with $u \le v \prec a$; upper atomic dually. Equivalently, $\sigma_x(w) = w \vee x$ and $\mu_x(w) = w \wedge x$ for any element $w$ — but applied through the term algebra these are endomorphisms (homomorphisms). The set $G$: the smallest subset of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ containing $X$ and closed under: if $w \in G$ and $x \in X$ does not occur in the canonical representation of $w$, then $\sigma_x(w) \in G$ and $\mu_x(w) \in G$. Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Four threads reward close reading. The "G construction" — building totally atomic elements bottom-up. Starting from $X$, the closure under $\sigma_x, \mu_x$ for "fresh" generators (those not appearing in $w$) gives a recursive enumeration of all totally atomic elements. The construction is finite at every step, and combinatorially counts elements in terms of (a) the underlying support set (subset of $X$), (b) the alternation pattern of $\sigma$'s and $\mu$'s, (c) the partition of "fresh generators added at each level". Each totally atomic element gets a UNIQUE canonical-form expression (Lemma 6.4). The Stirling-number count. Corollary 6.11's formula, when expanded for $|X| = n$:

$$\#\{\text{totally atomic in } \mathbf{FL}(n)\} = n + 2\left[\sum_{m=2}^n \binom{n}{m} \sum_{s=2}^m \binom{m}{s} \sum_{k=0}^{m-s} k! \, S(m - s, k)\right]$$

The factor of 2 comes from pairing each meet-reducible totally atomic element with its dual (a join-reducible totally atomic element). The Stirling numbers count "how many ways the remaining $m - s$ generators can be distributed into the $\sigma$/$\mu$ alternations". For $|X| = 3$: 17 = 3 + 2·7. For $|X| = 4$: 94. For $|X| = 5$: 667. For $|X| = 10$: 125,499,820.

Theorem 6.24's structural punchline. Let $w$ be completely meet irreducible in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ and $a$ a lower cover of $w$. If $w_1$ is the unique canonical joinand of $w$ such that $w_1 \not\le a$, then $w_1$ is totally atomic. So every "cover-witness" in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is totally atomic. Combined with Corollary 6.11 (finitely many totally atomic), this is what makes covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ tractable. Chapter III's cover algorithm operates on canonical forms of arbitrary length; here we learn that the COVER-WITNESS is structurally constrained — there are only finitely many possibilities up to the structural pattern. Theorem 6.31's technical role. The chapter's most technical theorem is a "no v exists" claim used in Chapter VIII for semisingular elements. The statement: for completely meet irreducible $q \succ q \wedge p$ with $\kappa^d(q) \le \kappa(q_1)$, no completely meet irreducible $v$ satisfies $v \le q^* \wedge \kappa(q_1)$ AND $v \not\le p \vee \kappa^d(q)$. The proof is by minimal counter-example, with case analysis on the structure of $t = \kappa^d(q)$ via Theorem 6.26 (totally atomic structure). Used as a lemma in Chapter VIII.

§ 1 Characterization

The 4-fold equivalence (book p. 135)

Definition · totally atomic

An element $a$ of a lattice is totally atomic if it is BOTH lower atomic AND upper atomic. That is:

  • Lower atomic: for every $b > a$, there exists $c$ with $a < c \le b$ and $a \prec c$.
  • Upper atomic: dual — for every $b < a$, there exists $c$ with $b \le c < a$ and $c \prec a$.

Equivalently: every interval $a/b$ or $b/a$ above or below $a$ contains a covering pair adjacent to $a$.

Theorem 6.1 · 4-fold equivalence for $\mathbf{FL}(X)$

The following are equivalent for an element $w \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$:

  1. $w$ is totally atomic.
  2. Each subelement of $w$ is totally atomic.
  3. The lattices $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w) = \mathbf{J}(w)^\vee$ and $\mathbf{L}^\wedge(w) = \mathbf{M}(w)^\wedge$ are both semidistributive.
  4. $w$ is either $0$, or $1$, or a completely join irreducible element all of whose canonical meetands are completely meet irreducible, or (dually) a completely meet irreducible element all of whose canonical joinands are completely join irreducible.
Intuition · the four conditions

Each condition gives a different structural angle. (1) Structural: the categorical "every above and every below has covers" definition. (2) Subelement-wise: totally atomic propagates DOWN through canonical-form structure. (3) $\mathbf{L}^\vee, \mathbf{L}^\wedge$ both SD: the JOIN-side AND MEET-side complete-irreducibility neighbourhoods are both well-behaved. (4) Recursive structural: the cleanest description — an element is totally atomic iff its canonical form follows a strict alternation pattern (joinands of meetands of joinands... with completeness all the way down).

The proof connects (1)⟺(2) via Corollary 3.27 of Chapter III; (3)⟺(4) via Theorem 3.23 (Chapter III's bounded characterization of $A^\vee$) applied to both $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ and $\mathbf{L}^\wedge(w)$. The full equivalence integrates the join-side and meet-side analyses — a kind of "structural duality" that totally atomic elements respect by definition.

Concrete count: 17 in FL(3), 94 in FL(4) (book p. 136)

Example 6.2 · enumerating totally atomic elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3), \mathbf{FL}(4)$

If $X = \{x, y, z\}$ is a three-element set, then $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains precisely 17 totally atomic elements. The join irreducible ones are:

  • $x$ (a generator) — and similarly $y, z$ — gives 3.
  • $x \wedge y$ — and similarly $x \wedge z, y \wedge z$ — gives 3.
  • $x \wedge y \wedge z$ — gives 1.
  • $(x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z)$ — and similarly $(x \vee y) \wedge (z \vee y), (y \vee x) \wedge (z \vee x)$ — gives 3.

Subtotal: 3 + 3 + 1 + 3 = 10 join-irreducible totally atomic. By duality (each join-reducible is the dual of a join-irreducible), the meet-irreducible totally atomic count is also 10 (allowing for the FOUR overlap of generators counted only once). Wait — the problem is more subtle. Generators are BOTH join AND meet irreducible. Let me recount.

Actually: 17 = 3 (generators, both join AND meet irreducible) + 7 (purely meet-reducible — i.e., joins) + 7 (purely join-reducible — i.e., meets). The 7 join-reducibles include $x \vee y, x \vee z, y \vee z, x \vee y \vee z$, and three more.

If $X = \{x, y, z, u\}$ is a four-element set, then $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains precisely 94 totally atomic elements. The join irreducible ones are the ones listed above (under permutations) and:

  • $x \wedge y \wedge z \wedge u$,
  • $(x \vee z \vee u) \wedge (y \vee z \vee u)$,
  • $(x \vee u) \wedge (y \vee u) \wedge (z \vee u)$,
  • $((x \wedge u) \vee (z \wedge u)) \wedge ((y \wedge u) \vee (z \wedge u))$.

and again their images under automorphisms of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (i.e., permutations of the variables). The duals give the meet-irreducible totally atomic elements. The total is 94.

The endomorphisms σ_x, μ_x and the set G (book pp. 136–137)

Definition · the endomorphisms $\sigma_u, \mu_u$ and the set G

Recall that an endomorphism of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is uniquely determined by its action on the generators. For every element $u \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ we define two endomorphisms $\sigma_u$ and $\mu_u$ of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ by

$$\sigma_u(x) = x \vee u, \qquad \mu_u(x) = x \wedge u$$

for $x \in X$. We also let $\sigma_u$ and $\mu_u$ denote the endomorphisms of the term algebra defined the same way.

Denote by $G$ the smallest subset of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that:

  1. $X \subseteq G$, and
  2. If $w \in G$ and $x$ is an element of $X$ NOT occurring in the canonical representation of $w$, then $\sigma_x(w) \in G$ and $\mu_x(w) \in G$.

The aim of §1 is to prove that $G$ is just the set of totally atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Every meet reducible element of $G$ can be written as

$$w = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\mu_{p_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_2}\mu_{p_2}\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$$

where

$$p_i = y_{i1} \wedge \cdots \wedge y_{il_i}, \qquad s_i = z_{i1} \vee \cdots \vee z_{ik_i}.$$

The $y_{ij}$'s and $z_{ij}$'s are distinct generators. We allow $n = 0$, in which case we just have $w = p_1 = \bigwedge y_{1j}$, and we allow $l_{n+1} = 0$ (i.e., $p_{n+1} = 1$). Otherwise we require $k_i, l_i \ge 1$, and $l_1 > 1$ (so that $w$ is meet reducible).

To get a feel for this notation, the last three elements given in Example 6.2 can be written as:

  • $\sigma_{z \vee u}(x \wedge y) = (x \vee z \vee u) \wedge (y \vee z \vee u)$
  • $\sigma_u(x \wedge y \wedge z) = (x \vee u) \wedge (y \vee u) \wedge (z \vee u)$
  • $\mu_u \sigma_z(x \wedge y) = ((x \wedge u) \vee (z \wedge u)) \wedge ((y \wedge u) \vee (z \wedge u))$

Technical lemmas establishing $G$ ⊆ totally atomic (book pp. 137–138)

Lemma 6.3 · $\sigma_x$ and $\mu_x$ preserve order strictness

Let $u, v \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ and $x \in X$ a generator occurring in NEITHER $u$ NOR $v$. Then $u \le v$ iff $\sigma_x(u) \le \sigma_x(v)$ iff $\mu_x(u) \le \mu_x(v)$.

Lemma 6.4 · the term at the right side of (1) is in canonical form

The expression $w = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_2}\mu_{p_2}\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$ is in canonical form.

Lemma 6.5 · technical bound on the inner-meet structure

For an element $w$ of the form (1), $w \le \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\mu_{p_2}\sigma_{s_1}(0) \vee y_{ij}$ for all $i$ and $j$.

Lemma 6.6 · explicit computation of $\mathbf{J}(w), \mathbf{K}(w), w_\dagger$ for $w \in G$

Let $w \neq 0$ be a meet reducible element of $G$, expressed as in (1). Then:

  1. $\mathbf{J}(w) = \{w\} \cup \{\mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\mu_{p_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_t}\mu_{p_t}(x) : t = 2, \ldots, n+1 \text{ and } x = y_{ij} \text{ or } z_{ij} \text{ for some } i < t \text{ and some } j\}$.
  2. If $u \in \mathbf{J}(w)$, then $u < w$ if and only if $u = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\mu_{p_t}(z_{t-1,j})$ for some $t = 2, \ldots, n+1$ and some $j$.
  3. $w_\dagger = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_2}\mu_{p_2}\sigma_{s_1}(0)$.
  4. $\mathbf{K}(w) = \{\mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\mu_{p_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_t}\mu_{p_t}(z_{ij}) : t = 2, \ldots, n+1 \text{ and } i < t\}$.
  5. $\bigvee \mathbf{K}(w) = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_1}\mu_{p_2}(s_1 \vee \cdots \vee s_n)$ and $w \not\le \bigvee \mathbf{K}(w)$.
Lemma 6.7 · every element of $G$ is totally atomic

By induction on the length of $w$. If $w$ is a generator or $0$ or $1$, it is clear that $w$ is totally atomic. Otherwise, by induction, the canonical meetands of $w$ are totally atomic. Lemma 6.6's data combined with Theorem 3.30 of Chapter III shows $w$ is completely join irreducible. By Corollary 3.8, $w$ is lower atomic. Dually, $w$ is upper atomic.

The reverse inclusion: totally atomic ⊆ $G$ (book pp. 138–140)

Lemma 6.8 · join-reducible totally atomic structure

Let $u \in G$ be join reducible with $u = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_n$ canonically, and let $x \in X$ such that $u_1 \not\le x$ and $u_i \le x$ for $2 \le i \le n$. Then $x \wedge u \in X^\wedge$.

Lemma 6.9 · meet-reducible totally atomic ⊆ $G$ structurally

Let $w = w_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge w_m$, where each $w_i$ is a meet irreducible element of $G$. For each $i$ let $w_i = \bigvee_j w_{ij}$ canonically (if $w_i \in X$, let $w_i = w_{i1}$), and assume that $w_{ij} \not\le w$ for all $j > 1$. Then $w \in G$.

Theorem 6.10 — the central characterization (book p. 140)

Theorem 6.10 · totally atomic = $G$

An element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is totally atomic if and only if it belongs to $G$.

Proof. By Lemma 6.7, $G \subseteq$ totally atomic. For the converse: induct on length of $w$. If $w$ is totally atomic, by Theorem 6.1 each subelement is totally atomic. If $w = w_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge w_m$ canonically with $m > 1$, by induction each $w_i$ is in $G$. Theorem 3.4 says: for each $i$, either $w_i$ is a generator OR all but one of the canonical joinands of $w_i$ are below $w$. Then Lemma 6.9 applies to give $w \in G$. $\square$

Corollary 6.11 — the exact count via Stirling numbers (book pp. 140–141)

Corollary 6.11 · finitely many totally atomic + Stirling-number formula

The free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains only FINITELY MANY totally atomic elements. Their number is $|X|$ plus two times the number of finite sequences $Y_0, Y_1, \ldots, Y_k$ of pairwise disjoint nonempty subsets of $X$ such that $|Y_0| \ge 2$.

If $n = |X|$, then the number of totally atomic elements in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is

$$n + 2\left[\sum_{m=2}^n \binom{n}{m} \sum_{s=2}^m \binom{m}{s} \sum_{k=0}^{m-s} k! \, S(m-s, k)\right]$$

where $S(r, k)$ is the Stirling number of the second kind, i.e., the number of partitions of an $r$-element set into $k$ blocks; $S(0, 0) = 1$.

Concrete values from the formula. $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has $\boxed{17}$ totally atomic elements. $\mathbf{FL}(4)$ has $\boxed{94}$. $\mathbf{FL}(5)$ has $\boxed{667}$. $\mathbf{FL}(10)$ has $\boxed{125,499,820}$.

Intuition · why the formula works

Theorem 6.10 says totally atomic = $G$. The structure of $G$ (Lemma 6.4): every meet-reducible element of $G$ has a UNIQUE canonical-form expression $\mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$. Counting such expressions: each $p_i$ is a meet of distinct generators ($Y_0 = \{y_{1j}\}$, $|Y_0| \ge 2$ since $l_1 > 1$); each $s_i$ is a join of distinct generators (the $z_{ij}$'s — call this $Y_i$); the $Y_0, Y_1, \ldots, Y_k$ are pairwise disjoint subsets of $X$ (since the same generator cannot occur in distinct $\sigma$/$\mu$ levels in canonical form).

So the number of meet-reducible totally atomic elements = number of finite sequences $Y_0, \ldots, Y_k$ of pairwise disjoint nonempty subsets of $X$ with $|Y_0| \ge 2$. Counting these:

  • Choose $m$ = total number of generators used ($2 \le m \le n$): $\binom{n}{m}$ ways.
  • Choose $|Y_0| = s$ ($2 \le s \le m$): $\binom{m}{s}$ ways for $Y_0$.
  • Distribute the remaining $m - s$ generators into $k$ ORDERED non-empty blocks $Y_1, \ldots, Y_k$ ($0 \le k \le m - s$): $k! \cdot S(m-s, k)$ ways (Stirling number times $k!$ for ordering).

Summing over $m$, $s$, $k$ gives the bracketed expression. Multiply by 2 (for join-reducible duals via Theorem 6.10's duality), add $n$ (for the generators themselves, which are both join AND meet irreducible), gives the total count.

Corollary 6.12 · automorphism-fixed κ for totally atomic

Let $w = w_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge w_n$ be a join irreducible totally atomic element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. For each permutation $\sigma$ of $\{1, \ldots, n\}$, there is an automorphism of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ which maps $w_i$ to $w_{\sigma(i)}$ (and so fixes $w$). Consequently, if $w_i \le \kappa(w)$ for some $i$, then $\kappa(w) = w_*$.

It can happen that $\kappa(w) = w_*$, but this is extremely rare. Theorem 8.6 of Chapter VIII shows that this occurs only at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Connection P2 · counting via Stirling numbers of the second kind

Corollary 6.11's count formula is one of the rare instances in the monograph where a structural classification has an EXPLICIT closed-form count via combinatorial identities. The Stirling numbers $S(r, k)$ — counting partitions of an $r$-element set into $k$ blocks — appear here because of the structural decomposition: each totally atomic element in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ corresponds to a finite sequence $Y_0, Y_1, \ldots, Y_k$ of pairwise disjoint nonempty subsets of $X$ with $|Y_0| \ge 2$.

The combinatorial structure. The σ/μ alternation pattern of a totally atomic element $w = \mu_{p_{n+1}} \sigma_{s_n} \cdots \sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$ partitions the generators used in $w$ into "layers": $Y_0 = \{y_{1j}\}$ (the bottom meet), $Y_1 = \{z_{1j}\}$ (the first σ), $Y_2 = \{y_{2j}\}$ or $\{z_{2j}\}$ (the second alternation), etc. The layers are pairwise disjoint (a generator cannot appear at two different alternation depths in canonical form). The Stirling number $S(m-s, k)$ counts the partitioning of the remaining $m - s$ generators into $k$ unordered blocks; multiplying by $k!$ orders them (giving the σ/μ sequence's order).

Concrete values. $\mathbf{FL}(3)$: 17. $\mathbf{FL}(4)$: 94. $\mathbf{FL}(5)$: 667. $\mathbf{FL}(6)$: ~5000-ish. $\mathbf{FL}(10)$: 125,499,820. The growth is roughly $n!$-rate (factorial in the number of generators), reflecting how the σ/μ alternation explodes combinatorially.

Relevance to the algorithm. Chapter III's cover algorithm operates on canonical-form terms of arbitrary length. Combined with Theorem 6.24 (cover-witnesses are totally atomic) and Corollary 6.11 (finitely many candidates), Chapter VI gives a SPECIALIZED ENUMERATION: when looking for cover-witnesses, enumerate the (polynomial in $n$) totally atomic candidates rather than the (exponential) canonical-form space. For $|X| = 3$: only 17 candidates to check; for $|X| = 4$: 94. The structural finiteness is what makes cover analysis tractable in practice.

Connection P1 · the G construction

The set $G$ is the chapter's central technical construction. Define recursively: $X \subseteq G$; if $w \in G$ and $x$ is a "fresh" generator (not occurring in the canonical representation of $w$), then $\sigma_x(w) \in G$ and $\mu_x(w) \in G$.

The "freshness" requirement is crucial. If $x$ already occurs in $w$, then $\sigma_x(w) = w \vee x$ has the SAME canonical form variables as $w$ — and in fact reduces (e.g., $\sigma_x(x) = x \vee x = x$). The freshness ensures $\sigma_x(w)$ has STRICTLY LARGER canonical form than $w$, preserving total atomicity.

Why $G$ characterizes totally atomic. The two-direction proof: (a) $G \subseteq$ totally atomic via Lemma 6.7 (the lemma uses Theorem 3.30's syntactic test; the inductive structure of $G$ matches the recursive structure of $\mathbf{J}(w)$). (b) Totally atomic ⊆ $G$ via Lemmas 6.8–6.9 (the canonical form of a totally atomic element decomposes into a $\sigma$/$\mu$ sequence on a sub-generator-set).

Algorithmic consequence. $G$ is enumerable: starting from $X$, repeatedly apply $\sigma_x, \mu_x$ for fresh $x$. The enumeration is finite (since fresh generators eventually exhaust $X$). For $|X| = n$, the algorithm terminates in $\le n$ levels of nesting.

The duality. $G$ is closed under both $\sigma_x$ AND $\mu_x$ — so totally atomic is a SELF-DUAL class. Each meet-reducible totally atomic element has a join-reducible totally atomic dual under the lattice anti-automorphism. This is why the count formula has the factor of 2: meet-reducible and join-reducible totally atomic elements pair up by duality, with the generators counted only once.

PART B · CANONICAL κ — §2

The canonical form of $\kappa(w)$ for totally atomic $w$ — Theorem 6.21

§ 2 Canonical Form of $\kappa$ of a Totally Atomic Element

Recursively: $\overline{\kappa}(w) = E$ for a meet of joins of meets of joins... pattern, where $E$ accumulates "complement-style" joins of $\kappa$ images. Theorem 6.21 gives the canonical form explicitly. Proof via Lemmas 6.13–6.20.

The recursive notation (book pp. 141–142)

Definition · $\phi_t, B_t, A_{ti}, E, \overline{\kappa}(w)$

As before, let $w = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_2}\mu_{p_2}\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$ where $s_i = z_{i1} \vee \cdots \vee z_{ik_i}$ and $p_i = y_{i1} \wedge \cdots \wedge y_{il_i}$, be a meet reducible, nonzero, totally atomic element. We know that $\kappa(w)$ exists and can be computed using Theorem 3.32. However, the formula in 3.32 does not give a canonical form for $\kappa(w)$. The purpose of the present section is to find the canonical form.

For $t = 0, \ldots, n$, define $\phi_t = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\mu_{p_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_{t+1}}\mu_{p_{t+1}}$, so that $w = \phi_0(s_1)$. (When $t = 0$, $\phi_0$ is the full prefix excluding $\sigma_{s_1}\mu_{p_1}$ at the inner end.) By Lemma 6.6(1), $\mathbf{J}(w) - \{w\}$ is the set of the elements $\phi_t(x)$ for $t = 1, \ldots, n$ and $x = y_{ij}$ or $z_{ij}$ with $i \le t$.

By induction on the length of $w$ we define an element $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ as follows. Set:

$$B_t = \bigwedge_{j=1}^t \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_j} \overline{\kappa}(\phi_t(y_{jc})),$$ $$A_{ti} = B_t \wedge \overline{\kappa}(\phi_t(z_{ti})),$$ $$E = \bigvee_{t=1}^n \bigvee_{i=1}^{k_t} A_{ti},$$ $$\overline{\kappa}(w) = \begin{cases}\bigvee(X - \{y_{11}, \ldots, y_{1l_1}\}) & \text{for } n = 0, \\ z_{11} \vee E & \text{for } n = 1 \text{ and } k_1 = 1, \\ E & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}$$

The aim is to show $\overline{\kappa}(w) = \kappa(w)$ AND that $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ is in canonical form.

Lemmas establishing the formula (book pp. 142–144)

Lemmas 6.13–6.18 · the technical lemmas

6.13: For any generator $x$, $x \le \overline{\kappa}(w)$ iff $p_{n+1} \wedge x \le \overline{\kappa}(w)$ iff $x \notin \{y_{11}, \ldots, y_{n+1, l_{n+1}}\}$. So $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ is BELOW exactly the right generators.

6.14: $\overline{\kappa}(w) \le \kappa(\phi_1(y_{1c}))$ for $1 \le c \le l_1$.

6.15: Let $1 \le t < n$. Then $\kappa(\phi_t(x)) \le \kappa(\phi_{t+1}(x))$, $\kappa(\phi_t(z_{ti})) \le \kappa(\phi_{t+1}(y_{t+1,c}))$ for any $c$, and $\kappa(\phi_t(y_{tc})) \le \kappa(\phi_{t+1}(y_{t+1,d}))$ for any $d$.

6.16: The element $k^\dagger$ used in Theorem 3.32 is $k^\dagger = \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_1} \kappa(\phi_1(y_{1c}))$. Moreover, $k^\dagger \le \bigwedge_{j=1}^t \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_j} \kappa(\phi_t(y_{jc}))$ for each $t = 1, \ldots, n$.

6.17: A "side lemma": let $w'$ be obtained from $w$ by replacing one of the generators $y_{11}, \ldots, y_{1l_1}$ with a new generator not occurring in $w$. Then $w \le \kappa(w')$.

6.18: An element $v \in \mathbf{J}(w) - \{w\}$ satisfies $w \not\le \kappa(v)$ iff $v = \phi_t(z_{ti})$ for some $t$ and $i$.

Lemma 6.19 · $\overline{\kappa}(w) = \kappa(w)$

The element $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ defined recursively above equals the κ-image $\kappa(w)$ from Theorem 3.32.

The proof uses Lemmas 6.13–6.18 plus Theorem 3.32. Specifically: the κ-formula from Theorem 3.32 is

$$\kappa(w) = \bigvee\{x \in X : w \not\le w_\dagger \vee x\} \vee \bigvee_{t,i} (k^\dagger \wedge \kappa(\phi_t(z_{ti}))).$$

Lemma 6.5 + Lemma 6.13 give the first term. Lemma 6.16 + the recursive definition of $E$ identify the second term as $E$ itself. Combining gives $\overline{\kappa}(w) = \kappa(w)$.

Lemma 6.20 · $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ is in canonical form

The recursive definition of $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ produces an expression in canonical form.

The proof inducts on $n$. For $n = 0$, $\overline{\kappa}(w) = \bigvee(X - \{y_{11}, \ldots, y_{1l_1}\})$ — a join of distinct generators, in canonical form trivially. For $n \ge 1$, the meetands $\overline{\kappa}(\phi_t(x))$ for various $t, x$ are pairwise incomparable (Lemma 6.13's variable-index argument), and by induction each is in canonical form. Theorem 1.18 of Chapter I (canonical-form characterization) confirms.

Theorem 6.21 — the canonical form of κ for totally atomic (book p. 144)

Theorem 6.21 · explicit canonical form for $\kappa(w)$

Let $w$ be a nonzero meet reducible totally atomic element, expressed as $w = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$. The canonical expression for $\kappa(w)$ is

$$\kappa(w) = \begin{cases}\bigvee(X - \{y_{11}, \ldots, y_{1l_1}\}) & \text{if } n = 0 \text{ and } w = p_1, \\ z_{11} \vee \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_1} \kappa(p_2 \vee y_{1c}) \wedge \kappa(p_2 \vee z_{11}) & \text{if } n = k_1 = 1, s_1 = z_{11}, \\ \bigvee_{t=1}^n \bigvee_{c=1}^{k_t} \big[\bigwedge_{j=1}^t \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_j} \kappa(\phi_t(y_{jc})) \wedge \kappa(\phi_t(z_{ti}))\big] & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}$$

Examples (book pp. 144–145)

Example 6.22 · $\kappa(w)$ for a small totally atomic $w$

Despite its imposing appearance, the formula can actually be used to calculate $\kappa(w)$ by hand when $n$ is small. Let $X = \{a, b, c, d, e\}$ and $w = \sigma_e \mu_d \sigma_c(a \wedge b)$. Then

$$\kappa(w) = (\overline{a} \vee \overline{b} \wedge \overline{d} \vee \overline{e}) \vee$$ $$\big[(e \vee (\overline{a} \wedge \overline{d} \wedge \overline{e})) \wedge (e \vee (\overline{c} \wedge \overline{d} \wedge \overline{e})) \wedge (e \vee (\overline{b} \wedge \overline{d} \wedge \overline{e}))\big]$$

where $\overline{a}$ is the join of the generators other than $a$, etc.

Example 6.23 · larger totally atomic elements

The formula is recursive, hence not hard to program. Let the first ten elements of $X$ be $a, b, c, d, e, f, g, h, i, j$ and let $w = \sigma_j \mu_i \sigma_h \mu_{f \wedge g} \sigma_{d \vee e}(a \wedge b \wedge c)$. Then $\kappa(w)$ is given canonically by a specific 8-line formula (book p. 145) involving meets of joins of meets of joins of $\overline{a}, \overline{b}, \ldots, \overline{j}$. The structure shows the recursive depth scales with the σ/μ alternation count.

Intuition · alternative proof via 6 conditions

An alternative way to prove $u = \kappa(w)$, more sophisticated than the brute-force approach above: if $w$ is a completely join irreducible element, then $u = \kappa(w)$ if and only if the following six conditions are satisfied:

  1. $u$ is meet irreducible.
  2. $\mathbf{M}(\kappa(w)) - \{\kappa(w)\} = \mathbf{M}(u) - \{u\}$.
  3. $\kappa(w)/0 \cap X \subseteq u/0 \cap X$.
  4. $\kappa(w)^\dagger \le u^\dagger$.
  5. $\kappa(w) B^d v$ implies $u \ge u^\dagger \wedge v$.
  6. $w \not\le u$.

Using the characterization of totally atomic elements and Lemma 2.63, this gives a different derivation of the formula in Theorem 6.21.

PART C · THE ROLE — §3

Cover-witnesses are totally atomic; structural consequences for κ-images

Theorem 6.24: every cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is witnessed by a totally atomic element. Theorems 6.26–6.30: when does $\kappa(w)$ have a lower cover, and what does its structural form imply. Theorem 6.31: the technical "no v exists" theorem used in Chapter VIII.

§ 3 The Role of Totally Atomic Elements

The chapter's structural payoff: every cover-witness in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is totally atomic. Combined with Corollary 6.11's finiteness, this is what makes covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ structurally tractable.

Cover-witnesses are totally atomic (book pp. 145–146)

Theorem 6.24 · cover-witness ⟹ totally atomic

Let $w$ be a completely meet irreducible element in a free lattice, and let $a$ be a lower cover of $w$. If $w_1$ is the unique canonical joinand of $w$ such that $w_1 \not\le u$, then $w_1$ is totally atomic.

Intuition · why covers force totally atomic

Theorem 6.24 is the chapter's central structural payoff. Given a covering pair $w \succ a$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $w$ completely meet irreducible: by Theorem 3.5 (Chapter III), the unique $w_1$ from the canonical joinands of $w$ such that $w_1 \not\le a$ is completely join irreducible. By Theorem 6.24, $w_1$ is moreover TOTALLY ATOMIC. So cover-witnesses inherit the strong structural condition.

Combined with Corollary 6.11 (finitely many totally atomic), this gives a powerful constraint: every covering pair in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is witnessed by one of finitely many totally atomic candidates. The polynomial-time cover algorithm of Chapter III §5 can be sped up further by enumerating totally atomic candidates first. For $|X| = 3$: only 17 candidates; for $|X| = 4$: only 94. Vast savings over the general canonical-form search space.

Lemma 6.25 · structural form when $\kappa(w)$ is also totally atomic

Let $w$ be a nonzero join irreducible totally atomic element. If $\kappa(w)$ is also totally atomic, then $w \in X^\wedge$. If a canonical joinand of $\kappa(w)$ is totally atomic, then either $w \in X^\wedge$ or $w = \mu_{y_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge y_l} \sigma_z (x_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge x_m)$ for pairwise distinct generators $x_1, \ldots, x_m, z, y_1, \ldots, y_l$ with $m \ge 2$ and $l \ge 0$, and the canonical joinand is the generator $z$.

Theorem 6.26 · when $\kappa(w)$ has a lower cover

Let $w$ be a totally atomic join irreducible element. Then $\kappa(w)$ has a lower cover if and only if either $w \in X^\wedge$ or $w = \mu_{y_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge y_l} \sigma_z (x_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge x_m)$ for pairwise distinct generators $x_1, \ldots, x_m, z, y_1, \ldots, y_l$ with $m \ge 2$, $l \ge 0$.

Theorem 6.27 — the upper cover of $\kappa(w)$ structure (book p. 146)

Theorem 6.27 · $\kappa(w)^* = w \vee \kappa(w)$ is not completely meet irreducible

Let $w$ be a join irreducible totally atomic element. Then $\kappa(w)^* = w \vee \kappa(w)$ is not completely meet irreducible.

Corollary 6.28 · u ≺ w with completely-meet-irreducible w forces u meet reducible

Let $w$ be a completely meet irreducible element. If $u \prec w$, then $u$ is meet reducible.

Lemma 6.29 · structural constraint on $\mathbf{J}(w) - \{w\}$ vs $\kappa(w)^*$

Let $w$ be a completely join irreducible element. Then no element of $\mathbf{J}(w) - \{w\}$ belongs to $\kappa(w)^*/\kappa(w)$. Moreover, if $w$ is meet reducible, then no canonical meetand of $w$ is below $\kappa(w)^*$.

Theorem 6.30 — completely-join-irreducibles in $\kappa(w)/w_*$ (book p. 146)

Theorem 6.30 · $\kappa(w)/w_*$ contains no completely-join-irreducible if $\kappa(w)$ is not totally atomic

Let $w$ be a completely join irreducible element such that $\kappa(w)$ is not totally atomic. Then the interval $\kappa(w)/w_*$ contains no completely join irreducible element.

Theorem 6.30 says: some intervals in a free lattice contain NO completely join irreducible elements. This is striking — it shows that the interval structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is genuinely constrained by the totally-atomic obligations on κ-images. By contrast, Day's theorem (Theorem 4.1) says that if $u \not\le v$ in a finitely generated free lattice, then there exists a completely join irreducible element $q$ with $q \le u$ and $v \le \kappa(q)$ (and hence $q \not\le v$). By way of contrast, the chapter's Theorem 6.31 below shows that there exist pairs $u, v$ with $u > v$ such that every completely meet irreducible element below $u$ is also below $v$.

Theorem 6.31 — the technical Chapter-VIII prerequisite (book pp. 147–148)

Theorem 6.31 · "no v exists" theorem

Suppose $q$ is a completely meet irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ and $q^* \succ q \succ p$. Let $q_1$ be the canonical joinand of $q$ not below $p$ (if $q \in X$ then $q_1 = q$) and suppose $\kappa^d(q) \le \kappa(q_1)$. Then there is no completely meet irreducible element $v$ such that

$$v \le q^* \wedge \kappa(q_1)$$ $$v \not\le p \vee \kappa^d(q).$$

The proof is by minimal counter-example. Let $t = \kappa^d(q)$. Suppose $v$ is a completely meet irreducible element of minimal complexity satisfying $v \le q^* \wedge \kappa(q_1)$ and $v \not\le p \vee t$. By Theorem 6.30 (applied dually), $t$ is totally atomic. Since $\kappa(t) = q$ has a lower cover, Corollary 6.26 applies, and $q_1 \in X$. By Theorem 6.26, $t$ takes one of three forms (Cases 1, 2, 3 of the proof, p. 148). Each case leads to a contradiction via configuration-forbidding lemmas (using Theorem 6.30, the dual of Theorem 3.30, and the structural setup).

The simplest concrete example of Theorem 6.31 is obtained by taking $q = x$, in which case the conclusion becomes Corollary 6.33 below.

Lemma 6.32 · counterexample-forbidding lemma for Theorem 6.31

Let $q$ be a completely meet irreducible element which is not a coatom, and $t = \kappa^d(q) = \bigwedge t_i$ canonically. Then for every completely meet irreducible element $w \in q^*/t$ there exists an $i$ such that $w^\dagger \wedge t_i = t$.

Corollary 6.33 · simplest concrete case of Theorem 6.31

If $X$ is finite, then there is no completely meet irreducible element $v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that

$$v \le x^* \wedge \overline{x}, \qquad v \not\le x_* \vee \underline{x}$$

where $\overline{x} = \bigvee(X - \{x\})$ and $\underline{x} = \bigwedge(X - \{x\})$.

Corollary 6.33 is what Chapter VIII §1's semisingular-elements analysis uses. The "no v" result is a powerful structural constraint: certain intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contain no completely-meet-irreducibles below them in specific configurations. Combined with Theorem 6.30, this gives Chapter VIII the leverage to classify semisingular elements.

Connection P3 · why totally atomic elements are central

Chapter VI's structural results explain why totally atomic elements appear "everywhere" in the fine-structure analysis of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

(1) Cover-witnesses are totally atomic (Theorem 6.24). Every covering pair $w \succ a$ with $w$ completely meet irreducible has a unique canonical-joinand witness $w_1 \not\le a$, and $w_1$ is totally atomic. This is the chapter's central structural fact.

(2) Finitely many totally atomic (Corollary 6.11). $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has finitely many totally atomic elements — explicit count via Stirling numbers. So cover-analysis in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has a FINITE search space.

(3) Structural duality. Totally atomic = self-dual class. The 17 totally atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ pair up: 7 join-reducibles ↔ 7 meet-reducibles, with 3 self-dual generators. This duality propagates through the canonical κ-formula (Theorem 6.21) and into the role-of-totally-atomic theorems.

(4) κ-images of totally atomic. Theorem 6.26: $\kappa(w)$ has a lower cover iff $w$ has a specific structural form (in $X^\wedge$ or specific $\mu_{y_1 \wedge \cdots} \sigma_z (x_1 \wedge \cdots)$ pattern). So the join-totally-atomic ↔ meet-totally-atomic correspondence under κ has explicit description.

(5) Constraints on intervals (Theorem 6.30). If $\kappa(w)$ is not totally atomic, the interval $\kappa(w)/w_*$ contains no completely join irreducible element. Hence intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ have structurally-constrained content depending on whether their endpoints are totally atomic.

(6) Chapter VII's covering-chain analysis uses Theorems 6.24, 6.27 to bound cover-chain lengths. Chapter VIII's semisingular-elements theory uses Theorem 6.31 / Corollary 6.33. Chapter IX's Tschantz theorem uses the "totally atomic finiteness" via the cover algorithm to construct incomparable elements in infinite intervals.

The chapter's title — "Totally Atomic Elements" — understates the scope: this is the chapter that delivers the FINITARY MACHINERY for fine-structure analysis of an infinite lattice.

Connection P4 · from cover-witnesses to structural classification

Theorems 6.24 and 6.27 together create a "cover-witness pipeline":

Step 1. A covering pair $w \succ a$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $w$ completely meet irreducible.

Step 2. The unique canonical joinand $w_1$ of $w$ not below $a$ is totally atomic (Theorem 6.24).

Step 3. $w_1 \in G$ — i.e., $w_1$ has a unique canonical-form expression $\mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$ with the σ/μ alternation determined by which generators it uses.

Step 4. Theorem 6.21 gives explicit canonical form for $\kappa(w_1)$, hence for $w_{1*} = w_1 \wedge \kappa(w_1)$ (the lower cover of $w_1$).

Step 5. Theorem 6.27 says $\kappa(w_1)^* = w_1 \vee \kappa(w_1)$ is NOT completely meet irreducible — providing the structural inequality used to classify cover chains.

Step 6. Theorem 6.30 says if $\kappa(w_1)$ is not totally atomic, the interval $\kappa(w_1)/w_{1*}$ has no completely-join-irreducible — constraining where covers in this interval can occur.

The pipeline is what Chapter VII's covering-chain analysis uses: starting from a completely-meet-irreducible $w$, peel off cover-witnesses one at a time, each totally atomic, until the chain bottoms out. The total length of the chain is bounded by the canonical-form depth of $w$.

$A_{ti}$
The auxiliary expression $A_{ti} = B_t \wedge \overline{\kappa}(\phi_t(z_{ti}))$ used in the recursive definition of $\overline{\kappa}(w)$ for totally atomic $w$. (Defined book p. 141.)
$B_t$
The auxiliary expression $B_t = \bigwedge_{j=1}^t \bigwedge_{c=1}^{l_j} \overline{\kappa}(\phi_t(y_{jc}))$. Used in defining $\overline{\kappa}(w)$.
Cover-witness
For a completely meet irreducible $w$ with lower cover $a$, the unique canonical joinand $w_1$ of $w$ such that $w_1 \not\le a$. By Theorem 6.24, every cover-witness is totally atomic.
$E$ (in §2)
The expression $E = \bigvee_{t=1}^n \bigvee_{i=1}^{k_t} A_{ti}$. The "complement" join used in the recursive definition of $\overline{\kappa}(w)$.
$G$
The smallest subset of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ containing $X$ and closed under: if $w \in G$ and $x \in X$ does not occur in the canonical representation of $w$, then $\sigma_x(w) \in G$ and $\mu_x(w) \in G$. Theorem 6.10: $G$ = totally atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Lower atomic
From Chapter III, Definition before Theorem 3.5: $a$ is lower atomic if for every $u < a$ there exists $v$ with $u \le v \prec a$. Equivalently: every interval $a/b$ contains an upper-edge cover of $a$.
$\mu_u$
The endomorphism of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ defined by $\mu_u(x) = x \wedge u$ for all generators $x$. For an element $u \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, $\mu_u$ is determined by its action on generators.
$\phi_t$
The partial composition $\phi_t = \mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\mu_{p_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_{t+1}}\mu_{p_{t+1}}$ for a totally atomic element $w$ expressed as $\mu_{p_{n+1}}\sigma_{s_n}\cdots\sigma_{s_1}(p_1)$. Used in §2 for the κ-formula.
$\sigma_u$
The endomorphism of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ defined by $\sigma_u(x) = x \vee u$ for all generators $x$. Used in the $G$ construction.
$S(r, k)$
Stirling number of the second kind — the number of ways to partition an $r$-element set into $k$ nonempty blocks. Used in Corollary 6.11's count formula. $S(0, 0) = 1$.
Subelement
From Chapter I: a subelement of $w$ is a subterm in the canonical-form expression of $w$. Theorem 6.1(2): $w$ is totally atomic iff every subelement of $w$ is totally atomic.

An element $a$ is totally atomic if it is BOTH lower atomic AND upper atomic. Equivalently (Theorem 6.1): $a$ is totally atomic iff $a \in G$, the recursively-generated set in §1.

List all 17 totally atomic elements of $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ explicitly, classified by structural type.

Generators (3 elements, both join AND meet irreducible).

  • $x, y, z$.

Meet-reducibles ($\bigwedge$ of generators, totally atomic — count: 4).

  • $x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z$ — three pairwise meets.
  • $x \wedge y \wedge z$ — the full meet.

Join-reducibles ($\bigvee$ of generators, totally atomic — count: 4, by duality).

  • $x \vee y, x \vee z, y \vee z$ — three pairwise joins.
  • $x \vee y \vee z$ — the full join.

$\sigma_z(x \wedge y) = (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z)$ (and its automorphism orbit — count: 3).

  • $(x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) = \sigma_z(x \wedge y)$.
  • $(x \vee y) \wedge (z \vee y) = \sigma_y(x \wedge z)$.
  • $(y \vee x) \wedge (z \vee x) = \sigma_x(y \wedge z)$.

The dual: $\mu_z(x \vee y) = (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$ (and orbit — count: 3).

  • $(x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z) = \mu_z(x \vee y)$.
  • $(x \wedge y) \vee (z \wedge y) = \mu_y(x \vee z)$.
  • $(y \wedge x) \vee (z \wedge x) = \mu_x(y \vee z)$.

Verification via formula. $n = 3$: $n + 2[\sum_{m=2}^3 \binom{3}{m} \sum_{s=2}^m \binom{m}{s} \sum_{k=0}^{m-s} k! S(m-s, k)]$.

$m = 3$: $\binom{3}{3} \sum_{s=2}^3 \binom{3}{s} \sum_{k=0}^{3-s} k! S(3-s, k)$.

  • $s = 2$: $\binom{3}{2} = 3$. $\sum_{k=0}^1 k! S(1, k) = 0! S(1, 0) + 1! S(1, 1) = 0 + 1 = 1$. Contribution: $3 \cdot 1 = 3$.
  • $s = 3$: $\binom{3}{3} = 1$. $\sum_{k=0}^0 k! S(0, k) = 1$. Contribution: $1 \cdot 1 = 1$.

$m = 3$ total: $1 \cdot (3 + 1) = 4$. Combining: $3 + 4 = 7$. So $3 + 2 \cdot 7 = 17$. ✓

Example VI.BVerifying Corollary 6.11 at $n = 4$ and $n = 5$
Use the Stirling-number formula to verify the counts 94 (for $\mathbf{FL}(4)$) and 667 (for $\mathbf{FL}(5)$).

For $n = 4$: compute the bracketed expression.

$m = 2$: $\binom{4}{2} \cdot 1 = 6$.

$m = 3$: $\binom{4}{3} \cdot [3 + 1] = 4 \cdot 4 = 16$. (Same inner sum as $n=3$ case for $m=3$.)

  • $s = 3$: $\binom{4}{3} = 4$. $\sum_{k=0}^1 k! S(1, k) = 1$. Contribution: $4 \cdot 1 = 4$.
  • $s = 4$: $\binom{4}{4} = 1$. $\sum_{k=0}^0 k! S(0, k) = 1$. Contribution: $1 \cdot 1 = 1$.

For $n = 5$: by similar (more tedious) calculation.

$m = 2$: $\binom{5}{2} \cdot 1 = 10$.

$m = 3$: $\binom{5}{3} \cdot [3 + 1] = 10 \cdot 4 = 40$.

$m = 4$: $\binom{5}{4} \cdot 23 = 5 \cdot 23 = 115$. (Using the inner sum from $n = 4$, $m = 4$.)

$m = 5$: $\binom{5}{5} \cdot \sum_{s=2}^5 \binom{5}{s} \sum_{k=0}^{5-s} k! S(5-s, k)$.

  • $s = 3$: $\binom{5}{3} = 10$. $\sum_{k=0}^2 k! S(2, k) = 3$. Contribution: $10 \cdot 3 = 30$.
