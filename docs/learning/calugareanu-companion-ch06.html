Independence and Semiatomic Lattices

The lattice analog of linear independence; the Goldie dimension as the canonical numerical invariant of non-noetherian module theory; the lattice form of the Krull–Schmidt theorem; and a clean characterisation of complemented compactly generated lattices via "semiatomic."

~80–95 min read 19 sections + 5 connection panels 15 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — independence as the master concept

The chapter introduces a single new idea — independent subsets, the lattice analog of linear independence — and rides it to four substantial payoffs: a working dimension theory (Goldie), the Krull–Schmidt uniqueness of indecomposable decompositions, a clean characterisation of complemented compactly generated lattices, and the structural foundation for Chapters 7-13.

The big idea: a subset $\{a_i\}$ of a complete lattice with $0$ is independent if for every $i$, $a_i \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j = 0$. Plain English: each element of the subset is "fully clear" of the join of the others — the subset is mutually disjoint at the join level. The join $\bigvee_i a_i$ is then called a direct sum and written $\bigoplus_i a_i$.

This is the lattice version of linear independence. In a vector space, $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ is linearly independent iff each $v_i$ is not in the span of the others — and the corresponding subspaces $\langle v_i\rangle$ are independent in the lattice sense above. The whole apparatus of dimension theory in linear algebra is a special case of the lattice apparatus this chapter develops.

Four major movements:

  1. Independence basics (Part A). Definition, direct sum, equivalent characterisations (Lemmas 6.1, 6.2), how independent sets combine and extend (Lemmas 6.3, 6.4, Proposition 6.1), the foundational Theorem 6.1: $M$ is maximal independent iff $\bigvee M$ is essential. This couples independence to Chapter 4's essentialness apparatus and Chapter 5's socle theory: in inductive lattices, the socle is a direct sum of atoms (Theorem 6.2).
  2. Variant notions (Part B). Călugăreanu's independence is the standard notion in this book, but the literature uses several variations: weakly independent, join independent (Stenström's convention), and Stanley's set-theoretic "independent collections." All four notions agree in well-behaved lattices but diverge in pathological cases.
  3. Goldie dimension (Part C). The chapter's punchline numerical invariant. Goldie dimension (also called uniform dimension) is the maximum cardinality of an independent subset of "uniform" elements (those with no proper essential extension below). Theorem 6.4 gives a four-way equivalence for finite Goldie dimension. This is the right notion of dimension for non-noetherian modules, where classical length and rank break down.
  4. Krull–Schmidt (Part D), and semiatomic lattices (Part E). Theorem 6.5 (Ore): in a finite-length modular lattice, indecomposable direct decompositions are unique up to permutation and "projective in pairs." This is the lattice form of the classical Krull–Schmidt theorem (groups, modules, abelian categories). Then Part E develops semiatomic lattices ($1$ = join of atoms): Theorems 6.6, 6.7 show modular upper continuous semiatomic lattices coincide with modular compactly generated complemented ones; Theorem 6.8 gives a four-way equivalence.
The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

Independence is the lattice abstraction of linear independence. Every working dimension theory in algebra — vector space dimension, module rank, group rank, transcendence degree — descends from this lattice notion. The Goldie dimension extends classical dimension to any modular lattice; Krull–Schmidt makes indecomposable decompositions unique; and semiatomic lattices are the abstract version of "module of simple parts." The chapter is the technical foundation for Chapters 7 (semisimple lattices), 8–9 (local and projective), and the dimension theory threaded through the rest of the book.

Four senses of "independence" in this chapter

The literature has accumulated four distinct (but related) notions of "independence" in lattices, all of which appear in this chapter:

(1) Independent (Călugăreanu's main notion): $a_i \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j = 0$ for every $i$.

(2) Weakly independent: only checked at $a$, $\bigvee(S - a) = 0$. Same definition as (1) shape, but written without the "for every $i$" universal — the proof techniques use the same condition, but applied differently.

(3) Join independent (Stenström's, refs [18], [34]): for every finite subset $T \subseteq S$, $(\bigvee T) \wedge (\bigvee(S - T)) = 0$. Stronger than (1).

(4) Independent (Stanley's, ref [21]): for any two finite subsets $X, Y \subseteq I$, $(\bigvee X) \wedge (\bigvee Y) = \bigvee(X \cap Y)$.

Lemma 6.9: in upper continuous modular lattices, weakly independent ⟹ independent. Proposition 6.6: in upper continuous lattices satisfying lower covering condition, weakly independent atoms are independent. The four notions can disagree in arbitrary lattices but tend to agree in modular upper continuous settings.

maximal independent subset An independent subset $M$ of $L \setminus \{0\}$ such that no strictly larger independent subset exists. Theorem 6.1: $M$ is maximal independent iff $\bigvee M$ is essential in $L$.

New · Ch 6 · Watch the four senses

weakly independent A collection $S$ of non-zero elements is weakly independent if $a \wedge \bigvee(S - a) = 0$ holds for each $a \in S$. The definition shape is identical to Călugăreanu's "independent" — but the term "weakly independent" is sometimes used in the literature to emphasize that only this single condition is checked (vs. variant senses that demand more). New · Ch 6

join independent (Stenström's convention, [18], [34]) A finite subset $S \subseteq L$ of non-zero elements is join independent if $a \wedge \bigvee(S - a) = 0$ holds for each $a \in S$. An arbitrary subset is join independent if all its finite subsets are. Same shape as weakly independent but framed for proofs that avoid upper continuity. The book uses this convention to prove Lemma 6.6 and the dimension equivalence theorems without upper continuity.

New · Ch 6 · Stanley's notion

Stanley's independent collections (in [21]) A subset $I$ of $L \setminus \{0\}$ is independent in Stanley's sense if for any two finite subsets of $I$ we have $(\bigvee X) \wedge (\bigvee Y) = \bigvee(X \cap Y)$. This is a distributivity-flavoured condition: the meet of joins equals the join of intersections. Stronger than Călugăreanu's notion — it works element-by-element via finite subsets. Used in some combinatorial settings.

New · Ch 6 · KEY DIMENSION CONCEPT

uniform element An element $a$ such that the principal ideal $a/0$ contains no two non-zero elements with trivial meet — equivalently, every non-zero $b \le a$ is essential in $a/0$. Plain English: "$a$ is irreducibly atomic-flavoured" — there's no internal independence. The building blocks of Goldie dimension theory.

Deep dive — Linear independence in vector spaces, lifted to lattices

The motivating instance. A clean demonstration of how lattice independence recovers linear-algebraic intuition.
Deep dive · Independence in linear algebra and lattices

Linear independence. In a vector space $V$, vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are linearly independent iff none is in the span of the others: $v_i \notin \langle v_j : j \ne i\rangle$. Equivalently: every linear combination $\sum c_j v_j = 0$ has all $c_j = 0$.

Lattice independence (this chapter). In the subspace lattice $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$, the subspaces $\langle v_1\rangle, \ldots, \langle v_n\rangle$ are independent iff $\langle v_i\rangle \cap \sum_{j \ne i} \langle v_j\rangle = 0$ — exactly the condition that $v_i \notin \langle v_j : j \ne i\rangle$. So linear independence of $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ matches lattice independence of $\{\langle v_i\rangle\}$. The two notions are the same idea at different levels of abstraction.

The direct-sum analogy. In linear algebra, $V = \langle v_1\rangle \oplus \cdots \oplus \langle v_n\rangle$ when $\{v_i\}$ is a basis. The lattice direct sum $\bigoplus \langle v_i\rangle = V$ records the same fact at the lattice level. The notation $\oplus$ is borrowed from algebra precisely to mark this correspondence.

What lifts and what doesn't. Lattice independence captures the core of linear independence — the "no-overlap" condition. But unlike linear algebra, lattice independence doesn't come with a choice of "coefficients" — there's no analog of "coefficients in a field." So lattice independence is more general (works in any modular lattice) but loses some of linear algebra's computational character.

Where this matters. The lattice apparatus generalises to any modular lattice — submodule lattices over arbitrary rings (not just fields), subgroup lattices, lattices of substructures. The Goldie dimension applies in these wider settings, giving a "dimension" where classical rank doesn't apply.

Deep dive — Goldie dimension and the structure of non-noetherian modules

The chapter's central numerical invariant, in module-theoretic context.
Deep dive · Goldie dimension

The motivating problem. In linear algebra, vector spaces have a well-defined dimension — the cardinality of any basis. For modules over a noetherian ring, finitely generated modules have a finite "rank" via Krull-Schmidt. But for modules that are neither finitely generated nor over a noetherian ring, classical dimension breaks down. What's the right notion?

Goldie's answer (1958). Alfred Goldie introduced what we now call the Goldie dimension (or uniform dimension): the supremum of $n$ such that the module contains a direct sum of $n$ non-zero uniform submodules. A uniform module has no internal direct-sum decomposition into two non-zero pieces — every non-zero submodule is essential. So the Goldie dimension counts "indecomposable essential pieces."

Why this works. Theorem 6.4's four-way equivalence shows that the Goldie dimension is well-defined: in any modular lattice, the following are equivalent:
(a) No infinite join-independent subsets;
(b) A finite join-independent subset of uniform elements with essential join;
(c) Bounded cardinality of join-independent subsets;
(d) Stabilization of every ascending chain at some finite essential extension.
So whenever any of these holds, all four numbers (cardinality, sup, etc.) coincide — the Goldie dimension is well-defined.

Applications. Goldie dimension is the right invariant for: (a) torsion-free modules over commutative integral domains (gives the rank); (b) right ideals of a ring of fractions (Goldie's theorem on classical rings); (c) injective hulls (the dimension of the injective hull equals the Goldie dimension); (d) modular lattice theory (this chapter).

Goldie's theorem. A ring $R$ is a (right) Goldie ring if its right module $R_R$ has finite Goldie dimension AND ACC on annihilators. Goldie's theorem (1958, 1960) says: a ring is right Goldie and semiprime iff it has a classical right ring of fractions which is semisimple Artinian. This is the foundational result of non-commutative ring theory; the lattice apparatus in this chapter is the abstract scaffold supporting it.

Deep dive — Krull–Schmidt theorem (Theorem 6.5's algebraic instances)

The "uniqueness of indecomposable decomposition" theorem, in its many forms.
Deep dive · Krull–Schmidt

The theorem in its various forms. Wolfgang Krull (1925), Otto Schmidt (1929) and Robert Remak (1911) independently developed the uniqueness of indecomposable decomposition for finite groups and for finite-dimensional modules. The theorem says: for a finitely generated module $M$ over a noetherian ring, if $M = M_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus M_n = N_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus N_m$ are two decompositions into indecomposable summands, then $n = m$ and after permutation $M_i \cong N_{\sigma(i)}$.

The lattice form. Theorem 6.5 (Ore, 1936): in a finite-length modular lattice, indecomposable direct decompositions are unique up to permutation and "projective in pairs." This is more general than the module-theoretic Krull-Schmidt: it doesn't require the module to come from a ring. It works in any modular lattice with finite length.

Why finite length is needed. Without finite length, Krull-Schmidt can fail. Example: the abelian group $\bigoplus_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb Z$ admits two indecomposable decompositions of different cardinalities (rearrangement can change the indecomposables) — but its lattice has infinite length. Finite length forces uniqueness.

The category-theoretic generalisation. Atiyah, Bass and others extended Krull-Schmidt to abelian categories: in any "Krull–Schmidt category" (one in which idempotents split and every object is a finite direct sum of objects with local endomorphism rings), indecomposable decomposition is unique up to permutation and isomorphism. Theorem 6.5 is the lattice underpinning of this categorical fact.

Connection to representation theory. For modular representations of finite groups in characteristic $p$ dividing $|G|$, the category of finite-dimensional representations is Krull–Schmidt (modules over the artinian group algebra $kG$). Indecomposable representations are classified up to isomorphism, and every representation decomposes uniquely. Auslander–Reiten theory (1970s) gives a powerful structural picture of how indecomposables relate to each other.

Deep dive — Matroids and the combinatorial face of independence

Lattice independence has a combinatorial cousin: matroids. They share much structure.
Deep dive · Matroids

Matroids (Whitney 1935). A matroid on a set $E$ is a non-empty collection $\mathcal I$ of subsets of $E$ (the "independent sets") satisfying:
(I1) $\emptyset \in \mathcal I$;
(I2) Hereditarity: $A \subseteq B \in \mathcal I \Rightarrow A \in \mathcal I$;
(I3) Exchange property: if $A, B \in \mathcal I$ with $|A| < |B|$, then $\exists b \in B \setminus A$ with $A \cup \{b\} \in \mathcal I$.
The exchange property is the key axiom — it gives matroids a "rank" function and makes them generalise both vector-space independence and graph-theoretic independence (forests).

The lattice connection. The collection of "flats" of a matroid (closed sets under the matroid closure operator) forms a geometric lattice: a finite atomistic lattice that is also semimodular. Geometric lattices are exactly the lattices of flats of finite matroids (Birkhoff's theorem). Conversely, every geometric lattice arises from a matroid.

How this chapter relates to matroids. Călugăreanu's "independent" subset (Definition above) is the lattice version of matroid independence. The exchange property (I3) corresponds to the chapter's Lemma 6.5 + Proposition 6.5: in a modular lattice, you can swap an element of an independent set with an outside element of the right form. Goldie dimension corresponds to matroid rank. Atomic modular complemented lattices coincide with the lattices of flats of matroids — and Theorem 6.8's equivalences carry the lattice perspective into matroid territory.

Examples of matroids. Vector matroids (independent = linearly independent), graphic matroids (independent = forest), uniform matroids (independent = subsets of size $\le k$), transversal matroids (independent = transversals of a set system). All of these have lattices of flats that fall under this chapter's apparatus.

References. Oxley, Matroid Theory (Oxford, 1992) is the standard reference. Welsh, Matroid Theory (Academic Press, 1976). Birkhoff, Lattice Theory (3rd ed., 1967) — the geometric lattice / matroid correspondence is in §IV.7.

SECTION A1 Definition — independent subset and direct sum

The chapter's central definition. Read it carefully — every later result rests on it.
Definition · independent subset

A subset $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ of non-zero elements of a complete lattice $L$ (with $0$) is called independent if for every $i \in I$ the equality $$a_i \wedge \Bigl(\bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j\Bigr) = 0$$ holds. In this case we use the notation $\bigoplus_{i \in I} a_i = \bigvee_{i \in I} a_i$ and we call this join a direct sum.

Read this as: each $a_i$ is "fully clear" of the join of the others. The join's meet with any single $a_i$ is exactly the bottom — there's no overlap. The condition mirrors linear independence in vector spaces: $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ are linearly independent iff each $\langle v_i\rangle$ has trivial intersection with $\sum_{j \ne i} \langle v_j\rangle$.

Examples

  • Vector space. In $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$ for a vector space $V$, distinct $1$-dimensional subspaces $\langle v_1\rangle, \ldots, \langle v_n\rangle$ are independent iff $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are linearly independent. The direct sum $\bigoplus \langle v_i\rangle$ matches the linear-algebra direct sum.
  • $\mathbb Z/p \oplus \mathbb Z/q$ submodule lattice. The submodules $\mathbb Z/p \oplus 0$ and $0 \oplus \mathbb Z/q$ are independent: their meet is $0$. Their direct sum is the whole module.
  • Ring of integers. In $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z) = \{n\mathbb Z : n \ge 0\}$, distinct non-zero $a\mathbb Z$ and $b\mathbb Z$ are NEVER independent: $a\mathbb Z \cap b\mathbb Z = \mathrm{lcm}(a,b)\mathbb Z \ne 0$. So the only independent subsets of $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z)$ are singletons (or empty).

The third example shows: independence is restrictive in non-distributive settings. In $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z)$, every two non-zero elements have non-trivial meet, so no two distinct elements can be independent.

Why "direct sum"?

The notation $\bigoplus$ comes from module theory. A direct sum of modules $M_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus M_n$ is the module whose elements are tuples $(m_1, \ldots, m_n)$, with the property that the only "overlap" between summands is at zero. The lattice direct sum $\bigoplus a_i$ records the same fact at the lattice level: the components don't interfere. When $L = S_R(M)$ and $\{a_i\}$ are submodules of $M$, the lattice direct sum $\bigoplus a_i = \bigvee a_i$ corresponds to the module-theoretic internal direct sum $\sum a_i$, which IS the external direct sum $\bigoplus a_i$ when independence holds.

SECTION A2 Lemmas 6.1 and 6.2 — basic equivalences

Two technical equivalences. Lemma 6.1: in upper continuous lattices, independence is local — the property is determined by finite subsets. Lemma 6.2: the finite-subset condition reformulated in terms of left/right joins.
Lemma 6.1

In an upper continuous lattice $L$, a subset $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ is independent if and only if each finite subset of it is independent.

Why · upper continuity gives directed joins

The condition is necessary trivially (if the whole set satisfies the equation, every finite subset does too).

Conversely, suppose each finite subset is independent. Fix $i_0 \in I$. We compute $a_{i_0} \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i_0} a_j$. The join $\bigvee_{j \ne i_0} a_j$ equals the directed join $\bigvee_{F} \bigvee_{i \in F} a_i$ over finite subsets $F$ not containing $i_0$. By upper continuity (Lemma 2.2 of Chapter 2, specifically the Scott-continuity property): $$a_{i_0} \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i_0} a_j = \bigvee_F \bigl(a_{i_0} \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} a_i\bigr) = \bigvee_F 0 = 0$$ where each finite-subset meet is $0$ by independence of the finite subset $F \cup \{i_0\}$.

Reading: in upper continuous lattices, you can check independence locally — verify it on each finite subset, and the global property follows. This is what makes infinite independent sets manageable in the abstract lattice setting.

Maximal independence via Zorn

Following Lemma 6.1, the book remarks: "if $B \subseteq L$ and $A$ is independent in $B$ (using Zorn's Lemma) then $A$ is contained in a maximal independent subset of $B$." The argument: chains of independent subsets, ordered by inclusion, have suprema (= the union) that remain independent (by Lemma 6.1's local condition + chain elements being independent). So Zorn applies. Every independent subset extends to a maximal independent subset.

Lemma 6.2

In an upper continuous lattice, the subset $M$ is independent if and only if for each finite subset $F \subseteq M$, $(\bigvee F) \wedge (\bigvee(M - F)) = 0$ holds.

Why · induction on $|F|$

Induction on $n = |F|$. For $n = 1$ this is exactly the definition of independence. For $n \ge 2$, write $F = \{a_j\}_{j \in J}$ and use modularity to break the meet $(\bigvee F) \wedge (\bigvee(M - F))$ into pieces that the induction hypothesis handles. The book's chain of equalities (p. 60) walks through it.

Lemma 6.2 reformulates independence: instead of checking each $a_i$ separately, check that the join of any "left half" and the join of the "right half" have trivial meet. This is cleaner for some proofs (and matches Stenström's "join independent" convention).

SECTION A3 Lemmas 6.3, 6.4 + Proposition 6.1 — combining and extending independent sets

Three tools for constructing larger independent sets from smaller ones. The associativity-style identity (Lemma 6.3), the disjoint-union rule (Lemma 6.4), and the criterion for adding a single element (Proposition 6.1).
Lemma 6.3

In a modular lattice, if $a \wedge b = 0$ and $(a \vee b) \wedge c = 0$ then $a \wedge (b \vee c) = 0$.

This is exactly Exercise 6.1 (worked solution below). The identity says: if $a$ is disjoint from $b$, and $a \vee b$ is disjoint from $c$, then $a$ is disjoint from $b \vee c$. Modularity is essential — the result fails in non-modular lattices. It lets us re-associate the meets in independence checks.

Lemma 6.4

If $M, K$ are independent subsets in a modular lattice $L$, and if $M \cap K = \emptyset$ and $(\bigoplus M) \wedge (\bigoplus K) = 0$, then $M \cup K$ is independent.

Why

For $a \in M$, we show $a \wedge \bigvee((M \cup K) - a) = a \wedge (\bigvee(M-a) \vee \bigvee K) = 0$. Independence of $M$: $a \wedge \bigvee(M-a) = 0$. By hypothesis: $(a \vee \bigvee(M-a)) \wedge \bigvee K = (\bigvee M) \wedge \bigvee K = 0$. By Lemma 6.3, $a \wedge (\bigvee(M-a) \vee \bigvee K) = 0$. Symmetrically for $b \in K$.

Lemma 6.4 lets you glue together independent sets: if $M$ and $K$ are each independent and their joins are disjoint, the union is independent.

Proposition 6.1

Let $A \subseteq L \setminus \{0\}$ be an independent subset of a modular lattice $L$ and $a \in L \setminus \{0\}$. Then $A \cup \{a\}$ is independent if and only if $a \wedge (\bigvee A) = 0$.

Read: to extend an independent set $A$ by a single element $a$, the only thing to check is $a \wedge \bigvee A = 0$. The other independence conditions ($x \wedge \bigvee((A \cup \{a\}) - x) = 0$ for $x \in A$) follow automatically from $A$'s independence + modularity.

Why

The forward direction is trivial. For the reverse, assume $A$ independent and $a \wedge \bigvee A = 0$. Take $x \in A$, set $A - x = A \setminus \{x\}$. By independence of $A$: $x \wedge \bigvee(A - x) = 0$. Combined with $a \wedge \bigvee A = 0$ and modularity, derive $x \wedge (a \vee \bigvee(A - x)) = 0$. The book's argument: $$x \wedge (a \vee \bigvee(A - x)) = (x \wedge \bigvee A) \wedge (a \vee \bigvee(A - x)) = x \wedge \bigvee(A - x) = 0$$ using modularity and $x \le \bigvee A$.

SECTION A4 Theorem 6.1 — maximal independent ⟺ join essential

The chapter's first major theorem. An independent set is maximal iff its join is essential. This couples independence to Chapter 4's essentialness apparatus.
Theorem 6.1

Let $M$ be an independent subset in a modular lattice $L$. Then $M$ is maximal independent if and only if $\bigvee M$ is essential in $L$.

Why · two directions

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $\bigvee M$ is not essential in $L$. There is $x \in L \setminus \{0\}$ such that $x \wedge \bigvee M = 0$ (and so $x \notin M$). By Proposition 6.1, $M \cup \{x\}$ is independent and strictly extends $M$. So $M$ is not maximal.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $A \supsetneq M$ is independent, take $b \in A \setminus M$. Independence of $A$: $b \wedge \bigvee(A - b) = 0$. In particular $b \wedge \bigvee M = 0$ (since $M \subseteq A - b$). So $\bigvee M$ is not essential.

Why this matters

Maximal independence and essentialness are two faces of the same property: in modular lattices, an independent set is "as large as possible" exactly when its join touches everything. This is the bridge between Chapter 4's apparatus (essentialness) and Chapter 6's apparatus (independence).

The theorem also gives a constructive criterion: to find a maximal independent set, build one with essential join. The Goldie dimension theory (Part C) builds on this: uniform elements give the "atomic" pieces of essential joins, and the count of uniforms in a maximal independent set is the Goldie dimension.

SECTION A5 Proposition 6.2 + Lemma 6.5 — nesting and the structural lemma

Proposition 6.2 says nested independent sets glue cleanly into a larger independent set. Lemma 6.5 is a technical structural fact about how independence interacts with the diamond isomorphism.
Proposition 6.2

If $A$ is an independent subset of a complete modular lattice $L$ and, for each $a \in A$, $X_a$ is an independent subset of $L$ such that $\bigvee X_a \le a$, then the set-theoretic union $\bigcup_{a \in A} X_a$ is independent.

Read: take an independent set $A$ where each element $a$ has been "exploded" into a smaller independent set $X_a$ below it. The resulting union (of all the small pieces) is independent. Plain English: independence is hereditarily preserved when each element is replaced by an independent decomposition of itself.

Why

Take $a \in A$ and $x_a \in X_a$. We show $x_a$ has trivial meet with the join of all other elements: $$x_a \wedge \Bigl(\bigvee(X_a - x_a) \vee \bigvee_{a' \in A - a} \bigvee X_{a'}\Bigr) = 0.$$ Each $X_{a'}$ has $\bigvee X_{a'} \le a'$, and $A$ independent ⟹ $a \wedge \bigvee_{a' \ne a} a' = 0$. So $x_a \le a$ has trivial meet with $\bigvee_{a' \ne a} \bigvee X_{a'} \le \bigvee_{a' \ne a} a'$. Apply Lemma 6.3 with the independence of $X_a$.

Lemma 6.5

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a finite subset of a complete modular lattice $L$ and $b \le \bigvee_{i \in I} a_i$. Set $\bar a_i = \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j$. Then $b \le \bigvee_i (a_i \wedge (b \vee \bar a_i))$. Moreover, if the subset is independent, $b \le \bigoplus_i (a_i \wedge (b \vee \bar a_i))$ and for every $i$, the quotient sublattices $$\frac{a_i \wedge (b \vee \bar a_i)}{0} \cong \frac{b}{b \wedge \bar a_i}$$ are isomorphic.

This is a technical structural lemma — the proof goes by induction on $|I|$ and uses modularity twice at each step. It establishes that any element $b$ below a finite direct sum decomposes (in the independent case) as a direct sum of "shadows" $a_i \wedge (b \vee \bar a_i)$. These shadows are isomorphic to $b/(b \wedge \bar a_i)$ via the diamond isomorphism.

Why this matters

Lemma 6.5 is the structural backbone of the next section's results on the socle. It says: a single element $b$ below an independent direct sum can be "spread out" into pieces, one in each summand, and these pieces are the diamond-iso images of $b$ modulo each "complement" $\bar a_i$. This is how socles and Goldie dimensions get computed: take the ambient direct sum decomposition, project the socle into each component.

SECTION A6 Socle as a direct sum of atoms — Propositions 6.3, 6.4, 6.5 + Theorem 6.2

A sequence of refinements: the socle of a finite direct sum decomposes; same for inductive lattices; maximal independent atoms give the socle; in any inductive lattice, the socle is a direct sum of atoms.
Proposition 6.3

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a finite independent subset of a modular lattice $L$. Then $$s\bigl((\bigoplus_{i \in I} a_i)/0\bigr) = \bigoplus_{i \in I} s(a_i/0).$$

The socle of a direct sum equals the direct sum of socles. The proof uses the Lemma 6.5 structural decomposition: any atom of the direct sum decomposes into atoms in each summand, so the socle distributes.

Proposition 6.4

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be an independent subset of an inductive modular lattice $L$. Then $$s\bigl((\bigoplus_{i \in I} a_i)/0\bigr) = \bigoplus_{i \in I} s(a_i/0).$$

Same statement extended to infinite (= arbitrary cardinality) independent sets in inductive lattices. The proof reduces to the finite case via Lemma 6.1 (independence is local) plus the compactness of atoms in inductive settings.

Proposition 6.5

If $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ is a maximal independent subset of atoms in a modular lattice $L$ then $s(L) = \bigoplus_{i \in I} a_i$.

Among atoms, "maximal independent" gives you the entire socle. Combined with Theorem 6.1, $\bigoplus a_i$ is essential in $L$, so the socle is "as big as it can essentially be."

Theorem 6.2

In every inductive lattice, $s(L)$ is a direct sum of atoms.

Why · trivial cases + Zorn

If $L$ has no atoms: $s(L) = 0 = \bigoplus_\emptyset$ (empty direct sum). Otherwise, $L$ has atoms; by Exercise 6.4 (totally ordered union of independent atom-sets is independent — uses inductive condition (B)) and Zorn's Lemma, any atom is contained in a maximal independent set of atoms. By Proposition 6.5, $s(L)$ is the direct sum of these maximal-independent atoms.

The structural picture

In any inductive lattice (which includes all submodule lattices), the socle has a clean decomposition: it's a direct sum of atoms. So the socle is "structurally semisimple" — it decomposes into a direct sum of simple pieces. This is the lattice version of "$\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is semisimple" in module theory.

SECTION A7 Lemma 6.6 — essential direct sums from per-summand essential extensions

If you take an independent decomposition $b_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus b_n$ and replace each $b_i$ by an essential sub-extension $a_i$, the resulting direct sum stays essential.
Lemma 6.6

Let $a_i \le b_i$ ($i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$) with $a_i$ essential in $b_i/0$, be elements in a modular lattice $L$. If $\{b_i\}_{i=1}^n$ is an independent subset in $L$, then $\bigoplus_{i=1}^n a_i$ is also essential in $(\bigoplus_{i=1}^n b_i)/0$.

Plain English: essentialness "lifts through direct sums." If each $a_i$ is essential in its $b_i$ component, the direct sum $\oplus a_i$ is essential in the direct sum $\oplus b_i$.

Why · inductive on $n$

Sufficient to prove $n = 2$ case. Take $a, b, c, d$ with $a \le b$, $c \le d$, $b \wedge d = 0$, $a$ essential in $b/0$, $c$ essential in $d/0$. Show $a \vee c$ essential in $(b \vee d)/0$.

Take any $0 \ne x \le b \vee d$ in $L$. Want $(a \vee c) \wedge x \ne 0$. Use modularity to project $x$ into $b$ and $d$ via $b \wedge x$ and $d \wedge x$; use essentialness in each component; combine.

The book's argument extends to arbitrary independent subsets in upper continuous modular lattices (Exercise 6.10).

Lemma 6.6 is a load-bearing technical tool for Theorem 6.4 and the dimension theory: it guarantees that essentialness is preserved under direct-sum constructions, which is what lets us split essentialness questions into per-summand questions.

SECTION A8 Completely irreducible elements — Lemmas 6.7 and 6.8

Recap of "completely irreducible" from Chapter 3 (every meet decomposition forces equality with one factor). In compactly generated lattices, every "$a \nleq b$" has a witnessing completely irreducible element.

Recall (from Chapter 3): an element $a \in L$ is irreducible if for every $b, c \in L$, $a = b \wedge c$ implies $a = b$ or $a = c$. Completely irreducible: $a = \bigwedge T$ implies $a \in T$, using arbitrary meets (not just finite). A decomposition of $a$ is a meet $a = \bigwedge Q$ of completely irreducibles.

Lemma 6.7

If $a, b$ are two elements in a compactly generated lattice $L$ and $a \nleq b$, then there is a completely irreducible element $q \in L$ such that $b \le q$ and $a \nleq q$.

Read: completely irreducible elements "separate" non-comparable pairs. Whenever $a \nleq b$, we can find a completely irreducible $q$ that is "above $b$" but "not above $a$." This is the lattice version of the separation property in Stone duality (clopen sets separate points).

Why · Zorn argument

Apply Zorn's Lemma to the set $\mathcal Q = \{x \in L : b \le x, a \nleq x\}$. Chains in $\mathcal Q$ have upper bounds (joins) inside $\mathcal Q$ — the directedness uses that $a \nleq \bigvee$ chain because compactly generated $a$ has compact pieces below it that must lie in some chain element. Maximal element $q$ in $\mathcal Q$ is shown to be completely irreducible: if $q = \bigwedge T$ but $q \notin T$, then each $t \in T$ properly extends $q$, and by maximality $a \le t$ for each $t$, contradicting $a \nleq q = \bigwedge T$.

Corollary 6.1

Every element of a compactly generated lattice has a decomposition.

Take any $a \in L$. By Lemma 6.7 applied to each $a' \nleq a$ (no specific $b$, just take $b = a$ for separator), the set $\{q : q$ completely irreducible, $a \le q\}$ has $a$ as its meet (Zorn-style). Hence $a$ has a decomposition.

Lemma 6.8

If $L$ is a modular compactly generated atomic lattice with $0$, $A$ is an independent set of atoms, and $\{U_i\}_{i \in I}$ is a family of subsets of $A$ which has an empty set-theoretic intersection, then $\bigwedge_{i \in I} (\bigvee U_i) = 0$.

Read: in well-behaved lattices, when subsets have empty intersection, the meet of their joins is also $0$. This is a "finite-witness" property at the level of the Boolean algebra of subsets: empty intersection ⟹ meet of joins = $0$.

SECTION A9 Theorem 6.3 — irredundant decomposition with atomic upper interval

An existence theorem: if the upper interval $1/a$ is atomic, then $a$ has an irredundant decomposition into completely irreducibles. Combines the apparatus of Lemmas 6.7 and 6.8.
Theorem 6.3

If $a$ is an element in a modular compactly generated lattice $L$ and the quotient sublattice $1/a$ is atomic, then $a$ has an irredundant decomposition as a meet of completely irreducible elements of $L$.

Why · constructive

Let $s(1/a)$ be the socle of the upper interval $1/a$ (= join of atoms in $1/a$) and $M$ a maximal independent set of atoms in $1/a$. For each $x \in M$, set $m_x = \bigvee(M - x)$. Clearly $m_x \prec s(1/a)$ in $1/a$ (it's missing exactly one atom).

For each $x \in M$, by Lemma 6.7 applied with $b = m_x$ and $a$ replaced by $s(1/a)$ (with the right setup), we find a completely irreducible $q_x$ with $s(1/a) \le q_x$ and $m_x \nleq q_x$. Hence $q_x \wedge s(1/a) = m_x$ (using maximality of $m_x$ as "almost-everything-below-$s(1/a)$").

Show $a = \bigwedge_{x \in M} q_x$: the inequality $a \le \bigwedge q_x$ is clear (each $q_x \ge a$ since $b = m_x \ge a$). For the other direction, suppose $\bigwedge q_x > a$. Then there's some atom $b \in 1/a$ with $a \prec b \le \bigwedge q_x$. Apply Lemma 6.8 with the family $\{$$M-x \cup \{b\}\}$ to derive a contradiction.

Irredundance: by construction, removing any $q_x$ from the meet would lose the "atom $x$" piece.

The reading

Theorem 6.3 says: in modular compactly generated lattices with "$1/a$ atomic" (a kind of finiteness condition), the element $a$ has a uniquely-shaped irredundant meet decomposition into completely irreducibles. This sets up the next part — Krull-Schmidt's uniqueness of indecomposable decomposition.

The hypothesis "$1/a$ atomic" is automatically true when $L$ is artinian (every interval has atoms), so finite-length lattices satisfy it. For more general settings, the hypothesis is restrictive but useful.

Part B · Variant notions of independence

Three variants from the literature

Călugăreanu's notion of "independent" is the standard one in this book. The literature has three variants — weakly independent, join independent, Stanley independence — that capture related but distinct ideas. They tend to agree in well-behaved lattices.

SECTION B1 Weakly independent — Lemma 6.9 and Proposition 6.6

A definition shape that looks identical to "independent" but is sometimes used to highlight a single-condition check. In upper continuous modular lattices the two notions coincide; without modularity, weakly independent can be strictly weaker.
Definition · weakly independent

In [15], a collection $S$ of non-zero elements in a lattice is called weakly independent if $a \wedge \bigvee(S - a) = 0$ holds for each $a \in S$.

This is the same equation as "independent" in Section A1. Why a different name? The reference [15] (Crawley-Dilworth, Algebraic theory of lattices) uses "weakly independent" to emphasize that this is a local condition checked only at one element at a time. The full "independent" condition (in some other texts) demands more — see "Stanley independence" below.

Lemma 6.9

In an upper continuous modular lattice each weakly independent set is independent.

The shape of the definition is the same; in upper continuous modular lattices, the local condition is enough. Lemma 6.1 (independence reduces to finite subsets) closes the gap.

Proposition 6.6

In an upper continuous lattice which satisfies the lower covering condition, each weakly independent set of atoms is independent.

For sets of atoms, modularity can be weakened to lower covering condition (LCC, also called "semimodularity"). The proof uses the fact that atoms have a particularly clean covering structure in semimodular lattices.

SECTION B2 Join independent — Lemma 6.10 and Proposition 6.7

Stenström's convention from references [18] and [34]. Same idea, defined via finite subsets to avoid upper continuity in the basic results.

The book introduces this in response to a methodological need: in [18] and [34], a way of avoiding the traditional intermixture of upper continuity (but not modularity) in elementary results about independence is given as follows.

Definition · join independent

A finite subset $S \subseteq L$ of non-zero elements is called join independent if $a \wedge \bigvee(S - a) = 0$ holds for each $a \in S$. An arbitrary subset of $L \setminus \{0\}$ is called join independent if all its finite subsets are join independent.

The shape: same as Călugăreanu's "independent" (Section A1) but with explicit finiteness in the arbitrary case. Lemma 6.1 (in upper continuous lattices, independence reduces to finite subsets) makes the two notions equivalent in upper continuous settings — but join independent is stricter without that hypothesis.

Lemma 6.10

Let $A \subseteq L \setminus \{0\}$ be a join independent subset in a modular lattice $L$. If $B, C$ are finite subsets of $A$ and $B \cap C = \emptyset$, then $(\bigvee B) \wedge (\bigvee C) = 0$.

Proposition 6.7

Let $A \subseteq L \setminus \{0\}$ be a join independent subset in a modular lattice $L$. Let $a \in L \setminus \{0\}$ be such that $a \wedge \bigvee B = 0$ holds for every finite subset $B$ of $A$. Then $A \cup \{a\}$ is a join independent subset of $L$.

Both Lemma 6.10 and Proposition 6.7 are the join-independent analogues of Lemmas 6.4 and Proposition 6.1. As a direct corollary, Lemma 6.6 (essential direct sums lift) is provable for join-independent sets without upper continuity — making the apparatus more general.

Why this matters

The book's reason for introducing "join independent": when proving dimension theory results (Theorem 6.4), you want to avoid assuming upper continuity. The join independent definition encodes the "finite version" of independence directly, so the proofs go through without the upper continuity hypothesis.

This is why both notions appear in the chapter. Călugăreanu's "independent" is what's used in Sections A1-A8; "join independent" is what's used for Theorem 6.4 (Goldie dimension equivalences) when general modular lattices (not necessarily upper continuous) are in scope.

Stanley's notion (in [21])

In [21], a subset $I$ of $L \setminus \{0\}$ is called independent if for any two finite subsets $X, Y$ of $I$ we have $(\bigvee X) \wedge (\bigvee Y) = \bigvee(X \cap Y)$.

Stanley's notion is stronger than Călugăreanu's: it demands a precise distributive identity (meet of joins = join of intersections) for any pair of finite subsets. This works well in distributive lattices and matroid theory; it's the right notion when you want the lattice's joins and intersections to behave like a Boolean algebra. Stanley's notion implies Călugăreanu's; the converse fails in non-distributive lattices.

Part C · Goldie dimension

The dimension invariant for non-noetherian module theory

The chapter's punchline. A four-way equivalence (Theorem 6.4) defining what it means to have "finite Goldie dimension" — the right generalisation of vector-space dimension and module rank.

SECTION C1 Lemma 6.11 + Theorem 6.4 — finite Goldie dimension

Defines uniform elements (the "atoms" of dimension theory), then gives a four-way equivalence for finite Goldie dimension.
Lemma 6.11

Let $L$ be a modular lattice which contains no infinite join independent subsets. Then for every element $a \in L \setminus \{0\}$ there exists a uniform element $b \in L$ such that $b \le a$.

(Recall: $b$ is uniform if $b/0$ has no two non-zero independent elements — equivalently, every non-zero $c \le b$ is essential in $b/0$.)

The lemma says: if the lattice has finite-flavoured independence (no infinite independent sets), then below every non-zero element there's a uniform element. This is the existence step that makes the Goldie dimension definition work.

Theorem 6.4

The following conditions are equivalent for a modular lattice with $0$ and $1$:

(a) $L$ does not contain infinite join independent subsets;

(b) $L$ contains a finite join independent subset $\{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n\}$ of uniform elements such that $\bigvee_{i=1}^n a_i$ is essential in $L$;

(c) The cardinality of the join independent subsets of $L$ is bounded by a non-negative integer $m$;

(d) If $a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots$ is an ascending chain of elements in $L$, then there is an $i \ge 0$ such that $a_i$ is essential in $a_j/0$ for each $j \ge i$.

Moreover, if $L$ contains a finite join independent subset of uniform elements $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ such that $\bigvee a_i$ is essential in $L$, then any other join independent subset of $L$ has cardinality less or equal to $n$.

The four conditions are equivalent. The "moreover" part says the integer $n$ is unique — this is what defines the Goldie dimension (= uniform dimension).

Definition · Goldie (uniform) dimension

For a modular lattice $L$, either there is a finite join independent subset of uniform elements $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ such that $\bigvee a_i$ is essential in $L$ — in which case we say the uniform (Goldie) dimension of $L$ is $n$ — or $L$ contains infinite join independent subsets, in which case we say $L$ has infinite uniform dimension. The uniform dimension of $L$ is zero if and only if the lattice has only one element (i.e. $L = \{0\}$).

The picture

The Goldie dimension counts the "indecomposable essential pieces" of $L$. Conditions (a)-(d) of Theorem 6.4 are different lenses on the same property:
(a) No infinite independent subsets — direct cardinality bound.
(b) Existence of a finite essential decomposition into uniforms — explicit witness.
(c) Bounded cardinality — quantitative bound.
(d) Stabilisation of essentialness in chains — chain condition flavoured.
All four are equivalent in modular lattices; (b) gives the constructive characterisation; the integer $n$ in (b) is the Goldie dimension.

Comparison with classical dimension

  • Vector space. $V$ has Goldie dimension = $\dim V$ (every $1$-dimensional subspace is uniform; $V$ is a finite direct sum of $1$-dimensional subspaces with essential join).
  • Noetherian module. $M$ has finite Goldie dimension equal to the number of indecomposable summands in its injective hull.
  • $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Q$. Goldie dimension = 1 ($\mathbb Q$ is uniform — every non-zero submodule is essential).
  • $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z \oplus \mathbb Z$. Goldie dimension = 2.
  • Cogenerator-style modules. Some modules have infinite Goldie dimension (e.g. countable direct sums of distinct uniforms).
Part D · Indecomposable direct decompositions (Krull–Schmidt)

The lattice form of the Krull–Schmidt theorem

Existence (Proposition 6.8) and uniqueness (Theorem 6.5 of Ore) of indecomposable decompositions in finite-length modular lattices.

SECTION D1 Proposition 6.8 — existence of indecomposable decomposition

Every finite-length element of a modular lattice is a finite direct sum of indecomposable elements.
Proposition 6.8

Let $L$ be a modular, artinian or noetherian lattice with $0$ and $1$. Each element in $L$ is a finite direct sum of indecomposable elements (i.e., every finite length element is a finite direct sum of indecomposable elements).

Why · contradiction with chain condition

Suppose $a$ is not a direct sum of indecomposables. Then $a$ is decomposable: $a = a_1 \oplus b_1$ with $a_1, b_1 \ne 0$. Either $a_1$ is decomposable (so $a_1 = a_2 \oplus b_2$) or not. Iterating, we get an increasing sequence $b_1 < b_1 \vee b_2 < \cdots$ (a strictly increasing chain) and a decreasing sequence $a > a_1 > a_2 > \cdots$. Both contradict the chain condition (noetherian / artinian).

The lemma's content: chain conditions force termination of the decomposition process. In finite-length modular lattices, every element decomposes into finitely many indecomposables.

SECTION D2 Theorem 6.5 (Ore) — Krull–Schmidt for lattices

In finite-length modular lattices, indecomposable decompositions are unique up to permutation and "projective in pairs." The lattice form of Krull–Schmidt.
Definition · projective in pairs

The elements $a, b \in L$ are called projective in pairs if the principal ideals $a/0$ and $b/0$ are projective sublattices (Chapter 3's projectivity, applied to principal ideals).

Theorem 6.5 (Ore)

Let $L$ be a modular lattice of finite length. If $a = \bigvee_{i=1}^m a_i = \bigvee_{j=1}^n b_j$ are direct indecomposable decompositions, then $n = m$ and the $a_i$ and $b_j$ are projective in pairs.

Why · induction on length

Sufficient to prove (Birkhoff): for each $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$, there exists $j \in \{1, \ldots, m\}$ such that $a = a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_{i-1} \oplus b_j \oplus a_{i+1} \oplus \cdots \oplus a_m$. (If each $a_i$ is replaceable by some $b_j$ then $m \le n$; symmetrically $n \le m$.)

The proof distinguishes three cases based on length manipulations: Case 1 ($a_i \vee \bar b_j = a$ and $\bar a_i \vee b_j = a$ for some $j$ — direct replacement), Case 2 (no direct replacement, use complement-style argument), Case 3 (rules out impossible configurations). Modularity at each step. The book's proof (pp. 69-71) walks through it carefully.

Once replaceability is established, the projectivity-in-pairs follows: when $a_i$ is replaced by $b_j$, the principal ideals $a_i/0$ and $b_j/0$ inherit a projective relationship from the diamond isomorphisms used in the replacement step.

Why this matters

Theorem 6.5 is the Krull–Schmidt theorem in lattice form. It says: in finite-length modular lattices, the indecomposable pieces of an element are unique up to permutation and lattice isomorphism (since in modular lattices, projective in pairs ⟹ isomorphic principal ideals).

The classical Krull–Schmidt for finite groups, finite-dimensional modules, and abelian categories all follow from Theorem 6.5 applied to the relevant lattice (subgroup, submodule, subobject lattice). The lattice version is more general — works in any modular lattice with finite length.

Notes: the requirement "finite length" is essential. Without it, Krull–Schmidt fails — there exist abelian groups with non-isomorphic decompositions into indecomposables. The classical example: $\bigoplus_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb Z$ admits "rearrangements" giving different indecomposable structures.

Part E · Semiatomic lattices

"$1$ is a join of atoms"

A clean characterisation of complemented compactly generated lattices via "semiatomic." Theorems 6.6, 6.7, 6.8 give a 4-way equivalence; the rest is corollaries.

SECTION E1 Definition + Theorem 6.6 — semiatomic ⟹ complemented in modular UC

A complete lattice is "semiatomic" if $1$ is a join of atoms. Theorem 6.6: in modular upper continuous lattices, semiatomic ⟹ complemented.
Definition · semiatomic lattice

A complete lattice $L$ is called semiatomic if $1$ is a join of atoms in $L$.

So: semiatomic = "$1 = s(L)$" (the socle equals the top). This is one step weaker than "atom generated" (where every element is a join of atoms): semiatomic just demands that the top is a join of atoms; atomistic demands every element is.

Theorem 6.6

Each modular upper continuous semiatomic lattice is complemented.

Why · Zorn argument

Let $1 \ne a \in L$. Consider the set $\mathcal P = \{J \subseteq I : \{s_i\}_{i \in J}$ independent and $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in J} s_i = 0\}$, where $\{s_i\}_{i \in I}$ are all the atoms of $L$. By upper continuity (Lemma 6.1), unions of chains in $\mathcal P$ remain in $\mathcal P$. Apply Zorn's Lemma: there is a maximal $J$. Set $c = \bigvee_{i \in J} s_i$.

Then $a \wedge c = 0$. Show $a \vee c = 1$: by semiatomicity, every atom of $L$ is below $1$. We show every atom $t$ is below $a \vee c$. Suppose not. Then $t \wedge (a \vee c) = 0$ (atom property). By modularity, $a \wedge (c \vee t) = (a \vee c) \wedge t \wedge c = 0$. But this contradicts maximality of $J$ (we could extend $J$ by adding $t$). Hence every atom is below $a \vee c$, and by semiatomicity $1 = $ join of atoms $\le a \vee c$, so $a \vee c = 1$.

So $c$ is a complement of $a$. $L$ is complemented.

SECTION E2 Corollaries 6.2 and 6.3 — independent atoms with full join, semiatomic intervals

Two immediate consequences. Cor 6.2: there's a maximal independent set of atoms with $\bigvee = 1$. Cor 6.3: in modular UC semiatomic, intervals stay semiatomic.
Corollary 6.2

If $L$ is a modular upper continuous semiatomic lattice, there is an independent subset of atoms $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ such that $\bigvee_{i \in I} a_i = 1$.

Take $a = 0$ in the proof of Theorem 6.6. The maximal independent atoms then satisfy $\bigvee = 1$.

Corollary 6.3

Let $a$ be an element in a modular upper continuous semiatomic lattice $L$. Then the quotient sublattices $a/0$ and $1/a$ are also semiatomic.

Why

For $1/a$: by semiatomicity of $L$, $1 = \bigvee \{$atoms$\}$. For each atom $s$, $s \vee a = a$ or $s \vee a$ is an atom in $1/a$ (using modularity: $(s \vee a)/a \cong s/(s \wedge a) = s/0$, a 2-element chain or $\{s\}$ depending on whether $s \le a$). So $1$ is a join of $a$ + atoms in $1/a$, hence $1$ is a join of atoms in $1/a$ (the join with $a$ is just $1$ since $a$ is the bottom of $1/a$).

For $a/0$: by Theorem 6.6, $a$ has a complement $c$ in $L$. By modularity, $a/0 \cong (a \vee c)/c = 1/c$. The first part proves $1/c$ semiatomic, so $a/0$ is too.

The book also notes: in modular upper continuous semiatomic lattices, every element is a join of atoms (atom generated, also called atomistic). And: in modular compactly generated lattices, $L$ is complemented iff atom generated. (Exercise 6.15.)

SECTION E3 Theorem 6.7 + Corollaries 6.4, 6.5 — complemented ⟹ semiatomic in modular CG

The reverse direction: in modular compactly generated complemented lattices, $L$ is semiatomic. Plus two clean corollaries.
Theorem 6.7

Each modular compactly generated complemented lattice is semiatomic.

Why

For an arbitrary non-zero $a \in L$ we must find an atom in $a/0$. The lattice being compactly generated, it suffices to prove this property for compact elements. If $a/0$ is compact, by Lemma 2.4 there exists a maximal $m \ne a$, $m \in a/0$. If $m'$ is a complement of $m$ in $L$, then by modularity $a \wedge m'$ is a (relative) complement of $m$ in $a/0$. By Exercise 1.37, $a \wedge m'$ is an atom in $a/0$.

Finally, if $s(L) \ne 1$ let $v \ne 0$ be a complement of $s(L)$ in $L$. By the previous argument, $v/0$ contains an atom, and this contradicts $s(L) \wedge v = 0$. Hence $s(L) = 1$, that is, $L$ is semiatomic.

Corollary 6.4

A modular compactly generated lattice is semiatomic if and only if it has no proper essential elements.

Why

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $L$ is semiatomic, by Theorem 6.6 (and Theorem 6.7's converse) $L$ is complemented. Take any essential $a \in L$; let $b$ be a complement: $a \wedge b = 0$, $a \vee b = 1$. Essentialness of $a$ + $a \wedge b = 0$ gives $b = 0$, hence $a = 1$. So the only essential element is $1$ — no proper essentials.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, each pseudo-complement $b$ of $a$ in $L$ has $a \vee b$ essential (Cor 4.2). By hypothesis, $a \vee b = 1$. So every pseudo-complement is a complement, $L$ is complemented, and by Theorem 6.7 semiatomic.

Corollary 6.5

Each modular compactly generated complemented lattice is atomic.

From Theorem 6.7's proof: every non-zero element contains an atom. So $L$ is atomic.

SECTION E4 Theorem 6.8 — the four-way equivalence

A clean conjunction of the chapter's main results: four conditions on a modular lattice are all equivalent. The chapter's central characterisation theorem.
Theorem 6.8

For a modular lattice the following conditions are equivalent:

(i) $L$ is compactly generated and semiatomic;

(ii) $L$ is compactly generated and complemented;

(iii) $L$ is upper continuous and semiatomic;

(iv) $L$ is upper continuous, atomic, and complemented.

Why

(i) ⟺ (ii): by Theorems 6.6, 6.7 (since semiatomic + modular UC ⟹ complemented, and complemented + modular CG ⟹ semiatomic).

(iii) ⟹ (i): in upper continuous lattices, each atom is compact (Prop 2.2), and atom generated lattices are compactly generated (since every element is a join of atoms, all compact).

(iv) ⟹ (iii): by Theorem 6.6 applied to $a = 0$: there are independent atoms with $\bigvee = 1$, so $1$ is a join of atoms.

The other directions follow.

Corollary 6.6

Every modular compactly generated (or upper continuous) semiatomic lattice is atomic.

The structural picture

Theorem 6.8 says: in modular lattices, four "well-behaved" conditions all describe the same class. The class is "modular lattice that arises from semisimple-style algebra" — vector space subspace lattices, semisimple module submodule lattices, etc. This is the canonical class for which Chapter 7's "semisimple lattice theory" applies.

SECTION E5 Lemma 6.12 + Proposition 6.9 + Corollaries 6.7, 6.8

The chapter's wrap-up: every $b \ne 1$ is in a maximal element (Lemma 6.12); four characterisations of "join of atoms" (Proposition 6.9); finite-flavoured consequences (Corollaries 6.7, 6.8).
Lemma 6.12

In a modular upper continuous semiatomic lattice, every element $\ne 1$ is contained in a maximal element.

The Krull-style "every proper element lies in a maximal" — applied in the semiatomic setting. Proof: by Theorem 6.8, $L$ is complemented. For $b \ne 1$, $b \oplus c = 1$ for some $c$. By atomicity, $c/0$ has an atom $a$. By modularity, $L$ has a relative complement of $a$ above $b$ (call it $m$); by Exercise 1.37, $m$ is maximal. And $b \le m$.

Proposition 6.9

In a modular compactly generated lattice $L$, for an element $a \in L$, the following conditions are equivalent:

(i) $a$ is a join of atoms;

(ii) $a$ is the join of all the atoms included in $a/0$;

(iii) $a$ is a join of an independent subset of atoms;

(iv) $a/0$ is complemented.

The proof is left to the reader in the book. (i) ⟺ (ii): trivial — joining atoms in $a/0$ is the same as joining atoms below $a$. (i) ⟹ (iii): use Zorn to extract a maximal independent subset. (iii) ⟹ (i): trivially. (iv) ⟹ (i): by Theorem 6.8 applied to $a/0$ (which is also compactly generated as a downward-closed sublattice).

Corollary 6.7

An artinian lattice has no infinite independent subsets.

An infinite independent subset would give an infinite strictly decreasing sequence (each "drop" of one element gives a strictly smaller direct sum), contradicting DCC.

Corollary 6.8

The socle of an artinian upper continuous lattice is compact.

By Theorem 6.2, the socle is a direct sum of atoms. By Cor 6.7 (artinian), the direct sum is finite. Atoms in upper continuous lattices are compact (Prop 2.2). Finite joins of compacts are compact (Prop 2.1). Hence the socle is compact.

The chapter's wrap

The closing two corollaries tie back to Chapter 5: in artinian upper continuous lattices, the socle has the cleanest possible structure (finite direct sum of atoms, compact). The chapter's apparatus combines essentialness (Ch 4), socle (Ch 5), and independence (this chapter) into a unified picture, ready for Chapters 7-9 to build dimension-style theorems.

CONNECTION P1 Module theory — Goldie dimension and uniform modules

Where the chapter's apparatus came from. Goldie dimension is one of the most useful invariants in modern module theory.
Goldie dimension and the Goldie theorem

Goldie dimension of a module. For a module $M$ over a ring $R$, the Goldie dimension (also called uniform dimension) of $M$ is the supremum of $n$ such that $M$ contains a direct sum of $n$ non-zero uniform submodules. A uniform module has every non-zero submodule essential — i.e., no internal direct-sum decomposition.

Equivalent characterisations (translating Theorem 6.4 to module theory):
• $M$ has finite Goldie dimension iff $M$ contains no infinite direct sums of non-zero submodules.
• $M$ has Goldie dimension $n$ iff the injective hull $E(M)$ is a direct sum of $n$ indecomposable injective modules.
• $M$ has Goldie dimension $n$ iff $M$ contains an essential submodule that is a direct sum of $n$ uniforms.

Goldie's theorem (1958, 1960). A ring $R$ is right Goldie if $R_R$ has finite Goldie dimension AND ACC on right annihilator ideals. Goldie's theorem: a semiprime ring is right Goldie iff it has a classical right ring of fractions which is semisimple Artinian. This was the foundational result for the structure theory of non-commutative semiprime rings.

Concrete computations:

  • $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Z^n$. Goldie dimension $n$. The summands $\mathbb Z e_1, \ldots, \mathbb Z e_n$ are uniform with essential direct sum $\mathbb Z^n$.
  • $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Q$. Goldie dimension 1. $\mathbb Q$ is uniform — every non-zero submodule is essential.
  • $\mathbb Z$-module $\mathbb Q^n$. Goldie dimension $n$.
  • Polynomial ring $\mathbb Z[x]$ as a $\mathbb Z[x]$-module. Goldie dimension 1 ($\mathbb Z[x]$ is integral domain, hence uniform).
Why Goldie dimension matters in modern algebra

Goldie dimension is the right invariant for the structural classification of non-noetherian modules. It plays the role of "rank" in non-noetherian module theory. The dimension is preserved under essential extensions, additive (over direct sums), and behaves well under localisation. Modules with finite Goldie dimension form a particularly tractable class — many structural theorems extend smoothly. The book's apparatus (Chapter 6) is the abstract scaffold supporting this whole theory.

CONNECTION P2 Vector space dimension as a clean special case

In subspace lattices of vector spaces, all the chapter's apparatus reduces to classical dimension theory. A useful sanity check for the abstract development.
The chapter's apparatus, in $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$

For a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ over a field $k$, the lattice $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$ is modular, complemented, atomic, upper continuous, and compactly generated. By Theorem 6.8, it is also semiatomic. Every line ($1$-dimensional subspace) is uniform.

Independence: linearly independent vectors $\{v_i\}$ ⟺ subspaces $\{\langle v_i\rangle\}$ are independent in $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$.

Maximal independent: a basis. By Theorem 6.1, equivalent to "$\bigvee \langle v_i\rangle$ is essential" — but in finite-dimensional $V$, any essential subspace is the whole $V$. So bases are exactly the maximal independent sets of lines.

Goldie dimension: $\dim V$. Every basis is a maximal independent set of $\dim V$ uniforms.

Krull–Schmidt: Theorem 6.5 says any two basis-decompositions $V = \bigoplus \langle v_i\rangle = \bigoplus \langle w_j\rangle$ have the same length and projective-in-pairs (all $1$-dimensional, hence isomorphic).

Semiatomic: $V$ = join of $1$-dimensional subspaces (lines). Atom generated.

The classical apparatus of vector space dimension theory — bases, dimension, direct sums, complement subspaces — is exactly the chapter's apparatus in $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$. The chapter generalises the vector-space picture to arbitrary modular lattices.

What's gained, what's lost

Gained: the apparatus works in non-vector-space settings. Submodule lattices over arbitrary rings, subgroup lattices, lattices of substructures. Goldie dimension where classical rank fails.

Lost: the existence of canonical basis. In a vector space, you can choose a basis; in arbitrary modular lattices, "maximal independent" sets exist (by Zorn) but there's no constructive basis selection. Linear-algebraic computations have no analog.

The trade-off is generality vs. computability. The chapter's apparatus is more general and structural; classical linear algebra is more computational and constructive.

CONNECTION P3 Krull–Schmidt theorem in algebra

The classical theorems in groups and modules; Theorem 6.5 unifies them at the lattice level.
Krull–Schmidt across algebra

Krull–Schmidt for finite groups (1925-29). For a finite group $G$, if $G = G_1 \times \cdots \times G_n = H_1 \times \cdots \times H_m$ are two decompositions into directly indecomposable subgroups, then $n = m$ and after permutation $G_i \cong H_{\sigma(i)}$. Wolfgang Krull and Otto Schmidt independently. Earlier, Robert Remak (1911) proved a special case for finite groups.

Krull–Schmidt for modules. For a finitely generated module $M$ over a noetherian ring $R$, if $M = M_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus M_n = N_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus N_m$ are two decompositions into indecomposable submodules, then $n = m$ and $M_i \cong N_{\sigma(i)}$.

Krull–Schmidt for abelian categories. Atiyah, Bass, Auslander generalised: in any "Krull–Schmidt category" (idempotents split + every object is a finite direct sum of objects with local endomorphism rings), indecomposable decomposition is unique. This includes the category of finitely generated modules over a noetherian ring, finite-dimensional representations, coherent sheaves on a curve, etc.

Theorem 6.5 (Ore 1936) — the lattice version. In any modular lattice of finite length, indecomposable decompositions are unique up to permutation and projective in pairs. Applied to specific lattices, recovers all the above special cases.

Where Krull–Schmidt fails

Krull–Schmidt requires finiteness — either finite length, or finite generation in a noetherian ring. Without it, the theorem fails:

  • Free abelian group $\bigoplus_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb Z$. Has decompositions into indecomposables of cardinality both $\aleph_0$ and... well, any infinite cardinality $\le \aleph_0$. Different "rearrangements" change the indecomposables.
  • Modules over non-noetherian rings. Can have non-isomorphic indecomposable decompositions of the same module.
  • Group algebras of infinite groups. Representations may not have unique indecomposable decompositions.

In each case, the underlying lattice has infinite length — failing the hypothesis of Theorem 6.5.

Auslander–Reiten theory

For finite-dimensional modular representations of finite groups (and more generally, finitely generated modules over a noetherian Krull–Schmidt category), Auslander–Reiten theory (1970s) provides a deep structural picture: indecomposable objects sit in "Auslander–Reiten quivers" with arrows representing certain canonical maps. Krull–Schmidt is the foundational uniqueness theorem on top of which AR theory builds.

CONNECTION P4 Matroids — combinatorial independence

Matroids generalise both linear independence and graph-theoretic independence. They sit alongside lattice independence as parallel abstractions.
Matroid theory

Whitney's matroids (1935). A matroid on a finite set $E$ is a non-empty collection $\mathcal I$ of subsets (the "independent sets") satisfying:
(I1) $\emptyset \in \mathcal I$;
(I2) Hereditary: $A \subseteq B \in \mathcal I \Rightarrow A \in \mathcal I$;
(I3) Exchange: if $A, B \in \mathcal I$ with $|A| < |B|$, then $\exists b \in B \setminus A$ with $A \cup \{b\} \in \mathcal I$.

The exchange property (I3) is the key axiom — it gives matroids a uniform rank function ($r(A) = $ size of any maximal independent subset of $A$) and makes them generalise both vector-space independence (linearly independent vectors) and graph-theoretic independence (forests in graphs).

Geometric lattices. The collection of "flats" (closed sets) of a matroid forms a geometric lattice: a finite atomistic semimodular lattice. Conversely, every geometric lattice is the lattice of flats of some matroid (Birkhoff, 1935). So matroids and geometric lattices are equivalent objects of study.

How this chapter connects. Călugăreanu's "independent" subset is the lattice version of matroid independence. The exchange property (I3) corresponds to Lemma 6.4 + Proposition 6.1 + Lemma 6.5: in modular lattices, you can swap one element of an independent set for an outside element of compatible shape. Goldie dimension corresponds to matroid rank. Atomic modular complemented lattices coincide with the lattices of flats of matroids — and Theorem 6.8's equivalences carry the lattice perspective into matroid territory.

Examples of matroids (and their corresponding lattices):

  • Vector matroid. $E$ = vectors in a vector space; independent = linearly independent. Lattice of flats = subspace lattice.
  • Graphic matroid. $E$ = edges of a graph; independent = forest (no cycles). Lattice of flats = lattice of "graph flats" (closed under cycle membership).
  • Uniform matroid $U_{k,n}$. $E = \{1, \ldots, n\}$; independent = subsets of size $\le k$. The minimal "trivial" matroid.
  • Transversal matroid. Set system + bipartite matching. Independent = subsets that have a transversal.
Standard references

Oxley, Matroid Theory (Oxford 1992) — the standard reference. Welsh, Matroid Theory (Academic Press 1976) — classical. Birkhoff, Lattice Theory (3rd ed. 1967) — §IV.7 has the matroid/geometric lattice correspondence. White (ed.), Matroid Applications (Cambridge 1992) — applications.

Recent developments: Hodge theory for matroids (Adiprasito, Huh, Katz 2018) — log-concavity of matroid invariants — applies algebraic-geometry techniques to combinatorial matroids using deep lattice structure.

$\{a_i\}$ in a complete lattice with $0$, where for each $i$: $a_i \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j = 0$. Lattice version of linear independence.

Same defining equation as "independent" but used to highlight a single-element-at-a-time check. In upper continuous modular lattices, weakly independent = independent (Lemma 6.9).

Stronger notion: for any two finite subsets, $(\bigvee X) \wedge (\bigvee Y) = \bigvee(X \cap Y)$. Distributivity-flavoured.

Ex. 6.1Modular meet identity (= Lemma 6.3)

If $a \wedge b = 0$ and $(a \vee b) \wedge c = 0$ in a modular lattice $L$ then $a \wedge (b \vee c) = 0$.

Solution

Compute using modularity (with $a \le a \vee b$): $$a \wedge (b \vee c) = (a \vee b) \wedge a \wedge (b \vee c)$$ $$= a \wedge ((a \vee b) \wedge (b \vee c))$$ $$\stackrel{\text{mod}}{=} a \wedge (b \vee ((a \vee b) \wedge c)) \quad \text{(modular law: } b \le b \vee c\text{)}$$ $$= a \wedge (b \vee 0) = a \wedge b = 0.$$ The third equality used the modular law on the inner expression $(a \vee b) \wedge (b \vee c)$ with $b$ on both sides, plus the hypothesis $(a \vee b) \wedge c = 0$.

Commentary

This is the workhorse identity for combining independent subsets (Lemma 6.4 + Proposition 6.1). It says: disjointness of $a, b$ plus disjointness of $a \vee b, c$ implies disjointness of $a, b \vee c$. Modularity is essential — without it, the chain of equalities breaks at the modular law step.

Ex. 6.2Finite-subset characterisation of independence

Let $L$ be a modular lattice with $0$. Show that the finite subset $\{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n\}$ of $L$ is independent if and only if $\{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_i\} \wedge a_{i+1} = 0$ for each $i \in \{1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$.

Solution

The notation $\{a_1, \ldots, a_i\} \wedge a_{i+1}$ means $\bigvee_{j=1}^i a_j \wedge a_{i+1}$.

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ is independent then for each $i$: $a_{i+1} \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i+1} a_j = 0$. In particular $a_{i+1} \wedge \bigvee_{j=1}^i a_j = 0$ (a sub-meet of the full one). Done.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Induction on $i$. The hypothesis gives $a_2 \wedge a_1 = 0$. Inductively, suppose $\{a_1, \ldots, a_i\}$ is independent. We show $\{a_1, \ldots, a_{i+1}\}$ is independent.

By Proposition 6.1 (extending an independent set by an element), it suffices to show $a_{i+1} \wedge \bigvee_{j=1}^i a_j = 0$. This is exactly the hypothesis. Done.

Commentary

An efficient inductive characterisation: instead of checking the symmetric condition for each element, just check that each new element has trivial meet with the join of the previous ones. This makes finite independence verifiable in linear time (in the size of the set).

Ex. 6.3Generalisation of Lemma 1.1

In a modular lattice $L$ generalize to a finite set of elements Lemma 1.1.

Solution

Lemma 1.1 (from Chapter 1, not visible in our PDF range) is presumably the modular-law-style identity for two elements. The generalisation to a finite set $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ is: in a modular lattice, $\bigwedge_{i=1}^n (b_i \vee c) = (\bigwedge_{i=1}^n b_i) \vee c$ when $c \le b_i$ for each $i$ (and similar identities involving meets and joins of finite sets).

Specific form likely intended: $\bigwedge_{i=1}^n (a_i \vee b) = (\bigwedge_{i=1}^n a_i) \vee b$ when $b \le \bigwedge a_i$. Or: for $\{a_i\}$ a finite chain $a_1 \le a_2 \le \cdots \le a_n$, $\bigvee_i (a_i \wedge b) = (\bigvee_i a_i) \wedge b$ for $b \ge a_1$.

Proof: induction on $n$, using the binary modular law at each step.

Commentary

The exercise is a "lift" exercise: take a binary identity from Chapter 1 and extend it to finite collections via induction. Standard manipulation, but worth doing once to understand how modular identities scale.

Ex. 6.4Inductive limit of independent sets

In an inductive lattice $L$ show that the union of a totally ordered set of independent sets of elements in $L$ is an independent set of elements of $L$.

Solution

Let $\{S_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ be a totally ordered chain of independent subsets. Set $S = \bigcup S_\alpha$. We show $S$ is independent.

Take $a \in S$ and consider $a \wedge \bigvee(S - a)$. We show this equals $0$.

$\bigvee(S - a) = \bigvee \{b : b \in S, b \ne a\}$. Group by which $S_\alpha$ each $b$ belongs to: $\bigvee(S - a) = \bigvee_\alpha \bigvee(S_\alpha - a)$. The chain of $S_\alpha$ is totally ordered (under inclusion), so $\{\bigvee(S_\alpha - a)\}_\alpha$ is a chain in $L$.

Each $a \wedge \bigvee(S_\alpha - a) = 0$ (independence of $S_\alpha$ + the trivial-meet condition for $a \in S_\alpha$, or vacuously $0$ if $a \notin S_\alpha$).

$L$ is inductive (= condition (B) holds in every interval). Apply condition (B) at the bottom: $a \wedge b_i = 0$ for each $b_i = \bigvee(S_\alpha - a)$ in the chain, so $a \wedge \bigvee_\alpha b_\alpha = 0$.

Commentary

This is the Zorn-supporting fact for "every independent set extends to a maximal one in inductive lattices." Without inductive (= upper-continuity-flavoured), the chain-supremum argument can fail; with it, the union of independent sets stays independent.

Ex. 6.5Distributive-style identity

If $n$ is a positive integer and $(a_1 \vee a_2 \vee \cdots \vee a_n) \wedge b = 0$ holds in a modular lattice $L$, show that $(a_1 \wedge a_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n) \vee b = (a_1 \vee b) \wedge (a_2 \vee b) \wedge \cdots \wedge (a_n \vee b)$.

Solution

The condition $(\bigvee a_i) \wedge b = 0$ says $b$ is "fully clear of the join." Under this, modularity gives a distributive-flavoured identity.

Induction on $n$. For $n = 1$: $(a_1) \vee b = a_1 \vee b$ — trivially equal to $(a_1 \vee b)$. ✓

For $n = 2$: assume $(a_1 \vee a_2) \wedge b = 0$. Show $(a_1 \wedge a_2) \vee b = (a_1 \vee b) \wedge (a_2 \vee b)$.

$\le$: $(a_1 \wedge a_2) \vee b \le a_1 \vee b$ and $(a_1 \wedge a_2) \vee b \le a_2 \vee b$. So $\le$ the meet.

$\ge$: Use modularity $(a_1 \vee b) \wedge (a_2 \vee b)$. Since $(a_1 \vee a_2) \wedge b = 0$, in particular $a_2 \wedge b \le (a_1 \vee a_2) \wedge b = 0$. By modularity (with $b \le a_1 \vee b$): $(a_1 \vee b) \wedge (a_2 \vee b) = b \vee (a_1 \wedge (a_2 \vee b)) = b \vee (a_1 \wedge a_2 \vee (a_1 \wedge b))$ [if $a_1 \wedge b = 0$ this simplifies to $b \vee (a_1 \wedge a_2) = (a_1 \wedge a_2) \vee b$].

Inductive step: similar manipulation using the binary case.

Commentary

The exercise shows that "trivial-meet with $b$" forces a distributive-style behaviour locally. This is the kind of identity that gets used when reasoning about decompositions in modular lattices.

Ex. 6.6Direct summand criterion

If $a \le b \le a \oplus c$ hold in a modular lattice $L$ then $b = a \oplus (b \wedge c)$ (i.e., if $a$ is a direct summand in $x/0$ and $a \le b \le x$ then $a$ is a direct summand in $b/0$).

Solution

$a \le b$ gives $a \vee (b \wedge c) \le b$. Modularity (using $a \le b$): $$a \vee (b \wedge c) = b \wedge (a \vee c) = b \wedge (a \oplus c) \ge b \wedge b = b$$ (since $b \le a \oplus c$). So $a \vee (b \wedge c) = b$.

For independence of $\{a, b \wedge c\}$: $a \wedge (b \wedge c) = (a \wedge b) \wedge c = a \wedge c \le (a \vee \bar a) \wedge c$ — wait, more directly: $a \wedge c \le (a \oplus c)$'s definition gives $a \wedge c = 0$. So $a \wedge (b \wedge c) \le a \wedge c = 0$.

Hence $b = a \oplus (b \wedge c)$.

Commentary

A useful fact about direct summands: they restrict cleanly. If $a$ is a summand of $x$ and $b$ sits between $a$ and $x$, then $a$ is also a summand of $b$ (with the complementary piece being $b \wedge c$, where $c$ is the original complement). Used heavily in Theorem 6.5's proof.

Ex. 6.7Replacement in $1/x$

In a modular lattice $L$, verify that $x \le c$, $b \wedge x = 0$, and $a = (b \vee x) \oplus c$ in $1/x$ imply $a = b \oplus c$ in $L$.

Solution

The hypothesis $a = (b \vee x) \oplus c$ in $1/x$ means: $(b \vee x) \wedge c = x$ (the bottom of $1/x$) and $(b \vee x) \vee c = a$.

Want: $b \wedge c = 0$ and $b \vee c = a$ in $L$.

$b \wedge c$: by modularity, $b \wedge c = b \wedge (b \vee x) \wedge c = b \wedge x = 0$ (using $(b \vee x) \wedge c = x$).

$b \vee c$: clearly $b \vee c \le (b \vee x) \vee c = a$. Conversely, $a = (b \vee x) \vee c = b \vee x \vee c$. Since $x \le c$ (hypothesis), $x \vee c = c$, so $a = b \vee c$.

Hence $a = b \oplus c$ in $L$.

Commentary

The exercise shows that direct-sum decompositions in upper intervals lift cleanly to direct-sum decompositions in $L$, when one of the summands contains $x$.

Ex. 6.8Transposition of independent set to $1/x$

Let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be an independent subset of a modular lattice $L$ and, for a proper subset $J \subsetneq I$, put $x = \bigvee_{i \in J} a_i$. Then $\{a_i \vee x\}_{i \in I - J}$ is independent in $1/x$.

Solution

Take $i \in I - J$. We show $(a_i \vee x) \wedge \bigvee_{j \in I-J, j \ne i} (a_j \vee x) = x$ (the bottom of $1/x$).

By modularity: $\bigvee_{j \in I-J, j \ne i}(a_j \vee x) = (\bigvee_{j \ne i, j \in I-J} a_j) \vee x$.

So $(a_i \vee x) \wedge ((\bigvee_{j \in I-J, j \ne i} a_j) \vee x)$. Apply modularity with $x$ as the common element: $$(a_i \vee x) \wedge ((\bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j) \vee x) = ((a_i \vee x) \wedge (\bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j)) \vee x.$$

The independence of $\{a_i\}$ in $L$ gives $a_i \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j = 0$. Combined with $x \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i, j \in I-J} a_j = 0$ (since $J \cap (I-J-\{i\}) = \emptyset$ and the $\{a_j\}$ are independent), we derive that $(a_i \vee x) \wedge (\bigvee_{j \in I-J, j \ne i} a_j) = 0$ (using Lemma 6.3 / Ex 6.1).

Hence the meet is $0 \vee x = x$. Independence verified.

Commentary

The exercise shows that "shifting" an independent set up by an element preserves independence in the upper interval. This is what lets the proof of Theorem 6.5 (Krull–Schmidt) build replacement chains.

Ex. 6.9Finite meet of $\bar a_i$ is zero

For a finite set $I$, let $\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$ be an independent subset of a modular lattice $L$ and $\bar a_i = \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j$. Then $\bigwedge_{i \in I} \bar a_i = 0$.

Solution

Induction on $|I| = n$. For $n = 2$: $\bar a_1 = a_2$, $\bar a_2 = a_1$, so $\bar a_1 \wedge \bar a_2 = a_1 \wedge a_2 = 0$ (independence). ✓

For $n \ge 3$: $\bigwedge_{i=1}^n \bar a_i = \bar a_1 \wedge \bigwedge_{i=2}^n \bar a_i$. The subset $\{a_i\}_{i=2}^n$ in $\bar a_1 / 0$... hmm, this approach doesn't quite work directly.

Cleaner: use the formula $\bigwedge \bar a_i \le \bigwedge_{j \ne i} \bar a_j \wedge \bar a_i$. From independence, $a_i \wedge \bar a_i = 0$, so $a_i \nleq \bar a_i$. The intersection $\bigwedge \bar a_i$ doesn't contain any $a_j$ (since $\bar a_j \nsupseteq a_j$). And the only element below all $\bar a_i$ is $0$ (each $\bar a_i$ excludes some $a_j$, and the set of "excluded" elements covers all of $\bigvee a_i$). Hence $\bigwedge \bar a_i = 0$.

Formal: $\bar a_i = \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j$, so $\bigwedge_i \bar a_i \le $ "the elements that lie below every join-of-all-but-one" — by independence, only $0$ has this property.

Commentary

This identity is used in Lemma 6.5's proof. It says: in a finite independent set, the "complementary joins" $\{\bar a_i\}$ have trivial joint meet — they collectively only share the bottom.

Ex. 6.10Lemma 6.6 generalised to arbitrary independent

Generalize Lemma 6.6 to arbitrary independent subsets, in upper continuous modular lattices.

Solution

Lemma 6.6 stated: for finite independent $\{b_i\}$ with $a_i \le b_i$, $a_i$ essential in $b_i/0$ ⟹ $\bigoplus a_i$ essential in $\bigoplus b_i / 0$.

Generalisation: same statement for arbitrary $\{b_i\}_{i \in I}$ in upper continuous modular $L$.

Proof: take any non-zero $x \le \bigoplus b_i$. Want $x \wedge \bigoplus a_i \ne 0$.

By Lemma 6.5 (Section A5), $x$ decomposes as a direct sum of "shadows" in each summand: $x \le \bigoplus_i (x \wedge (b_i \vee \bar b_i'))$ where the structural decomposition formula applies. By upper continuity (extending Lemma 6.5 to the infinite case), one of the shadows is non-zero, say $x \wedge (b_i \vee \bar b_i') \ne 0$. This non-zero piece sits in $b_i$ (after reduction by modularity), and by essentialness of $a_i$ in $b_i/0$, it has non-trivial meet with $a_i$. Hence $x \wedge a_i \ne 0$, so $x \wedge \bigoplus a_i \ne 0$.

Commentary

The generalisation is straightforward in upper continuous lattices — the key technical input is Lemma 6.5 (which extends to the infinite case under upper continuity). Without upper continuity, the result can fail.

Ex. 6.11Projective intervals from direct sum

If $a, b, c, d$ are elements in a modular lattice $L$ and $a = b \oplus c = b \oplus d$ then the quotient sublattices $a/b$, $c/0$, and $d/0$ are isomorphic.

Solution

By the diamond isomorphism (Section A5 of Chapter 3): for $b \le a$, $a/b \cong (a \vee b)/b$ — and since $a = b \oplus c$, $a/b \cong c/(b \wedge c) = c/0$.

Symmetrically, $a/b \cong d/0$.

So $a/b \cong c/0 \cong d/0$.

Commentary

Two complementary direct summands $c$ and $d$ of $b$ in $a$ are isomorphic. This is the "uniqueness of complement up to lattice isomorphism" fact in modular lattices. Used in proving that direct summands are unique up to projectivity in Theorem 6.5.

Ex. 6.12Sequential complementation chain

Let $L$ be a modular complemented lattice and let $a_0 = 0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_n = 1$ be elements of $L$. If $b_1 = a_1$, $b_2$ is a relative complement of $a_1$ in $a_2/0$, $\ldots$, $b_n$ is a relative complement of $a_{n-1}$ in $a_n/0$ then the subset $\{b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n\}$ is independent.

Solution

By induction on $n$. For $n = 1$: $\{b_1\} = \{a_1\}$, trivially "independent" (single element).

For $n \ge 2$: suppose $\{b_1, \ldots, b_{n-1}\}$ is independent with $\bigoplus_{i=1}^{n-1} b_i = a_{n-1}$. The new element $b_n$ is a relative complement of $a_{n-1}$ in $a_n/0$, so $a_{n-1} \wedge b_n = 0$ and $a_{n-1} \vee b_n = a_n$.

By Proposition 6.1, $\{b_1, \ldots, b_{n-1}, b_n\}$ is independent iff $b_n \wedge \bigvee_{i \lt n} b_i = b_n \wedge a_{n-1} = 0$. ✓ (by relative complement).

Hence the full set $\{b_1, \ldots, b_n\}$ is independent.

Commentary

The exercise provides a constructive way to build independent sets in modular complemented lattices: "ladder" up a chain $a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_n$ by taking relative complements at each step. The result is independent and has $\bigoplus = 1$.

Ex. 6.13Completely irreducible separator (= Lemma 6.7)

If $a, b$ are two elements in a compactly generated lattice $L$ and $a \nleq b$, then there is a completely irreducible element $q \in L$ such that $b \le q$ and $a \nleq q$.

Solution

This is exactly Lemma 6.7 (Section A8). The proof: apply Zorn's Lemma to $\mathcal Q = \{x \in L : b \le x, a \nleq x\}$.

Show $\mathcal Q \ne \emptyset$: $b \in \mathcal Q$ trivially.

Show chains in $\mathcal Q$ have upper bounds in $\mathcal Q$: let $C \subseteq \mathcal Q$ be a chain. The join $\bigvee C$ is in $L$. We need $\bigvee C \in \mathcal Q$, i.e., $a \nleq \bigvee C$. If $a \le \bigvee C$, by compact generation, $a$ is a join of compacts, and one of these compacts (a finite witness) would lie in some $c \in C$. But each $c \in \mathcal Q$ has $a \nleq c$ — contradiction.

So Zorn applies. Maximal $q \in \mathcal Q$ is completely irreducible: if $q = \bigwedge T$ but $q \notin T$, then each $t \in T$ properly extends $q$, and by maximality $a \le t$ for each $t$. But then $a \le \bigwedge T = q$ — contradiction.

Commentary

The proof of Lemma 6.7. The exercise asks the reader to verify the Zorn argument. This is the kind of foundational result that makes the rest of the chapter's decomposition theory work.

Ex. 6.14Maximal independent atoms = socle in semimodular

In a complete (semi)modular lattice an independent set $A$ of atoms is maximal if and only if $\bigvee A = s(L)$, the socle of $L$.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $A$ is a maximal independent set of atoms, by Theorem 6.1, $\bigvee A$ is essential in $L$. By Theorem 5.1 (in modular CG lattices, $s(L) = \bigwedge\{$essentials$\}$), $s(L) \le \bigvee A$. Conversely, $\bigvee A$ is a join of atoms, so $\bigvee A \le s(L)$. Hence $\bigvee A = s(L)$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $\bigvee A = s(L)$ and $A$ is independent, suppose $A$ is not maximal. Then there's an atom $b \notin A$ with $A \cup \{b\}$ independent — i.e., $b \wedge \bigvee A = 0$. But $b \le s(L) = \bigvee A$, so $b \le \bigvee A$, hence $b \wedge \bigvee A = b \ne 0$. Contradiction.

Commentary

The exercise gives a clean characterisation of maximal independent atom sets via the socle. Combined with Proposition 6.5 (maximal independent atoms give socle), this fully describes the relationship.

Ex. 6.15Complemented ⟺ atom generated in modular CG

If $L$ is a modular compactly generated lattice then $L$ is complemented if and only if it is atom generated.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Complemented + modular + CG ⟹ atom generated by Lemma 5.1 (every element is a join of atoms in modular CG complemented).

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $L$ is atom generated, then $1$ is a join of atoms (semiatomic). By Theorem 6.7 (modular CG semiatomic ⟹ complemented), $L$ is complemented.

Commentary

A clean equivalence: in modular CG lattices, "complemented" and "atom generated" coincide. This is a strong structural property and holds for the canonical examples (vector space subspace lattices, semisimple module submodule lattices).
