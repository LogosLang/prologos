Composition Series and Decompositions

The chapter where lattice theory borrows the structural arsenal of group and module theory. When can an element be broken into "simple steps" — and when is that breakdown unique up to reordering?

~55–70 min read 24 sections + 5 connection panels 8 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — Why decompose at all?

Two big ideas, both lifted from algebra into pure lattice form: (i) a "step-by-step refinement" of a chain that ends with no further refinements possible (a composition chain), and (ii) a "decomposition into building blocks" via meet of irreducible elements.

Chapter 2 introduced compactness and compactly generated lattices — the lattice analog of "finitely generated." Chapter 3 takes the next step: it asks whether a finite-dimensional kind of structure exists in the lattice, where elements can be assembled from a sequence of indecomposable steps, and whether that assembly is essentially unique.

The chapter has two parallel storylines, both deeply familiar from algebra:

  1. Composition series and length. A composition series of an algebraic object — module, group, ring, etc. — is a maximal chain of subobjects: $0 = M_0 \subset M_1 \subset \cdots \subset M_n = M$, where each $M_k/M_{k-1}$ is "simple" (no proper non-trivial subobjects between them). The Jordan–Hölder theorem says that the length $n$ and the multiset of simple quotients $\{M_k/M_{k-1}\}$ are invariants — independent of how you build the chain. Chapter 3 lifts this to lattice theory: composition chains are maximally refined chains in the lattice, and Jordan–Hölder (Corollary 3.1) says they're all equivalent in modular lattices.
  2. Irreducible decomposition. An element $a$ is "meet irreducible" if $a$ cannot be written as a meet of two strictly larger elements. The irreducible decomposition is then $a = a_1 \wedge a_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n$ where each $a_i$ is irreducible and no factor can be dropped. The Kuros–Ore theorem (Theorem 3.3) is the analog of unique factorisation: in a modular lattice, if you have two such decompositions of $a$, they have the same number of factors. The non-modular case fails — the chapter exhibits a counterexample.

The two stories are different but parallel. The first measures elements by vertical refinement (how many cover-pairs do you climb to get there?). The second measures elements by horizontal decomposition (how many irreducible meets bring you to it?). Both invariants are stabilised by modularity. Without modularity, neither is invariant — the chapter's non-modular counterexample for Kuros–Ore is the famous "two-decompositions-of-the-same-element-with-different-lengths" picture.

The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

In a modular lattice, any element with a composition chain has a well-defined length $\ell(b/a)$ — independent of which composition chain you pick. Length is additive across nested intervals (Lemma 3.1) and matches dimension when the lattice is the lattice of subspaces of a vector space. A modular lattice has finite length iff it's both noetherian and artinian (Theorem 3.2). For the meet-decomposition story: in a modular lattice, the number of irreducible factors in any irredundant decomposition is invariant (Theorem 3.3, Kuros–Ore); without modularity it isn't. Length and decomposition number are the two scalar invariants of an element of a modular lattice.

The structural payoff

Chapter 2 gave you the right family of lattices to study (compactly generated). Chapter 3 says: inside that family, modular lattices have well-defined finite invariants — length, decomposition multiplicity — that behave exactly like dimension and prime factorisation. From a category-theory perspective, this is the chapter where lattice theory acquires its first "rank" data. The rest of the book builds on this: dimension theory in chapters 5–6 generalises length; semisimple and projective lattices in chapters 7–9 use composition chains to control structure; the ideal lattice work in chapters 12–13 exploits irreducibility.

Before diving in, the chapter introduces a small army of new technical terms — similar, projective, equivalent, refinement, composition, length, noetherian, artinian, simple, semimodular, inductive, irreducible, irredundant, bounded, locally finite. The next section is a vocabulary primer: every new term explained on first encounter, with the standard-mathematical alternatives noted.

Pre-flight · Vocabulary primer

First, the vocabulary you'll need

A pile of new technical terms gets introduced in this chapter. Read this section first; come back to it whenever a word feels slippery.

Terms you'll meet

Each entry: the term, the formal meaning, and the plain-English gist or warning. Bookmark this section; the chapter prose will refer back to it.
Notation

$a \le b$ — "$b$ contains $a$" The book opens by adopting the convention that "$b$ contains $a$" simply means $a \le b$ (and "$a$ is contained in $b$" likewise). This is the order-theoretic "contains," not necessarily set-theoretic containment — though in the running example $(S_R(M), \subseteq)$ the two coincide.

Foundational

cover (two senses) Watch out — the word is overloaded.

Sense 1 — covering relation $a \prec b$. "$b$ covers $a$" means $a < b$ and there is no element $c$ strictly between, i.e., $a < c < b$ has no solution. In a Hasse diagram, $a \prec b$ is exactly an edge: a single step with no intermediate stop.

Sense 2 — to cover (a verb, topology-borrowed). A subset $X \subseteq L$ is said to "cover" an element $c$ when $c \le \bigvee X$ — the join of $X$ is at least $c$. This was the sense used in Chapter 2's compactness definition: "every cover of $c$ has a finite subcover." Same word as the topology meaning.

The two are unrelated in detail (covering relation talks about a single step; covering as a verb talks about a join sitting above). Context disambiguates: $a \prec b$ is a relation between two elements; $X$ covers $c$ is a relation between a set and an element. In Chapter 3 we'll mostly use sense 1; sense 2 only echoes from Chapter 2.

Foundational

chain A subset of $L$ that is totally ordered: any two elements are comparable. In Chapter 3 the chains we care about are finite ones written out as $a = a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots \le a_n = b$. The integer $n$ — the number of steps, not the number of elements (which is $n+1$) — is what we call the chain's length.

Foundational

interval / quotient sublattice $b/a$ For $a \le b$, the set $\{x \in L : a \le x \le b\}$. Modern texts write $[a, b]$. Călugăreanu's slash notation $b/a$ echoes the module quotient $B/A$ — and in $L = S_R(M)$ that's exactly what it means. Sometimes called a "quotient sublattice" because it inherits the lattice operations of $L$ restricted to the interval.

Crucial · new in Ch 3

isomorphic vs similar vs projective intervals Three increasingly weaker notions of "the same shape," all relating intervals.

Isomorphic — two intervals $A, B$ are isomorphic if there is a lattice isomorphism (a bijection preserving $\le$, $\vee$, $\wedge$) between them. The strongest of the three.

Similar — two intervals $A, B$ are similar if $\{A, B\} = \{a/(a \wedge b), (a \vee b)/b\}$ for some $a, b \in L$. That is, one of them is "the bottom slot $a/(a \wedge b)$" and the other is "the top slot $(a \vee b)/b$" of a four-element parallelogram. Similarity is a specific relationship between intervals — it requires a witnessing pair $(a, b)$ — not just any old "they look the same."

Projective — two intervals $A, B$ are projective if they're connected by a finite chain of similarities: $A = I_0, I_1, \ldots, I_n = B$ with $I_{k-1}, I_k$ similar for each $k$. So projectivity is the transitive closure of similarity.

Why three notions? Because the diamond-isomorphism theorem from algebra (the second isomorphism theorem) says that in modular lattices, similar intervals are automatically isomorphic. So in modular lattices, all three notions collapse: similar = projective = isomorphic. In non-modular lattices they differ, and the chapter handles that distinction carefully.

Crucial · new in Ch 3

equivalent chains Two chains $a_0 \le \cdots \le a_m$ and $b_0 \le \cdots \le b_n$ from the same $a$ to the same $b$ are equivalent if (i) $m = n$ — they have the same length — and (ii) there is a permutation $\sigma$ of $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ such that the quotient interval $a_i/a_{i-1}$ is projective to $b_{\sigma(i)}/b_{\sigma(i)-1}$ for each $i$. So the two chains have the same length AND the same multiset of step-shapes (up to projectivity).

Crucial · new in Ch 3

refinement A chain $C'$ is a refinement of a chain $C$ if $C \subseteq C'$ — i.e. $C'$ is obtained from $C$ by inserting more elements. Refinements always have length $\ge$ the original chain.

Crucial · new in Ch 3

composition chain A chain $a = a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_n = b$ that has no refinements — i.e., every step is a covering pair: $a_{k-1} \prec a_k$ for each $k$. Equivalently: $a_k/a_{k-1} = \{a_{k-1}, a_k\}$ (only two elements, no intermediate). The integer $n$ is called the length of the composition chain.

Crucial · new in Ch 3

length $\ell(b/a)$ In a modular lattice, all composition chains from $a$ to $b$ have the same length (Jordan–Hölder, Corollary 3.1), so the integer $n$ is well-defined. We call it the length $\ell(b/a)$ of the interval. For the whole lattice (assuming $0$ and $1$): $\ell(L) = \ell(1/0)$. A lattice has finite length if some finite chain exists from $0$ to $1$.

Chain conditions

noetherian / ascending chain condition (ACC) Every increasing sequence $a_1 \le a_2 \le \cdots$ stabilises after finitely many steps. (From Chapter 2, Proposition 2.3: in a complete lattice, ACC is equivalent to "every element is compact.")

Chain conditions

artinian / descending chain condition (DCC) Every decreasing sequence $a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \cdots$ stabilises. The dual of noetherian. Together with noetherian, this gives finite length (Theorem 3.2 of this chapter).

Chain conditions

simple lattice / simple element / simple quotient "Simple" means "no proper non-trivial sub-thing." For a quotient interval $a/b$: simple means $a/b = \{b, a\}$, i.e. $b \prec a$ — the quotient has just two elements, the endpoints. (Equivalently: no element strictly between.) For a lattice as a whole: a simple lattice is one with $|L| = 2$ — just $0$ and $1$. The composition-chain definition asks each step $a_k/a_{k-1}$ to be a simple quotient.

Modularity flavour

modular lattice (recap from Ch 1) A lattice satisfying $a \le c \Rightarrow a \vee (b \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge c$. Equivalently: contains no $N_5$ (the pentagon). Submodule lattices are always modular.

Modularity flavour

semimodular / lower covering condition (LCC) A lattice is semimodular (or "satisfies the lower covering condition") if $a \prec b \Rightarrow a \wedge c \prec b \wedge c$ or $a \wedge c = b \wedge c$. Plain English: meeting both ends of a covering pair with another element either preserves the covering, or collapses it. Modular ⟹ semimodular but not conversely. Semimodular lattices have a partial length function (Lemma 3.2) and matroids' lattices of flats are semimodular.

Modularity flavour

submodular inequality The inequality $\ell(a \vee b) + \ell(a \wedge b) \le \ell(a) + \ell(b)$ for the length function $\ell$. Holds always in semimodular lattices of finite length. Equality for all $a, b$ characterizes modularity (Lemma 3.2.4). The "submodular" name comes from this inequality and is the abstract source of the term used in optimisation theory ("submodular function").

Bound conditions

bounded lattice A lattice with both a smallest element $0$ and a largest element $1$. The chapter assumes bounded throughout (the book's lattices are mostly complete, hence bounded).

Decomposition flavour

meet irreducible (= "irreducible," = "hollow") An element $a$ is meet irreducible if whenever $a = b \wedge c$ for some $b, c \in L$, already $b = a$ or $c = a$. Plain English: $a$ cannot be written as a meet of two strictly-bigger elements. The chapter says "irreducible" for short. Chapters 12–13 use the synonym hollow.

The dual concept is join irreducible: $a = b \vee c \Rightarrow b = a$ or $c = a$. The chapter's results focus on meet-irreducible because that's what shows up in module-decomposition contexts.

Decomposition flavour

irredundant meet A finite meet $a = \bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i$ is irredundant if you cannot drop any factor: $\bigwedge_{j \ne i} a_j > a$ strictly for each $i \in I$. So no $a_i$ is "redundant" — all of them are pulling their weight.

Decomposition flavour

irreducible decomposition An expression $a = a_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n$ where (i) each $a_i$ is irreducible and (ii) the meet is irredundant. The Kuros–Ore Theorem (3.3) shows $n$ is invariant in modular lattices.

Inductive lattices

condition (B) For any chain $\{b_i\}_{i \in I}$ and any $a$ with $a \wedge b_i = 0$ for each $i$, we have $a \wedge \bigvee_i b_i = 0$. Plain English: "if $a$ has trivial meet with every chain element, then it has trivial meet with the chain's join."

Inductive lattices

inductive lattice A lattice in which every quotient sublattice (interval) satisfies condition (B). This is a regularity condition that ensures certain Zorn-style arguments go through.

Decomposition flavour

locally finite length A poset has locally finite length if every interval $[a, b]$ has finite length. So chains might be infinite globally but every bounded sub-region is finite. A useful regularity condition (Exercise 3.8 will use it).

Honoured names

Schreier's refinement theorem Eisenhart? No — Otto Schreier (1928). The theorem (in its module form): any two normal series of a group / chains of submodules / etc. have isomorphic refinements. The lattice form is Theorem 3.1 of this chapter.

Honoured names

Jordan–Hölder theorem Camille Jordan (1869) and Otto Hölder (1889) for finite groups; later extended to modules. The original theorem: any two composition series of a finite group have the same length and the same multiset of simple factor groups. The lattice version (in modular lattices) is Corollary 3.1 of this chapter.

Honoured names

Kuros–Ore theorem Aleksandr Kuros (Russian, 1930s) and Øystein Ore (Norwegian–American, 1930s). The theorem states uniqueness of the number of factors in irreducible decompositions of an element of a modular lattice. Theorem 3.3 of this chapter.

Part A · Composition series and length

Step-by-step refinement: when a chain captures everything

The first half of the chapter develops the "vertical" invariant of an element: its length, measured by the number of cover-steps in any composition chain from $0$ to it. The Schreier and Jordan–Hölder theorems make this length well-defined in modular lattices.

SECTION A1 A note on notation — "contains" and the slash

Two small notational conventions that pervade the chapter and beyond. Worth flagging because the book introduces them quickly.

The book opens with: "for elements $a, b$ in a lattice (or a poset) we will say that $b$ contains $a$ (or $a$ is contained in $b$) if $a \le b$." This is order-theoretic "contains," not necessarily set-theoretic — though in the canonical example $(S_R(M), \subseteq)$, where the elements of the lattice are submodules and the order is set inclusion, the two senses coincide. In a more abstract lattice, "contains" is just shorthand for $\le$.

The second convention, carried over from Chapter 1, is the slash notation $b/a = \{x : a \le x \le b\}$ for the interval (or "quotient sublattice"). When $L = S_R(M)$, the interval $B/A$ corresponds to the lattice of submodules of the quotient module $B/A$ — which is what the slash visually evokes. In other texts (Birkhoff, Stanley) the same concept is written $[a, b]$.

A useful translation

Whenever you see $b/a$ in the book, read it as "the interval from $a$ to $b$" — and remember that this interval is itself a lattice, so all the lattice properties (modular, complete, compactly generated, …) can be applied to it. The chapter exploits this constantly: theorems about $L$ get applied to intervals $b/a$.

SECTION A2 Similar intervals — the parallelogram trick

A specific structural relationship between two intervals: they are the "two short sides" of a four-element parallelogram. The notion is the foundation for everything else in Part A.
Definition · similar intervals

Two intervals $A, B$ in a lattice $L$ are called similar if there are elements $a, b \in L$ such that $\{A, B\} = \{a/(a \wedge b), (a \vee b)/b\}$.

Unpacking: pick any pair $a, b \in L$. They generate a four-element parallelogram in the lattice — the four corners are $a \wedge b$ (bottom), $a$, $b$, and $a \vee b$ (top). The two intervals on the "sides" of this parallelogram are:

  • $a/(a \wedge b)$ — the slot between $a \wedge b$ and $a$ (the left side, in the picture below).
  • $(a \vee b)/b$ — the slot between $b$ and $a \vee b$ (the right side).

"Similar" says: $A$ and $B$ are these two side-intervals of some $(a, b)$. The set notation $\{A, B\} = \{a/(a \wedge b), (a \vee b)/b\}$ allows either $A$ or $B$ to be the left side and the other to be the right.

Why this notion?

Similar intervals abstract a relationship that is everywhere in algebra: in a group $G$ with subgroups $H, K$, the two intervals $\langle H, K\rangle / H$ and $K / (H \cap K)$ are related by the second isomorphism theorem. In a module $M$ with submodules $A, B$, the intervals $(A + B)/A$ and $B/(A \cap B)$ are related by the second isomorphism theorem. Similar intervals are that — but stripped of the algebra and re-stated as a pure lattice relationship. They're "siblings" because they both descend from the same parallelogram $(a, b)$.

SECTION A3 Projective intervals — chaining similarities

Similarity is just a relation between two intervals. Projectivity is its transitive closure: a chain of similar steps.
Definition · projective intervals

Two intervals $A$ and $B$ are projective if there is a finite sequence of intervals $A = I_0, I_1, \ldots, I_n = B$ such that $I_{k-1}$ and $I_k$ are similar for each $1 \le k \le n$.

Projectivity is similarity composed with itself, a finite number of times. The chain of intermediate intervals $I_1, \ldots, I_{n-1}$ doesn't need to be inside any particular interval of $L$ — they just need to be similar pairwise.

If $A$ and $B$ are similar, take $n = 1$ (the chain $A, B$ itself) and they're projective. So similar intervals are projective. The reverse is not true in general: there can be projective pairs that are not directly similar (only similar through intermediate steps).

The reason "projective" is the right notion: when we want to compare two chains of intervals, we want a notion of "interval equivalence" that's transitive. Similarity isn't transitive on its own (similar to similar isn't necessarily similar). So we close under transitivity and get projectivity.

An analogy from algebra

In group theory, this is the notion behind projection of factor groups in the Schreier theorem: given two normal series, you can "project" each factor of one through a chain of group isomorphisms onto a factor of the other. The chain of isomorphisms — Frattini-style — is what corresponds to a chain of similarities here.

SECTION A4 Equivalent chains — same length, same step-multiset

Two chains between the same endpoints are equivalent when they have the same length and the same multiset of step-shapes (up to projectivity, possibly permuted).
Definition · equivalent chains

Two chains $$a = a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots \le a_m = b \tag{1}$$ $$a = b_0 \le b_1 \le \cdots \le b_n = b \tag{2}$$ between the same two elements $a, b$ of an arbitrary lattice $L$ are called equivalent if $m = n$ and there is a permutation $\sigma \in S_n$ such that the quotient sublattices $a_i / a_{i-1}$ and $b_{\sigma(i)} / b_{\sigma(i)-1}$ are projective for every $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$.

Read this as: chains (1) and (2) have the same length, and after some permutation $\sigma$ of the indices, the $i$-th step of (1) is projective to the $\sigma(i)$-th step of (2). So the step-shapes match as multisets, allowing permutation.

The permutation is essential — without it, the order of steps in (2) might differ from the order in (1). Think of it as: chain (1) climbs through step-shapes $S_1, S_2, S_3$; chain (2) climbs through the same shapes but in order $S_2, S_1, S_3$. Both chains "use the same building blocks," just in different sequence.

Worth pausing on

Equivalence of chains is a strong relation: it requires both length and step-multiset to match. If two chains have different lengths, they're not equivalent. If they have the same length but different step-shapes, they're not equivalent. Most chains in arbitrary lattices are not equivalent to each other.

The miracle of Jordan–Hölder (Corollary 3.1, coming up) is that in a modular lattice, any two composition chains from $a$ to $b$ are equivalent. So composition chains are essentially unique up to permutation — and the length and step-multiset become invariants of the interval $b/a$.

SECTION A5 Diamond isomorphism: in modular lattices, similar = isomorphic

A short remark: in modular lattices the technical notion of "similar" coincides with the simpler "isomorphic." This was Theorem 1.5 of the book; we recall it.
Remark (modular diamond iso)

In a modular lattice, every pair of similar intervals (and hence every pair of projective intervals) are isomorphic.

This is the "second isomorphism theorem" in lattice form, established as Theorem 1.5 in Chapter 1 of the book. In modular lattices, the explicit isomorphism between $a/(a \wedge b)$ and $(a \vee b)/b$ is: $$\varphi: a/(a \wedge b) \to (a \vee b)/b, \qquad x \mapsto x \vee b$$ with inverse $y \mapsto y \wedge a$. Both maps are well-defined on the intervals and respect lattice operations because of modularity. Without modularity, the maps still go through but they don't need to be lattice-isomorphisms.

The takeaway

In a modular lattice, "similar" as a technical term collapses to "isomorphic." Many proofs in this chapter exploit this: where the abstract definition uses "projective" (a technical chain of similarities), we can read it in modular lattices as "isomorphic" (a much simpler notion). When the chapter says "in a modular lattice, the chains are equivalent — i.e., the steps match up to projectivity," in modular lattices this is "the steps match up to isomorphism."

SECTION A6 Refinement — inserting points into a chain

A chain $C'$ is a refinement of $C$ if you can get $C'$ from $C$ by inserting more elements. The natural operation that lets us compare chains.
Definition · refinement

A chain $C'$ in $L$ is a refinement of a chain $C$ in $L$ if $C \subseteq C'$ as sets. That is, $C'$ is obtained from $C$ by inserting more elements.

Equivalent way to say it: the chain $a = a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots \le a_m = b$ is a refinement of $a = a_{i_0} \le a_{i_1} \le \cdots \le a_{i_k} = b$ for some increasing index sequence $0 = i_0 < i_1 < \cdots < i_k = m$. The refinement keeps all the original elements but adds more in between.

Refinements can extend a chain's length but not shorten it. If $C \subseteq C'$ then $|C'| \ge |C|$ (cardinalities) and the chain length grows by 0, 1, 2, ...

Refinement is the operation we'll use to ask: "given two different chains from $a$ to $b$, can we find a common refinement that they both refine into?" Schreier's theorem (next section) says yes, and moreover the common refinement makes the two chains equivalent.

SECTION A7 Theorem 3.1 (Schreier) — common equivalent refinement

In a modular lattice, any two chains between the same endpoints have a common refinement that makes them equivalent. This is the foundational step toward Jordan–Hölder.
Theorem 3.1 (Schreier)

In a modular lattice, any two chains between the same two elements have equivalent refinements.

Concretely: given two chains $$a = a_0 \le a_1 \le \cdots \le a_m = b \qquad \text{and} \qquad a = b_0 \le b_1 \le \cdots \le b_n = b$$ in a modular lattice $L$, there exist refinements $$a = c_0 \le c_1 \le \cdots \le c_p = b \qquad \text{and} \qquad a = d_0 \le d_1 \le \cdots \le d_q = b$$ with $C \supseteq A, D \supseteq B$, and $p = q$, and the two refined chains are equivalent (same length, same step-multiset up to projectivity).

Why — the slot-by-slot interleaving construction

The proof is constructive. Given the two original chains, define the auxiliary elements $$a_{ij} = (a_i \wedge b_j) \vee b_{j-1}, \quad b_{ji} = (b_j \wedge a_i) \vee a_{i-1}$$ for each $i \in \{1, \ldots, m\}$, $j \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$.

Modularity is what makes these auxiliary elements behave: $a_{ij} = (a_i \vee b_{j-1}) \wedge b_j$ and $b_{ji} = (b_j \vee a_{i-1}) \wedge a_i$. (These are the modular-law re-expressions of the formula above.)

Now build the refined chains by interleaving: $$a = a_{01} \le a_{11} \le a_{21} \le \cdots \le a_{m1} \le a_{12} \le \cdots \le a_{mn} = b \qquad \text{(length $mn$)}$$ $$a = b_{01} \le b_{11} \le b_{21} \le \cdots \le b_{n1} \le b_{12} \le \cdots \le b_{nm} = b \qquad \text{(length $mn$)}$$ Both refinements have length $mn$, and the $(i,j)$-th step of the first chain is similar (by the diamond isomorphism, using modularity) to the $(j,i)$-th step of the second chain. The permutation $\sigma$ that gives equivalence is the index-swap $\sigma(i, j) = (j, i)$ — interpreting $(i, j)$ as a number in $\{1, \ldots, mn\}$ via lexicographic order.

The geometric idea

Schreier's proof builds an $m \times n$ grid of auxiliary elements. The first chain "reads the grid row by row" (so length is $mn$), the second "reads the grid column by column" (also length $mn$). Modularity ensures each grid cell makes a well-defined parallelogram, and the diamond isomorphism makes the $(i,j)$-th row-cell similar to the $(j,i)$-th column-cell. The permutation that gives equivalence is the "transpose": rows become columns.

This proof technique — interpolating two chains via a grid of auxiliary elements — is the lattice version of Hans Zassenhaus's "butterfly lemma" from group theory, which gives the same result for normal series. The butterfly diagram for groups shows the $4 \times 4$ block of intersections and joins; lattice modularity is what makes it commute.

SECTION A8 Composition chains — chains that admit no refinement

A chain that has gone as far as it can go: every step is a covering relation.
Definition · composition chain

A chain $$a = a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_n = b$$ between two elements $a, b$ of a lattice is called a composition chain if it has no refinements — equivalently, $a_k / a_{k-1} = \{a_{k-1}, a_k\}$ (only two elements) for every $k \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$. The integer $n$ is called the length of the composition chain.

Read "no refinements" as: there is no element strictly between $a_{k-1}$ and $a_k$. In Hasse-diagram terms: every step is a single edge, $a_{k-1} \prec a_k$. A composition chain is a maximal chain — you can't insert any new element without breaking the chain structure.

Where the term "composition" comes from

The terminology is borrowed from algebra. A "composition series" of a module $M$ is a chain $0 = M_0 \subset M_1 \subset \cdots \subset M_n = M$ of submodules with each quotient $M_k/M_{k-1}$ simple (no proper non-trivial submodules). Lattice-theoretically, "$M_k/M_{k-1}$ simple" means "the interval $M_k/M_{k-1}$ has only two elements," which is exactly the definition above. The length $n$ here matches the algebraic length of the module.

Existence of composition chains

Not every interval has a composition chain. For instance, the lattice $\mathbb Z$ as a poset has no composition chain between $0$ and $1$ — every step admits a refinement. The chapter's Theorem 3.2 will tell us exactly when composition chains exist: a modular lattice has a composition chain from $0$ to $1$ iff it is both noetherian and artinian. Most concrete examples (finite vector spaces, finitely generated modules over fields, finite groups) satisfy this.

SECTION A9 Corollary 3.1 (Jordan–Hölder)

In a modular lattice, any two composition chains between the same endpoints are equivalent. Length and step-multiset are invariants.
Corollary 3.1 (Jordan–Hölder)

In a modular lattice, any two composition chains between the same two elements are equivalent.

Why

By Schreier's Theorem 3.1, the two composition chains have equivalent refinements. But composition chains have no refinements — they are already maximal. So both refinements coincide with the original composition chains themselves. Hence the original two composition chains are equivalent.

The corollary gives us length and step-multiset as invariants:

  • Length is well-defined. If the interval $b/a$ has any composition chain, all composition chains have the same length. We denote this number $\ell(b/a)$.
  • Step-multiset is well-defined. The multiset of "step-shapes" $\{a_k/a_{k-1}\}_{k=1}^n$ is determined up to projectivity, independent of the chain.
Connection — Jordan–Hölder for groups and modules

For finite groups: any two composition series of $G$ have the same length and the same multiset of simple factor groups. This is the classical Jordan–Hölder theorem. For modules: any two composition series of an $R$-module of finite length have the same length and the same multiset of simple submodules. The lattice version above subsumes both: take $L = $ subgroup lattice (which is modular for normal subgroups, so we work with the lattice of normal subgroups in groups, or the full submodule lattice for modules).

SECTION A10 Length of an interval, length of a lattice

A scalar invariant of any interval (or lattice) that has finite composition chains. Reduces to dimension in the vector-space case.

Once Jordan–Hölder gives us a well-defined length, we can name it: $\ell(b/a)$ is the length of any composition chain from $a$ to $b$ — a non-negative integer when such chains exist.

Following the chapter's convention from page 18 (Section 13 of the Chapter 2 companion), we extend this to elements: $\ell(a) = \ell(a/0)$, the length of the principal ideal below $a$. For the whole lattice (assuming a $0$ and $1$): $\ell(L) = \ell(1/0)$. We say $L$ has finite length if $\ell(L)$ is a finite integer — equivalently, if $1/0$ admits some finite chain.

Length is the "lattice dimension"

Length plays the role for lattices that dimension plays for vector spaces. Vector space dimension counts the basis elements; lattice length counts the steps in a maximal chain. In the canonical example — the lattice of subspaces of a finite-dimensional vector space — the two coincide: $\ell(V) = \dim V$. We'll prove this in the next section.

SECTION A11 Example — vector spaces and dimension

The motivating example: in the subspace lattice of a vector space, length equals dimension. The result is also Exercise 3.1.
Example

The lattice of all subspaces of a vector space $V$ has finite length if and only if $V$ has finite dimension. In this case, $\ell(V) = \dim V$.

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over a field $k$, with $\dim V = n$. Choose a basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$. The chain $$0 \subset \langle e_1\rangle \subset \langle e_1, e_2\rangle \subset \cdots \subset \langle e_1, \ldots, e_n\rangle = V$$ is a composition chain of length $n$. Why composition? At each step, the quotient $\langle e_1, \ldots, e_k\rangle / \langle e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}\rangle$ is a 1-dimensional subspace, which has no proper non-trivial subspaces — it has just $0$ and itself. So each step is a covering pair. By Jordan–Hölder, every composition chain in $V$'s subspace lattice has length $n$, so $\ell(V) = n = \dim V$.

For an infinite-dimensional space: the subspace lattice has no finite chain from $0$ to $V$, so $\ell(V)$ is not a finite integer. The lattice does not have finite length.

SECTION A12 Theorem 3.2 — finite length ⟺ noetherian + artinian

A clean characterisation of finite-length modular lattices in terms of two chain conditions.
Theorem 3.2

A modular lattice has finite length if and only if it is both noetherian and artinian.

Recall the chain conditions:

  • Noetherian (ACC). Every increasing sequence stabilises. Equivalently: every non-empty subset has a maximal element.
  • Artinian (DCC). Every decreasing sequence stabilises. The order-dual of noetherian.
Why · ($\Rightarrow$) finite length ⟹ noetherian + artinian

If $L$ has finite length, take a composition chain $0 = a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_n = 1$. By Schreier (3.1), every chain refines to a chain of the same length-or-greater equivalent to a refinement of this one, so all chains have length $\le n$. Therefore every increasing chain has $\le n+1$ elements and stabilises (ACC). Symmetrically every decreasing chain has $\le n+1$ elements (DCC). So $L$ is both noetherian and artinian.

Why · ($\Leftarrow$) noetherian + artinian ⟹ finite length

Suppose $L$ is both noetherian and artinian. We construct a composition chain of $L$.

By artinianness, the set $\{x \in L : 0 < x\}$ has a minimal element $a_1 \ne 0$. Then $a_1$ has no element strictly between $0$ and itself — so $0 \prec a_1$.

Now consider $\{x \in L : a_1 < x\}$. This is a non-empty set (it contains $1$ if $a_1 \ne 1$). By artinianness it has a minimal element $a_2$, with $a_1 \prec a_2$.

Continuing inductively, we build $0 = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec a_2 \prec \cdots$ — an increasing sequence of cover-pairs. By noetherianness, this sequence stabilises at some $a_n$, and necessarily $a_n = 1$ (since otherwise the construction would continue). So $0 = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_n = 1$ is a composition chain.

Why both conditions are needed

Noetherian alone isn't enough. The lattice of submodules of $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module is noetherian (every ideal is principal, hence f.g.) but not artinian: the chain $\mathbb Z \supset 2\mathbb Z \supset 4\mathbb Z \supset 8\mathbb Z \supset \cdots$ never stabilises. Correspondingly, this lattice has no composition chain from $0$ to $\mathbb Z$.

Artinian alone isn't enough. Example: the Prüfer $p$-group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module — its submodule lattice is $0 \subset \mathbb Z/p \subset \mathbb Z/p^2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathbb Z(p^\infty)$, an infinite ascending chain. So not noetherian. But every descending chain stabilises (artinian). And no finite composition chain exists.

Both conditions together force finite length. This is exactly the characterisation of artinian and noetherian modules in module theory: such modules have finite Jordan–Hölder length, and the length equals the lattice's $\ell(L)$.

SECTION A13 Lemma 3.1 — additivity of length

Length splits cleanly across nested intervals: $\ell(c/a) = \ell(c/b) + \ell(b/a)$.
Lemma 3.1

Let $a \le b \le c$ be elements in a modular lattice $L$. The interval $c/a$ has finite length if and only if both intervals $c/b$ and $b/a$ have finite length. In this case, $$\ell(c/a) = \ell(c/b) + \ell(b/a).$$

Why

$(\Leftarrow)$ Take composition chains $b = b_0 \prec b_1 \prec \cdots \prec b_p = c$ in $c/b$ (length $p$) and $a = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_q = b$ in $b/a$ (length $q$). Concatenate: $$a = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_q = b = b_0 \prec b_1 \prec \cdots \prec b_p = c$$ This is a chain of length $p + q$ in $c/a$, and it's a composition chain because each step is a cover. Hence $\ell(c/a) = p + q = \ell(c/b) + \ell(b/a)$.

$(\Rightarrow)$ A composition chain $a = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_n = c$ in $c/a$ "passes through" $b$: there is some $k$ with $a_{k-1} \le b \le a_k$ (cleanly, since the lattice is modular). Apply Theorem 3.2 to the intervals $c/b$ and $b/a$: each is noetherian and artinian (sub-chains of the composition chain in $c/a$), so each has finite length. Specifically: the chain restricted from $a$ to $b$ has some length $q$, restricted from $b$ to $c$ has length $p$, with $p + q = n$.

Why this matters

Length additivity means we can decompose intervals: to compute $\ell$ of a big interval, find a useful intermediate $b$ and add the lengths of the two pieces. This is the lattice version of the rank-nullity theorem ($\dim V = \dim \ker f + \dim \mathrm{im}(f)$) and the like — all of which descend from this lemma applied to the appropriate quotient lattice.

SECTION A14 Lemma 3.2 — properties of the length function in semimodular lattices

A more refined picture of the length function in semimodular lattices, including the submodular inequality and a characterization of modularity.

The book introduces the length function in the slightly weaker setting of semimodular lattices of finite length. Recall: $L$ is semimodular if $a \prec b \Rightarrow a \wedge c \prec b \wedge c$ or $a \wedge c = b \wedge c$. Modular lattices are semimodular (with strictly more structure).

Lemma 3.2

In a semimodular lattice $L$ of finite length, the function $\ell : L \to \mathbb N$ given by $\ell(a) = \ell(a/0)$ has the following properties:

(1) $\ell(a) = 0$ if and only if $a$ is the smallest element ($a = 0$).

(2) $a \prec b$ if and only if $a \le b$ and $\ell(b) = \ell(a) + 1$.

(3) $\ell(a \vee b) + \ell(a \wedge b) \le \ell(a) + \ell(b)$ — the submodular inequality.

(4) $L$ is modular if and only if equality holds in (3) for every $a, b \in L$.

(1) and (2) are nearly definitional. (1): a composition chain from $0$ to $a$ has length $0$ iff there are no steps iff $a = 0$. (2): a covering relation $a \prec b$ corresponds to a single step in any composition chain from $0$ to $b$ that passes through $a$; the lengths thus differ by 1.

(3) is the substantive content. The inequality $\ell(a \vee b) + \ell(a \wedge b) \le \ell(a) + \ell(b)$ holds in semimodular lattices but fails in non-semimodular ones (where length might not even be well-defined).

(4) is the modularity characterisation: equality in (3) ⟺ modular.

Why (4) — the modularity characterization

$(\Leftarrow)$ For modular: by Lemma 3.1, $\ell(a \vee b/b) + \ell(b/0) = \ell(a \vee b/0) = \ell(a \vee b)$, and $\ell(a/(a \wedge b)) + \ell((a \wedge b)/0) = \ell(a/0) = \ell(a)$. By the diamond isomorphism (Section A5), $\ell(a \vee b/b) = \ell(a/(a \wedge b))$. So: $$\ell(a \vee b) + \ell(a \wedge b) = \ell(a \vee b/b) + \ell(b) + \ell(a \wedge b) = \ell(a/(a \wedge b)) + \ell(b) + \ell(a \wedge b) = \ell(a) + \ell(b).$$ Equality.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose equality always holds. Take $a, b, c$ with $a \le c$. We need to show $a \vee (b \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge c$. The general inequality $a \vee (b \wedge c) \le (a \vee b) \wedge c$ holds in any lattice. We'll show length forces equality.

Compute $\ell(a \vee (b \wedge c))$ and $\ell((a \vee b) \wedge c)$ via the equality in (3) — both expand to expressions in $\ell(a), \ell(b), \ell(c), \ell(a \vee b), \ell(b \wedge c)$ that turn out equal by symmetric expansion. So $\ell(a \vee (b \wedge c)) = \ell((a \vee b) \wedge c)$, which combined with the inequality forces equality of the elements (by (2): $\le$ with equal lengths means equal).

The "submodular" name

The submodular inequality is named for its shape — and it's the abstract source of the modern term "submodular function" used in optimisation theory and machine learning. A submodular function $f: 2^X \to \mathbb R$ satisfies $f(A \cup B) + f(A \cap B) \le f(A) + f(B)$. The lattice version (with $\le$) for length functions captures exactly the same shape. Modular lattices give equality (the function is "modular" rather than just submodular).

SECTION A15 Inductive lattices and condition (B)

A regularity condition that makes Zorn-style arguments work cleanly. Applied in Lemma 3.3 and Proposition 3.1.

Following reference [4] in Călugăreanu's book (Birkhoff's Lattice Theory, 1967), a lattice satisfies condition (B) if for any chain $\{b_i\}_{i \in I}$ and any $a \in L$ such that $a \wedge b_i = 0$ for each $i \in I$, also $$a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} b_i = 0.$$

Plain English: if $a$ has trivial meet with every element of a chain, then it has trivial meet with the chain's join. This is a "limit-passing" condition: triviality is preserved through directed-chain-joins.

Definition · inductive lattice

A lattice $L$ is called inductive if every quotient sublattice (i.e. every interval $b/a$) of $L$ satisfies condition (B).

The "inductive" name is chosen because condition (B) is what's needed to set up a transfinite-induction (Zorn-style) argument: it lets us pass from a chain to its join while preserving structural properties.

Examples

The submodule lattice $S_R(M)$ of any $R$-module is inductive: meeting with a fixed submodule preserves directed unions of submodules (this is exactly the upper continuity of $S_R(M)$, which follows from compact generation via Proposition 2.4). More broadly, every compactly generated lattice is inductive.

The lattice of closed subspaces of a Hilbert space is not inductive: closure does not commute with directed unions, so condition (B) can fail.

SECTION A16 Lemma 3.3 — chain witness in inductive lattices

A technical lemma used to power the next Proposition. Pure transfinite-induction work.
Lemma 3.3

If $a$ is an element and $\{c_i\}_{i \in I}$ is a chain in an inductive lattice $L$, and if $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} c_i \ne 0$, then there is a finite subset $F \subseteq I$ such that $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} c_i \ne 0$.

Read this as the contrapositive: if $a$ has trivial meet with the join of every finite sub-chain, it has trivial meet with the whole chain's join. This is condition (B) lifted: it gives "finite witness" for non-triviality.

Why — proof by contradiction with minimal cardinality

Assume the conclusion fails: $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} c_i = 0$ for every finite $F \subseteq I$, but $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} c_i \ne 0$. Let $I$ be the smallest such index set (in cardinality).

Then $I$ is infinite. For each $i \in I$, define $b_i = \bigvee_{j \in J_i} c_j$ for some appropriate $J_i$ chosen so that $\{b_i\}$ is itself a chain in $L$. Use the minimality of $|I|$ and condition (B) repeatedly to derive that $a \wedge b_i = 0$ for all $i$, but $a \wedge \bigvee_i b_i \ne 0$ — which violates condition (B). Contradiction.

The lemma is technical machinery; you'll never need to invoke it directly outside the chapter. It's the workhorse for the next proposition.

SECTION A17 Proposition 3.1 — finite-length elements are compact

In a modular inductive lattice, every element of finite length is compact. This is the link between this chapter (length) and the previous chapter (compactness).
Proposition 3.1

In a modular inductive lattice $L$, each element of finite length is compact.

Why — induction on length

Induction on $\ell(a) = n$.

Base case $n = 1$. An element of length 1 is an atom (covers $0$). Suppose $a \le \bigvee_{i \in I} t_i$ for some family $\{t_i\}$. Then $a \wedge \bigvee_i t_i = a \ne 0$. By Lemma 3.3 (with $\{c_i\} = \{t_i\}$ as a chain — well, an arbitrary index set, but pass through directed-up version), there is a finite $F \subseteq I$ with $a \wedge \bigvee_{i \in F} t_i \ne 0$. Atomicity of $a$ then forces $a \wedge \bigvee_F t_i = a$, i.e. $a \le \bigvee_F t_i$. So $a$ is compact.

Inductive step $n \ge 2$. Take $a$ of length $n$. There is some $b \prec a$ of length $n - 1$. By induction hypothesis, $b$ is compact. Suppose $a \le \bigvee_I t_i$. Then $b \le a \le \bigvee_I t_i$, and compactness of $b$ gives a finite $F \subseteq I$ with $b \le \bigvee_F t_i$.

Now $a$ is an atom in $1/b$ (the interval above $b$), so by the base case applied within $1/b$, $a \le \bigvee_F (b \vee t_i)$ for some finite $F' \subseteq I$ with $b \le \bigvee_{F'} t_i$. Combining: $a \le b \vee \bigvee_{F'} t_i \le \bigvee_F t_i \vee \bigvee_{F'} t_i = \bigvee_{F \cup F'} t_i$, where $F \cup F'$ is finite. So $a$ is compact.

Why this matters

This proposition closes the conceptual loop with Chapter 2. Compact = "finite witness." Length = "finite chain to $0$." Both are finiteness conditions; in modular inductive lattices, finite length is one specific way to be compact. So elements of finite length live in the compact-elements ideal — they're a particularly well-behaved subset of compact elements with a numerical invariant attached.

SECTION A18 Proposition 3.2 — every chain refines to a composition chain

In a modular lattice of finite length, you can always insert intermediate elements to turn any chain into a composition chain.
Proposition 3.2

In a modular lattice of finite length, each chain admits a composition chain as refinement.

Why

By Schreier (Theorem 3.1), the given chain has an equivalent refinement common to itself and to any composition chain that exists. By Theorem 3.2 (finite length ⟺ noetherian + artinian), composition chains do exist in this lattice. The Schreier-built common refinement therefore goes "all the way down" — every step is a covering pair — i.e., it's itself a composition chain.

This is the natural completion of Schreier's theorem in the finite-length setting: not just "any two chains have equivalent refinements," but "any single chain refines all the way to a composition chain." The chain you started with is a "coarse" version; the refinement makes every step minimal.

A practical use

If you're trying to compute the length of an interval $b/a$, a useful approach is: (1) find any chain from $a$ to $b$, (2) refine it to a composition chain via Proposition 3.2. The number of new steps you've inserted, plus the original chain's length, is $\ell(b/a)$.

Part B · Irreducible decompositions

The horizontal invariant: meets of irreducibles

Where Part A measured an element by vertical climb (length of a composition chain to $0$), Part B measures it by horizontal decomposition (number of irreducible factors). Kuros–Ore makes this number invariant in modular lattices.

SECTION B1 Meet irreducible elements (= "irreducible," = "hollow")

An element that cannot be written as a meet of two strictly larger elements. The lattice analog of "prime" or "indecomposable."
Definition · meet irreducible

In a lattice $L$, an element $a$ is called meet irreducible (or briefly irreducible; or, in Chapters 12–13, hollow) if for each $b, c \in L$, $$b \wedge c = a \quad\Longrightarrow\quad b = a \text{ or } c = a.$$

Read this as: $a$ has no proper meet decomposition. If you write $a$ as a meet of two elements, one of them is forced to equal $a$ itself. Plainly: you can't strictly factor $a$ as a meet.

Examples

(1) The largest element $1$ is irreducible: $b \wedge c = 1$ requires $b = c = 1$ (in any bounded lattice).

(2) In a chain (totally ordered lattice), every element is irreducible: $b \wedge c = a$ in a chain forces $b \le a$ or $c \le a$, hence $b = a$ or $c = a$ by the trivial direction.

(3) In a Boolean lattice $\mathcal P(M)$ on a 3-element set $M = \{1, 2, 3\}$: irreducible elements are exactly the doubletons (and the top, $\{1,2,3\}$). The singletons $\{1\}$ are not irreducible because $\{1\} = \{1,2\} \cap \{1,3\}$ is a proper meet decomposition.

(4) In the submodule lattice of an $R$-module: an element is irreducible iff it cannot be written as the intersection of two strictly larger submodules. For a primary ideal in a commutative ring (e.g., $p^2 \mathbb Z$), the corresponding element is irreducible — this is why primary decomposition in commutative algebra naturally lives in this language.

Dual: join irreducible

An element $a$ is join irreducible if $b \vee c = a \Rightarrow b = a$ or $c = a$. This is the order-dual of meet irreducibility. In a power set $\mathcal P(M)$, join-irreducibles are the singletons (and the empty set $\emptyset$) — the building blocks under union. Meet-irreducibles are different: they're the "factor-of-1" elements that resist intersection.

The chapter focuses on meet irreducibility because that's what shows up in module-decomposition contexts (intersection of submodules, primary decomposition of ideals). For join-decomposition, swap meets and joins throughout.

SECTION B2 Irredundant meets and irreducible decompositions

An irredundant meet is one with no removable factors. An irreducible decomposition is an irredundant meet of irreducibles.
Definition · irredundant meet

A finite meet $\bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i$ is called irredundant if for every $i \in I$, $$\bigwedge_{j \in I, j \ne i} a_j > \bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i.$$ That is, dropping any factor strictly increases the meet.

Definition · irreducible decomposition

An expression $a = a_1 \wedge a_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_n$ is an irreducible decomposition of $a$ if (i) each $a_i$ is irreducible (in the sense of B1), and (ii) the meet is irredundant.

Two operations: irreducibility says each factor is indecomposable; irredundance says the decomposition is minimal — no factor is wasted.

Easy fact: every irreducible decomposition of $a$ is an irredundant meet of irreducibles, by definition. Conversely, any irreducible decomposition can be obtained from a (possibly redundant) finite meet of irreducibles by dropping redundant factors. So in passing from a finite meet of irreducibles to an irreducible decomposition, you only "trim" — never add.

Analogy with prime factorisation

Think of the prime factorisation of an integer $n$. Each prime is "irreducible" (can't be factored further). The factorisation $n = p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_k^{a_k}$ is "irredundant" — you can't drop any prime power without making the product smaller. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic says: this irredundant decomposition is unique. The Kuros–Ore theorem (next section) is the lattice analog: in modular lattices, the number of irreducible factors in an irreducible decomposition is invariant. (Stronger uniqueness — exact factor identity — fails in general; only the count is invariant.)

SECTION B3 Theorem 3.3 (Kuros–Ore) — uniqueness of decomposition length

In a modular lattice, all irreducible decompositions of the same element have the same number of factors.
Theorem 3.3 (Kuros–Ore)

Let $L$ be a modular lattice and $a \in L$. If $$a = b_1 \wedge b_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge b_n = c_1 \wedge c_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge c_m$$ are two irredundant meets of irreducible elements of $L$, then $n = m$.

Two important caveats. First: the theorem says only that the number of factors agrees, not that the factors themselves agree. The actual irreducibles $\{b_i\}$ and $\{c_j\}$ can differ. Second: the theorem requires both decompositions to be irredundant, not just irreducible factors — irredundance is what makes the count meaningful.

Why — exchange argument

The proof is the classical "exchange lemma" argument. Inductive setup: define $\bar b_i = b_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge b_{i-1} \wedge b_{i+1} \wedge \cdots \wedge b_n$ (the meet of all $b_j$ except $b_i$). Irredundance gives $a < \bar b_i$ and $a = b_i \wedge \bar b_i$.

Define $d_j = \bar b_i \wedge c_j$ for each $j$. Then $a \le d_j \le \bar b_i$ and $a \le c_j$, and $a = \bigwedge_j d_j$. The element $b_i$ is irreducible, and by modularity (using Theorem 1.5's diamond isomorphism), $b_i$ remains irreducible in the quotient $\bar b_i / a$. So among $\{d_j\}$, at least one $d_k$ equals $a$. That gives a "match" between $b_i$ and one of the $c_j$'s, in the sense that $a = b_i \wedge \bar b_i = \bar b_i \wedge c_k$ — so the index $i$ in the $\{b\}$ decomposition can be exchanged for index $k$ in the $\{c\}$ decomposition.

Iterating this exchange (Steinitz-style), each $b_i$ is matched to some $c_{\sigma(i)}$, giving an injection $\sigma: \{1, \ldots, n\} \to \{1, \ldots, m\}$. By symmetry the reverse injection exists, so $n = m$.

SECTION B4 Why modularity is essential — the non-modular counterexample

The Kuros–Ore theorem fails in non-modular lattices. The book displays a clean counterexample with two irreducible decompositions of different lengths.
Counterexample to Kuros–Ore in non-modular lattices

The theorem fails outside the modular setting. The book shows a 9-element non-modular lattice with an element having two irredundant meets of irreducibles, of different lengths (one with $n = 3$ factors, one with $m = 2$). Modularity is required.

The lesson: irreducible decomposition length is a robust invariant only in modular lattices. The book's counterexample demonstrates that without modularity, you genuinely can have decompositions of the same element with different lengths.

Where modularity enters the proof

The Kuros–Ore proof uses modularity twice: once when applying the diamond iso theorem to transport $b_i$'s irreducibility to the quotient $\bar b_i/a$, and again implicitly when using Theorem 1.5 to identify quotients. Both steps fail in non-modular lattices — the diamond iso doesn't hold, irreducibility doesn't transport — so the exchange argument breaks.

SECTION B5 Proposition 3.3 — existence of irreducible decompositions

In any noetherian lattice (modular or not), every element has a finite irreducible decomposition. So the question of existence is settled by ACC; uniqueness of length is settled by modularity.
Proposition 3.3

If $L$ is a noetherian lattice, each element of $L$ is a finite meet of irreducible elements.

Why — extremal-element argument

Let $A = \{x \in L : x$ is not a finite meet of irreducibles$\}$. We want to show $A = \emptyset$.

Suppose $A \ne \emptyset$. Since $L$ is noetherian, $A$ has a maximal element $m$. Then $m$ is itself not a meet of irreducibles. In particular $m$ is not irreducible, so there exist $x, y \in L$ with $m = x \wedge y$, $m < x$, $m < y$.

By maximality of $m$ in $A$, neither $x$ nor $y$ is in $A$ (since both are strictly bigger than $m$). So both $x$ and $y$ are finite meets of irreducibles. Then $m = x \wedge y$ is also a finite meet of irreducibles — contradicting $m \in A$.

The relationship of B5 + B3 + B4

Putting Propositions 3.3 + Theorem 3.3 + non-modular counterexample together: in a noetherian modular lattice, every element has an irreducible decomposition, AND the length of this decomposition is invariant. So Kuros–Ore holds with full force in noetherian modular lattices — exactly the natural class for module theory.

In non-noetherian or non-modular lattices, one or the other half fails: existence may fail (non-noetherian) or uniqueness may fail (non-modular). The book's primary class — submodule lattices over noetherian rings — has both properties.

SECTION B6 Lemma 3.4 — irreducibility through intervals

A technical lemma: irreducibility in an interval $b/0$ corresponds to irreducibility in the larger lattice $L$ via meeting with $b$.
Lemma 3.4

Let $L$ be a bounded upper continuous modular lattice, and $a \le b$ in $L$. Then $a$ is irreducible in $b/0$ if and only if there is an irreducible element $c$ in $L$ such that $a = b \wedge c$.

Read this as a "lift and project" rule. Forward direction: if $a$ is irreducible in the interval $b/0$, then there's an irreducible $c$ in the full lattice $L$ such that $a = b \wedge c$. Reverse direction: meeting an irreducible of $L$ with $b$ produces an irreducible of $b/0$.

Why · ($\Rightarrow$) — Zorn's Lemma in $\mathcal C$

Suppose $a$ is irreducible in $b/0$. Define $\mathcal C = \{x \in L : a = b \wedge x\}$. This is non-empty (contains $a$ itself, since $b \wedge a = a$ given $a \le b$). The upper continuity of $L$ ensures that the join of any chain in $\mathcal C$ stays in $\mathcal C$ (intersection commutes with directed joins).

By Zorn's Lemma, $\mathcal C$ has a maximal element $c$. Claim: $c$ is irreducible in $L$.

Suppose $c = u \wedge v$ for some $u, v \in L$. Then $a = b \wedge c = b \wedge u \wedge v = (b \wedge u) \wedge (b \wedge v)$. By irreducibility of $a$ in $b/0$ — applied to the meet $a = (b \wedge u) \wedge (b \wedge v)$ — we get $a = b \wedge u$ or $a = b \wedge v$. Either way, $u$ or $v$ is in $\mathcal C$. By maximality of $c$ in $\mathcal C$, this forces $c = u$ or $c = v$. So $c$ is irreducible.

$(\Leftarrow)$ The reverse direction uses modularity to verify $a = b \wedge c$ is irreducible in $b/0$ via the diamond isomorphism $b/(b \wedge c) \cong (b \vee c)/c$, which transports $c$'s irreducibility down.

A useful working principle

Lemma 3.4 says: irreducibility transfers cleanly through interval restriction (in modular upper-continuous lattices). When you want to find irreducibles of $b/0$, you can equivalently find irreducibles of $L$ (the full lattice) and meet with $b$. This is a useful reduction: typically the irreducibles of $L$ are easier to enumerate.

CONNECTION C1 Vector spaces and dimension

The simplest concrete instance: subspace lattice has length = dimension.
Length = dimension in subspace lattices

For any vector space $V$ over a field $k$, the lattice of all subspaces $L = \mathrm{Sub}(V)$ is modular (it satisfies the modular law because intersection and span behave like the algebraic identity). When $V$ is finite-dimensional, $L$ has finite length, and $\ell(V) = \dim V$.

The dimension function on subspaces — $\dim : \mathrm{Sub}(V) \to \mathbb N \cup \{\infty\}$ — is exactly the length function $\ell$ of $L$. The submodular inequality $\ell(U \vee W) + \ell(U \wedge W) \le \ell(U) + \ell(W)$ becomes the familiar dimension formula: $\dim(U + W) + \dim(U \cap W) = \dim U + \dim W$ (with equality, since $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$ is modular).

The classical proof: for finite-dimensional $V$, choose a basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$. The chain $0 \subset \langle e_1\rangle \subset \langle e_1, e_2\rangle \subset \cdots \subset V$ is a composition chain (every step is a $1$-dimensional extension, which has no proper non-trivial subspaces). By Jordan–Hölder, every composition chain has the same length $n = \dim V$.

The chapter's apparatus extends this picture to modules over arbitrary rings (Connection C2), groups (Connection C3), and arbitrary modular lattices.

Connection to flag varieties: the variety of all complete flags $0 \subset V_1 \subset V_2 \subset \cdots \subset V_n = V$ (where $\dim V_k = k$) is exactly the variety of all composition chains in $\mathrm{Sub}(V)$. The Schubert decomposition of the flag variety encodes how flags can refine.

CONNECTION C2 Module composition length

The classical analog: composition series for modules over a ring, with simple quotients as building blocks.
Composition length of a module

For an $R$-module $M$ over a ring $R$, a composition series is a chain $0 = M_0 \subset M_1 \subset \cdots \subset M_n = M$ of submodules where each quotient $M_k / M_{k-1}$ is a simple $R$-module (no proper non-trivial submodules). Equivalently, in the submodule lattice $S_R(M)$, this is a composition chain. Jordan–Hölder for modules: any two composition series of $M$ have the same length and the same multiset of simple factor modules (up to isomorphism).

The length $n$ — when finite — is called the composition length of $M$, written $\ell_R(M)$. By Theorem 3.2, $\ell_R(M)$ is finite iff $M$ is both noetherian and artinian as an $R$-module.

Examples:

  • Over a field $k$, every $k$-module is a vector space, simple modules are 1-dimensional, and $\ell_k(V) = \dim V$.
  • For $R = \mathbb Z$ and $M = \mathbb Z/p^n\mathbb Z$ (cyclic group of prime power order): the composition series $0 \subset \mathbb Z/p \subset \mathbb Z/p^2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathbb Z/p^n$, with each quotient $\cong \mathbb Z/p$ (simple). Length = $n$.
  • For $R = \mathbb Z$ and $M = \mathbb Z$ itself (the integers as a $\mathbb Z$-module): noetherian but not artinian, no finite composition series, $\ell(\mathbb Z)$ infinite.

The arrow finite-length module ⟹ finite-length lattice is one direction; conversely (in the other direction), finite-length lattices that arise as $S_R(M)$ correspond exactly to finite-length modules. This is the chapter's apparatus translated into module theory: finite length is the right finiteness condition for "modules built from simple pieces."

The Krull–Schmidt theorem (Chapter 4 of the book) builds on this: in a finite-length module, the decomposition into indecomposable summands is unique up to permutation. That theorem is the next-chapter sequel to Kuros–Ore.

CONNECTION C3 Group-theoretic Jordan–Hölder

The original Jordan–Hölder theorem, in its first home: finite groups.
Jordan–Hölder for groups (Camille Jordan 1869, Otto Hölder 1889)

For a finite group $G$, a composition series is a chain $$\{e\} = G_0 \triangleleft G_1 \triangleleft \cdots \triangleleft G_n = G$$ of normal subgroups (each $G_{k-1}$ normal in $G_k$, but not necessarily in $G$) with each quotient $G_k / G_{k-1}$ a simple group. Jordan–Hölder: any two composition series have the same length $n$ and the same multiset of simple factor groups (up to isomorphism).

The classification of finite simple groups (CFSG, completed in the late 20th century) lists all the possible simple factors — the periodic table of finite groups, in some sense. Jordan–Hölder says that every finite group is built from these simples in a unique-up-to-permutation way.

The lattice version (Corollary 3.1 of this chapter) is more general: it applies to any modular lattice. The lattice of normal subgroups of a group is modular (this is a classical fact), so Jordan–Hölder for groups follows immediately from the lattice version. But the lattice version also applies to module theory (Connection C2), to lattices of substructures of universal algebras, and to abstract modular lattices that don't arise from any group.

A key historical note

Jordan's 1869 theorem was for finite groups. Hölder strengthened it in 1889 to identify the simple factors up to isomorphism (not just count them). Schreier (1928) then proved that any two normal series can be refined to equivalent ones (the Schreier refinement theorem, our Theorem 3.1). The lattice generalization came later — Garrett Birkhoff and Marshall Hall in the 1930s pushed it into pure lattice theory, and Crawley–Dilworth's Algebraic Theory of Lattices (1973) gave it the modern lattice framing this chapter follows.

CONNECTION C4 Abelian categories and quiver representations

In modern algebra, finite-length objects in an abelian category satisfy Jordan–Hölder. Quiver representations give a vivid concrete instance.
Finite-length objects in abelian categories

An abelian category $\mathcal A$ is a category with kernels, cokernels, and exact sequences satisfying the same axioms as the category of modules over a ring. The notions of "subobject," "simple," and "composition series" all transfer. Jordan–Hölder for abelian categories: any two composition series of a finite-length object are equivalent (same length, same multiset of simple factors).

Quiver representations make this vivid. A quiver $Q$ is a directed graph with finite vertices and arrows. A representation assigns a vector space to each vertex and a linear map to each arrow. The category of representations $\mathrm{Rep}_k(Q)$ is abelian. For an acyclic quiver, finite-dimensional representations form a finite-length category, and the simple representations are exactly the "single-vertex, zero-arrow" representations — one for each vertex.

Gabriel's theorem (1972) classifies the quivers whose representation category has finitely many indecomposables: they are exactly the simply-laced Dynkin quivers ($A_n, D_n, E_6, E_7, E_8$). The number of indecomposables equals the number of positive roots of the corresponding Lie algebra. This is a deep and surprising connection between Jordan–Hölder–style decomposition and Lie theory.

The unifying picture

Anywhere there is an "abelian-category-like" setting with a notion of "finite length," Jordan–Hölder applies. This is one of the deepest unifying threads of mathematics: from group theory to module theory to vector spaces to quiver representations to coherent sheaves on a curve, the same chain-of-simples structure shows up. Călugăreanu's lattice formulation captures the essence — any modular lattice with finite length has the structure — and the algebraic instances are special cases.

CONNECTION C5 Prologos — composition chains as Hasse-path refinement

The chapter's apparatus connects to Prologos's Hyperlattice Conjecture: composition chains are the maximally refined Hasse paths, the optimal parallel decompositions of monotone information accumulation.

Prologos's Hyperlattice Conjecture states: every computable function is expressible as a fixpoint computation on lattices, and the Hasse diagram of the lattice is the optimal parallel decomposition of the computation. Composition chains, in this picture, are exactly the longest possible "step-by-step refinement paths" through the lattice — a maximally fine-grained schedule of how a cell value evolves from $\bot$ to its fixpoint.

Where Chapter 3's apparatus lives in Prologos

The length of the longest possible such update chain from $\bot$ to a final value is the "depth" — exactly the lattice's length $\ell$. For a finite, modular cell lattice (e.g., the QTT multiplicity lattice $\{m_0, m_1, m_\omega\}$), the length $\ell = 2$, and computations terminate in 2 BSP rounds in the worst case. See racket/prologos/mult-lattice.rkt.

(2) Jordan–Hölder ↔ confluence of monotone derivations. Two monotone derivations of the same final value (two composition chains in the cell's value lattice) are equivalent up to permutation by Corollary 3.1 — i.e., they use the same multiset of "single-step refinements." This is the lattice statement of confluence for monotone computations: any two paths to the same result use the same building blocks.

(3) Kuros–Ore ↔ direct-sum realisation B uniqueness. The chapter's irreducible decomposition is the abstract framework for Prologos's direct-sum decompositions — and the SRE Lattice Lens's "Module Theory Realization B" (bitmask-tagged layers on a shared carrier — see .claude/rules/structural-thinking.md) is exactly the lattice-theoretic structure that makes Kuros–Ore go through. A lattice with both $0$ (smallest) and $1$ (largest).

Ex. 3.1Subspace lattice — length = dimension

The lattice of all subspaces of a vector space has finite length if and only if it has finite dimension. In this case the length is exactly its dimension.

Solution

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $k$, and $L = \mathrm{Sub}(V)$ its subspace lattice. $L$ is modular (this was Theorem 1.5 / a basic fact of linear algebra).

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $L$ has finite length $n$, with composition chain $0 = V_0 \subset V_1 \subset \cdots \subset V_n = V$. Each step is a cover, so $V_k/V_{k-1}$ is a simple subspace — i.e., a 1-dimensional subspace. Pick a non-zero element $e_k \in V_k \setminus V_{k-1}$ for each $k$. Then $V_k = V_{k-1} \oplus \langle e_k\rangle$, and inductively $V = \langle e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n\rangle$. The set $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is linearly independent (any dependency would put some $e_k$ in $V_{k-1}$, contradiction), so it's a basis. Hence $\dim V = n$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $V$ has finite dimension $n$, with basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$. The chain $V_k = \langle e_1, \ldots, e_k\rangle$ is a composition chain of length $n$ (each step adds one basis vector). So $L$ has finite length, and by Jordan–Hölder $\ell(L) = n$.

Commentary

The cleanest possible instance of length-as-dimension. Note that the proof works over any field — the field doesn't matter. In particular, infinite-dimensional spaces over any field have infinite-length subspace lattices.

For modules over more general rings, "length" and "dimension" diverge (modules don't have a basis in general); composition length is the right generalisation. Connection C2 expands on this.

Ex. 3.2Finite-length lattice modular ⟺ both covering conditions

A finite length lattice is modular if and only if it satisfies both covering conditions.

(The "covering conditions" are the upper covering condition (UCC) and lower covering condition (LCC), which the book introduces in Chapter 1. UCC: $a \prec b \Rightarrow a \vee c \prec b \vee c$ or $a \vee c = b \vee c$. LCC: $a \prec b \Rightarrow a \wedge c \prec b \wedge c$ or $a \wedge c = b \wedge c$.)

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ A modular lattice satisfies both covering conditions automatically — this is a classical fact (the modular implication chain: modular ⟹ semimodular and "co-semimodular," giving both UCC and LCC).

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $L$ has finite length and satisfies both UCC and LCC. We use Lemma 3.2 to characterise modularity. Define $\ell : L \to \mathbb N$ as in Lemma 3.2 (well-defined under semimodular, and UCC + LCC implies semimodular).

From Lemma 3.2.4, modularity is equivalent to: $\ell(a \vee b) + \ell(a \wedge b) = \ell(a) + \ell(b)$ for every $a, b$. Take any $a, b \in L$, and choose covering chains $a \wedge b \prec x_1 \prec \cdots \prec x_p = a$ (length $p = \ell(a) - \ell(a \wedge b)$). At each step, applying UCC with $c = b$: $x_{k-1} \vee b \prec x_k \vee b$ or $x_{k-1} \vee b = x_k \vee b$. Climbing from $a \wedge b$ to $a$, the joins climb correspondingly: $b = (a \wedge b) \vee b \le \cdots \le a \vee b$. The number of "covering steps" along this climb is $\le p$. Symmetrically by LCC, descending from $a \vee b$ to $b$ via meets uses $\le \ell(a) - \ell(a \wedge b) = p$ steps as well. The two together force $\ell(a \vee b) - \ell(b) = p = \ell(a) - \ell(a \wedge b)$, i.e. the modular equality.

Commentary

The exercise is the converse to a classical fact: modular ⟹ both covering conditions. Without the finite-length hypothesis the converse can fail (you can have lattices satisfying both covering conditions that aren't modular). Finite length is what lets us use $\ell$ as a tool.

This exercise also shows why the "submodular equality" framing of modularity is so useful — modularity becomes a local-to-global lifting from cover-pairs to all pairs.

Ex. 3.3Finite length ⟹ bounded

Verify that each lattice of finite length is bounded.

Solution

"Bounded" means the lattice has both a smallest element $0$ and a largest element $1$.

If $L$ has finite length, then $L$ has a composition chain $a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_n$. Without loss of generality, this chain is maximal (if not, refine it; refinement doesn't lose composition-ness). The smallest element of the chain, $a_0$, has nothing below it in $L$ — otherwise $L$ would have an element $x < a_0$ and the chain $x < a_0 < \cdots$ would extend, contradicting that $a_0$ was the chain's start in a finite-length lattice. So $a_0 = 0$. Symmetrically $a_n = 1$. Hence $L$ is bounded.

Commentary

This is a useful warm-up that justifies our convention throughout the chapter: "lattice of finite length" implies $0$ and $1$ exist, so $\ell(L) = \ell(1/0)$ is well-defined. The exercise confirms the assumption is automatic, not an additional hypothesis we have to add.

Ex. 3.4Jordan–Dedekind condition without length function

A poset satisfies the Jordan–Dedekind condition if for each two elements $a \le b$, all maximal chains between $a$ and $b$ have the same length. Give an example of a finite poset which satisfies the Jordan–Dedekind condition but has no length function.

Solution

The Jordan–Dedekind condition says: well-defined chain lengths between any pair of comparable elements. The "length function" $\ell : P \to \mathbb N$ requires $\ell(a) = $ length of the longest chain from a "bottom" up to $a$. For this to be a function, the poset must be bounded below (have a $0$).

Counterexample: the poset $P = \{a, b, c, d\}$ with $a < c, a < d, b < c, b < d$ (no comparable pair across $\{a, b\}$ or $\{c, d\}$). This is a "diamond minus the top" or 2x2 antichain crossbar.

Maximal chains are exactly $\{a, c\}, \{a, d\}, \{b, c\}, \{b, d\}$ — all length 1. Jordan–Dedekind: trivially satisfied (each pair has the same length).

But: there's no $0$ — both $a$ and $b$ are minimal, and they're incomparable. So $\ell(c) = ?$ — depends on whether you measure from $a$ or from $b$. Since $\ell(a) = \ell(b) = 0$ (or both undefined), and $\ell(c) = 1$ from $a$ or $1$ from $b$ — we can define $\ell$ consistently here ($\ell = 1$ for $c, d$), but the lattice analog (length of $1/0$) doesn't apply.

A cleaner example: take the poset of finite multisets of two elements, with the multiset $[2,0]$ and $[0,2]$ as minima — also no length function from "0".

The simplest example that genuinely has no length function: any poset that is not bounded below. Take $\{a, b\}$ with $a$ incomparable to $b$ and no element below them. Trivially Jordan–Dedekind (all maximal chains have length 0); has no length function because there's no canonical bottom.

Commentary

The exercise highlights that "length function" is a convenience that requires the poset to have a $0$. Jordan–Dedekind is the more general property — well-definedness of chain lengths between pairs, regardless of a global anchor. Modular lattices satisfy Jordan–Dedekind globally (Corollary 3.1) and have a $0$ (in our setting), so they have a length function.

Ex. 3.5Find irreducible elements of the given lattice

Find the irreducible elements of the following lattice (the book displays a 6-element diamond-and-extra-point lattice with elements $0, a, b, c, d, e, 1$ where $0 \prec a \prec c \prec 1$, $0 \prec b \prec e \prec 1$, $0 \prec d$ in the middle, and $d \prec c, d \prec e$).

Solution

The lattice diagram from p. 37 shows: $0$ at the bottom, $a$ and $b$ as atoms on the left and right, $d$ as a middle atom, $c$ above $a$ and $d$, $e$ above $b$ and $d$, and $1$ at the top covering both $c$ and $e$. Hasse diagram: a 6-element bipyramid-like shape.

To find meet-irreducibles: an element is irreducible iff it cannot be written as $b' \wedge c'$ with $b', c' > $ itself.

$1$: trivially irreducible (no element strictly above; $b' \wedge c' = 1$ requires $b' = c' = 1$).

$c$: in the lattice, $c$ has only one element strictly above it (namely $1$), so any meet $b' \wedge c' = c$ with $b', c' > c$ requires $b' = c' = 1$. But $1 \wedge 1 = 1 \ne c$. So $c$ is irreducible (no proper meet representation).

$e$: by symmetry, irreducible.

$d$: above $d$ in the lattice are $c$, $e$, and $1$. We have $c \wedge e = d$ (the meet of two distinct elements above $d$ in the diamond). This is a proper meet decomposition, so $d$ is not irreducible.

$a$: above $a$ are $c$ and $1$. $c \wedge 1 = c \ne a$. Are there other elements above $a$? From the diagram, $a$ has only $c$ as immediate cover. The only meet that lands on $a$ would require two distinct elements both above $a$, but $a$'s upper-set is the chain $\{a, c, 1\}$, so no such pair exists. $a$ is irreducible.

$b$: by symmetry, irreducible.

$0$: $0 = a \wedge b$ (or $0 = a \wedge d$, etc.), so $0$ is not irreducible.

Irreducible elements: $\{a, b, c, e, 1\}$. Non-irreducibles: $\{0, d\}$.

Commentary

The exercise is a small finger-exercise in identifying irreducibles. The pattern: an element is irreducible iff it has no two distinct elements strictly above it whose meet equals it. For elements with linearly ordered upper-set (like atoms with a single tower above), they're automatically irreducible. The middle element $d$ in this diagram is the prototype of a non-irreducible element — it's a "joining point" where two different chains converge.

Ex. 3.6Lattice is a chain ⟺ all elements irreducible

Show that a lattice is a chain (totally ordered) if and only if all its elements are irreducible.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $L$ is a chain. Take any $a \in L$ and suppose $a = b \wedge c$. In a chain, $b$ and $c$ are comparable; assume $b \le c$. Then $b \wedge c = b$, so $a = b$. Done.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose every element is irreducible. Take any two elements $b, c \in L$. We want to show $b \le c$ or $c \le b$. Their meet is $a = b \wedge c$. By irreducibility of $a$, the equation $b \wedge c = a$ forces $b = a$ or $c = a$, i.e. $b = b \wedge c$ (so $b \le c$) or $c = b \wedge c$ (so $c \le b$). Either way, $b$ and $c$ are comparable. So $L$ is a chain.

Commentary

This is a clean characterisation of chains via irreducibility — a lattice is "as un-decomposable as possible" iff it has no off-diagonal interactions, i.e. it's a chain. This characterisation also gives an instant feel for what irreducibility means: it's a "no-non-trivial-meet" property, which becomes universal exactly in a totally ordered setting.

Ex. 3.7Length of an epimorphic image is bounded

Prove that the length of an epimorphic image of a lattice by an order morphism is less than or equal to the length of the lattice (domain).

Solution

Let $\varphi: L \to L'$ be an order epimorphism (surjective order-preserving map). Suppose $L$ has finite length $n$, with composition chain $0 = a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_n = 1$ in $L$.

Apply $\varphi$: $\varphi(a_0) \le \varphi(a_1) \le \cdots \le \varphi(a_n)$ in $L'$. This is a chain (possibly with repetitions, since $\varphi$ doesn't have to be injective). Removing repetitions gives a chain of length $\le n$ in $L'$. Since $\varphi$ is surjective, $\varphi(0) = 0_{L'}$ and $\varphi(1) = 1_{L'}$, so this chain runs from $0_{L'}$ to $1_{L'}$.

Refining if necessary (by Proposition 3.2), this chain refines to a composition chain of $L'$ of some length $\ell(L')$. The refined chain still has $\le n$ steps coming from the originals plus $0$ or more new ones from refinement; but the refined chain has at most as many steps as a maximal chain in $L'$, which is $\ell(L')$.

Wait — careful. The refined chain has more steps if we add inserted elements. So we need the bound $\ell(L') \le n$ from a different angle. Argument: any chain in $L'$ pulls back through $\varphi^{-1}$ to a chain in $L$ (preimages of comparable elements are comparable, and a chain of length $m$ in $L'$ pulls back to a chain of length $\ge m$ in $L$). Since $\ell(L) = n$, every chain in $L$ has length $\le n$, so every chain in $L'$ has length $\le n$, i.e. $\ell(L') \le n = \ell(L)$.

Commentary

The exercise is a sanity check: order morphisms can "collapse" structure but cannot "create" it. The length of an image is at most the length of the source. This is the lattice analog of "the dimension of a quotient is at most the dimension of the parent" for vector spaces — a basic fact about how surjective maps interact with rank.

Ex. 3.8Locally finite length and the second isomorphism inequality

A poset has locally finite length if each interval has finite length. Verify that if $a, b, c$ are elements in a modular lattice of locally finite length and $a \le b$ then $\ell(b/a) \ge \ell((b \wedge c)/(a \wedge c))$ or $\ell(b/a) \ge \ell((b \vee c)/(a \vee c))$.

Solution

This is the lattice version of the inequality "dimension drops under restriction." Let $\varphi: b/a \to (b \vee c)/(a \vee c)$ be the map $x \mapsto x \vee c$. By the modular law (since $a \le x \le b$), this map is well-defined and order-preserving. By the diamond isomorphism (Section A5), $b/a$ is isomorphic to a quotient of $(b \vee c)/(a \vee c)$ — so $\ell(b/a) \ge \ell((b \vee c)/(a \vee c))$.

Symmetrically, $\psi : b/a \to (b \wedge c)/(a \wedge c)$, $x \mapsto x \wedge c$, gives $\ell(b/a) \ge \ell((b \wedge c)/(a \wedge c))$.

Both inequalities hold simultaneously (not just one or the other) — but the exercise's "or" reflects that either inequality is enough for the working principle.

Commentary

This is the "second-isomorphism-inequality" — the modular-lattice analog of the rank-nullity theorem. In a modular lattice, the diamond isomorphism gives equalities $\ell(b/a) = \ell((b \vee c)/(a \vee c))$ when $b/a \cong (b \vee c)/(a \vee c)$, but in general we only get $\ge$ (the meet/join versions of $b/a$ might be strictly shorter). The exercise codifies the inequality, which is useful when modularity is in play but exact equality isn't certain.
