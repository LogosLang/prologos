Free Lattices

From abstract universal-algebra construction to concrete combinatorial structure: the free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$, Whitman's algorithm for the word problem, and the surprising appearance of the semidistributive laws.

~85–95 min read 10 sections + 5 connections 11 worked exercises PDF pp. 62–74

Freedom's just another word for nothing left to lose ….

— Kris Kristofferson, Me and Bobby McGee (Janis Joplin, 1971); chapter epigraph chosen by Nation

If $x$, $y$, and $z$ are elements of a lattice, then $x \vee (y \vee (x \wedge z)) = x \vee y$ is always true, while $x \vee y = z$ is usually not. Is there an algorithm that, given two lattice expressions $p$ and $q$, decides whether $p = q$ holds for every substitution of the variables in every lattice? The answer is yes. The algorithm — and finding it is the original motivation for the entire chapter — falls out of the structure of the free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$.

The chapter has two distinct flavours. The existence proof (Theorem 6.1) is a cathedral of universal algebra — three "Basic Principles" that are really the First and Second Isomorphism Theorems and a subdirect-product lemma, all from Chapter 5 — applied to the word algebra $W(X)$ of formal lattice expressions. It is abstract, structural, and works verbatim for any nontrivial equational class. The combinatorial structure (Theorems 6.2 onward) is concrete and surprising. Whitman in 1941 (and Skolem already in 1920, though forgotten) proved that membership of $\text{FL}(X)$ is governed by six conditions, the deepest being the Whitman condition

$$\text{(W)}\qquad p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2 \iff p_1 \le q_1 \vee q_2 \;\;\text{or}\;\; p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2 \;\;\text{or}\;\; p \le q_1 \;\;\text{or}\;\; p \le q_2.$$

Day's 1970 doubling construction $\mathcal{L}[I]$ gives a slick proof. From these conditions we extract: a unique canonical form for each element (minimal-length term, unique up to associativity/commutativity); a refinement quasiorder on finite subsets; the surprising fact that free lattices satisfy the semidistributive laws $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$ and $\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}$; and a deep structure theorem (Nation 1982): a finite lattice embeds in a free lattice if and only if it satisfies $(W)$, $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$, and $\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}$.

Free lattices have a paradoxical character. $\text{FL}(2)$ has only four elements; $\text{FL}(3)$ is already infinite. In a precise sense: every countable ordered set embeds in $\text{FL}(3)$, but no uncountable chain embeds in any free lattice. The chapter sets the tools — Day's doubling, Whitman's six conditions, semidistributivity — that recur throughout the rest of lattice theory.

VOCABULARY PRIMER Setting expectations before the chapter

A three-pane glance: terms carried forward from earlier chapters, terms introduced new in Chapter 6, and the deep dives the chapter rewards close reading on.
Carried forward · from Chs 1–5

The chapter assumes the language of lattices ($\mathcal{L}$, $\vee$, $\wedge$, sublattice, lattice morphism, embedding, modular, distributive, $\mathcal{N}_5$, $\mathcal{M}_3$ — Chs 1, 2), the subalgebra closure operator $\text{Sg}$ from algebraic lattices (Ch 3), the partition-lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ and the principle that every lattice embeds in some $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ (Ch 4), and from Chapter 5 the entire machinery of congruences $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ — including First / Second Isomorphism Theorems, principal congruence $\text{con}(a, b)$, and Birkhoff's subdirect representation. Theorem 6.1's proof literally invokes Theorems 5.1, 5.4, and 5.5 (Birkhoff subdirect) under the new names "Basic Principles 1, 2, 3" — universal-algebra forms of those lattice-specific theorems. Also recalled: join irreducible $J(\mathcal{L})$, weakly atomic, cover relation $\prec$ (Ch 1), and the 2-element lattice $\textbf{2}$ used as the codomain for separating homomorphisms.

New · Chapter 6 vocabulary

The new vocabulary clusters around four concepts. Free generation: a lattice $\mathcal{F}$ is freely generated by $X \subseteq F$ if $\mathcal{F}$ is a lattice, $X$ generates $\mathcal{F}$, and every set-map $h_0 : X \to L$ to any lattice extends uniquely to a lattice homomorphism $h : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{L}$. Such an $\mathcal{F}$ is unique up to isomorphism — call it $\text{FL}(X)$. Special case $|X| = n$: $\text{FL}(n)$. Word algebra: $W(X)$ — the absolutely-free algebra of formal lattice expressions on $X$. Whitman's condition (W): the central inequality governing membership in $\text{FL}(X)$. Day's doubling: for an interval $I = u/v$ in $\mathcal{L}$, the new lattice $\mathcal{L}[I] = (L - I) \cup (I \times \mathbf{2})$ "doubles" the interval into two parallel copies linked by $i \le j$ in $\mathbf{2}$. Refinement: $A \ll B$ if every $a \in A$ has $b \in B$ with $a \le b$. Canonical form: the minimal-length term representing $w \in \text{FL}(X)$, unique up to associativity and commutativity. Canonical joinands / meetands: the components of $w$ in its canonical form. Semidistributive laws: $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}: u = a \vee b = a \vee c \Rightarrow u = a \vee (b \wedge c)$ — and the dual $\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}$.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. The word problem and its history — Skolem 1920 (rediscovered by Cosmadakis & Freese in the late 1980s, popularised by Burris 1995), Whitman 1941, the modern Freese-Ježek-Nation algorithms in the 1995 book; the modular-lattice word problem is undecidable for $|X| \ge 4$ (Hutchinson, Lipshitz, Freese — covered in Ch 7). Day's doubling construction as a structural template — splitting lattices, bounded-homomorphic images, congruence normality (Day's later papers), the all-or-nothing inflation. The semidistributive laws and the Jónsson conjecture — semidistributivity introduced by Jónsson in 1961 in his work on free lattices; Theorem 6.9 (Nation 1982) proves a finite lattice embeds in a free lattice iff $(W) + \text{SD}_\vee + \text{SD}_\wedge$. The Freese-Ježek-Nation 1995 monograph Free Lattices is the canonical reference for everything in this chapter.

PART A · GENERATION AND EXISTENCE

Free generation, the word algebra, and Theorem 6.1

What does it mean for $X$ to freely generate a lattice? Why does such a thing exist? And why does the answer come from universal algebra rather than lattice theory proper?

§ 6.1 Generation and free generation

Two concepts that sound similar but differ profoundly: generated by and freely generated by.
Definition · generation

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is generated by a set $X \subseteq L$ if no proper sublattice of $\mathcal{L}$ contains $X$. Equivalently, in the language of Chapter 3's subalgebra closure operator $\text{Sg}$: $\text{Sg}(X) = \mathcal{L}$.

Definition · free generation

A lattice $\mathcal{F}$ is freely generated by $X$ if

  1. $\mathcal{F}$ is a lattice,
  2. $X$ generates $\mathcal{F}$,
  3. for every lattice $\mathcal{L}$, every map $h_0 : X \to L$ can be extended to a lattice homomorphism $h : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{L}$.

A free lattice is a lattice that is freely generated by one of its subsets.

Condition (I) is "sort of redundant" — Nation's phrasing — but is included so that the construction in Theorem 6.1 lands inside the variety of lattices, not merely inside an algebra of $\vee$ and $\wedge$ that happens to satisfy the lattice axioms. In the presence of (II), there is at most one extension $h$ in (III): if $x, y, z \in X$, then $h(x \vee (y \wedge z))$ is forced to be $h_0(x) \vee (h_0(y) \wedge h_0(z))$. The content of (III) is that this natural extension is well defined — equivalently, that two lattice terms in the variables $X$ are equal in $\mathcal{F}$ if and only if they are equal in every lattice.

Intuition · "no accidental equations"

$\mathcal{F}$ is free on $X$ when the only equations between $X$-built terms that hold in $\mathcal{F}$ are those that hold by lattice axioms alone — never by some accidental coincidence forced on $\mathcal{F}$. Equivalently: $\mathcal{F}$ is a "universal worst case" for $X$. Whatever inequality fails to hold for some specific lattice with $X$-image fails in $\mathcal{F}$ too.

Free generation is a purely universal property. It tells you nothing about how to compute meets and joins inside $\mathcal{F}$ — and indeed Theorem 6.1's proof yields an existence statement without describing $\text{FL}(X)$'s arithmetic at all. That arithmetic is the subject of Theorems 6.2 onward.

Recap · varieties / equational classes

An equational class (or variety) is the class of all algebras over a fixed signature satisfying a fixed set of equations. Lattices form a variety: the signature is $\{\vee, \wedge\}$ (both binary), the equations are idempotency, commutativity, associativity, and absorption. Free distributive lattices, free modular lattices, free Arguesian lattices all exist by the same general result, since each of these classes is defined by adding more equations. The variety is nontrivial if it contains an algebra with more than one element — the equation $x \approx y$ (which forces a single-element algebra) defines the trivial variety.

§ 6.2 Existence: Theorem 6.1

The existence of $\text{FL}(X)$ as a corollary of three "Basic Principles" — universal-algebra forms of the isomorphism theorems and the subdirect-representation theorem from Chapter 5.
Theorem 6.1 · existence of free lattices

For any nonempty set $X$, there exists a free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$ generated by $X$.

The proof uses three results re-stated for arbitrary algebras. They correspond, for lattices, to Theorems 5.1, 5.4, and 5.5 — the First Isomorphism Theorem, a Second-Iso variant, and Birkhoff's subdirect representation. Their lattice proofs use nothing special about $\vee$ and $\wedge$. The same arguments work for any nontrivial variety, so Theorem 6.1's argument is really about free algebras in any equational class, with lattices as the running example.

Basic Principle 1

If $h : \mathcal{A} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{B}$ is a surjective homomorphism, then $\mathcal{B} \cong \mathcal{A}/\ker h$. (First Iso for arbitrary algebras.)

Basic Principle 2 · the lifting lemma

If $f : \mathcal{A} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{B}$ and $g : \mathcal{A} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{C}$ are surjective homomorphisms with $\ker g \le \ker f$, then there exists $h : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{B}$ such that $f = h \circ g$.

Basic Principle 3 · subdirect product

If $\psi = \bigwedge_{i \in I} \theta_i$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$, then $\mathcal{A}/\psi$ is isomorphic to a subalgebra of the direct product $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{A}/\theta_i$.

The construction

Given the set $X$, define the word algebra $W(X)$ to be the set of all formal expressions (strings of symbols) generated by:

  1. $X \subseteq W(X)$,
  2. if $p, q \in W(X)$, then $(p \vee q)$ and $(p \wedge q)$ are in $W(X)$,
  3. only the expressions given by the first two rules are in $W(X)$.

$W(X)$ is the absolutely free algebra with operation symbols $\vee$ and $\wedge$ generated by $X$ — it has no equations imposed beyond the formal closure rules. Two terms in $W(X)$ are equal iff they are identical strings. Idempotency $x \vee x = x$, commutativity $x \vee y = y \vee x$, absorption $x \wedge (x \vee y) = x$ all fail in $W(X)$ (they identify distinct strings). $W(X)$ is, however, generated by $X$ and has the universal property (III) for all algebras of signature $\{\vee, \wedge\}$.

To produce a lattice, identify those pairs of terms that evaluate the same in every lattice. Concretely: let $\Lambda = \{\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,W(X) : W(X)/\theta \text{ is a lattice}\}$, and let $\lambda = \bigwedge \Lambda$.

Why $W(X)/\lambda$ is the answer

By Basic Principle 3 applied to $\lambda = \bigwedge \Lambda$, $W(X)/\lambda$ embeds in the product $\prod_{\theta \in \Lambda} W(X)/\theta$. Each factor is a lattice (by definition of $\Lambda$), so the product is a lattice, so $W(X)/\lambda$ — being a subalgebra of a product of lattices — is a lattice. (This step uses that lattices are equationally defined: subalgebras of products of lattices are lattices.) Clearly $W(X)/\lambda$ is generated by $\{x\lambda : x \in X\}$. And because there exist nontrivial lattices, $x \neq y$ in $X$ implies $x\lambda \neq y\lambda$ — the natural map $X \to W(X)/\lambda$ is injective.

The mapping property. Given $\mathcal{L}$ and $f_0 : X \to L$, define $h_0 : X/\lambda \to L$ by $h_0(x\lambda) = f_0(x)$ — well-defined by the preceding paragraph. Extend to $f : W(X) \to \mathcal{L}$ via $W(X)$'s universal property (it's absolutely free). The image of $f$ is some sublattice $\mathcal{S} \le \mathcal{L}$; by Basic Principle 1, $W(X)/\ker f \cong \mathcal{S}$, so $\ker f \in \Lambda$, hence $\ker f \ge \lambda$. By Basic Principle 2 applied with $f : W(X) \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{S}$ and $\varepsilon : W(X) \twoheadrightarrow W(X)/\lambda$, there is $h : W(X)/\lambda \to \mathcal{L}$ with $h \circ \varepsilon = f$. This $h$ extends $h_0$.

Intuition · the construction in one phrase

Take all formal expressions ($W(X)$); identify two whenever every lattice identifies them ($\lambda$); the result is a lattice ($W(X)/\lambda$) that is a "universal worst case" for $X$. Each step is one of the three Basic Principles.

It is easy to see, using property (III), that two free lattices on sets of the same cardinality are isomorphic. We can therefore speak of the free lattice on $X$; we denote it $\text{FL}(X)$, or $\text{FL}(n)$ when $|X| = n$.

$\text{FL}(2)$ vs $\text{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 3$

$\text{FL}(2)$ has just four elements: $x \wedge y < x, y < x \vee y$. It is the diamond minus the middle bar — the four-element distributive lattice. $\text{FL}(3)$, by contrast, is infinite. The chapter's Theorem 6.10 will refine this dramatically: every countable ordered set embeds in $\text{FL}(3)$.

Deep dive · why this works for any variety

Theorem 6.1's proof uses no properties special to lattices except that the operation symbols are $\vee$ and $\wedge$ — and these can be replaced with arbitrary operation symbols. With this minor modification, the proof shows that every nontrivial equational class admits free algebras on arbitrary nonempty sets. This is one face of Birkhoff's HSP theorem (1935): a class of algebras is a variety iff it is closed under homomorphic images, subalgebras, and products. A class so closed automatically admits free algebras, and the universal property (III) is exactly the adjoint of the forgetful functor variety → set. Equational logic, the variety, the free algebra, and Birkhoff's HSP closure are four faces of the same object. Lattice theory inherits the existence of free distributive, free modular, and free Arguesian lattices automatically — though as Nation notes "there is no free lunch": the general construction tells us nothing about arithmetic.

PART B · WHITMAN'S SOLUTION

Six conditions, the doubling construction, and the word problem

From abstract existence to concrete arithmetic: Whitman 1941 (Skolem 1920, forgotten) characterises $\text{FL}(X)$ by six elementary conditions, and Day's 1970 doubling construction supplies a slick proof of the deepest one.

§ 6.3 Theorem 6.2: six conditions, including (W)

A characterisation of inequalities in $\text{FL}(X)$ — directly recursive, eventually reducing every question to comparisons among generators.
Theorem 6.2 · Whitman 1941

Every free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$ satisfies the following conditions, where $x, y \in X$ and $p, q, p_1, p_2, q_1, q_2 \in \text{FL}(X)$.

  1. $x \le y$ iff $x = y$.
  2. $x \le q_1 \vee q_2$ iff $x \le q_1$ or $x \le q_2$.
  3. $p_1 \wedge p_2 \le x$ iff $p_1 \le x$ or $p_2 \le x$.
  4. $p_1 \vee p_2 \le q$ iff $p_1 \le q$ and $p_2 \le q$.
  5. $p \le q_1 \wedge q_2$ iff $p \le q_1$ and $p \le q_2$.
  6. $p = p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2 = q$ iff $p_1 \le q$ or $p_2 \le q$ or $p \le q_1$ or $p \le q_2$.

Finally, $p = q$ iff $p \le q$ and $q \le p$.

Condition (6) is Whitman's condition, denoted $(W)$. Conditions (1)–(5) are routine — (4) and (5) hold by definition of LUB and GLB in any lattice; (1)–(3) say generators behave as one would hope. The work is in (6).

Conditions (1)–(5): the easy part

Properties (4) and (5) hold in every lattice. The "if" parts of the remaining conditions hold in every lattice as well. Only the "only if" parts of (1), (2), (3), (6) require freeness.

To prove (1) and (2) simultaneously: fix $x \in X$, and let

$$G_x = \{w \in \text{FL}(X) : w \ge x \text{ or } w \le \bigvee F \text{ for some finite } F \subseteq X - \{x\}\}.$$

Then $X \subseteq G_x$ (every $y \in X$ either equals $x$ or lies in $\bigvee\{y\} \subseteq \bigvee(X - \{x\})$), and $G_x$ is closed under joins and meets. So $G_x = \text{FL}(X)$ — i.e., every $w$ is either above $x$ or below some finite join from $X - \{x\}$.

Properties (1) and (2) follow if this "or" is exclusive: $x \not\le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq X - \{x\}$. To see this, define $h_0 : X \to \mathbf{2}$ by $h_0(x) = 1$ and $h_0(y) = 0$ for $y \in X - \{x\}$. By freeness, $h_0$ extends to $h : \text{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$. For finite $F \subseteq X - \{x\}$ we have $h(\bigvee F) = 0 \neq 1 = h(x)$, hence $x \not\le \bigvee F$.

Condition (3) is the dual of (2), proved by replacing $\vee$ with $\wedge$ throughout.

Condition (W): the depth

Condition (6) — the Whitman condition — says: if $p = p_1 \wedge p_2$ and $q = q_1 \vee q_2$ in $\text{FL}(X)$, then $p \le q$ only by one of the four obvious reasons: $p_1 \le q$, $p_2 \le q$, $p \le q_1$, $p \le q_2$. No "miracle" inequality — none arising from algebra alone — can hold in $\text{FL}(X)$. If $p \wedge q \le r \vee s$ with no covering inequality, then $\text{FL}(X)$ refuses to satisfy that inequality.

Intuition · why (W) is the heart of free

An inequality $p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2$ is the most general non-trivial inequality between meet and join. For it to hold in every lattice, one of the four trivial reasons must apply. (W) is the assertion that $\text{FL}(X)$ refuses any non-trivial coincidence at exactly this depth. Compare this to a generic non-distributive lattice $\mathcal{N}_5$ or $\mathcal{M}_3$: there, $p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2$ can hold for "exotic" reasons. Free lattices contain $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$ as quotients but do not satisfy their relations.

The "if" direction of (W) is trivial: if any of $p_1 \le q$, $p_2 \le q$, $p \le q_1$, $p \le q_2$ holds, then by transitivity and join-/meet-monotonicity, $p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2$. The "only if" — that no other path is possible — is what Day's construction proves.

§ 6.4 Day's doubling construction

Given an interval $I = u/v$ in $\mathcal{L}$, build a new lattice $\mathcal{L}[I]$ that "doubles" the interval — a tool of unreasonable utility, due to Alan Day (1970).
Definition · interval doubling $\mathcal{L}[I]$

Let $I = u/v$ be an interval in a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ (so $v \le u$). Define $\mathcal{L}[I]$ to have universe $(L - I) \cup (I \times \mathbf{2})$, where $\mathbf{2} = \{0, 1\}$ with $0 < 1$. Order: for $x, y \in L - I$ and $i, j \in \{0, 1\}$,

$$x \le y \quad\text{if } x \le_{\mathcal{L}} y,$$ $$(x, i) \le y \quad\text{if } x \le_{\mathcal{L}} y,$$ $$x \le (y, j) \quad\text{if } x \le_{\mathcal{L}} y,$$ $$(x, i) \le (y, j) \quad\text{if } x \le_{\mathcal{L}} y \text{ and } i \le j.$$

Geometrically, $\mathcal{L}[I]$ replaces the interval $I$ inside $\mathcal{L}$ with two parallel copies — the $i = 0$ "lower copy" and the $i = 1$ "upper copy." Connections inside $\mathcal{L}$ outside $I$ are unchanged; pairs in $I$ are split; the natural map $\kappa : \mathcal{L}[I] \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{L}$ sending $(x, i) \mapsto x$ and $x \mapsto x$ on $L - I$ is a surjective lattice homomorphism.

Intuition · the doubling construction in one image

Imagine $\mathcal{L}$ drawn as a Hasse diagram; an interval $I$ lights up. Cut along the boundary of $I$, make a parallel copy of $I$, glue both copies in such that $(I, 0)$ sits where $I$ was and $(I, 1)$ sits "in front of it" with $(x, 0) \le (x, 1)$. Top and bottom of $I$ are doubled; everything outside $I$ is untouched.

Day's proof of (W)

Suppose for contradiction that $a, b, c, d$ witness a failure of $(W)$ in $\text{FL}(X)$ — that is, $a \wedge b \le c \vee d$, but none of $a \le c \vee d$, $b \le c \vee d$, $a \wedge b \le c$, $a \wedge b \le d$ holds. Let $u = c \vee d$, $v = a \wedge b$, $I = u/v$ (a non-degenerate interval since $v \le u$ but $v \neq u$). Build $\mathcal{L}[I]$.

Define $h_0 : X \to \text{FL}(X)[I]$ by $h_0(x) = x$ if $x \notin I$, and $h_0(y) = (y, 0)$ if $y \in I$. By freeness extend to $h : \text{FL}(X) \to \text{FL}(X)[I]$. Now $\kappa h : \text{FL}(X) \to \text{FL}(X)$ is a homomorphism; since $\kappa h(x) = x$ for all $x \in X$, it is the identity. So $h(w) \in \kappa^{-1}(w)$ for all $w$. Each $w \in \text{FL}(X)$ lies above one of $w$, $(w, 0)$, $(w, 1)$. Since $a, b, c, d \notin I$, $h(t) = t$ for $t \in \{a, b, c, d\}$. Now $v = a \wedge b \le c \vee d = u$ in $\text{FL}(X)$, so $h(v) \le h(u)$ in $\text{FL}(X)[I]$. But:

$$h(v) = h(a) \wedge h(b) = a \wedge b = (v, 1) \not\le (u, 0) = c \vee d = h(c) \vee h(d) = h(u),$$

a contradiction. Hence $(W)$ holds in $\text{FL}(X)$. $\square$

Intuition · why doubling works for (W)

The doubling construction gives us a homomorphic image where $v$ has been "lifted" from $u$ — pushed into the upper copy $(v, 1)$ — but $u$ remains at the bottom of its doubled interval $(u, 0)$. This artificially separates $v$ and $u$ in the new lattice. If $(W)$ were to fail by a non-trivial path in $\text{FL}(X)$, freeness would force the failure to persist in $\text{FL}(X)[I]$ as well — but the doubling has cut that path. Contradiction.

Recap · the doubling family

Day's doubling — interval $I$ — was generalised to convex subsets ("doubling of convex sets in lattices and a generalised semidistributivity condition"), to all-or-nothing sets, and to arbitrary subsets in various ways. It became the central technique for studying splitting lattices, bounded homomorphic images of free lattices, and congruence normality. The Freese-Ježek-Nation 1995 monograph Free Lattices uses doubling repeatedly. Alan Day kept returning to the construction throughout his career — its simplicity hides remarkable depth.

§ 6.5 Solution to the word problem

The corollary that gives Theorem 6.2 its original motivation.
Corollary · the word-problem algorithm

Let $p, q \in W(X)$. To decide whether $p \le q$ in $\text{FL}(X)$, apply the conditions of Theorem 6.2 recursively. To test whether $p = q$, check both $p \le q$ and $q \le p$.

The algorithm is direct. Given $p \le q$:

  • If $p, q \in X$ — apply (1).
  • If $p \in X$ and $q = q_1 \vee q_2$ — apply (2): reduce to $p \le q_1$ or $p \le q_2$.
  • If $p = p_1 \wedge p_2$ and $q \in X$ — apply (3): reduce to $p_1 \le q$ or $p_2 \le q$.
  • If $p = p_1 \vee p_2$ — apply (4): reduce to $p_1 \le q$ AND $p_2 \le q$.
  • If $q = q_1 \wedge q_2$ — apply (5): reduce to $p \le q_1$ AND $p \le q_2$.
  • If $p = p_1 \wedge p_2$ and $q = q_1 \vee q_2$ — apply (W) (6): reduce to $p_1 \le q$ OR $p_2 \le q$ OR $p \le q_1$ OR $p \le q_2$.

The algorithm terminates because the total length of the input pair $(p, q)$ strictly decreases at each recursive step. At the leaves, the comparisons reduce to "$x \le y$ for $x, y \in X$," which by (1) is "$x = y$" — a finite check.

Significance. The word problem is not always solvable for free algebras. Hutchinson and Lipshitz showed that the word problem for free modular lattices $\mathcal{F}_M(X)$ is undecidable for $|X| \ge 4$ (covered in Ch 7). Whitman's algorithm exploits the lack of "extra equations" beyond lattice axioms — adding modularity breaks termination of the analogous reduction.

Deep dive · Skolem's priority and Burris's rediscovery

Thoralf Skolem's 1920 paper Logisch-kombinatorische Untersuchungen über die Erfüllbarkeit und Beweisbarkeit mathematischer Sätze — best known for the Löwenheim-Skolem theorem — also solved the word problem not only for free lattices but for finitely-presented lattices as well. By the 1930s, when lattice theory awakened, Skolem's solution had been forgotten; Whitman 1941 became the standard reference. Cosmadakis and (independently) Freese rediscovered Skolem's polynomial-time algorithm in the late 1980s; Stanley Burris's 1995 paper Polynomial Time Uniform Word Problems documented the rediscovery and proved efficiency. The original Whitman algorithm is exponential in the worst case; the Skolem-Cosmadakis-Freese algorithm is polynomial. Both decide the same problem.

PART C · CANONICAL FORM AND REFINEMENT

From the word algorithm to a unique normal form

Each element of $\text{FL}(X)$ is represented by an equivalence class of terms, but among them is a canonical minimal-length representative — unique up to associativity and commutativity.

§ 6.6 Theorem 6.3: characterisation of free generation

Whitman's six conditions, plus two additional characterising properties of generators.
Theorem 6.3

A lattice $\mathcal{F}$ is freely generated by its subset $X$ if and only if $\mathcal{F}$ is generated by $X$, $\mathcal{F}$ satisfies $(W)$, and the following two conditions hold for each $x \in X$:

  1. if $x \le \bigvee G$ for some finite $G \subseteq X$, then $x \in G$;
  2. if $x \ge \bigwedge H$ for some finite $H \subseteq X$, then $x \in H$.

Conditions (1) and (2) of Theorem 6.3 say that every generator $x$ is both completely join prime and completely meet prime with respect to its co-generators. They isolate exactly the conditions that generators in a free lattice satisfy, beyond the structural condition $(W)$.

Theorem 6.3 has a useful structural consequence. From $(W)$ it follows that no element of $\text{FL}(X)$ is properly both a meet and a join: if $w = w_1 \wedge w_2 = w_3 \vee w_4$, then $(W)$ applied to "$w_1 \wedge w_2 \le w_3 \vee w_4$" gives one of the four cases — but each case forces a degeneracy that contradicts properness. Equivalently, every element is either join-irreducible or meet-irreducible. The generators are the only elements that are both meet- and join-irreducible.

Intuition · the generating set of $\text{FL}(X)$ is unique

This is unusual. The free group on $\{x, y\}$ is also generated by $\{x, xy\}$ — generators are interchangeable. Free lattices are different: their generating set is determined by the structure (it is exactly the doubly-irreducible elements). $\text{FL}(X)$ "remembers" its $X$.

Comparison: $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ vs $\text{FL}(X)$

Two great Cayley-style theorems frame lattice theory: every lattice embeds into $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ (Whitman 1946 / Jónsson, Ch 4), and every lattice is a homomorphic image of $\text{FL}(X)$ for some $X$ (this chapter). The first says lattices are subobjects of equivalence-relation lattices; the second says lattices are quotients of free lattices. Both are universal in opposite senses — every lattice's structure is encoded as a particular embedding into $\textbf{Eq}\,X$, or as a particular quotient $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$.

§ 6.7 Refinement and minimal-length terms

A pre-order on finite subsets and the Lemmas 6.4–6.6 connecting it to canonical-form analysis.
Definition · refinement

Let $A, B$ be finite subsets of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$. We say $A$ refines $B$, written $A \ll B$, if for each $a \in A$ there exists $b \in B$ with $a \le b$. Dually, $C \gg D$ if for each $c \in C$ there exists $d \in D$ with $c \ge d$.

Refinement formalises the notion "$A$'s elements are dominated by $B$'s" — without requiring equality. Note the reversed quantifier between $A \ll B$ and $B \gg A$ : the two relations are not the same.

Lemma 6.4 · structure of minimal-length terms

Let $t = \bigvee t_i$ in $W(X)$, where each $t_i$ is either a generator or a meet. Assume $\varepsilon(t) = w$ and $\varepsilon(t_i) = w_i$ under the evaluation map $\varepsilon : W(X) \to \text{FL}(X)$. If $t$ is a minimal-length term representing $w$, then:

  1. each $t_i$ is of minimal length;
  2. the $w_i$'s are pairwise incomparable;
  3. if $t_i$ is not a generator (so $t_i = \bigwedge_j t_{ij}$), then $\varepsilon(t_{ij}) = w_{ij} \not\le w$ for all $j$.

Property (3) is the heart. Suppose $w_i = \bigwedge w_{ij}$ in $\text{FL}(X)$ and $w_{i j_0} \le w$ for some $j_0$. Then $w \ge w_i \ge w_{i j_0}$, so $w = w_{i j_0} \vee \bigvee_{k \neq i} w_k$ — a shorter term, contradicting minimality.

Lemma 6.5 · refinement quasiorder

The refinement relation $\ll$ on the finite subsets of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has the following properties:

  1. $A \ll B$ implies $\bigvee A \le \bigvee B$;
  2. $\ll$ is a quasiorder on the finite subsets of $L$;
  3. if $A \subseteq B$, then $A \ll B$;
  4. if $A$ is an antichain, $A \ll B$ and $B \ll A$, then $A \subseteq B$;
  5. if $A$ and $B$ are antichains with $A \ll B$ and $B \ll A$, then $A = B$;
  6. if $A \ll B$ and $B \ll A$, then $A$ and $B$ have the same set of maximal elements.

(2) — the relation is a quasiorder, but not a partial order; many distinct sets can refine each other in both directions. (5) — among antichains, $\ll$ becomes antisymmetric. This is the key property linking refinement to canonical form: the maximal antichain inside any equivalence class is unique.

Lemma 6.6 · refinement chain

Let $w = \bigvee_{1 \le i \le m} w_i = \bigvee_{1 \le k \le n} u_k$ in $\text{FL}(X)$. If each $w_i$ is either a generator or a meet $w_i = \bigwedge_j w_{ij}$ with $w_{ij} \not\le w$ for all $j$, then $\{w_1, \dots, w_m\} \ll \{u_1, \dots, u_n\}$.

Lemma 6.6 connects the structure of minimal-length joinands (Lemma 6.4(3)) to the refinement order (Lemma 6.5). It is the bridge to uniqueness.

§ 6.8 Theorem 6.7: canonical form

Each element of $\text{FL}(X)$ has a unique minimal-length representative term.
Theorem 6.7 · canonical form

The minimal-length term representing an element $w \in \text{FL}(X)$ is unique up to associativity and commutativity.

Proof sketch

Let $t = \bigvee t_i$ and $s = \bigvee s_j$ be two minimal-length terms with $\varepsilon(t) = \varepsilon(s) = w$. By Lemma 6.4(1), each $t_i$ is minimal-length for its $\varepsilon(t_i) = w_i$, and each $s_j$ is minimal-length for its $\varepsilon(s_j) = u_j$. By induction on length, these are unique up to AC. So we may assume $t_i = s_j$ whenever $w_i = u_j$. By Lemma 6.4(2), the sets $\{w_i\}$ and $\{u_j\}$ are antichains. By Lemma 6.4(3), the elements $\{w_i\}$ satisfy the hypothesis of Lemma 6.6, so $\{w_1, \dots, w_m\} \ll \{u_1, \dots, u_n\}$. Symmetrically, $\{u_1, \dots, u_n\} \ll \{w_1, \dots, w_m\}$. Lemma 6.5(5) yields $\{w_1, \dots, w_m\} = \{u_1, \dots, u_n\}$, hence $\{t_1, \dots, t_m\} = \{s_1, \dots, s_n\}$. So the joinands match up to AC. $\square$

Definition · canonical form, joinands, meetands

The minimal-length term representing $w$ is the canonical form of $w$. If $w = \bigvee w_i$ canonically, the elements $w_i$ are the canonical joinands of $w$; dually, $\bigwedge w_j$ canonically gives canonical meetands. By the proof of Lemma 6.6, the canonical joinands of $w$ refine any other expression $w = \bigvee U$.

Corollary · refinement of joinands

If $w$ is a proper join in $\text{FL}(X)$ and $w = \bigvee U$, then the set of canonical joinands of $w$ refines $U$.

The corollary will be the lever for Theorem 6.8 (semidistributivity).

Algorithm for canonical form

The proof of Lemma 6.4 gives a constructive algorithm. Let $t = \bigvee t_i$ in $W(X)$ where each $t_i$ is a generator or a meet. Inductively put each $t_i$ into canonical form. For each $t_i = \bigwedge t_{ij}$ that is not a generator: check whether $t_{ij} \le t$ in $\text{FL}(X)$ for any $j$; if so, replace $t_i$ by $t_{ij}$. Continue until condition (3) of Lemma 6.4 holds. Finally, check whether $u_i \le u_j$ for $i \neq j$; if so, delete $u_i$. The result is the canonical form. The Whitman word-problem algorithm provides each $\le$ test.

Intuition · canonical form as decision procedure

An element of $\text{FL}(X)$ is identified with its canonical-form term. Equality is identity of canonical forms (up to AC). Order $w \le w'$ is decided by Whitman's algorithm. Thus $\text{FL}(X)$ is fully computable — every question about $\text{FL}(X)$'s arithmetic reduces to a finite computation on canonical forms.

PART D · STRUCTURAL CONSEQUENCES

Semidistributivity, embeddability, and weak atomicity

The canonical-form / refinement machinery yields four substantial structural results: free lattices satisfy the semidistributive laws (Theorem 6.8 — Jónsson 1961), characterise their finite sublattices (Theorem 6.9 — Nation 1982), embed every countable poset (Theorem 6.10), and are weakly atomic (Theorem 6.11).

§ 6.9 Theorem 6.8: the semidistributive laws

A weakening of distributivity, satisfied by free lattices, that becomes a major structural concept in the modern theory.
Theorem 6.8 · Jónsson 1961

Free lattices satisfy the following implications, for all $u, v, a, b, c \in \text{FL}(X)$:

$$\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}\quad \text{if } u = a \vee b = a \vee c, \text{ then } u = a \vee (b \wedge c),$$ $$\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}\quad \text{if } v = a \wedge b = a \wedge c, \text{ then } v = a \wedge (b \vee c).$$

The implications $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$ and $\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}$ are the semidistributive laws.

Proof of $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$

Suppose $u = a \vee b = a \vee c$ in $\text{FL}(X)$. We may assume $u$ is a proper join (otherwise $u = a$ and $u = a \vee (b \wedge c)$ trivially). Let $u = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_n$ be the canonical join decomposition.

By the Corollary above, the canonical joinands $\{u_1, \dots, u_n\}$ refine both $\{a, b\}$ and $\{a, c\}$. Hence each $u_i$ satisfies $u_i \le a$ or $u_i \le b$, and likewise $u_i \le a$ or $u_i \le c$. Any $u_i$ not below $a$ must be below both $b$ and $c$ — hence below $b \wedge c$. So $\{u_1, \dots, u_n\} \ll \{a, b \wedge c\}$. By Lemma 6.5(1), $u = \bigvee u_i \le a \vee (b \wedge c)$. The reverse $a \vee (b \wedge c) \le u$ is automatic. $\square$

Intuition · semidistributive ≠ distributive

$\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$ says: if two joins of $a$ with different things equal the same $u$, then the join with their meet also equals $u$. Distributivity says: $a \vee (b \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge (a \vee c)$ — a full distribution law. Semidistributivity is properly weaker. The pentagon $\mathcal{N}_5$ fails $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$ (in $\mathcal{N}_5$, $a \vee b = a \vee c$ but $a \vee (b \wedge c) = a \vee 0 = a$, properly below $u$); $\mathcal{M}_3$ fails $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$ similarly. So distributive ⟹ semidistributive, and free lattices are semidistributive (but not distributive — they contain non-distributive sublattices).

Theorem 6.8 has profound consequences. Semidistributivity is now recognized as one of the central structural concepts in modern lattice theory. Convex geometries are exactly the duals of finite join-semidistributive lattices (Edelman, Jamison; Adaricheva, Gorbunov, Tumanov 2003); the fundamental theorem of finite semidistributive lattices (Reading-Speyer-Thomas 2019) gives a bijection between finite congruence-uniform semidistributive lattices and certain combinatorial structures. Free lattices were the original example.

§ 6.10 Theorems 6.9, 6.10, 6.11: embeddability and atomicity

Three structural theorems: when does a finite lattice embed in some free lattice; what ordered sets can; and the weak-atomicity property.

Theorem 6.9 — finite sublattices of free lattices

Theorem 6.9 · Nation 1982

A finite lattice can be embedded in a free lattice if and only if it satisfies $(W)$, $\text{(SD}_\vee\text{)}$, and $\text{(SD}_\wedge\text{)}$.

The "only if" direction is automatic: free lattices satisfy all three (Theorem 6.2 + 6.8), and these are inherited by sublattices. The "if" direction — the deep one — is from J. B. Nation's 1982 Trans. AMS paper Finite sublattices of a free lattice. The proof confirms a conjecture of Bjarni Jónsson from his 1961 paper introducing semidistributivity. The result is a culmination: it identifies exactly three identities/quasi-identities that capture finite-sublattice membership in a free lattice.

R. A. Dean in 1956 had shown that every equation failing in some lattice fails in some finite lattice. Theorem 6.9 thus also characterises which finite lattices "live inside the free world," sharpening Dean's general result for sublattices.

Theorem 6.10 — chains and dimension

Theorem 6.10 · Crawley-Dean / Jónsson / Nation-Schmerl

Every countable ordered set can be embedded in $\text{FL}(3)$. On the other hand, every chain in a free lattice is countable, so no uncountable chain can be embedded in a free lattice. If $\mathcal{P}$ is an infinite ordered set that can be embedded in a free lattice, then the dimension $d(\mathcal{P}) \le \mathfrak{m}$, where $\mathfrak{m}$ is the smallest cardinal such that $|\mathcal{P}| \le 2^\mathfrak{m}$.

Three results in one. Crawley & Dean (1959): countable posets embed in $\text{FL}(3)$. Jónsson (1961): chains in free lattices are countable. Nation & Schmerl (1990): dimension bound. Together they give a remarkable picture: $\text{FL}(3)$ is "as wide as countably possible" but cannot accommodate uncountable chains; for very large posets, the order dimension is bounded by the cardinality.

Intuition · the paradox of $\text{FL}(3)$

$\text{FL}(3)$ has only countably many elements, yet every countable poset embeds in it. So $\text{FL}(3)$ contains a copy of the rationals, a copy of every finite tree, a copy of every recursive ordinal below $\omega_1^{\text{CK}}$. By contrast, an uncountable chain cannot fit. The constraint is exactly the chain length, not the overall size: $\text{FL}(3)$'s width is $\aleph_0$, but its depth (longest chain) is also $\aleph_0$.

Theorem 6.11 — weak atomicity

Theorem 6.11

If $X$ is finite, then $\text{FL}(X)$ is weakly atomic.

Recall (Ch 3): a lattice is weakly atomic if every interval $a > b$ contains a covering pair $u \succ v$ with $a \ge u \succ v \ge b$. The proof leverages Day's doubling: given $a > b$ in $\text{FL}(X)$, doubling the interval $a/b$ produces a covering pair $(b, 0) \prec (b, 1)$ in $\text{FL}(X)[a/b]$, which projects back to a covering pair in $\text{FL}(X)$. Theorem 6.11 implies $\text{FL}(X)$ is rich in cover relations — every comparable pair has a refining cover.

For infinite $X$, weak atomicity can fail. The proof of weak atomicity uses finiteness of $X$ in an essential way (via Skolem-style reasoning on finite generating subsets).

Recap · the canonical reference

The book Free Lattices by Freese, Ježek, and Nation — Mathematical Surveys and Monographs vol. 42, AMS 1995 — contains far more information about the surprisingly rich structure of free lattices. Its first part is a complete exposition of the basic theory; its second part covers projective lattices, bounded homomorphic images, Whitman's W-condition refinements, and concludes with a full analysis of algorithms. It is the canonical reference. Two later papers by Ralph Freese (1989, 1990) extend the analogous structure theory to finitely-presented lattices.

PANEL P2 Birkhoff's varieties theorem and free algebras

The universal-algebra context: free lattices are one face of the HSP theorem.
Birkhoff 1935 · the HSP theorem

Garrett Birkhoff's 1935 paper On the structure of abstract algebras (Proc. Cambridge Phil. Soc. 31, 433–454) established the theorem now known as Birkhoff's HSP theorem or the variety theorem: a class of algebras of fixed signature is a variety (= equational class) if and only if it is closed under homomorphic images ($H$), subalgebras ($S$), and direct products ($P$). Every nontrivial variety contains a free algebra on every nonempty set.

Theorem 6.1's proof is the variety case for lattices, but the argument is identical for any nontrivial variety. The Basic Principles 1–3 are universal-algebra forms of standard isomorphism results — they apply to any algebra of any signature.

Where free lattices fit in the variety landscape

  • Variety of all lattices: free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$ is the focus of this chapter.
  • Variety of distributive lattices: $\mathcal{F}_D(X) = 2^{2^X}$ — the lattice of antichains in the Boolean lattice $2^X$. Finite for finite $X$. Studied by Birkhoff, Dilworth.
  • Variety of modular lattices: $\mathcal{F}_M(X)$ — Dedekind 1900 computed $|\mathcal{F}_M(3)| = 28$. Word problem undecidable for $|X| \ge 4$.
  • Variety of Arguesian lattices: $\mathcal{F}_A(X)$ — strictly between modular and distributive; the structure is delicate.
  • Variety of all Heyting algebras: free Heyting algebras have the same structure as proof terms in intuitionistic propositional logic — the Diego-Köhler theorem / Curry-Howard correspondence.

Each free algebra encodes the free deductive structure of the corresponding equational logic. The free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$ is the "Lindenbaum-Tarski algebra" of pure lattice theory.

PANEL P3 Day's doubling lineage

A modest construction with surprising staying power.
Doubling · the family of constructions

Alan Day introduced interval doubling in 1970 to give a slick proof of $(W)$. The idea — replace an interval by two parallel copies — turned out to have remarkable depth.

  • 1970 (Day, Canad. Math. Bull. 13): interval doubling, simple proof of (W). The original.
  • 1977 (Day, Algebra Universalis 7): Splitting lattices generate all lattices. A finite lattice is splitting iff it is a bounded image of $\text{FL}(X)$.
  • 1979 (Day, Canad. J. Math. 31): characterisation of finite bounded-homomorphic images of free lattices using doubling iteratively.
  • Convex set doubling (Day's later work): generalising from intervals to convex subsets — needed for the SD generalisation in Doubling convex sets in lattices and a generalised semidistributivity condition (Order, 1989).
  • Congruence normality (Day 1994 + Nation addendum): doubling characterises a congruence-theoretic property called congruence normality.
  • All-or-nothing inflation: more recent generalisation by Adaricheva-Nation (2017) for studying convex geometries.

Why doubling is structurally fundamental

Doubling preserves the lattice structure outside the doubled interval and cleanly separates the interval into two layers. It is dual-symmetric (handles meet and join uniformly), composes with itself (you can double again and again), and respects sublattice / homomorphism / congruence relations in controlled ways. As Joel Berman observed: "Alan was particularly proud of his doubling construction because it is a method which is simultaneously powerful and simple, with subtleties that go beyond the surface." That description captures why it remains in active use.

PANEL P4 Semidistributivity and the Jónsson conjecture

From a Lemma about free lattices to a major modern structural theory.
Semidistributivity · the modern story

Bjarni Jónsson introduced the semidistributive laws in his 1961 work on free lattices (Sublattices of a free lattice, Canad. J. Math. 13). His conjecture: a finite lattice embeds in a free lattice iff it satisfies $(W) + \text{SD}_\vee + \text{SD}_\wedge$. Settled by Nation 1982 — Theorem 6.9.

Semidistributive lattices in modern combinatorics and algebra

  • Convex geometries (Edelman 1980, Jamison 1980): a closure system $(X, \tau)$ is a convex geometry iff $\tau$ is "anti-exchange," and these are exactly the duals of finite join-semidistributive lattices. Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov (2003): join-semidistributive lattices are characterised as quasi-varieties of convex geometries.
  • Cluster algebras and Cambrian lattices (Reading 2007): the Cambrian lattices arising from cluster algebras are congruence-uniform semidistributive lattices. Free distributive structure on a finite root system.
  • Fundamental theorem of finite semidistributive lattices (Reading-Speyer-Thomas 2019): a bijection between finite congruence-uniform semidistributive lattices and certain combinatorial data (positive labellings of join-irreducibles). A vast generalisation of Birkhoff's representation theorem for finite distributive lattices.
  • Lattice of regions of a hyperplane arrangement (Björner-Edelman-Ziegler 1990): the weak order on a Coxeter group is congruence-uniform semidistributive. The connection runs through Tits' theorem.
  • Stacked simplicial complexes: triangulations of polytopes give finite SD lattices.

The chapter's quiet contribution

Theorem 6.8 in Nation's chapter is a 1.5-page proof using only the canonical-form / refinement machinery already built. From this proof grew an entire structural theory. The notes in Nation's Free Lattices book and ongoing papers connect SD-lattices to nearly every active corner of finite lattice theory: clusters, convex geometries, Coxeter combinatorics, simplicial complexes.

PANEL P5 Prologos cross-references

Prologos's compiler takes WS-mode (whitespace-mode) `.prologos` files through a sequence of intermediate representations: tree-parser → preparse → elaboration. Solution. By the partial order definition, every pair of elements has a meet and a join. Six cases by where the elements live: $L - I$ vs $L - I$, $L - I$ vs $I \times \mathbf{2}$, $I \times \mathbf{2}$ vs $I \times \mathbf{2}$. For instance, if $x \in L - I$ and $(y, j) \in I \times \mathbf{2}$:

  • If $y \le_{\mathcal{L}} x$, then $x \vee_{\mathcal{L}[I]} (y, j) = x \vee_{\mathcal{L}} y$. Where this lives depends on whether the result is in $I$.
  • If $x \le_{\mathcal{L}} y$, then $x \le (y, j)$ in the new order, so $x \vee (y, j) = (y, j)$.
  • Otherwise, $x \vee_{\mathcal{L}} y$ is in $L - I$, and we identify $x \vee (y, j) = x \vee_{\mathcal{L}} y$.

Similar case-analysis for meets, and for $(x, i) \vee (y, j)$ in $I \times \mathbf{2}$. The natural map $\kappa : \mathcal{L}[I] \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{L}$ sending $(x, i) \mapsto x$ is a surjective lattice homomorphism — confirming the structure is consistent. In particular, lattice axioms (idempotency, commutativity, associativity, absorption) all transfer from $\mathcal{L}$ to $\mathcal{L}[I]$.

Use the doubling construction to repair the $(W)$-failures in the lattices in Figure 6.5. (Don't forget to double elements that are both join and meet reducible.) Then repeat the process until you either obtain a lattice satisfying $(W)$, or else prove that you never will get one in finitely many steps.

Solution. Figure 6.5(a) is a hexagon-with-diagonal that fails $(W)$ at, e.g., $a \wedge b \le c \vee d$. Ex. 6.3Canonical form computation
(a) Show that $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z) \not\le y \vee (z \wedge (x \vee y))$ in $\text{FL}(X)$. (b) Find the canonical form of $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z))$. (c) Find the canonical form of $(x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z))) \vee (y \wedge z)$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Apply Whitman's algorithm. We need to disprove $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z) \le y \vee (z \wedge (x \vee y))$. The LHS is a meet $p_1 \wedge p_2$ where $p_1 = x$, $p_2 = (x \wedge y) \vee z$. The RHS is a join $q_1 \vee q_2$. Apply (W): we need to falsify $p_1 \le q$, $p_2 \le q$, $p \le q_1$, $p \le q_2$. Each case reduces by recursion to comparisons of generators or finite expressions. The key check: $x \not\le y \vee (z \wedge (x \vee y))$ in $\text{FL}(X)$ — use $h_0 : X \to \mathbf{2}$ with $h_0(x) = 1$, $h_0(y) = h_0(z) = 0$ to separate. Since the LHS evaluates to 1 and the RHS to 0, we have a separating homomorphism. The other cases also fail. Thus $(W)$ shows $\le$ does not hold.
  • (b) $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z))$. By absorption, $(x \wedge y) \le x$, so applying (W)/(SD) repeatedly: in any lattice, $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ (modular law applies because of the common factor $x$). The canonical form is $(x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$.
  • (c) Similar: leveraging that $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)) \ge (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ by inclusion, then taking $\vee (y \wedge z)$ outside. The canonical form is $(x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$.

Note that part (a)'s inequality is equivalent to "not every lattice is distributive" — the ranges where these expressions evaluate distinctly are exactly the non-distributive lattices.

Ex. 6.4Five small lattices failing $\text{SD}_\vee$ minimally
There are five small lattices that fail $\text{SD}_\vee$, but have no proper sublattice failing $\text{SD}_\vee$. Find them.

Solution. The minimal $\text{SD}_\vee$-failures are five small lattices of order $\le 6$:

  1. $\mathcal{M}_3$ — the diamond. $a \vee b = a \vee c = 1$, but $a \vee (b \wedge c) = a \vee 0 = a < 1$.
  2. $\mathcal{N}_5$ — the pentagon. With its central element configured so that the $\vee = $ collapse occurs.
  3. The "fence" lattice with 5 elements arranged in a zigzag pattern.
  4. The "brushed" diamond — $\mathcal{M}_3$ with one extra element between $0$ and one of $a, b, c$.
  5. A specific 6-element non-modular lattice with a particular cover structure.

The complete enumeration is in Day's papers. The construction proceeds: take any candidate failure, check its proper sublattices for $\text{SD}_\vee$ failure; if all proper sublattices satisfy $\text{SD}_\vee$, the candidate is a "minimal failure." Five such lattices exist up to isomorphism. They serve as the structural building blocks of $\text{SD}_\vee$-violation: a finite lattice fails $\text{SD}_\vee$ iff it contains one of these as a sublattice.

Ex. 6.5Equivalent characterisations of $\text{SD}_\vee$
Show that the following conditions are equivalent (to $\text{SD}_\vee$) in a finite lattice. (a) $u = a \vee b = a \vee c$ implies $u = a \vee (b \wedge c)$. (b) For each $m \in M(\mathcal{L})$ there is a unique $j \in J(\mathcal{L})$ such that for all $x \in L$, $m^* \wedge x \not\le m$ iff $x \ge j$. (c) For each $a \in L$, there is a set $C \subseteq J(\mathcal{L})$ such that $a = \bigvee C$, and for every subset $B \subseteq L$, $a = \bigvee B$ implies $C \ll B$. (d) $u = \bigvee_i u_i = \bigvee_j v_j$ implies $u = \bigvee_{i,j}(u_i \wedge v_j)$.

Solution sketch. The four conditions characterise $\text{SD}_\vee$ for finite lattices. Each takes a different perspective:

  • (a) is the original semidistributive law.
  • (b) gives a 1-1 correspondence between meet-irreducibles $M(\mathcal{L})$ and join-irreducibles $J(\mathcal{L})$ via the "splitter" $j$ — this is the perspective of congruence-uniform lattices.
  • (c) is the canonical-decomposition characterisation: every element has a unique-up-to-refinement set of canonical joinands. The set $C$ is what's called the Jónsson-Kiefer canonical joinands in the finite case.
  • (d) is the binary distributive expansion: any two expressions for $u$ as joins admit a common refinement via meets.

The proof that (a) ⟺ (b) ⟺ (c) ⟺ (d) is by case-analysis: (a) ⟺ (d) by induction on length of joins; (a) ⟺ (c) by the corollary of Theorem 6.7 (canonical joinands refine any expression); (b) ⟺ (a) by interpreting $j = $ canonical joinand of $m^* / m$. Reference: Jónsson and Kiefer 1962, Finite sublattices of a free lattice, Canad. J. Math. 14, 487–497.

Ex. 6.6Join prime in finite SD$_\vee$ lattices
An element $p \in L$ is join prime if $p \le x \vee y$ implies $p \le x$ or $p \le y$; meet prime dually. Let $\text{JP}(\mathcal{L})$ denote the set of join-prime elements and $\text{MP}(\mathcal{L})$ the set of meet-primes. Consider a finite lattice $\mathcal{L}$ satisfying $\text{SD}_\vee$. (a) Prove that the canonical joinands of $1$ are join-prime. (b) Prove that the coatoms of $\mathcal{L}$ are meet-prime. (c) Show that for each $q \in \text{MP}(\mathcal{L})$ there exists a unique element $\eta(q) \in \text{JP}(\mathcal{L})$ such that $L$ is the disjoint union of $\downarrow q$ and $\uparrow\eta(q)$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Suppose $1 = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_n$ canonically. Each $u_i$ is join-irreducible (Lemma 6.4(2): the joinands are pairwise incomparable). For join-prime: suppose $u_i \le x \vee y$. By the corollary of Thm 6.7, the canonical joinands of $u_i \vee x \vee y$ refine both $\{u_i, x\}$ and $\{u_i, y\}$. Apply $\text{SD}_\vee$ structurally: any joinand of $u_i \vee x \vee y$ that's not below $u_i$ must be below both $x$ and $y$. Since $u_i$'s "block" is irreducible, this forces $u_i \le x$ or $u_i \le y$. So $u_i$ is join-prime.
  • (b) A coatom $m$ has $m^* = 1$. Since $m$ is meet-irreducible (only one upper cover, namely $1$), and the dual of (a) shows meet-irreducibles in $\text{SD}_\wedge$ are meet-prime — and in finite SD$_\vee$ lattices the dual SD$_\wedge$ also holds (the implications are dual-symmetric at this level), coatoms are meet-prime.
  • (c) By (a) and the duality, $\text{JP}(\mathcal{L})$ and $\text{MP}(\mathcal{L})$ are in bijection — the bijection is: $q \in \text{MP}$ corresponds to the join-prime $\eta(q)$ characterised by "$\eta(q) \not\le q$ but $\eta(q) \le 1$." The disjoint union $\downarrow q \cup \uparrow\eta(q) = L$ follows from join-primeness of $\eta(q)$ and meet-primeness of $q$.
Ex. 6.7Prove Lemma 6.5
Prove Lemma 6.5: the refinement relation $\ll$ on finite subsets of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has the six properties stated.

Solution outline.

  • (1) $A \ll B \Rightarrow \bigvee A \le \bigvee B$. For each $a \in A$, choose $b_a \in B$ with $a \le b_a$. Then $a \le b_a \le \bigvee B$ for each $a \in A$, so $\bigvee A \le \bigvee B$.
  • (2) $\ll$ is a quasiorder. Reflexivity: $A \ll A$ — take $b = a$ for each $a$. Transitivity: if $A \ll B$ via $a \to b_a$ and $B \ll C$ via $b \to c_b$, then $A \ll C$ via $a \to c_{b_a}$.
  • (3) $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow A \ll B$. For $a \in A \subseteq B$, take $b = a$.
  • (4) $A$ antichain, $A \ll B$, $B \ll A \Rightarrow A \subseteq B$. For $a \in A$: $a \le b$ for some $b \in B$ (by $A \ll B$), and $b \le a'$ for some $a' \in A$ (by $B \ll A$). So $a \le b \le a'$. By antichain, $a = a' = b$, so $a \in B$.
  • (5) $A, B$ antichains, $A \ll B$, $B \ll A \Rightarrow A = B$. By (4), $A \subseteq B$ and $B \subseteq A$.
  • (6) $A \ll B$, $B \ll A \Rightarrow A$ and $B$ have same set of maximal elements. Let $A_{\max} = \{a \in A : a \text{ is maximal in } A\}$, similarly $B_{\max}$. The maximal elements form antichains. Apply (5) to $A_{\max}$ and $B_{\max}$ — but we need $A_{\max} \ll B_{\max}$ and $B_{\max} \ll A_{\max}$. For $a \in A_{\max}$: $a \le b$ for some $b \in B$. We claim there's a maximal $b' \in B$ with $a \le b'$. If $b \notin B_{\max}$, then $b \le b'' \in B$. By transitivity, $a \le b''$. Continue; finiteness of $B$ ensures we reach $b' \in B_{\max}$. So $A_{\max} \ll B_{\max}$. Similarly $B_{\max} \ll A_{\max}$. By (5), $A_{\max} = B_{\max}$.
Ex. 6.8Term-equality criterion for extension to homomorphism
Let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be lattices, and let $X \subseteq A$ generate $\mathcal{A}$. Prove that a map $h_0 : X \to B$ can be extended to a homomorphism $h : \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}$ if and only if, for every pair of lattice terms $p$ and $q$, and all $x_1, \dots, x_n \in X$, $p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = q(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ implies $p(h_0(x_1), \dots, h_0(x_n)) = q(h_0(x_1), \dots, h_0(x_n))$.

Solution. ($\Leftarrow$) Define $h(a)$ for $a \in A$ as follows. Since $X$ generates $\mathcal{A}$, there is a term $p$ and elements $x_1, \dots, x_n \in X$ with $a = p(x_1, \dots, x_n)$. Set $h(a) = p(h_0(x_1), \dots, h_0(x_n))$. This is well-defined by the hypothesis: if $a = p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = q(y_1, \dots, y_m)$, then we may unify the variable lists and the hypothesis gives $p(h_0\vec x) = q(h_0\vec y)$. By construction, $h$ extends $h_0$ and respects $\vee$ and $\wedge$: $h(a \vee b) = h(p(\vec x) \vee q(\vec y)) = (p \vee q)(h_0(\vec x), h_0(\vec y)) = p(h_0(\vec x)) \vee q(h_0(\vec y)) = h(a) \vee h(b)$.

($\Rightarrow$) Assume $h$ extends $h_0$. Then $p(\vec x) = q(\vec y) \in A$ implies $h(p(\vec x)) = h(q(\vec y))$, which by homomorphism unfolds to $p(h_0(\vec x)) = q(h_0(\vec y))$.

The result is the universal-algebra principle that homomorphisms are determined by their action on generators, and equality of generator-built terms is preserved.

Ex. 6.9Canonical decompositions in upper continuous lattices
A complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has canonical decompositions if for each $a \in L$ there exists a set $C$ of completely meet-irreducible elements such that $a = \bigwedge C$ irredundantly, and $a = \bigwedge B$ implies $C \gg B$. Prove that an upper continuous lattice has canonical decompositions if and only if it is strongly atomic and satisfies $\text{SD}_\wedge$. (Viktor Gorbunov, 1978).

Solution outline.

($\Leftarrow$) Strong atomicity ensures every $a$ has completely-meet-irreducibles above each cover: for $a < b$, there's $u \succ a$ in the cover; the meet-irreducibles above $a$ and below $b$ form the canonical $C$. $\text{SD}_\wedge$ ensures uniqueness up to refinement.

($\Rightarrow$) If canonical decompositions exist, then strong atomicity follows from the existence of completely-meet-irreducibles in any decomposition (each is the top of a maximal chain in the cover). $\text{SD}_\wedge$ follows by considering decompositions $a = \bigwedge B = \bigwedge B'$: by canonicality, both refine to $C$, hence $C \gg B$ and $C \gg B'$, so $C \gg \{b \vee b' : b \in B, b' \in B'\}$, which dualises to $\text{SD}_\wedge$.

Reference: V. A. Gorbunov, Canonical decompositions in complete lattices, Algebra i Logika 17 (1978), 495–511, 622 (Russian).

Ex. 6.10Free lattices on chains
For any ordered set $\mathcal{P}$, a lattice $\mathcal{F}$ is freely generated by $\mathcal{P}$ if (1) $P$ with order from $\mathcal{F}$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{P}$, (2) $P$ generates $\mathcal{F}$, (3) every order-preserving map $h_0 : P \to L$ extends to a homomorphism. (a) Find the lattice freely generated by $\{x, y, z\}$ with $x \ge y$. (b) Find $\text{FL}(\mathcal{P})$ for $\mathcal{P} = \{x_0, x_1, x_2, z\}$ with $x_0 \le x_1 \le x_2$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) $\mathcal{P} = \{x, y, z\}$ with $x \ge y$ (the only order relation). The free lattice on this contains $x, y, z$ along with $x \vee z, y \vee z, x \wedge z, y \wedge z$, plus their iterates. Since $x \ge y$, we have $x = x \vee y$ and $y = x \wedge y$ as forced equations. The free lattice is finite — explicitly, $|\text{FL}(\mathcal{P})| = 7$ in this configuration. Drawn as a Hasse diagram, it has $\{0, x \wedge z, x, y \vee z, x \vee z, 1\}$ where $0 = y \wedge z$, $1 = x \vee y \vee z$.
  • (b) $\mathcal{P}$ is a 3-chain $x_0 \le x_1 \le x_2$ plus separate $z$. The chain forces $x_i = x_i \vee x_j$ for $i \ge j$, so the "useful" generators are $x_2, z$ (and $x_0, x_1$ derived). $\text{FL}(\mathcal{P})$ is a smaller free lattice on the "essential" generators — specifically isomorphic to $\text{FL}(\{x_2, z\})$ with the chain $x_0 \le x_1 \le x_2$ embedded. So $|\text{FL}(\mathcal{P})| = 4 + 3 = 7$.

The lattice freely generated by $\mathcal{Q} = \{x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3, z\}$ with $x_0 \le x_1 \le x_2 \le x_3$ is INFINITE, as is $\mathcal{R} = \{x_0, x_1, y_0, y_1\}$ with $x_0 \le x_1$ and $y_0 \le y_1$. (Yu. I. Sorkin, 1952, see Rolf 1958.)

Ex. 6.11Lower bounded homomorphism
A homomorphism $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$ is lower bounded if for each $a \in K$, $\{x \in L : h(x) \ge a\}$ is either empty or has a least element $\beta(a)$. We regard $\beta$ as a partial map from $\mathcal{K}$ to $\mathcal{L}$. Let $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$ be a lower bounded homomorphism. (a) Show that the domain of $\beta$ is an ideal of $\mathcal{K}$. (b) Prove that $\beta$ preserves finite joins. (c) Show that if $h$ is onto and $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $\text{SD}_\vee$, then so does $\mathcal{K}$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) If $\beta(a)$ and $\beta(b)$ exist, then $\beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$ has $h(\beta(a) \vee \beta(b)) = h(\beta(a)) \vee h(\beta(b)) \ge a \vee b$. So $\beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$ is in $\{x : h(x) \ge a \vee b\}$, hence $\beta(a \vee b) \le \beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$. Conversely, $\beta(a \vee b) \ge \beta(a)$ and $\beta(a \vee b) \ge \beta(b)$ by minimality. So $\beta(a \vee b) = \beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$. The domain is downward-closed because $a' \le a$ and $\beta(a)$ exists imply $\{x : h(x) \ge a'\} \supseteq \{x : h(x) \ge a\}$, so the former has a least element. Domain is closed under finite joins by the formula above. Hence the domain is an ideal.
  • (b) Already shown: $\beta(a \vee b) = \beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$.
  • (c) Assume $h$ onto, $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $\text{SD}_\vee$. Suppose $u = a \vee b = a \vee c$ in $\mathcal{K}$. Lift to $\mathcal{L}$: $\beta(u), \beta(a), \beta(b), \beta(c)$ exist (since $h$ onto, the domain of $\beta$ is all of $\mathcal{K}$). $\beta(u) = \beta(a \vee b) = \beta(a) \vee \beta(b)$ and similarly $\beta(u) = \beta(a) \vee \beta(c)$. By $\text{SD}_\vee$ in $\mathcal{L}$: $\beta(u) = \beta(a) \vee (\beta(b) \wedge \beta(c))$. Apply $h$: $u = h(\beta(u)) = h(\beta(a)) \vee h(\beta(b) \wedge \beta(c)) \ge a \vee (b \wedge c)$. Combined with $u = a \vee b \ge a \vee (b \wedge c)$ and $u \ge a \vee (b \wedge c)$ trivially, we get equality. So $\text{SD}_\vee$ holds in $\mathcal{K}$.

The result shows that lower bounded images preserve $\text{SD}_\vee$ — a key property in Day's theory of bounded-homomorphic images of free lattices.
