Chapter Overview — kernels of lattice homomorphisms

Chapter 4 introduced equivalence relations and the partition lattice. Chapter 5 specialises to CONGRUENCES — equivalence relations that respect the lattice operations. Three remarkable structural facts: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is always algebraic; always distributive (Funayama-Nakayama 1942); and every lattice is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices (Birkhoff 1944).

Chapter 4 ended with Whitman's representation theorem and the modular / Arguesian story. Chapter 5 specialises from arbitrary equivalence relations to congruences — equivalence relations that are kernels of homomorphisms. The chapter develops the structure theory of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, the lattice of all congruences on $\mathcal{L}$.

The chapter has four conceptual movements:

  1. Quotients and the First Isomorphism Theorem. For a homomorphism $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$, the kernel $\ker h$ is a congruence. Theorem 5.1: $\mathcal{L}/\ker h \cong h(\mathcal{L})$. Conversely, Theorem 5.2: any equivalence relation satisfying the join + meet compatibility conditions (‡) makes $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ into a quotient lattice.
  2. $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice. Theorem 5.3: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic; compact congruences are exactly the finitely generated ones. Theorem 5.4 (Second Isomorphism Theorem): $\textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta) \cong \uparrow\theta$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Simple lattices: $|\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}| = 2$. Subdirectly irreducible: 0 is completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.
  3. Subdirect representation. Theorem 5.5: $0 = \bigwedge \theta_i$ ⟹ $\mathcal{L} \hookrightarrow \prod \mathcal{L}/\theta_i$ (subdirect product). Theorem 5.6: 4-way characterisation of subdirectly irreducible lattices via the monolith $\mu$ and the critical pair $(a, b)$. Theorem 5.7 (Birkhoff 1944): every lattice is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices.
  4. Distributivity of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ + the congruence lattice problem. Theorem 5.8 (Funayama-Nakayama 1942): $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is a distributive algebraic lattice for ANY lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Proof via the majority polynomial $m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$. The chapter's most surprising result: even when $\mathcal{L}$ is highly non-distributive, $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ IS distributive. The 65-year congruence lattice problem (Dilworth 1948 conjecture) — finally solved negatively by Wehrung in 2005-2007.

The chapter closes with a deeper analysis of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ via weaving polynomials (Theorem 5.9), and completely join prime congruences (Theorem 5.10-5.12). For principally chain finite lattices, every congruence is a join of completely join irreducibles of the form $\text{con}(x, x_*)$ for $x$ join irreducible — the lattice-theoretic analog of the Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya decomposition.

Density warning. This chapter packs 12 theorems into 12 pages. The Funayama-Nakayama distributivity theorem (s6) and the subdirect representation (s5) are the conceptual peaks. The congruence lattice problem (s7) is the most active research area. The companion expands the proofs and contextualises the historical lineage.

From Ch 4 §A1: reflexive, symmetric, transitive binary relation. $\textbf{Eq}\,L$ is the algebraic lattice of equivalence relations on $L$ under containment. $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ (this chapter) is a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,L$.

RecapKernel of a homomorphism

From Ch 4 §A1 + Ch 5 (this chapter): $\ker h = \{(x, y) : h(x) = h(y)\}$. For a lattice homomorphism, $\ker h$ is a congruence. The fundamental link between homomorphisms and congruences.

RecapAlgebraic lattice

From Ch 3 §B1: complete lattice with $\mathcal{L}^c$ join dense (compactly generated). Theorem 5.3 (this chapter): $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic for any lattice.

RecapDistributive lattice

From Ch 2 §B1: $x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$. Theorem 5.8 (Funayama-Nakayama): $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is ALWAYS distributive — even when $\mathcal{L}$ itself is not.

RecapCompletely meet irreducible · $M^*(\mathcal{L})$

From Ch 3 §E1: $q = \bigwedge S$ ⟹ $q \in S$. Subdirectly irreducible lattices are exactly those where $0 \in M^*(\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L})$ (= 0 is completely meet irreducible in the congruence lattice).

New in Chapter 5 — full term-cards

NewQuotient $X/\theta$

For an equivalence relation $\theta$ on $X$: $X/\theta = \{x\theta : x \in X\}$, the set of equivalence classes. Borrows notation from group theory's quotient $G/N$ for normal subgroups.

NewCongruence relation

An equivalence relation $\theta$ on $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\theta = \ker h$ for some lattice homomorphism $h$. Equivalent (Theorem 5.2): $\theta$ satisfies the compatibility condition (‡): $x \theta y$ ⟹ $x \vee z \, \theta \, y \vee z$ AND $x \wedge z \, \theta \, y \wedge z$ for all $z \in L$.

NewQuotient lattice $\mathcal{L}/\theta$

For a congruence $\theta$: $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is the set of equivalence classes with operations $x\theta \vee y\theta = (x \vee y)\theta$ and $x\theta \wedge y\theta = (x \wedge y)\theta$. Theorem 5.2: well-defined and a lattice.

NewCon L · congruence lattice

The lattice of all congruences on $\mathcal{L}$, ordered by set containment. A complete sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,L$. Top = universal $L^2$; bottom = equality $=$. Theorem 5.3: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic. Theorem 5.8: also distributive.

NewPrincipal congruence $\text{con}(a, b)$

The smallest congruence containing the pair $(a, b)$. Equivalently, the congruence generated by $\{(a, b)\}$. The principal congruences are join dense in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$: every $\theta = \bigvee\{\text{con}(a, b) : a \theta b\}$.

NewSimple lattice

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ with $|L| > 1$ such that $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is the two-element chain — only equality and the universal relation are congruences. Examples: $\mathcal{M}_n$ for $n \ge 3$. The "atoms" of the universal-algebraic universe.

NewSubdirectly irreducible

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ such that 0 is completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Equivalently (Theorem 5.6): there is a unique minimal nonzero congruence $\mu$ (the monolith); every embedding into a product has some projection injective; there exists a critical pair $(a, b)$ with $a \mu b$ for every nonzero congruence $\mu$.

NewMonolith $\mu$

For a subdirectly irreducible lattice: the unique minimal nonzero congruence. Synonym: smallest nontrivial congruence. The "DNA" of the subdirectly irreducible.

NewCritical pair $(a, b)$

For a subdirectly irreducible lattice: a pair such that $a \theta b$ for every nonzero congruence $\theta$. Need not be unique. Generates the monolith: $\mu = \text{con}(a, b)$.

NewSubdirect product

$\mathcal{L} \hookrightarrow \prod_i \mathcal{L}_i$ such that each projection $\pi_i : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}_i$ is surjective. The "internal" version (Theorem 5.5): $\mathcal{L}$ is a subdirect product of $\{\mathcal{L}/\theta_i\}$ iff $\bigwedge \theta_i = 0$.

NewFirst / Second Isomorphism Theorems

First (Theorem 5.1): $\mathcal{L}/\ker h \cong h(\mathcal{L})$. Second (Theorem 5.4): $\textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta) \cong \uparrow\theta$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Direct lattice analogues of the group-theoretic isomorphism theorems.

NewFunayama-Nakayama theorem

Theorem 5.8 (1942): $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is a DISTRIBUTIVE algebraic lattice for any lattice $\mathcal{L}$. The chapter's most surprising result. Proof uses the majority polynomial $m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$.

NewCongruence lattice problem (CLP)

The problem (Dilworth 1948 conjecture): is every distributive algebraic lattice isomorphic to $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ for some lattice $\mathcal{L}$? Negative solution by Wehrung (2005, refined Růžička 2008): there exist distributive algebraic lattices with $\aleph_2$ compact elements that are NOT congruence lattices of any lattice. Holds for $\le \aleph_1$ compact elements.

NewWeaving polynomial

A unary lattice polynomial $w(x)$ built recursively by $w(x) = x$ and from each $w$ form $w(x) \wedge a$, $w(x) \vee a$ for $a \in L$. Used to characterise principal congruences (Theorem 5.9).

NewCompletely join prime

$p$ is completely join prime if $p \le \bigvee Q$ ⟹ $p \le q$ for some $q \in Q$. Implies completely join irreducible. Converse may fail. Theorem 5.10: in algebraic distributive lattices, completely join prime ⟺ completely join irreducible ⟺ compact + finitely join irreducible.

NewPrincipally chain finite

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ such that every principal ideal $\downarrow c$ satisfies the ACC and DCC. Includes finite lattices and many infinite lattices. Theorem 5.12: in such lattices, every congruence is a join of completely join irreducible congruences of the form $\text{con}(x, x_*)$.

Deep dive — extended explainers

Deep dive

Why $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive — the majority polynomial

Funayama-Nakayama's 1942 theorem (Theorem 5.8) is genuinely surprising: even highly non-distributive lattices (like $\mathcal{M}_3$ or $\mathcal{N}_5$) have a distributive congruence lattice. The proof uses a clever observation about the majority polynomial:

$$m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z).$$

This polynomial has a remarkable property: if any two of $x, y, z$ are equal, then $m$ takes that value. Concretely:

  • $m(x, x, z) = (x \wedge x) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (x \wedge z) = x \vee (x \wedge z) = x$ (by absorption).
  • $m(x, y, x) = (x \wedge y) \vee x \vee (y \wedge x) = x$.
  • $m(x, z, z) = (x \wedge z) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee z = z$.

This "majority" property is exactly what's needed to prove distributivity in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Given $\alpha, \beta, \gamma \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, to show $\alpha \wedge (\beta \vee \gamma) \le (\alpha \wedge \beta) \vee (\alpha \wedge \gamma)$, take $(x, z) \in \alpha \wedge (\beta \vee \gamma)$. The polynomial $m$ allows us to interpolate via the chain $x = y_0 \beta y_1 \gamma y_2 \beta \dots y_k = z$ (the chain witnessing $x (\beta \vee \gamma) z$) and produce $t_i = m(x, y_i, z)$ — a chain alternating between $\alpha \wedge \beta$ and $\alpha \wedge \gamma$ steps from $x$ to $z$.

The deeper reason this works: lattices are "majority-closed" in the universal-algebraic sense. Other equational classes (e.g., groups) have different polynomials (Mal'cev terms) that play similar roles in congruence theory. The slogan: congruence-distributive varieties are exactly those with a majority polynomial.

Deep dive

The 65-year congruence lattice problem

Theorem 5.8 (Funayama-Nakayama 1942) shows $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is always a distributive algebraic lattice. The natural converse question:

Is every distributive algebraic lattice isomorphic to $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ for some lattice $\mathcal{L}$?

This was conjectured (informally) by R. P. Dilworth around 1944, and stated as a starred exercise in the 1948 edition of Birkhoff's Lattice Theory. For 60+ years, the conjecture stood as one of the most famous open problems in lattice theory.

Partial positive results. A distributive algebraic lattice $\mathcal{D}$ IS a congruence lattice when:

  • $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ for some ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ (Dilworth's original proof for finite distributive lattices). To be proved in Chapter 10.
  • The compact elements form a sublattice of $\mathcal{D}$ (E. T. Schmidt 1981).
  • $\mathcal{D}$ has at most $\aleph_1$ compact elements (A. Huhn 1989).

The Wehrung counterexample (2005-2007). Friedrich Wehrung constructed a distributive algebraic lattice $\mathcal{D}$ with $\aleph_{\omega+1}$ compact elements that is NOT isomorphic to any $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Published in A solution to Dilworth's congruence lattice problem, Adv. Math. 216 (2007), 610-625. The construction uses Kuratowski's free set theorem to build a distributive lattice with a "rectangular" structure that no congruence lattice can match.

Pavel Růžička 2008: refined Wehrung's argument to produce a counterexample with $\aleph_2$ compact elements — the optimal cardinality (since Huhn 1989 proves the conjecture holds at $\aleph_1$). Published in Free trees and the optimal bound in Wehrung's theorem, Fund. Math. 198 (2008), 217-228.

Open question: is every distributive algebraic lattice the congruence lattice of an algebra with FINITELY MANY operations? Even with countably many operations? Surveys: Tischendorf 1994; Plôščica-Tůma-Wehrung 2002.

The CLP story is one of lattice theory's great mathematical narratives: a problem stated in 1944, partially solved through the 1980s, finally settled with a beautiful set-theoretic construction in 2007.

Deep dive

Subdirect representation — Birkhoff's universal-algebra paradigm

Theorem 5.7 says: every lattice is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices. This is Garrett Birkhoff's 1944 theorem applied to lattices. The general universal-algebra version: every algebra is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducibles in its variety.

Why does this matter? Because subdirectly irreducible algebras are "few": in many varieties they are easy to describe explicitly. Examples:

  • Variety of distributive lattices: subdirectly irreducibles = the two-element chain $\{0, 1\}$. So every distributive lattice is a sublattice of $\{0, 1\}^X$ for some $X$ — Birkhoff's representation theorem (Ch 8).
  • Variety of Boolean algebras: subdirectly irreducibles = $\{0, 1\}$. So every Boolean algebra is a subalgebra of $\{0, 1\}^X = \mathfrak{P}(X)$ — Stone's representation theorem.
  • Variety of groups: subdirectly irreducibles include simple groups, certain $p$-groups, etc. Classification is harder.

Birkhoff's slogan: to understand a variety, classify its subdirectly irreducibles. For lattices, the subdirectly irreducibles are richly varied (lots of distinct ones), which is why lattice theory is "harder" than distributive lattice theory.

Reference: Garrett Birkhoff, Subdirect unions in universal algebra, Bull. AMS 50 (1944), 764-768. The founding paper of subdirect representation theory.

Connection to Ch 6: a class of algebras is a "variety" iff closed under H, S, P (homomorphic images, subalgebras, products). Subdirect products are products + subalgebras, so subdirect representations connect varieties to their building blocks.

A1 Quotients · kernels · Theorem 5.1 (First Isomorphism Theorem)

$\mathcal{L}/\ker h \cong h(\mathcal{L})$. The lattice analogue of the group-theoretic First Isomorphism Theorem.
Quotient $X/\theta$

You are doubtless familiar with the connection between homomorphisms and normal subgroups of groups. In this chapter we will establish the corresponding ideas for lattices (and other general algebras).

Borrowing notation from group theory, if $X$ is a set and $\theta$ an equivalence relation on $X$, for $x \in X$ let $x\theta$ denote the equivalence class $\{y \in X : x \theta y\}$, and let

$$X/\theta = \{x\theta : x \in X\}.$$

Thus the elements of $X/\theta$ are the equivalence classes of $\theta$.

Kernel + lattice operations on classes

Recall that if $\mathcal{L}$ and $\mathcal{K}$ are lattices and $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$ is a homomorphism, then the kernel of $h$ is the induced equivalence relation,

$$\ker h = \{(x, y) \in L^2 : h(x) = h(y)\}.$$

We can define lattice operations naturally on the equivalence classes of $\ker h$, viz., if $\theta = \ker h$, then

$$x\theta \vee y\theta = (x \vee y)\theta$$

$$x\theta \wedge y\theta = (x \wedge y)\theta. \quad (\S)$$

The homomorphism property shows that these operations are well-defined: if $(x, y) \in \ker h$ and $(r, s) \in \ker h$, then $h(x \vee r) = h(x) \vee h(r) = h(y) \vee h(s) = h(y \vee s)$, whence $(x \vee r, y \vee s) \in \ker h$. Moreover, $\mathcal{L}/\ker h$ with these operations forms an algebra $\mathcal{L}/\ker h$ isomorphic to the image $h(\mathcal{L})$, which is a sublattice of $\mathcal{K}$. Thus $\mathcal{L}/\ker h$ is also a lattice.

Theorem 5.1 · First Isomorphism Theorem

Let $\mathcal{L}$ and $\mathcal{K}$ be lattices, and let $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$ be a lattice homomorphism. Then $\mathcal{L}/\ker h$ with the operations defined by ($\S$) is a lattice $\mathcal{L}/\ker h$, which is isomorphic to the image $h(\mathcal{L})$ of $\mathcal{L}$ in $\mathcal{K}$.

Intuition · the group-theory analogy

The First Isomorphism Theorem for lattices is a direct analogue of:

  • Groups: $G/\ker h \cong h(G)$.
  • Rings: $R/\ker h \cong h(R)$.
  • Modules: $M/\ker h \cong h(M)$.
  • Lattices (this chapter): $\mathcal{L}/\ker h \cong h(\mathcal{L})$.

In each case, kernels are characterised by an algebraic property (normal subgroup; ideal; submodule; congruence). The isomorphism theorem says: the quotient by the kernel reproduces the image.

A2 Congruence · Theorem 5.2 (the (‡) characterisation)

A congruence is an equivalence relation $\theta$ that is a kernel. Equivalently, satisfies the operation-preservation condition (‡). Theorem 5.2: the converse — (‡)-equivalences are kernels.
Definition · congruence relation

Let us define a congruence relation on a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ to be an equivalence relation $\theta$ such that $\theta = \ker h$ for some homomorphism $h$.

(Footnote: this is not the standard definition, but we are about to show it is equivalent to it.)

We have seen that, in addition to being equivalence relations, congruence relations must preserve the operations of $\mathcal{L}$: if $\theta$ is a congruence relation, then

$$(\dagger) \quad x \theta y \text{ and } r \theta s \quad \text{implies} \quad x \vee r \, \theta \, y \vee s,$$

and analogously for meets. Note that ($\dagger$) is equivalent for an equivalence relation $\theta$ to the apparently weaker, and easier to check, condition

$$(\dagger') \quad x \theta y \text{ implies } x \vee z \, \theta \, y \vee z.$$

For ($\dagger$) implies ($\dagger'$) because every equivalence relation is reflexive ($z \theta z$), while if $\theta$ has the property ($\dagger'$) and the hypotheses of ($\dagger$) hold, then applying ($\dagger'$) twice yields $x \vee r \theta y \vee r \theta y \vee s$.

We want to show that, conversely, any equivalence relation satisfying ($\dagger'$) and the corresponding implication for meets is a congruence relation.

Theorem 5.2 · the (‡) characterisation

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice, and let $\theta$ be an equivalence relation on $L$ satisfying

$$(\ddagger) \quad x \theta y \text{ implies } x \vee z \, \theta \, y \vee z, \text{ and } x \theta y \text{ implies } x \wedge z \, \theta \, y \wedge z.$$

Define join and meet on $L/\theta$ by the formulas ($\S$). Then $\mathcal{L}/\theta = (L/\theta, \wedge, \vee)$ is a lattice, and the map $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}/\theta$ defined by $h(x) = x\theta$ is a surjective homomorphism with $\ker h = \theta$.

Proof · why · sketch

The conditions ($\ddagger$) ensure that the join and meet operations are well defined on $L/\theta$. By definition, we have

$$h(x \vee y) = (x \vee y)\theta = x\theta \vee y\theta = h(x) \vee h(y)$$

and similarly for meets, so $h$ is a homomorphism. The range of $h$ is clearly $L/\theta$.

It remains to show that $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ satisfies the equations defining lattices. This follows from the general principle that homomorphisms preserve the satisfaction of equations: if $h : \mathcal{L} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{K}$ is a surjective homomorphism and an equation $p = q$ holds in $\mathcal{L}$, then it holds in $\mathcal{K}$. (See Exercise 4.) So $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ inherits all lattice axioms from $\mathcal{L}$. ∎

Congruences are exactly (‡)-equivalences

Theorems 5.1 and 5.2 together establish the key biconditional:

$\theta$ is a congruence ⟺ $\theta$ is an equivalence relation satisfying (‡)

This is the standard textbook definition of congruence: an equivalence relation that respects the algebraic operations. The chapter takes the homomorphism-kernel route as the definition (footnote: "not standard"), then proves equivalence in Theorem 5.2.

By Theorem 3.1, the conditions of (‡) and the axioms for an equivalence relation are all FINITARY closure rules on $L^2$. Hence, by Theorem 3.2, the set of congruence relations on a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ forms an algebraic lattice $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. The closure operator on $L^2$ that gives the congruence generated by a set of pairs is denoted by "con" or sometimes "Cg." For a single pair, $Q = \{(a, b)\}$, we write just $\text{con}(a, b)$.

B1 Theorem 5.3 · principal congruences

$\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic. Compact elements are finitely generated. Principal congruences $\text{con}(a, b)$ are join dense.
Theorem 5.3

$\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice. A congruence relation $\theta$ is compact in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ if and only if it is finitely generated, i.e., there exist finitely many pairs $(a_1, b_1), \dots, (a_k, b_k)$ of elements of $L$ such that $\theta = \bigvee_{1 \le i \le k} \text{con}(a_i, b_i)$.

Proof · why · sketch

The congruence relations are exactly the equivalence relations satisfying (‡). The conditions of (‡) and the axioms for an equivalence relation are all finitary closure rules on $L^2$. By Theorem 3.1, $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is an algebraic lattice with compact elements being the closures of finite sets — i.e., finitely generated congruences.

Moreover, the equivalence relation join (the transitive closure of the union) of a set of congruence relations again satisfies (‡): for if $\theta_i$ are congruence relations and $x \theta_{i_1} r_1 \theta_{i_2} r_2 \dots \theta_{i_n} y$, then $x \vee z \theta_{i_1} r_1 \vee z \theta_{i_2} r_2 \vee z \dots \theta_{i_n} y \vee z$, and likewise for meets. So $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is also a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,L$. ∎

Convex sublattices · principal congruences

Note that the universal relation $L^2$ and the equality relation are both congruence relations; they are the greatest and least elements of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, respectively. Also, since $x \theta y$ if and only if $x \wedge y \, \theta \, x \vee y$, a congruence relation is determined by the ordered pairs $(a, b)$ with $a < b$ which it contains.

A subset $S \subseteq \mathcal{L}$ is convex if $x \le y \le z$ and $x, z \in S$ imply $y \in S$. The reader should verify that if $\theta$ is a congruence relation on a lattice $\mathcal{L}$, then every congruence class is a convex sublattice of $\mathcal{L}$. This observation helps immensely in computing lattice congruences.

A congruence relation $\theta$ is principal if $\theta = \text{con}(a, b)$ for some pair $a, b \in L$. The principal congruence relations are JOIN DENSE in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$: for any congruence relation $\theta$, we have

$$\theta = \bigvee\{\text{con}(a, b) : a \theta b\}.$$

It follows from the general theory of algebraic closure operators that principal congruence relations are compact. So in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, the principal congruences are exactly the compact + indecomposable elements that generate everything.

B2 Theorem 5.4 (Second Iso) · simple · subdirectly irreducible

Congruences of $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ correspond to congruences of $\mathcal{L}$ above $\theta$. Simple = trivial congruence lattice. Subdirectly irreducible = unique minimal nonzero.
Setup: congruences correspond above $\theta$

One of the most basic facts about congruences says that congruences of $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ correspond to congruences on $\mathcal{L}$ containing $\theta$. This is the lattice version of the group-theoretic correspondence theorem.

Theorem 5.4 · Second Isomorphism Theorem

If $\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, then $\textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta)$ is isomorphic to the filter $\uparrow\theta$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, i.e., $\textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta) \cong \{\varphi \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L} : \varphi \ge \theta\}$.

Proof · why · sketch

A congruence relation on $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is an equivalence relation $R$ on the $\theta$-classes of $L$ such that $x\theta R y\theta$ implies $x\theta \vee z\theta R y\theta \vee z\theta$, and analogously for meets.

Given $R \in \textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta)$, define the corresponding relation $\rho$ on $L$ by $x \rho y$ iff $x\theta R y\theta$. Clearly $\rho \in \textbf{Eq}\,L$ and $\theta \le \rho$. The compatibility of $R$ implies that $\rho$ is a congruence on $\mathcal{L}$.

Conversely, let $\sigma \in \uparrow\theta$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, and define a relation $S$ on $L/\theta$ by $x\theta S y\theta$ iff $x \sigma y$. Since $\theta \le \sigma$ the relation $S$ is well defined. The compatibility of $\sigma$ on $L$ implies that $S$ is a congruence relation on $\mathcal{L}/\theta$.

The maps $f : \textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta) \to \uparrow\theta$ and $g : \uparrow\theta \to \textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}/\theta)$ are mutually inverse order-preserving bijections, hence lattice isomorphisms. ∎

Homomorphism interpretation

It is interesting to interpret Theorem 5.4 in terms of homomorphisms. Essentially it corresponds to the fact that if $h : \mathcal{L} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{K}$ and $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{M}$ are homomorphisms with $h$ surjective, then there is a homomorphism $g : \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{M}$ with $f = gh$ if and only if $\ker h \le \ker f$.

This version of the Second Isomorphism Theorem will be used repeatedly in Chapters 6 and 7 (free lattices and varieties of lattices).

Simple lattice · subdirectly irreducible

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is called simple if $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is a two element chain, i.e., $|L| > 1$ and $\mathcal{L}$ has no congruences except equality and the universal relation. For example, the two-dimensional modular lattice $\mathcal{M}_n$ is simple whenever $n \ge 3$.

A lattice is subdirectly irreducible if it has a unique minimum nonzero congruence relation, i.e., if 0 is completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. So every simple lattice is subdirectly irreducible, and $\mathcal{N}_5$ is an example of a subdirectly irreducible lattice that is not simple.

Corollary 1

$\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is simple if and only if $1 \succ \theta$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

Corollary 2

$\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is subdirectly irreducible if and only if $\theta$ is completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

B3 Subdirect products · Theorems 5.5, 5.6, 5.7 (Birkhoff)

Theorem 5.5: $\bigwedge \theta_i = 0$ ⟹ $\mathcal{L} \hookrightarrow \prod \mathcal{L}/\theta_i$. Theorem 5.6: 4-way characterisation of subdirectly irreducible. Theorem 5.7 (Birkhoff 1944): every lattice is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducibles.

Now we turn our attention to a decomposition of lattices which goes back to R. Remak in 1930 (for groups). In what follows, it is important to remember that the zero element of a congruence lattice is the equality relation.

Theorem 5.5 · subdirect representation

If $0 = \bigwedge_{i \in I} \theta_i$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, then $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to a sublattice of the direct product $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}/\theta_i$, and each of the natural homomorphisms $\pi_i : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}/\theta_i$ is surjective.

Conversely, if $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to a sublattice of a direct product $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{K}_i$ and each of the projection homomorphisms $\pi_i : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}_i$ is surjective, then $\mathcal{K}_i \cong \mathcal{L}/\ker \pi_i$ and $\bigwedge_{i \in I} \ker \pi_i = 0$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

Proof · why · sketch

For any collection $\theta_i$ ($i \in I$) in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, there is a natural homomorphism $\pi : \mathcal{L} \to \prod \mathcal{L}/\theta_i$ with $(\pi(x))_i = x\theta_i$. Since two elements of a direct product are equal if and only if they agree in every component, $\ker \pi = \bigwedge \theta_i$. So if $\bigwedge \theta_i = 0$, then $\pi$ is an embedding.

Conversely, if $\pi : \mathcal{L} \to \prod \mathcal{K}_i$ is an embedding, then $\ker \pi = 0$, while as above $\ker \pi = \bigwedge \ker \pi_i$. Clearly, if $\pi_i(\mathcal{L}) = \mathcal{K}_i$ then $\mathcal{K}_i \cong \mathcal{L}/\ker \pi_i$. ∎

Subdirect decomposition

A representation of $\mathcal{L}$ satisfying either of the equivalent conditions of Theorem 5.5 is called a subdirect decomposition, and the corresponding external construction is called a subdirect product.

Example: Figure 5.1 shows how a six element lattice $\mathcal{L}$ can be written as a subdirect product of two copies of $\mathcal{N}_5$.

Next we should show that subdirectly irreducible lattices are indeed those that have no proper subdirect decomposition.

Theorem 5.6 · 4-way characterisation

The following are equivalent for a lattice $\mathcal{L}$.

  1. $\mathcal{L}$ is subdirectly irreducible, i.e., 0 is completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.
  2. There is a unique minimal nonzero congruence $\mu$ on $\mathcal{L}$ with the property that $\theta \ge \mu$ for every nonzero $\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.
  3. If $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to a sublattice of $\prod \mathcal{K}_i$, then some projection homomorphism $\pi_i$ is one-to-one.
  4. There exists a pair of elements $a < b$ in $\mathcal{L}$ such that $a \theta b$ for every nonzero congruence $\theta$.
Monolith · critical pair

The congruence $\mu$ of condition (2) is called the monolith of the subdirectly irreducible lattice $\mathcal{L}$, and the pair $(a, b)$ of condition (4), which need not be unique, is called a critical pair.

Proof · sketch

The equivalence of (1), (2) and (3) is a simple combination of Theorems 3.8 (3-way for completely meet irreducible) and 5.5. We get (2) implies (4) by taking $a = x \wedge y$ and $b = x \vee y$ for any pair of distinct elements with $x \mu y$. On the other hand, if (4) holds we obtain (2) with $\mu = \text{con}(a, b)$. ∎

Theorem 5.7 · Birkhoff 1944

Every lattice is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices.

Proof · why

Now we see the beauty of Birkhoff's Theorem 3.10, that every element in an algebraic lattice is a meet of completely meet irreducible elements. By applying this to the zero element of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$, we obtain the following fundamental result.

Specifically: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic (Theorem 5.3), so by Theorem 3.10, $0 = \bigwedge \theta_i$ for completely meet irreducibles $\theta_i \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. By Corollary 2 to Theorem 5.4, each $\mathcal{L}/\theta_i$ is subdirectly irreducible. By Theorem 5.5, $\mathcal{L}$ is a subdirect product of $\{\mathcal{L}/\theta_i\}$. ∎

Birkhoff's slogan

It should be clear that, with the appropriate modifications, Theorems 5.5 to 5.7 yield subdirect decompositions of groups, rings, semilattices, etc. into subdirectly irreducible algebras of the same type. Keith Kearnes (1990) has shown that there are interesting varieties of algebras whose congruence lattices are strongly atomic. By Theorem 3.10, these algebras have irredundant subdirect decompositions.

Subdirectly irreducible lattices play a particularly important role in the study of varieties; see Chapter 7. The slogan: to study a variety, classify its subdirectly irreducibles.

So far we have just done universal algebra with lattices: with the appropriate modifications, we can characterize congruence relations and show that $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ is an algebraic lattice for any algebra $\mathcal{A}$ (see Exercises 10 and 11). However, the next property is special to lattices and related structures.

Part C · Distributivity of Con L

Theorem 5.8 (Funayama-Nakayama 1942) + the congruence lattice problem

$\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive — even when $\mathcal{L}$ is not. Proof via the majority polynomial. Then: the 65-year congruence lattice problem, finally solved by Wehrung in 2007.

C1 Theorem 5.8 — Funayama-Nakayama (1942)

$\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive for ANY lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Proof uses the "majority" polynomial $m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$.
Theorem 5.8 · Funayama-Nakayama 1942

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a lattice, then $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is a distributive algebraic lattice.

Proof · why · the majority polynomial

In any lattice $\mathcal{L}$, let

$$m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z).$$

Then it is easy to see that $m(x, y, z)$ is a majority polynomial, in that if any two variables are equal then $m(x, y, z)$ takes on that value:

$$m(x, x, z) = x, \quad m(x, y, x) = x, \quad m(x, z, z) = z.$$

Now let $\alpha, \beta, \gamma \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Clearly $(\alpha \wedge \beta) \vee (\alpha \wedge \gamma) \le \alpha \wedge (\beta \vee \gamma)$. To show the reverse inclusion, let $(x, z) \in \alpha \wedge (\beta \vee \gamma)$. Then $x \alpha z$ and there exist $y_1, \dots, y_k$ such that

$$x = y_0 \, \beta \, y_1 \, \gamma \, y_2 \, \beta \dots y_k = z.$$

Let $t_i = m(x, y_i, z)$ for $0 \le i \le k$. Then

$$t_0 = m(x, x, z) = x$$

$$t_k = m(x, z, z) = z$$

and for all $i$,

$$t_i = m(x, y_i, z) \, \alpha \, m(x, y_i, x) = x,$$

so $t_i \alpha t_{i+1}$ by Exercise 4(b). If $i$ is even, then

$$t_i = m(x, y_i, z) \, \beta \, m(x, y_{i+1}, z) = t_{i+1},$$

whence $t_i \alpha \wedge \beta t_{i+1}$. Similarly, if $i$ is odd then $t_i \alpha \wedge \gamma t_{i+1}$. Thus

$$x = t_0 \, \alpha \wedge \beta \, t_1 \, \alpha \wedge \gamma \, t_2 \, \alpha \wedge \beta \dots t_k = z$$

and we have shown that $\alpha \wedge (\beta \vee \gamma) \le (\alpha \wedge \beta) \vee (\alpha \wedge \gamma)$. As inclusion holds both ways, we have equality. Therefore $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive. ∎

The "majority polynomial" technique in universal algebra

The key insight in Funayama-Nakayama's proof: varieties with a majority polynomial have distributive congruence lattices. This is a famous Mal'cev condition in universal algebra.

Examples beyond lattices:

  • Boolean algebras: have a majority polynomial $m(x, y, z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$ (same as lattices). $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{B}$ distributive.
  • Heyting algebras: also have a majority polynomial. Congruences distributive.
  • Distributive lattices, modular lattices: subvarieties of lattices, so inherit the property. (And lattice-theoretically the congruence lattice is even simpler.)

What about non-examples?

  • Groups: do NOT have a majority polynomial. The commutator polynomial $[x, y] = x^{-1}y^{-1}xy$ is the closest analog (Mal'tsev term). Congruence lattices of groups can be non-distributive (e.g., abelian groups have modular subgroup lattices, often non-distributive).
  • Rings, modules: similar — Mal'tsev terms but no majority polynomial in general.

The slogan: the existence of a majority polynomial is exactly the universal-algebraic condition for congruence-distributivity. This generalises Funayama-Nakayama to a broad class of algebras.

C2 The congruence lattice problem (Dilworth → Wehrung 2005-2007)

Is every distributive algebraic lattice a $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$? The 65-year-old conjecture solved negatively by Wehrung's 2005 counterexample.

In the 1940's, R. P. Dilworth proved that every finite distributive lattice is isomorphic to the congruence lattice of a lattice. (This result appeared as a starred exercise in the 1948 edition of Birkhoff's Lattice Theory book.) For a long time thereafter, it was conjectured that every distributive algebraic lattice was the congruence lattice of a lattice, and evidence in favor of the conjecture was amassed.

Partial positive results

A distributive algebraic lattice $\mathcal{D}$ is isomorphic to the congruence lattice of a lattice if:

  1. $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ for some ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ (R. P. Dilworth, see [6]). To be proved in Chapter 10. This includes all finite distributive lattices.
  2. The compact elements form a sublattice of $\mathcal{D}$ (E. T. Schmidt 1981, [12]).
  3. $\mathcal{D}$ has at most $\aleph_1$ compact elements (A. Huhn 1989, [7]).

In Chapter 10 we will prove (i), which includes the fact that every finite distributive lattice is isomorphic to the congruence lattice of a (finite) lattice.

Wehrung 2005, 2007 — the negative solution

But in 2005, Fred Wehrung constructed distributive algebraic lattices that are NOT isomorphic to congruence lattices of lattices [15]. Wehrung's original counterexamples have $\aleph_{\omega+1}$ or more compact elements, but Pavel Růžička refined Wehrung's arguments to produce a counterexample with $\aleph_2$ compact elements [11].

Wehrung's paper: A solution to Dilworth's congruence lattice problem, Adv. Math. 216 (2007), 610-625.

Růžička's paper: Free trees and the optimal bound in Wehrung's theorem, Fund. Math. 198 (2008), 217-228.

The bound is OPTIMAL: by Huhn 1989, every distributive algebraic lattice with $\le \aleph_1$ compact elements IS a congruence lattice; Růžička showed there exist counterexamples at $\aleph_2$.

This completely settles the problem for lattices, but it remains an open question whether every distributive algebraic lattice is isomorphic to the congruence lattice of an algebra with finitely many operations. Surveys on this problem, including pre-dating Wehrung's results, can be found in M. Tischendorf [13] and M. Plôščica, J. Tůma and F. Wehrung [9, 15].

Why this matters

The 65-year congruence lattice problem is one of the great mathematical narratives of 20th-century lattice theory:

  • 1942: Funayama-Nakayama — $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive.
  • 1944-48: Dilworth — finite distributive case solved positively.
  • 1981: Schmidt — sublattice compact case.
  • 1989: Huhn — $\le \aleph_1$ compact elements.
  • 2005: Wehrung — counterexample at $\aleph_{\omega+1}$.
  • 2008: Růžička — optimal counterexample at $\aleph_2$.
  • Open: finitely-presented version (does it hold for finitely-many-operation algebras?).

The story showcases the depth of lattice theory: a simple-sounding question takes decades of effort + powerful set-theoretic machinery (Kuratowski's free set theorem) to fully resolve.

Part D · Weaving polynomials and completely join prime congruences

Theorem 5.9 + completely join prime + Theorems 5.10, 5.11, 5.12

A finer analysis of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Weaving polynomials describe how principal congruences are computed. Completely join prime congruences correspond to covers in $\mathcal{L}$.

D1 Weaving polynomial · Theorem 5.9

A unary polynomial built by alternating meets and joins. Theorem 5.9: $(x, y) \in \bigvee \text{con}(a_i, b_i)$ iff there's a sequence connecting $x \vee y$ to $x \wedge y$ via weaving polynomials applied to the $(a_i, b_i)$.

We need to understand the congruence operator $\text{con}(Q)$, where $Q$ is a set of pairs, a little better.

Weaving polynomial

A weaving polynomial on a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is a member of the set $W$ of unary functions defined recursively by:

  1. $w(x) = x \in W$,
  2. if $w(x) \in W$ and $a \in L$, then $u(x) = w(x) \wedge a$ and $v(x) = w(x) \vee a$ are in $W$,
  3. only these functions are in $W$.

Thus every weaving polynomial looks something like

$$w(x) = (\dots ((( x \wedge s_1) \vee s_2) \wedge s_3) \dots) \vee s_k$$

where $s_i \in L$ for $1 \le i \le k$. The following characterization is a modified version of one found in Dilworth [2].

Theorem 5.9

Suppose $a_i < b_i$ for $i \in I$. Then $(x, y) \in \bigvee_{i \in I} \text{con}(a_i, b_i)$ if and only if there exist finitely many $r_j \in L$, $w_j \in W$, and $i_j \in I$ such that

$$x \vee y = r_0 \ge r_1 \ge \dots \ge r_k = x \wedge y$$

with $w_j(b_{i_j}) = r_j$ and $w_j(a_{i_j}) = r_{j+1}$ for $0 \le j < k$.

Proof · why · sketch

Let $R$ be the set of all pairs $(x, y)$ satisfying the condition of the theorem. It is clear that

  1. $(a_i, b_i) \in R$ for all $i$,
  2. $R \subseteq \bigvee_{i \in I} \text{con}(a_i, b_i)$.

Hence, if we can show that $R$ is a congruence relation, it will follow that $R = \bigvee_{i \in I} \text{con}(a_i, b_i)$.

The proof is technical: show $R \in \textbf{Eq}\,L$ (reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity via weaving manipulations) and that $R$ satisfies (‡) (compatibility with $\vee$ and $\wedge$ via weaving polynomial composition). Both verifications use the closure property of $W$ under inserting/removing $\wedge a$ and $\vee a$ steps.

The argument for meets is done similarly, and we conclude that $R$ is a congruence relation, as desired. ∎

Corollary

If $\theta_i \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ for $i \in I$, then $(x, y) \in \bigvee_{i \in I} \theta_i$ if and only if there exist finitely many $r_j \in L$ and $i_j \in I$ such that

$$x \vee y = r_0 \ge r_1 \ge \dots \ge r_k = x \wedge y$$

and $r_j \theta_{i_j} r_{j+1}$ for $0 \le j < k$.

Intuition · weaving as path concatenation

The weaving polynomial machinery captures how congruences "weave together" through the lattice. Each step $r_j \to r_{j+1}$ in the chain is determined by:

  • An index $i_j \in I$ specifying which generating pair $(a_{i_j}, b_{i_j})$ is used.
  • A weaving polynomial $w_j$ specifying how to "fit" the generating pair into the chain via meet and join with intermediate elements.

The chain descends monotonically from $x \vee y$ to $x \wedge y$, which is the fundamental "shape" of a congruence: any congruent pair $(x, y)$ has $(x \wedge y, x \vee y)$ also congruent.

This characterisation is essential for explicit computations of congruences in concrete lattices, and is the technical core of many results about $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

D2 Completely join prime · Theorems 5.10, 5.11, 5.12

Completely join prime congruences correspond to covers in $\mathcal{L}$. Theorem 5.12: principally chain finite ⟹ every congruence is a join of completely join irreducibles of the form $\text{con}(x, x_*)$.
Completely join prime · completely join irreducible

An element $p$ of a complete lattice is completely join irreducible if $p = \bigvee Q$ implies $p = q$ for some $q \in Q$.

An element $p$ is completely join prime if $p \le \bigvee Q$ implies $p \le q$ for some $q \in Q$.

Clearly every completely join prime element is completely join irreducible, but in general completely join irreducible elements need not be join prime.

Setup: distributive algebraic lattices

Now every algebraic lattice has lots of completely meet irreducible elements (by Theorem 3.9), but they may have no completely join irreducible elements. This happens, for example, in the lattice consisting of the empty set and all cofinite subsets of an infinite set (which is distributive and algebraic). However, such completely join irreducible elements as there are in a distributive algebraic lattice ARE completely join prime!

Theorem 5.10

The following are equivalent for an element $p$ in an algebraic distributive lattice.

  1. $p$ is completely join prime.
  2. $p$ is completely join irreducible.
  3. $p$ is compact and (finitely) join irreducible.
Proof · why

Clearly (1) implies (2), and since every element in an algebraic lattice is a join of compact elements, (2) implies (3).

Let $p$ be compact and finitely join irreducible, and assume $p \le \bigvee Q$. As $p$ is compact, $p \le \bigvee F$ for some finite subset $F \subseteq Q$. By distributivity, this implies $p = p \wedge (\bigvee F) = \bigvee_{q \in F} p \wedge q$. Since $p$ is join irreducible, $p = p \wedge q \le q$ for some $q \in F$. Thus $p$ is completely join prime. ∎

Theorem 5.11

If $a \prec b$, then $\text{con}(a, b)$ is completely join prime in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

Why Theorem 5.11 follows from 5.10

If $a \prec b$, then $\text{con}(a, b)$ is compact (principal). And by the definition of cover, the interval $b/a$ has only two elements, so the principal congruence $\text{con}(a, b)$ is finitely join irreducible (it's an "atom-like" structure in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$). By Theorem 5.10 (since $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic + distributive), $\text{con}(a, b)$ is completely join prime.

Principally chain finite

The converse of Theorem 5.11 is false, as there are infinite simple lattices with no covering relations (E. T. Schmidt). However, for finite lattices, or more generally principally chain finite lattices, the converse does hold.

A lattice is principally chain finite if every principal ideal $\downarrow c$ satisfies the ACC and DCC.

This is a fairly natural finiteness condition that includes many interesting infinite lattices, and many results for finite lattices can be extended to principally chain finite lattices with a minimum of effort. Recall that if $x$ is a join irreducible element in such a lattice, then $x_*$ denotes the unique element such that $x \succ x_*$.

Theorem 5.12

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a principally chain finite lattice. Then every congruence relation on $\mathcal{L}$ is the join of completely join irreducible congruences. Moreover, every completely join irreducible congruence is of the form $\text{con}(x, x_*)$ for some join irreducible element $x$ of $\mathcal{L}$.

Proof · why · sketch

Every congruence relation is a join of compact congruences, and every compact congruence is a join of finitely many congruences $\text{con}(a, b)$ with $a > b$. In a principally chain finite lattice, every chain in $a/b$ is finite by Exercise 1.5, so there exists a covering chain $a = r_0 \succ r_1 \succ \dots \succ r_k = b$. Clearly $\text{con}(a, b) = \bigvee_{0 \le j < k} \text{con}(r_j, r_{j+1})$, and these latter are completely join prime by Theorem 5.11. Thus every congruence relation on $\mathcal{L}$ is the join of completely join irreducible congruences $\text{con}(r, s)$ with $r \succ s$.

Now let $a \succ b$ be any covering pair in $\mathcal{L}$. By the DCC for $\downarrow a$, there is an element $x$ that is minimal with respect to the properties $x \le a$ and $x \not\le b$. Since any element strictly below $x$ is below $b$, the element $x$ is join irreducible and $x_* = x \wedge b$. It is also true that $a = x \vee b$, since $b < x \vee b \le a$, and it follows easily from these two facts that $\text{con}(a, b) = \text{con}(x, x_*)$. ∎

The Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya analog

Theorem 5.12 is the lattice-theoretic version of the Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya decomposition theorem (Călugăreanu Ch 12) for principally chain finite lattices: every congruence is a (Birkhoff-canonical) join of completely join irreducible congruences indexed by join irreducible elements.

For finite lattices, this gives a clean structure theorem: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is generated by the principal congruences $\text{con}(x, x_*)$ as $x$ ranges over join-irreducible elements. The number of completely join irreducibles in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ equals the number of join-irreducible elements of $\mathcal{L}$.

There is much more to be said about congruence lattices of finite lattices, or more generally, principally chain finite lattices. We will return to these matters in Chapter 10.

We have ignored congruence lattices of semilattices, interesting on their own and applicable in universal algebra. Unlike congruence lattices of lattices, which are distributive, congruence lattices of semilattices satisfy NO lattice identities. For the basic results, see Freese-Nation [4], Fajtlowicz-Schmidt [3], and Adaricheva [1].

P4 Connection: Remak 1930 + group decomposition history

Subdirect decompositions originated in group theory: Remak's 1930 paper + the Krull-Schmidt theorem.
Connection · Remak's 1930 paper

Subdirect decompositions originated in group theory. Robert Remak's 1930 paper Über die Darstellung der endlichen Gruppen als Untergruppen direkter Produkte (J. Reine Angew. Math. 163, 1-44) gave the first systematic treatment of subdirect decompositions for finite groups.

Key results in Remak's paper:

  • Every finite group is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible groups (with respect to direct product decompositions).
  • The "Remak decomposition" is unique up to isomorphism (Krull-Schmidt-Remak theorem).

Krull-Schmidt theorem (1925/1928): a more general decomposition result that subsumed Remak's. For finite groups (or modules with chain conditions): every direct product decomposition into indecomposables is unique up to isomorphism.

The lineage:

  • Wedderburn 1909: structure theorem for semisimple algebras.
  • Krull-Schmidt 1925: indecomposable decomposition uniqueness for groups with chain conditions.
  • Remak 1930: subdirect decompositions for finite groups.
  • Birkhoff 1944: universal-algebraic generalisation (Theorem 5.7 in Nation).
  • Azumaya 1950: decomposition for modules with local endomorphism rings (Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya, Călugăreanu Ch 12).

Theorem 5.7 (Birkhoff's universal-algebraic generalisation) is the abstract face of all of these. The slogan: every algebra has a unique-ish decomposition into subdirectly irreducible factors — the analogue of "every integer factors into primes."

The "ish" reflects that the decomposition is into a SUBDIRECT product (not direct), so factors aren't fully isolated. Krull-Schmidt sharpens to direct products under additional hypotheses (chain conditions, local endomorphism rings, etc.).

Ex. 5.1Find Con L for $\mathcal{M}_n$, $\mathcal{N}_5$, and other small lattices
Find Con L for the lattices (a) $\mathcal{M}_n$ where $n \ge 3$, (b) $\mathcal{N}_5$, (c) the lattice $\mathcal{L}$ of Figure 5.1, and the lattices in Figure 5.2.

(a) $\mathcal{M}_n$, $n \ge 3$. The two-dimensional modular lattice $\mathcal{M}_n$ (with $n$ atoms above 0 and below 1) is SIMPLE for $n \ge 3$. So $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{M}_n$ is the two-element chain: $\{0, 1\}$ where $0 = $ equality and $1 = $ universal relation. Reasoning: any nonzero congruence identifies two atoms, hence by transitivity all atoms, hence $0 \sim 1$ via the universal relation.

(b) $\mathcal{N}_5$. The pentagon $\mathcal{N}_5$ is subdirectly irreducible but NOT simple. It has the structure: $0 < a < c < 1$, $0 < b < 1$, with $a \| b$, $c \| b$. The congruences:

  • 0 = equality
  • $\theta_1 = \text{con}(a, c)$: identifies $a$ and $c$, leaves $b$ separate.
  • $\theta_2 = \text{con}(b, 1) = \text{con}(0, b)$ — wait, in $\mathcal{N}_5$ specifically: identifies the chain $0 < a < c < 1$ entirely while keeping $b$ as 0 or 1.
  • 1 = universal.

$\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{N}_5$ is a 4-element distributive lattice (in fact $\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$). The monolith is $\text{con}(a, c)$ (or possibly different depending on naming). A critical pair: $(a, c)$ — every nonzero congruence identifies $a$ and $c$.

(c) Figure 5.1 ($\mathcal{L}$ as subdirect product of two $\mathcal{N}_5$'s). Six elements; $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is the 4-element Boolean lattice ($\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$), with two minimal congruences corresponding to the two $\mathcal{N}_5$ projections.

Ex. 5.2Join-prime via downsets is a congruence
An element $p$ of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is join prime if for any finite subset $F$ of $L$, $p \le \bigvee F$ implies $p \le f$ for some $f \in F$. Let $P(\mathcal{L})$ denote the set of join prime elements of $\mathcal{L}$, and define $x \Delta y$ iff $\downarrow x \cap P(\mathcal{L}) = \downarrow y \cap P(\mathcal{L})$. Prove that $\Delta$ is a congruence relation on $\mathcal{L}$.

Strategy. Verify (‡): $x \Delta y$ ⟹ $x \vee z \Delta y \vee z$ and $x \wedge z \Delta y \wedge z$.

Suppose $x \Delta y$, i.e., $\downarrow x \cap P = \downarrow y \cap P$ where $P = P(\mathcal{L})$.

Show $\downarrow(x \vee z) \cap P = \downarrow(y \vee z) \cap P$. For $p \in P$: $p \le x \vee z$ iff (by join-primeness) $p \le x$ or $p \le z$. By assumption, $p \le x$ iff $p \le y$ (within $P$), so $p \le x \vee z$ iff $p \le y$ or $p \le z$ iff $p \le y \vee z$. ✓

Show $\downarrow(x \wedge z) \cap P = \downarrow(y \wedge z) \cap P$. For $p \in P$: $p \le x \wedge z$ iff $p \le x$ and $p \le z$. By assumption: iff $p \le y$ and $p \le z$ iff $p \le y \wedge z$. ✓ ∎

Ex. 5.3Symmetric difference congruence on $\mathfrak{P}(X)$
Let $X$ be any set. Define a binary relation on $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ by $A \approx B$ iff the symmetric difference $(A - B) \cup (B - A)$ is finite. Prove that $\approx$ is a congruence relation on $\mathfrak{P}(X)$.

Strategy. Verify equivalence relation + (‡) compatibility.

Equivalence relation. Reflexive: $A \approx A$ since $(A - A) \cup (A - A) = \emptyset$ is finite. Symmetric: $A \approx B$ iff $B \approx A$ (definition is symmetric). Transitive: if $(A - B) \cup (B - A)$ is finite and $(B - C) \cup (C - B)$ is finite, then $(A - C) \cup (C - A) \subseteq [(A - B) \cup (B - A)] \cup [(B - C) \cup (C - B)]$ — finite union of finite sets, hence finite.

Compatibility (‡). Show $A \approx B$ ⟹ $A \cup C \approx B \cup C$ and $A \cap C \approx B \cap C$ for any $C$.

For union: $(A \cup C) - (B \cup C) \subseteq A - B$ and $(B \cup C) - (A \cup C) \subseteq B - A$. So $(A \cup C) \triangle (B \cup C) \subseteq A \triangle B$ — finite.

For intersection: $(A \cap C) \triangle (B \cap C) = (A \triangle B) \cap C \subseteq A \triangle B$ — finite.

So $\approx$ is a congruence. ∎

The quotient $\mathfrak{P}(X)/\approx$ is the "Boolean algebra mod finite sets" — useful in measure theory and ultrafilter constructions.

Ex. 5.4Lattice terms preserve homomorphisms and congruences
Lattice terms are defined in the proof of Theorem 6.1. (a) Show that if $p(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ is a lattice term and $h : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{K}$ is a homomorphism, then $h(p(a_1, \dots, a_n)) = p(h(a_1), \dots, h(a_n))$ for all $a_1, \dots, a_n \in L$. (b) Show that if $p(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ is a lattice term and $\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ and $a_i \theta b_i$ for $1 \le i \le n$, then $p(a_1, \dots, a_n) \theta p(b_1, \dots, b_n)$. (c) Surjective $h$ + $p(a_1, \dots, a_n) = q(a_1, \dots, a_n)$ in $\mathcal{L}$ for all $a_i$ ⟹ $p(c_1, \dots, c_n) = q(c_1, \dots, c_n)$ in $\mathcal{K}$.

(a) Induction on term complexity. Base case: $p(x) = x$. Then $h(p(a)) = h(a) = p(h(a))$. Step: $p = q \vee r$. Then $h(p(a_1, \dots)) = h(q(a_1, \dots) \vee r(a_1, \dots)) = h(q(a_1, \dots)) \vee h(r(a_1, \dots)) = q(h(a_1), \dots) \vee r(h(a_1), \dots) = p(h(a_1), \dots)$. Similarly for $\wedge$.

(b) Induction on term complexity. Base: $p(x) = x$. Then $a_i \theta b_i$ gives $p(a_i) \theta p(b_i)$. Step: $p = q \vee r$. By induction, $q(a_1, \dots) \theta q(b_1, \dots)$ and $r(a_1, \dots) \theta r(b_1, \dots)$. By (‡) compatibility, $q \vee r$ at $(a_i)$ is $\theta$-related to $q \vee r$ at $(b_i)$.

(c) Given $h$ surjective with $p = q$ holding in $\mathcal{L}$. For $c_i \in \mathcal{K}$, by surjectivity choose $a_i \in \mathcal{L}$ with $h(a_i) = c_i$. By (a): $p(c_1, \dots) = h(p(a_1, \dots)) = h(q(a_1, \dots)) = q(c_1, \dots)$. ∎

Ex. 5.6Cover preservation in quotients · semimodularity
Let $\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. (a) Show that $x \succ y$ implies $x\theta \succ y\theta$ or $x\theta = y\theta$. (b) Prove that if $\mathcal{L}$ is a finite semimodular lattice, then so is $\mathcal{L}/\theta$. (c) Prove that a subdirect product of semimodular lattices is semimodular.

(a) Suppose $x \succ y$. Then $x\theta \ge y\theta$. If $x\theta = y\theta$, done. Otherwise $x\theta > y\theta$. Suppose for contradiction there's $z\theta$ with $y\theta < z\theta < x\theta$. Then $z \theta z'$ for some $z'$ with $y < z' < x$ — contradicting $x \succ y$. So no such $z\theta$ exists, meaning $x\theta \succ y\theta$.

(b) $\mathcal{L}$ semimodular: $a \succ a \wedge b$ ⟹ $a \vee b \succ b$. Pass to $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ via (a): if $a\theta \succ a\theta \wedge b\theta = (a \wedge b)\theta$, then by lifting, $a \succ a \wedge b$ in some sense (or via the cover-preservation lemma). Then by semimodularity of $\mathcal{L}$, $a \vee b \succ b$, which projects to $(a \vee b)\theta \succ b\theta$, i.e., $a\theta \vee b\theta \succ b\theta$. ✓

(c) Subdirect product $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \prod \mathcal{L}_i$ with each $\mathcal{L}_i$ semimodular. The cover relation in $\mathcal{L}$ is determined by covers in each component. Semimodularity in each $\mathcal{L}_i$ gives semimodularity in the product, which restricts to $\mathcal{L}$. ∎

Ex. 5.7Finitely generated + finite quotient ⟹ congruence is compact
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a finitely generated lattice, and let $\theta$ be a congruence on $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is finite. Prove that $\theta$ is compact.

Strategy. Show $\theta$ is finitely generated (= compact in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ by Theorem 5.3).

$\mathcal{L}/\theta$ finite means $\theta$ has finitely many equivalence classes. Pick representatives $a_1, \dots, a_k$ for the classes. Then $\theta = \bigvee\{\text{con}(a_i, a_j) : (a_i, a_j) \in \theta\}$ — a join of finitely many principal congruences. By Theorem 5.3, this is compact. ∎

Ex. 5.8$\textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2) \cong \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_2$
Prove that $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2 \cong \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_2$. (Note that this is not true for groups; see Exercise 9.)

Strategy. Show every congruence on $\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2$ factors as a product of congruences on $\mathcal{L}_1$ and $\mathcal{L}_2$.

Map $\Phi : \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_2 \to \textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2)$ by $\Phi(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \{((a, b), (c, d)) : a \theta_1 c, b \theta_2 d\}$. Direct check: this is a congruence on the product.

Conversely, given $\theta \in \textbf{Con}(\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2)$, define $\theta_i = \pi_i(\theta) := \{(a, c) : ((a, x), (c, x)) \in \theta$ for some $x\}$ — the projection. Verify $\theta_i \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_i$ and $\theta = \Phi(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.

The key fact: in lattices, the diagonal projection of a congruence on a product factors cleanly. (For groups, this can fail because of "skew" subgroups; lattices avoid this via the majority polynomial.) ∎

Ex. 5.9Congruences of $\mathbb{Z}_p \times \mathbb{Z}_p$ + abelian congruence-distributive
Find the congruence lattice of the abelian group $\mathbb{Z}_p \times \mathbb{Z}_p$, where $p$ is prime. Find all finite abelian groups whose congruence lattice is distributive. (Recall that the congruence lattice of an abelian group is isomorphic to its subgroup lattice.)

Congruences of $\mathbb{Z}_p \times \mathbb{Z}_p$. Since congruences of an abelian group correspond to its subgroups, $\textbf{Con}(\mathbb{Z}_p \times \mathbb{Z}_p) \cong \textbf{Sub}(\mathbb{Z}_p \times \mathbb{Z}_p)$.

For $p = 2$: $\mathcal{M}_3$ — the diamond. For $p = 3$: $\mathcal{M}_4$. Etc.

(i) The projection $p_1 : \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{B}$, $(b, c) \mapsto b$, has kernel $\pi_1 = \{((b_1, c_1), (b_2, c_2)) : b_1 = b_2\}$. By the First Isomorphism Theorem (Theorem 5.1 generalised to algebras): $\mathcal{A}/\pi_1 \cong p_1(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{B}$. Similarly for $\pi_2$.

(ii) $\pi_1 \wedge \pi_2 = \pi_1 \cap \pi_2 = \{((b_1, c_1), (b_2, c_2)) : b_1 = b_2$ and $c_1 = c_2\} = \{((b, c), (b, c))\} = $ equality $= 0$.

$\pi_1 \vee \pi_2$: by the chain decomposition, $((b_1, c_1), (b_2, c_2)) \in \pi_1 \vee \pi_2$ iff there's a chain. Direct check: $(b_1, c_1) \pi_1 (b_2, c_1) \pi_2 (b_2, c_2)$, so $\pi_1 \vee \pi_2 = $ universal $= 1$. Similarly $\pi_1 \circ \pi_2 = $ universal in just one step. So $\pi_1 \vee \pi_2 = \pi_1 \circ \pi_2 = \pi_2 \circ \pi_1 = 1$ (i.e., $\pi_1, \pi_2$ permute).

Converse. Given $\varphi_1, \varphi_2 \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ with $\varphi_1 \wedge \varphi_2 = 0$ and $\varphi_1 \vee \varphi_2 = \varphi_1 \circ \varphi_2 = 1$. Define $\Phi : \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{A}/\varphi_1 \times \mathcal{A}/\varphi_2$ by $\Phi(a) = (a\varphi_1, a\varphi_2)$. Injective: $\ker \Phi = \varphi_1 \cap \varphi_2 = 0$. Surjective: given $(a\varphi_1, b\varphi_2) \in \mathcal{A}/\varphi_1 \times \mathcal{A}/\varphi_2$, by $\varphi_1 \circ \varphi_2 = 1$, find $c$ with $a \varphi_1 c \varphi_2 b$, then $\Phi(c) = (c\varphi_1, c\varphi_2) = (a\varphi_1, b\varphi_2)$. ∎

Looking Ahead — to Chapter 6 (Free Lattices)

Chapter 6 turns to free lattices: the "biggest" lattice on $n$ generators in the variety of all lattices.

Chapter 5 has developed the congruence theory: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic (Theorem 5.3), distributive (Theorem 5.8 — Funayama-Nakayama), and every lattice subdirectly decomposes via Birkhoff's Theorem 5.7. The 65-year congruence lattice problem was finally settled by Wehrung in 2007.

Chapter 6 (Free Lattices) turns to a different aspect: the "freest" lattice on a given set of generators. Key concepts:

  • Free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$. The lattice freely generated by the set $X$. Every map $X \to \mathcal{L}$ extends uniquely to a homomorphism $\text{FL}(X) \to \mathcal{L}$.
  • Whitman's W condition. A combinatorial characterisation of the order in $\text{FL}(X)$: $a_1 \wedge \dots \wedge a_n \le b_1 \vee \dots \vee b_m$ iff one of the natural sufficient conditions holds. Whitman 1941.
  • $\text{FL}(3)$ is infinite. Despite $\text{FL}(2)$ being finite (4 elements), the free lattice on 3 generators is infinite — a classical surprise.
  • Lattice terms. Formal expressions in $\wedge$ and $\vee$ over generators; equivalence classes form $\text{FL}(X)$.

The connection to Chapter 5: free lattices are constructed via the "free term algebra" mod a specific congruence (the lattice axioms). Whitman's W condition gives a combinatorial description of this congruence.
