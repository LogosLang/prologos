Basic Notions and Results

The foundational vocabulary: posets, lattices, the modular and distributive laws, $N_5$/$M_5$ obstructions (Dedekind 1900), upper continuity, atoms, complements, ACC/DCC.

Where the book starts. Order theory basics: posets, chains, comparable elements, sup/inf, max/min/largest/smallest. The Axiom of Choice and equivalents (Zorn, Hausdorff, Zermelo). Lattices via two equivalent definitions — order-theoretic (joins + meets exist) and universal-algebraic (commutative + associative + absorption, Theorem 1.1). Complete lattices, bounded lattices, examples ($\mathcal{P}(M)$, $S_R(M)$, $S(A, \Omega)$). Sublattices, intervals $b/a$, ideals, filters, lattice morphisms (Remarks 1.3-1.5). Modular law $a \le c \Rightarrow (a \vee b) \wedge c = a \vee (b \wedge c)$ — the lattice abstraction of "submodules behave nicely." Theorem 1.3 (cancellation form), Theorem 1.4 ($N_5$ obstruction — Dedekind 1900), Theorem 1.5 (the second isomorphism theorem for lattices). Distributive lattices, Theorems 1.6, 1.7 ($M_5$ obstruction). Upper continuity, atoms, complements, complemented vs relatively complemented (Ex 1.36: equivalent in modular). Chain conditions: Propositions 1.3, 1.4, 1.5. Connection panels: Birkhoff's 1940 textbook · Dedekind's 1900 paper · Boolean algebras + logic · universal algebra · Prologos.

Compactly Generated Lattices

The lattice analog of "finitely generated" — and the abstract setting in which submodule lattices live.

Defines the compact element (Nachbin–Stenström): a lattice element that is "captured by a finite slice" of any cover. Develops the equivalence with directed-cover S-compactness, the key examples ($\mathcal P(M)$, subalgebra lattices, submodule lattices), the compactly generated / algebraic class (Crawley–Dilworth), upper continuity, weak atomicity, and Crawley's Theorem 2.1 — modularity follows from the second-isomorphism shape in any compactly generated lattice. Krull's lemma on maximal elements. Two refinements (H-noetherian, join inaccessible). Five connection panels: domain theory · universal algebra · topology & Stone duality · abstract interpretation · Prologos.

Composition Series and Decompositions

When can a lattice element be broken into simple pieces — and is that decomposition unique?

The chapter where lattice theory borrows the structural arsenal of group and module theory: similar and projective intervals, equivalent chains, Schreier's refinement theorem, composition chains (= maximally refined), the Jordan–Hölder corollary, length as a well-defined invariant in modular lattices, finite length ⟺ noetherian + artinian, the additivity of length, the modular characterization via the submodular equality. Then irreducible decompositions: the Kuros–Ore theorem (uniqueness of decomposition length in modular lattices) with the famous non-modular counterexample. Five connection panels: vector spaces & dimension · module composition length · Jordan–Hölder for groups · abelian categories & quivers · Prologos.

Essential Elements and Pseudo-complements

Two new notions in counterpoint — "thick everywhere" and "maximally disjoint" — and the apparatus that ties them together.

An element is essential if it touches every non-zero piece; essentially closed if it has no proper essential extensions. A pseudo-complement of $b$ is an element maximal with $b \wedge c = 0$. The chapter's deepest result (Cor 4.3): in upper continuous modular lattices, pseudo-complements ARE the essentially closed elements. Includes Lemma 4.1 (modular complement ⟹ pseudo-complement) with $N_5$ counterexample, Prop 4.2's chain of equivalences, Lemma 4.3 (existence of maximal essential extensions = injective hulls). Five connection panels: modules & injective hulls · Heyting & intuitionism · residuation deep-dive (left/right, Heyting implication, quantales, ideal quotients) · Boolean/Heyting/p-c disambiguation · Prologos.

Socle. Torsion Lattices

The bottom-most "atomic foundation" of a lattice, and the lattices that grow from atoms upward step-by-step.

The socle $s(L)$ — join of all atoms — is the lattice's irreducible base. Theorem 5.1 says it equals the meet of all essential elements (in compactly generated modular lattices), tying socle to essentialness from Ch 4. Then torsion lattices: every upper interval has atoms, so the lattice is climbed atom-by-atom (also called semiartinian). The Loewy series $0 = s_0 \le s_1 \le \cdots$ iterates socle taking transfinitely; Prop 5.5 says torsion ⟺ this series reaches $1$. Then torsion elements form a principal ideal $t(L)/0$ with the radical property; RSC (restricted socle condition); torsion-free elements (a subtle dual concept!); and cycles. Five connection panels: modules & primary decomposition · group theory & locally cyclic · Loewy & representation theory · t-structures & radical theory · Prologos.

Independence and Semiatomic Lattices

The lattice analog of linear independence; the Goldie dimension as the canonical numerical invariant of non-noetherian module theory; the lattice form of the Krull–Schmidt theorem.

Independent subsets generalise linearly-independent vectors: $a_i \wedge \bigvee_{j \ne i} a_j = 0$. Direct sums $\bigoplus a_i$ are the corresponding "non-overlapping" joins. Theorem 6.1: maximal independent ⟺ join essential — coupling Ch 6's apparatus to Ch 4's essentialness. Goldie dimension (uniform dimension) — Theorem 6.4's four-way equivalence defines what it means to have finite-dimensional non-noetherian module structure. Krull–Schmidt (Theorem 6.5 of Ore): in finite-length modular lattices, indecomposable decompositions are unique up to permutation. Semiatomic lattices (Theorem 6.8): four-way equivalence characterising "module of simple parts." Five connections: modules & Goldie · vector space dimension · Krull–Schmidt · matroids · Prologos.

Radical, Superfluous and Fully Invariant Elements

The lattice form of the Jacobson radical and Frattini subgroup; characteristic-style decomposition-stable elements.

An element is superfluous if it can be removed from any cover of $1$. The radical $r(L)$ is the meet of all maximal elements. Theorem 7.1: in compactly generated lattices, $r(L)$ equals the join of all superfluous elements — recovering Bass's characterisation of the Jacobson radical and Frattini's 1885 theorem on non-generators. Theorem 7.3 gives a five-way characterisation of "atom generated of finite length and complemented" — the lattice form of Wedderburn–Artin. Then fully invariant elements: those that respect every direct decomposition of $1$. The lattice version of characteristic / fully invariant subgroups. Five connection panels: Jacobson radical · Frattini subgroup · Frattini subalgebra · characteristic vs fully invariant · Prologos.

Lattices of Finite Uniform Dimension

Goldie dimension as the central invariant; the Osofsky–Smith theorem in lattice form.

An element is uniform (= co-irreducible) if every non-zero element below it is essential — the "atomic units" of dimension theory. Theorem 8.1 establishes invariance of dimension. Theorem 8.2 gives a six-way characterisation of finite uniform dimension: bounded independent cardinality, ACC⊕, eventual essentialness, no infinite independent subsets, $0$ as finite meet of irreducibles. Theorem 8.3 + Cor 8.1 bound dimension via $a/0$ and $1/a$. Proposition 8.2 ties essential closure to dimension equality. Then CS (= extending) lattices — pseudo-complements coincide with complements — and the lattice form of the Osofsky–Smith theorem (Theorem 8.4): a compact modular CG H-noetherian RSC lattice with completely CS principal ideals has finite uniform dimension. Five connection panels: uniform modules · Goldie dimension · CS modules & Osofsky-Smith · quasi-injective & continuous · Prologos.

Purity and Neatness in Lattices

The lattice form of Prüfer's pure subgroups (1923) and Honda's neat subgroups (1956).

An element is pure (T. Head 1972, generalising Prüfer 1923) if it has a complement in every compact upper interval; neat (Honda 1956) if it has a complement in every atomic upper interval. Pure ⟹ neat (in upper continuous, Theorem 9.14). Theorem 9.3: in compactly generated modular, every element pure ⟺ sectionally complemented. Theorem 9.7's four-way equivalence ties purity to atom-generation, complementation, and basis existence. Theorem 9.8 (the chapter's keystone): every maximal essential extension is neat. The R1/R2 refinements of RSC; Theorem 9.9 (in R1 lattices, neat ⟺ essentially closed — Honda's theorem in lattice form). Theorem 9.13 characterises "every element is neat" via $t(0)$. Five connection panels: Prüfer's pure subgroups · Honda's neat subgroups · pure submodules (Cohn) · closed/essentially-closed · Prologos.

Coatomic Lattices

The order-dual of atomic. Bass's 1960 B-objects in lattice form. Local lattices and the structural duality with Chapter 5's essentialness theory.

A lattice is coatomic if every $a \ne 1$ has a maximal element above it (the dual of "atomic"). Bass introduced the module version (B-objects) in 1960 to study perfect rings. Krull's Lemma: compact ⟹ coatomic. Theorem 10.1: modular semiatomic inductive ⟹ complemented + atom generated. Theorem 10.2: in modular inductive lattices, semiatomic ⟺ coatomic + every maximal complemented. The chapter's central result is Theorem 10.3: under condition (D), an element is superfluous iff $a/0$ is coatomic and $a \le r(L)$ — perfectly dual to Chapter 5's essentialness characterisation. Local lattices (Proposition 10.8): $L - \{1\}$ has a largest element ⟺ coatomic + $r(L)$ maximal. Exercises 10.1-10.10 classify abelian groups with local subgroup lattice (cyclic prime power + pure dense in $p$-adics). Five connection panels: Bass's B-objects · divisible & reduced abelian groups · local lattices & rings · nearly divisible groups · Prologos.

Co-compact Lattices

The order-dual of compactness. Finitely cogenerated modules in lattice form. Theorem 11.2 ties co-compact to "socle compact and essential."

An element $a$ is co-compact if whenever $a \ge \bigwedge S$ there is a finite $F \subseteq S$ with $a \ge \bigwedge F$ — the order-dual of compactness. A lattice is co-compact when $0$ is a co-compact element. By Stenström's terminology, co-compact = finitely cogenerated. Lemma 11.2: in modular lattices, co-compact elements are closed under finite joins. Proposition 11.2: an artinian lattice is co-compact (the dual of "noetherian ⟹ compact"). Theorem 11.1 (three-way): in compactly generated modular lattices with $r(L) = 0$, co-compact ⟺ semiatomic with finite atoms ⟺ artinian. Theorem 11.2 (keystone): in compactly generated modular lattices, co-compact ⟺ socle is compact and essential — order-dual of "compact ⟺ radical is co-compact and superfluous." Connection panels: finitely cogenerated modules · order duality (compact ↔ co-compact) · linearly compact modules (Onodera 1973) · QF rings & Morita duality · Prologos.

Supplemented Lattices · Locally Artinian Lattices

A supplement is a minimal join-witness — the order-dual of pseudo-complement. Corollary 12.2 is the keystone: in compact modular lattices, supplemented ⟺ $1$ is an irredundant join of local elements. The chapter's second half lifts Wisbauer's four-way Z-module exercise to lattice form.

An element $c$ is a supplement of $b$ if it is minimal with $b \vee c = 1$ — equivalently (Prop 12.1) $b \vee c = 1$ and $b \wedge c$ superfluous in $c/0$. Lemma 12.1: every complement is a supplement in bounded modular. The seven-part Proposition 12.2 unpacks how supplements behave in compactly generated modular lattices: descent along $\le$, compactness, radical-witness for maximal $b$, perturbation by superfluous, quotient transport. Hollow lattices (every $\ne 1$ superfluous) and local lattices ($L \setminus \{1\}$ has top) are introduced; the Prüfer $p$-group's subgroup lattice is the canonical hollow-not-local example. Corollary 12.2 (the keystone): in compact modular lattices, supplemented ⟺ every maximal has a supplement ⟺ $1$ is a join of hollow elements ⟺ $1$ is an irredundant join of local elements — the lattice form of the semiperfect-module structure theorem. The chapter's second half — locally artinian lattices — generalises Wisbauer's four-way Z-module exercise: locally artinian ⟺ DCC on cyclic ⟺ essential socle ⟺ torsion. Three of the four equivalences lift cleanly under H-noetherian + (RSC); the fourth requires cyclically generated + condition (U) (Prop 12.9). Connection panels: Wisbauer's Foundations §41 · Krull-Schmidt-Azumaya · locally artinian Z-modules · order duality theme · Prologos.

Several Dimensions

The closing chapter: four dimension concepts on lattices — dual Goldie (hollow), infinite Goldie via the ideal lattice, the transfinite Krull dimension of Gabriel-Rentschler-Hart, and Gabriel dimension. Krause's punchline: $k$-dim $\le g$-dim $\le k$-dim$+1$.

Chapter 13 collects four distinct notions of dimension. Dual Goldie (hollow) dimension $h$-dim$(L) = $ dim$(L^0)$ — the order-dual of Goldie dim via $L \mapsto L^0$. Theorem 13.1 gives the five-way characterisation in bounded modular. Infinite Goldie via the ideal lattice $I_0(L)$: a compactly generated upper continuous home into which $L$ embeds; Theorem 13.4 (Crawley-style cardinal bound), Theorem 13.5 (4-way: $L$ has u-basis ⟺ each non-zero element contains a uniform). Krull dimension via Gabriel-Rentschler deviation — a transfinite ordinal recursive definition. Lemmonier's Theorem 13.6: $A$ has no Krull dim ⟺ $A$ contains a copy of the dyadic numbers $\mathbf{D}$. Hart's $\alpha$-critical lattices as building blocks (Lemmas 13.7, 13.8). Krause's Theorem 13.7: upper continuous modular + Krull dim ⟹ finite uniform dim. Gabriel dimension $g$-dim$(L)$: $0 \iff L = \{0\}$; $1 \iff $ torsion. Krause's comparison Theorem 13.9: $k$-dim$(L) \le g$-dim$(L) \le k$-dim$(L) + 1$. Theorem 13.10: noetherian ⟹ $g$-dim $= k$-dim $+ 1$. Connection panels: Goldie's theorem · Krull dim in commutative algebra → Gabriel-Rentschler · Gabriel categories + localization · synthesis (the four-dimension fingerprint) · Prologos. The closing "Looking Back" section synthesizes the journey from Chapter 1's compactness primitives to the four-dimensional fingerprint at Chapter 13's close.
