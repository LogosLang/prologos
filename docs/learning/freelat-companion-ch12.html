Chapter XII · The volume's epilogue

Term Rewrite Systems and Varieties of Lattices

Two main results frame the entire chapter: the variety of all lattices admits no finite, convergent AC term rewrite system (Theorem 12.8); yet an infinite family of finitely generated lattice varieties — those generated by $\mathbf{L}^\vee\!\left(\bigwedge_i \bigvee_j x_{ij}\right)$ — does (Theorem 12.28). Between these poles sits the open question of $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$.

~140–170 min reading book pp. 255–277 · 23 pages 6 sections · 28 named results 1 figure (Venn diagram) 1 open problem

Scope (paraphrased from Freese-Ježek-Nation, p. 255): Term rewrite systems give a strong form of the solution of the word problem for free algebras in certain varieties. For example, one can tell whether two lattice terms are equal in all distributive lattices by rewriting each as a join of meets of variables, applying absorption, and comparing the join normal forms. When properly formalized, this algorithm becomes an associative-and-commutative TRS for distributive lattices. The chapter contains two main results: the variety of all lattices has no such TRS, and an infinite collection of varieties — each generated by a finite lower bounded lattice — does. Along the way, results on varieties generated by finite lower bounded lattices that are of independent interest are proved.

— Free Lattices, p. 255 (paraphrased)

OVERVIEW The chapter at a glance

Whitman's canonical form solves the lattice word problem in polynomial time (Chapter XI). But "polynomial time" is not the same as "term rewrite system." A TRS demands more: a finite set of local rewrite rules that reduces every term to its canonical form, and that terminates regardless of the order in which rules are applied. Chapter XII shows the variety $\mathcal{L}$ of all lattices fails this stronger demand — but a remarkably large positive class of subvarieties succeeds.

The chapter falls into three asymmetric arcs. §1 sets terminology: what TRS, AC TRS, equational TRS, and normal-form TRS mean precisely, with Lemma 12.1's four basic preservation facts and Lemmas 12.2-12.3 carrying lattice-specific machinery from Chapter III over.

§2 proves the negative result (Theorem 12.8). The proof is a beautiful diagonalization: assume a finite, convergent AC TRS exists; produce a specific term longer than any rule's left-hand side that nevertheless cannot have any rule applied to it. The construction uses the family of canonically structured terms in (4)-(7) of Lemma 12.7, all of which are in normal form by symmetry arguments via Lemmas 12.4-12.6. §3 extends the negative result by weakening the definition of "terminal" — and shows that even this weaker AC TRS notion still does not exist for lattices. Footnote 3 (in §6) sketches how the same argument generalizes to a whole interval of varieties $\mathcal{U} \le \mathcal{V} \le \mathcal{L}$ between a small locally-finite variety $\mathcal{U}$ and all lattices.

§§4-6 turn around: the variety $V(\mathbf{L})$ generated by a finite lower bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is studied structurally, with explicit characterizations of homomorphic images (Theorem 12.11), sublattices (Theorem 12.12), $HS(\mathbf{L})$ (Theorem 12.13), and a concrete algorithm for $\mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ (Corollary 12.14, Lemma 12.15, Theorem 12.19, Corollary 12.16). For the specific lower bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}_2 = \mathbf{L}^\vee(x \wedge (y \vee z))$, an explicit AC TRS is constructed: rules (9)-(29), terminating via the Alimov-inspired measure $|\cdot|$ (Lemma 12.22). Theorem 12.28 sketches the extension to $V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(w))$ where $w = \bigwedge_{i=1}^m \bigvee_{j=1}^{n_i} x_{ij}$. The chapter — and the entire monograph — closes with Problem 12.27 still open: does $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$ have an AC TRS?

Strategy at a glance

The negative proof (§2) uses three technical lemmas (12.4-12.6) to establish that certain canonically structured terms are already in normal form. Then any candidate rewrite rule must apply to a strictly shorter term — and Lemma 12.7 packages four such normal forms (4)-(7) that exhaust all "shorter terms with an endomorphism onto $x \wedge w$." This forces the contradiction. The positive constructions (§§5-6) hinge on the V(L)-canonical form: characterize $A_X(\mathbf{L}) = \{u \in J(\mathbf{FL}(X)) : \mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L})\}$ syntactically, then derive rewrite rules from each violation pattern via Venn-diagram-driven case analysis (Figure 12.1).

VOCABULARY Vocabulary primer

The chapter mixes two communities' jargon. Term-rewriting concepts (substitution, one-step rewrite, terminal, convergent, AC subterm) come from the Knuth-Bendix tradition; lower-bounded lattice concepts (the maps $\beta_h, \beta_0$; the partial map $\gamma$ from Lemma 12.15; the V(L)-canonical form) come from Chapter II. This section primes both.
Term-rewriting vocabulary

Term rewrite system (TRS). A finite or infinite set $R$ of ordered equations, written $p \to q$. A term $r$ is rewritten to $t$ (notation: $r \to_R t$) if some subterm of $r$ has the form $\sigma(p)$ for some substitution $\sigma$ and rewrite rule $p \to q \in R$, and $t$ is obtained by replacing one such occurrence by $\sigma(q)$.

Substitution. An endomorphism of the term algebra. Equivalently: a function from variables to terms, extended homomorphically.

Terminating, convergent, normal form. A TRS is terminating if no infinite rewrite chain exists from any term. A terminating TRS is convergent if every rewrite chain from a given term ends in the same final term, called the normal form $\mathrm{nf}_R(s)$.

Equational TRS, AC TRS. A pair $(E_0, R)$ where $E_0$ is a set of regular equations and $R$ is a TRS, allowing alternating rewrites of the form $s_0 \equiv s_1 \to_R s_2 \equiv s_3 \to_R \cdots$, where $\equiv$ means $E_0$-equivalent. Regular means the variable sets on both sides agree. When $E_0$ is the associative + commutative laws for meet and join, the system is an AC TRS.

$E_0$-subterm, AC-subterm. $v$ is an $E_0$-subterm of $u$ if $v$ is a subterm of some $u' \equiv u$. AC-subterm specializes this when $E_0$ is AC.

Term equivalence problem (TEP). For a class $\mathcal{X}$ of lattices: given two terms $u, v$, decide whether $u \approx v$ holds in every lattice of $\mathcal{X}$.

Lattice-theoretic vocabulary (carried from Chapter II)

Lower bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}$. A finite lattice such that every homomorphism $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}$ from a finitely-generated free lattice has an associated map $\beta_h$ (the "least preimage" map) defined on the principal ideal $h(1_{\mathbf{FL}(X)})/0$.

The map $\beta_0$. For a homomorphism $h : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}$, the map $\beta_0(a) = \bigwedge\{x \in X : h(x) \ge a\}$, mapping each $a \in \mathbf{L}$ to a meet of generators in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

The map $\beta_h$. The unique extension of $\beta_0$ from $\mathbf{L}$ into $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that maps each $a$ to its least preimage. Constructed inductively (Theorem 2.47): $\beta_h(a) = \beta_0(a) \wedge \bigwedge_{B \in \mathfrak{M}^*(a)} \bigvee_{b \in B} \beta_h(b)$.

The partial map $\gamma : \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}) \to X^\wedge$. A practical surrogate for $\beta_0|_{\mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L})}$, defined directly on join-irreducibles. Lemma 12.15 gives necessary and sufficient conditions for $\gamma$ to come from some homomorphism: a domain condition (1) and a covering condition (2).

$V(\mathbf{L})$-canonical form. The Whitman canonical form of the largest element of $A_X(\mathbf{L})^\vee$ below the term being normalized. For a finite lower bounded $\mathbf{L}$, this gives a normal form for the equational theory of $V(\mathbf{L})$.

$A_X(\mathbf{L})$. The set $\{u \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X)) : \mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L})\}$, equivalently $\{\beta_f(c) : c \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X))\}$. Theorem 12.9 gives $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X) \cong A_X(\mathbf{L})^\vee$.

$\mathbf{L}_n = \mathbf{L}^\vee(x \wedge (y_1 \vee \cdots \vee y_n))$. The infinite family of lower bounded lattices for which Theorem 12.28 produces an AC TRS. $\mathbf{L}_2$ is the pentagon-like lattice diagrammed in Figure 3.2(1) on book p. 83.

$x \wedge w$ where $w = \bigvee_{i=1}^n (x \wedge \underline{z}_i)$. The specific term family used to defeat any candidate AC TRS in Theorem 12.8. Here $\underline{z}_i = \bigwedge_{j \neq i} z_j$.

Part A · Foundations

Term rewrite systems for lattices, formally

§1 imports the term-rewriting framework from Knuth-Bendix and Peterson-Stickel, then carries the lattice-specific machinery (Whitman's criterion, Tschantz's Lemma 3.28) over for use in §2's proof.

§ 1 Term Rewrite Systems

The historical and definitional bedrock. Lemma 12.1 collects four properties of normal forms in finite, normal-form, equational TRSs; Lemma 12.2 (Tschantz) gives the canonical-joinand inequality used pervasively in §2; Lemma 12.3 records the basic fact that every element of $\mathbf{FL}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is comparable to each $x_k$.

Historical development (book p. 256)

Term rewrite systems were pioneered by Trevor Evans (1951) with a convergent TRS for quasigroups [44 in the bibliography]. The subject was popularized by Knuth and Bendix (1970) [91], who gave methods that could sometimes convert equational axioms into a convergent TRS — most famously a convergent TRS for groups. Equational TRSs were introduced by Lankford and Ballantyne (1977) [93] and, independently, by Peterson and Stickel (1981) [111]. Peterson and Stickel exhibited convergent AC TRSs for free commutative groups, commutative rings with unit, and — crucially for this chapter — distributive lattices.

The general references the chapter recommends are Dershowitz and Jouannaud (1990) [37] and Jouannaud and Kirchner (1986) [85]. The specific question — does lattice theory have a finite, convergent AC TRS? — is Problem 32 of Dershowitz, Jouannaud, and Klop (1991), "Open Problems in Rewriting" [38], suggested to the authors by George McNulty.

Definition · Term rewrite system (TRS)

A set $R$ of ordered equations $p \to q$. A substitution $\sigma$ is an endomorphism of the term algebra. If $(p \to q) \in R$ and $r$ is a term with a subterm $\sigma(p)$, then replacing one occurrence by $\sigma(q)$ yields the one-step rewrite $r \to_R t$.

$R$ is terminating if no infinite rewrite chain exists; convergent if for every $s$, every rewrite chain from $s$ ends in the same term, the normal form $\mathrm{nf}_R(s)$. An equational theory $E$ has a convergent TRS provided $s \approx t \in E \iff \mathrm{nf}(s) = \mathrm{nf}(t)$.

Definition · Equational TRS, AC TRS

If $E_0$ is a set of regular equations (variable sets agree on both sides), define $s \equiv t$ iff $E_0 \models s \approx t$. An equational TRS is a pair $(E_0, R)$ where $R$ is a TRS, allowing rewrite chains of the form $s_0 \equiv s_1 \to_R s_2 \equiv s_3 \to_R \cdots$. A term $u$ is terminal if no rewrite rule applies to $u$ or to any $u' \equiv u$. The system is normal form equational if every chain from $s$ eventually ends in a terminal element whose $\equiv$-class is unique. When $E_0$ is the AC laws for meet and join, the system is an AC TRS.

Lemma 12.1 — preservation properties of normal forms (book p. 257)

Lemma 12.1

Suppose $(E_0, R)$ is a finite, normal-form, equational TRS. Then:

  1. If $v$ is an $E_0$-subterm of $u$ and $\mathrm{nf}(u) \equiv u$, then $\mathrm{nf}(v) \equiv v$.
  2. If $w$ is a term and $\sigma$ is an automorphism of the term algebra, then $\mathrm{nf}(\sigma(w)) \equiv \sigma(\mathrm{nf}(w))$.
  3. If $u = \sigma(v)$ for some endomorphism of the term algebra and $\mathrm{nf}(u) \equiv u$, then $\mathrm{nf}(v) \equiv v$.
  4. The variables which occur in $\mathrm{nf}(w)$ all occur in $w$.

These four facts drive almost every argument in §§2-3. (1) is "subterms of normal forms are normal forms." (2) is "automorphism-equivariance of normal form." (3) is the load-bearing one: if a term $u$ is a substitution-image of $v$, and $u$ is in normal form, then $v$ is too. (4) ensures rewriting cannot introduce new variables — used to argue that the introduced variables in (5) of Lemma 12.7 cannot grow.

Lemma 12.2 — canonical joinand inequality (Tschantz, book p. 258)

Lemma 12.2

Let $u_1$ be a canonical joinand of $u$. Then:

  1. If $u_1 \le s \wedge t \le u$, then either $s \le u$ or $t \le u$ or $u_1 = s \wedge t$;
  2. If $u_1 \le s \vee t \le u$, then either $u_1 \le s$ or $u_1 \le t$;
  3. If $u_1 \le x \le u$ where $x \in X$, then $u_1 = x$.

This is essentially the argument of Lemma 3.28 (Chapter III), repackaged for direct citation. It is the workhorse for the "subterm-substitution" arguments in §2: when a normal-form term is below another, the canonical joinands of the lower one must "land" on canonical joinands of the upper one in a structurally restricted way.

Lemma 12.3 (book p. 258)

If $u > \underline{x}_i$ in $\mathbf{FL}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$, then $u \ge x_j$ for some $j \neq i$.

An easy fact about the elements $\underline{x}_i = \bigwedge_{j \neq i} x_j$ — the meets of all generators except one. These elements are central to the family (4)-(7) in Lemma 12.7.

Part B · The negative result

No AC TRS for the variety of all lattices

Theorem 12.8 is the chapter's flagship negative result. The proof exhibits a specific lattice term that no rewrite rule can match — assuming any candidate rule's left-hand side must be shorter than the term itself, every shorter substitution-image is already in normal form via Lemma 12.7.

§ 2 No AC TRS for Lattice Theory

A length-and-symmetry argument: assume the AC TRS exists; pick a term $w$ whose length exceeds every rule's LHS; show that every term that could be a candidate match is already in normal form. Lemmas 12.4-12.6 establish the symmetry; Lemma 12.7 packages the normal forms; Theorem 12.8 closes by counting.

Lemma 12.4 — formal join structure of normal forms (book p. 258)

Lemma 12.4

Let $t = t_1 \vee \cdots \vee t_n$ be in canonical form with $n > 1$. Then $\mathrm{nf}(t)$ is a formal join $u = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_m$, and there is a map $\sigma$ from $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ onto $\{1, \ldots, m\}$ such that $t_i \le u_{\sigma(i)}$ for all $i$. In particular, $1 < m \le n$. Moreover, $u_1, \ldots, u_m$ form an antichain.

Proof sketch. Since $n > 1$, $t \not\approx x$ for any variable, so $u$ cannot be a variable. If $u$ were a formal meet $u = u_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge u_m$, then $u_i \approx u$ for some $i$ (since $u \approx t$ is meet-irreducible, by Whitman + canonical form), and Lemma 12.1 would give $u_i \equiv \mathrm{nf}(u_i) = \mathrm{nf}(u) \equiv u$ — false. So $u$ is a formal join. The refinement property of Whitman canonical form (Theorem 1.19) gives the map $\sigma$. The AC-subterm $u' = \bigvee_{j \in \mathrm{range}\,\sigma} u_j$ is in normal form (Lemma 12.1(1)) and $\approx u$, forcing $u' \equiv u$, hence $\sigma$ is onto. ∎

Lemma 12.5 — adding a variable preserves normal form (book p. 258)

Lemma 12.5

Suppose $w$ is in normal form, $w$ is a formal meet, and $x$ is a variable such that $x \vee w$ is in canonical form. Then $x \vee w$ is in normal form.

Proof structure. By Lemma 12.4, $\mathrm{nf}(x \vee w) = s \vee t$ with $x \le s$ and $w \le t$. Show $s = x$ and $t = w$: if $w < t$, then by Lemma 12.2 $t$ has a subterm $v \vee r$ with $r \approx w$ (and $v$ possibly a join). Then map every variable to $x$ via the endomorphism that sends $u \mapsto u' \approx x$. The same rewrite chain applies to $x \vee w'$, eventually rewriting back to $x \vee w'$ — an infinite chain, contradiction. Hence $t = w$. A similar argument forces $s = x$. ∎

Lemma 12.6 — symmetry strengthens Lemma 12.4 (book p. 259)

Lemma 12.6

Suppose $t = t_1 \vee \cdots \vee t_n$ canonically with $n > 1$ and there is a subgroup $\mathbf{G} \cong \mathbf{S}_n$ of the term-algebra automorphism group acting faithfully on $\{t_1, \ldots, t_n\}$. Let $\mathrm{nf}(t) = r = r_1 \vee \cdots \vee r_m$. Then $m = n$ and, after reordering, $r_i \ge t_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$, and $r_i \not\ge t_j$ for $i \neq j$.

The pigeonhole counting: any $r_i$ that's "above $k$ of the $t_j$'s" must, by transitivity of $\mathbf{S}_n$ on $k$-subsets, have $\binom{n}{k}$ symmetric copies among the $r$'s. Lemma 12.4 caps the count at $n$, so $\binom{n}{k} \le n$, forcing $k = 1$ (or $k = n - 1$, which produces redundancy).

Lemma 12.7 — four normal-form patterns (book p. 259)

Lemma 12.7

Let $x, y, z_1, \ldots, z_n, e_1, \ldots, e_s$ be distinct variables, and let $\underline{z}_i = \bigwedge_{j \neq i} z_j$. If $n, s \ge 1$ and $k \ge 0$, then the following are all in normal form:

  • (4) $z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n$
  • (5) $\bigvee_{i=1}^n (x \wedge \underline{z}_i)$
  • (6) $\bigvee_{i=1}^n (x \wedge z_i)$
  • (7) $x \wedge \left[\bigvee_{i=1}^k (x \wedge z_i) \vee \bigvee_{j=1}^s e_j\right]$

This is the package the diagonalization needs. Each is in canonical form (Theorem 1.18). For (4) — a meet of all $z_i$'s — Lemma 12.6 with $\mathbf{S}_n$ acting on the $z_i$'s gives $\mathrm{nf}(z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n) = r_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge r_n$; the obvious endomorphism $z_i \mapsto r_i$ + Lemma 12.1(3) forces them equal. For (5), a similar argument plus Lemma 12.3 forces $r_i = a_i = x \wedge \underline{z}_i$. (6) reduces to (5) via endomorphism. (7) follows from (5)/(6) and the dual of Lemma 12.5.

Theorem 12.8 — the negative result (book p. 260)

Theorem 12.8

There is no finite, convergent AC term rewrite system for the equational theory of lattices.

Proof of Theorem 12.8

Suppose such a TRS exists, with normal form $\mathrm{nf}$. Let $w = \bigvee_{i=1}^n (x \wedge z_i)$ as in (6) of Lemma 12.7, where $n$ is large enough that the length of $w$ exceeds every rewrite rule's LHS. Clearly $w \le x$, so $x \wedge w \approx w$, and some rule must apply to $x \wedge w$ — but $w \in \mathrm{nf}$ (Lemma 12.7), so every proper AC-subterm of $x \wedge w$ is in normal form. The matched LHS must be of length $\le |x \wedge w|$ — too short to be $x \wedge w$ itself — yet it must have a substitution-image equal to $x \wedge w$.

What terms shorter than $x \wedge w$ have a substitution onto $x \wedge w$? Besides $a$ and $a \wedge b$, the most general candidate is

$a \wedge \left[(c_1 \wedge d_1) \vee \cdots \vee (c_k \wedge d_k) \vee e_1 \vee \cdots \vee e_s\right]$

where some/all $c_i$'s may equal each other or $a$, but the other variables are distinct. Every such candidate has a further endomorphism onto

$a \wedge \left[(a \wedge d_1) \vee \cdots \vee (a \wedge d_k) \vee e_1 \vee \cdots \vee e_s\right]$

which is in normal form by Lemma 12.7's pattern (7). Hence (Lemma 12.1(3)) every candidate LHS is itself in normal form — but no rule's LHS can be in normal form (else it could not match). Contradiction. ∎

Intuition

The argument is a beautiful diagonal. Whitman canonical form and AC normalization are both "best-shape" choices — and yet they don't agree as locally rewritable rules. The deep reason: the rule system would need a rule whose left-hand-side has the same structure as some big term in normal form. By Lemma 12.7, every "structurally similar" shorter term is already in normal form — there's no shorter rule available to apply. AC equivalence is finitely generated, but normal-form rewriting on lattices isn't. The negative is not about decidability (we have polytime decidability from Chapter XI) — it's about local decidability: rules cannot be both purely local AND finite AND convergent for $\mathcal{L}$.

§ 3 An Extension

A weakening exercise: define "terminal" so that $u' \equiv u$ rewriting to $v$ requires $v \equiv u$, then ask whether Theorem 12.8 still holds. It does — most of the proof goes through, except Lemma 12.7's proofs need patching with explicit AC-equivalence reasoning (book p. 261-262).

The extension shows the negative result is robust: even if we relax the meaning of "no rule applies" from "no AC-subterm has a match" to "every match recovers an AC-equivalent term," the absence of an AC TRS persists. The proof reuses Lemmas 12.4-12.6 unchanged. The patches concern Lemma 12.7's elements (6) and (7): under the weaker definition, the rewrite $\bigvee (x \wedge z_i) \to r_1 \vee \cdots \vee r_n$ must yield an AC-equivalent term (since the LHS is in normal form), forcing $r_i \in \{x \wedge z_i, z_i \wedge x\}$ — pattern (6) is still in normal form. A similar argument handles (7).

The TEP (term equivalence problem) for special classes (book p. 262)

Peterson and Stickel [111] showed that the equational theory of distributive lattices does have a convergent AC TRS — so distributive lattices succeed where general lattices fail. But the TEP for distributive lattices is harder than for general lattices: Bloniarz, Hunt, and Rosenkrantz [12] showed it is co-NP complete. In fact, they showed the TEP for $\mathcal{X} = \{\mathbf{L}\}$ is co-NP complete for any finite, nontrivial lattice $\mathbf{L}$.

Meanwhile, the equational theory of modular lattices was shown to be undecidable by Freese (1980) [49]. So the TEP landscape has three regimes:

For distributive lattices and the $V(\mathbf{L}_n)$ varieties, we have local rewriting, but the decision is harder (co-NP). Local structure (TRS) and global structure (decidability) are not the same dimension.

Footnote 3 — extension to a wider variety (book p. 274)

Deeper observation

The proof of Theorem 12.8 uses only words in $X^{\wedge\vee\wedge}$ — terms of depth at most three (a meet of joins of meets of variables). Whitman's condition (W) was used only weakly: it was required that the elements not be both meet- and join-reducible. (W) is also used implicitly in the proof of Lemma 12.2, but can be replaced there by an upper-and-lower-bound argument when these exist in relatively free lattices.

Let $\mathcal{U}$ denote the variety generated by all the finite lattices $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ and $\mathbf{L}^\wedge(w)$ where $w$ appears explicitly in the proof of Theorem 12.8. This is a locally finite variety. By following the argument of Theorem 12.8 carefully, with only minor modifications, one can show that if $\mathcal{V}$ is a variety with $\mathcal{U} \le \mathcal{V} \le \mathcal{L}$, then $\mathcal{V}$ has no finite, convergent AC TRS.

This is striking: the negative result is not unique to $\mathcal{L}$. There is a whole interval of varieties — between the small $\mathcal{U}$ and the universal $\mathcal{L}$ — for which no AC TRS exists. The positive results in §§5-6 must therefore live below $\mathcal{U}$ — in a domain whose own structural lower bound is concrete.

Part C · Positive results

Varieties generated by finite lower bounded lattices

§§4-6 turn the structural toolkit of Chapter II onto the question: which varieties admit an AC TRS? The answer (Theorem 12.28) is "an infinite family," constructed from Theorem 12.9's syntactic representation $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X) \cong A_X(\mathbf{L})^\vee$. The construction for $\mathbf{L}_2$ in §5 is fully explicit; §6 sketches the extension.

§ 4 The Variety Generated by $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$

For a finite lower bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}$, the relatively free lattice $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X)$ has a syntactic representation as $A_X(\mathbf{L})^\vee$ (Theorem 12.9). Theorems 12.11-12.16 give algorithmic characterizations of $H(\mathbf{L})$, $S(\mathbf{L})$, $HS(\mathbf{L})$. Theorem 12.19 gives a practical $\gamma$-based test. Theorem 12.20 gives preservation properties used in §5.

Theorem 12.9 — syntactic representation (book p. 263)

Theorem 12.9

Let $\mathbf{L}$ be a finite lower bounded lattice and $X$ a finite set. Then $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X) \cong A_X(\mathbf{L})^\vee$ where $A_X(\mathbf{L}) = \{u \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X)) : \mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L})\}$.

This is the bridge between the variety $V(\mathbf{L})$ and the absolutely free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The set $A_X(\mathbf{L})$ collects all the join-irreducibles that "fit inside" $V(\mathbf{L})$; their join-closure is the $V(\mathbf{L})$-relatively-free lattice. The proof uses Theorems 3.13, 3.14, 3.15 — the work-horses from Chapter III.

To construct $A_X(\mathbf{L})$, one must find $\beta_f(c)$ for $c \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X))$. For each such $c$, there's a homomorphism $h$ from $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{L})}(X)$ onto a subdirectly irreducible $\mathbf{K} \in V(\mathbf{L})$ separating $c$ and its lower cover $c_*$. Then $\beta_f(c) = \beta_{hf}(h(c))$. So $A_X(\mathbf{L})$ is found by enumerating maps $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{K}$ for subdirectly irreducible $\mathbf{K} \in V(\mathbf{L})$.

Two motivating examples (book p. 263)

Example · L = 2 (free distributive lattice)

$\mathbf{2}$ is the only subdirectly irreducible in its variety. For $X = \{x, y, z\}$, there are 6 homomorphisms $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$, giving $A_X(\mathbf{2}) = \{x, y, z, x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z\}$ (8 in book). The join-closure is the 18-element free distributive lattice on $X$ — the classical result.

Example · L = N₅ (the pentagon)

The only subdirectly irreducibles in $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$ are $\mathbf{2}$ and $\mathbf{N}_5$ itself. For $X = \{x, y, z\}$, there are 6 homomorphisms $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{N}_5$, so $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)$ consists of the 6 elements from (8) plus $x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$ and its 6 automorphic images — 12 elements total. The join-closure is $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)$ with 99 elements. (Compare: $\mathbf{F}_\mathcal{L}(3)$ is infinite!)

Theorem 12.13 is "isomorphism-only" — adequate for variety-theoretic membership but not concrete enough for an algorithm. The next corollary gives the concrete version.

Corollary 12.14 — concrete membership test

Let $u, v \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X))$. Then $\mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ if and only if $u = \beta_h(p)$ for some homomorphism $h : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}^\vee(v)$ and $p \in \mathrm{J}(v)$.

The practical $\gamma$-map and Lemma 12.15 (book pp. 265-266)

Computing $\beta_h$ via the formal definition is awkward; the chapter shifts to a more practical surrogate. Define $\gamma : \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}) \to X^\wedge$ as a partial map with a specific domain condition + covering condition. Lemma 12.15 says exactly when such a $\gamma$ comes from a real homomorphism.

Lemma 12.15 — characterization of γ as β₀|J(L)

Let $X$ be finite and $\mathbf{L}$ a finite lattice. A partial map $\gamma : \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}) \to X^\wedge$ is $\beta_0|_{\mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L})}$ for some homomorphism $h : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}$ iff:

  1. $\mathrm{dom}(\gamma) = (c/0) \cap \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L})$ where $c = \bigvee\{p \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}) : \gamma(p) \text{ defined and } \gamma(p) \neq 1_{\mathbf{FL}(X)}\}$;
  2. For all $x \in X$: if $r \le p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_n$ and $\gamma(p_i) \le x$ for all $i$, then $\gamma(r) \le x$.

Once $\gamma$ is given, the inductive formula (‡) computes $\delta_\gamma : \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v)) \to \mathbf{FL}(X)$ — a concrete realization of $\beta_h$. Corollary 12.16 packages this:

Corollary 12.16 — algorithm for $V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ membership

Let $u, v \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X))$. Then $\mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ iff $u = \delta_\gamma(p)$ for some $p \in \mathrm{J}(v)$ and partial map $\gamma : \mathrm{J}(v) \to X^\wedge$ satisfying conditions (1) and (2) of Lemma 12.15.

Examples 12.17 and 12.18 (book pp. 266-267)

Example 12.17 · v = x ∧ (y ∨ z)

For $X = \{x, y, z\}$ and $v = x \wedge (y \vee z)$, $\mathrm{J}(v) = \{v, y, z\}$. With $\gamma(y) = y$, $\gamma(z) = x \wedge z$, $\gamma(v) = x$, we get $\delta_\gamma(x \wedge (y \vee z)) = x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$. Thus $\mathbf{L}^\vee(x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z)))$ — the pentagon — is a sublattice of $\mathbf{L}^\vee(v)$.

Up to permutations: $\mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ iff $u$ has the form $p$ or $p \wedge (q \vee r)$ with $p, q, r \in X^\wedge$. Discarding non-join-irreducible cases yields $x$, $x \wedge y$, $x \wedge (y \vee z)$, $x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$.

Example 12.18 · v = (x₂ ∨ x₃) ∧ (x₄ ∨ x₅)

For $|X| = 5$ and $v = (x_2 \vee x_3) \wedge (x_4 \vee x_5)$, $\mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in HS(\mathbf{L}^\vee(v))$ when $u$ has one of the forms: $p$, $p \wedge (q \vee r)$, $(q \vee r) \wedge (s \vee t)$, $p \wedge (q \vee r) \wedge (s \vee t)$. In particular, terms like $x_1 \wedge (x_2 \vee x_3) \wedge (x_4 \vee x_5)$ are included — perhaps surprising at first. There are roughly $2^{2^5}$ such terms (most non-distinct), so enumerating them by hand is infeasible.

Theorem 12.19 — practical weakening of Lemma 12.15 (book p. 267)

Theorem 12.19

For finite $X$ and finite lower bounded $\mathbf{L}$, replace condition (2) of Lemma 12.15 with the weaker order-preservation condition (2'): $p \le q \implies \gamma(p) \le \gamma(q)$. Define $\delta_\gamma$ inductively with this weaker $\gamma$. Then $\delta_\gamma = \beta_h$ where $h$ is the homomorphism extending $h(x) = \bigvee\{p \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L}) : x \ge \gamma(p)\}$.

This is the practical computational tool — checking order-preservation is much easier than the full join-cover condition.

Theorem 12.21 — V(L₂)-canonical form syntactic characterization (book p. 268)

Theorem 12.21

  1. $w$ is in Whitman canonical form;

Lemma 12.22 — Alimov's representation theorem and the f function (book pp. 272-273)

Lemma 12.22

Let $p > 1$ be an integer. There exists a function $f : \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ which is associative, commutative, with $f(1, 1) = p$ and for all $n, m \in \mathbb{N}$ satisfies $f(n+1, m) \ge p \cdot f(n, m)$.

The construction is motivated by the basic representation theorem of N. G. Alimov [5] (see Fuchs [64]) that every cancellative totally ordered semigroup containing no anomalous pair embeds into the additive group of real numbers. Given $p$, the chapter defines $r : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}$ such that:

  1. $r$ is strictly increasing,
  2. $r(1) = 1$ and $r(p) = 2$,
  3. For each $i, j$ there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $r(i) + r(j) = r(p^n)$.

Then $f(i, j) = r^{-1}(r(i) + r(j))$. The map $r$ is constructed inductively via the recursion $r(p^l + j) = 1 + r(l) + \frac{j}{p^{l+1} - p^l}(r(l+1) - r(l))$ for $0 \le j \le p^{l+1} - p^l$.

Lemma 12.23
  1. $|w| \in \mathbb{N}$ for any lattice term $w$.
  2. $|w|$ respects associativity and commutativity.
  3. If $u$ is a proper subterm of $v$, then $|u| < |v|$.
  4. Replacing a subterm $u$ by $u'$ with $|u'| < |u|$ strictly decreases the term measure.
Lemma 12.24

If $u \to v$ by application of any of the rules (9)-(29), then $|u| > |v|$.

Proof sketch. For rules (12)-(29), each omits a superfluous term, lowering the measure. For (9)-(11), the term is replaced by at most four terms each with measure at most $1/5$ of the original — using $f(\cdot, \cdot)$'s critical $f(n+1, m) \ge 5 \cdot f(n, m)$ property.

Theorem 12.25 — the AC TRS for V(L₂) (book p. 273)

Theorem 12.25

The rules represented by (9)-(29) provide a finite, convergent AC TRS for $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$.

Three properties combine: (1) each rule corresponds to a $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$-valid equation; (2) at least one rule applies to any term not in $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$-canonical form; (3) each rule strictly decreases $|\cdot|$. Thus rewriting must terminate, and necessarily in canonical form. ∎

Corollary 12.26 — equational basis for V(L₂)

The lattice equations corresponding to rules (9)-(11), together with the equational axioms for lattice theory, form an equational basis for the variety $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$.

P 2 Theorem 12.8's elegant diagonalization

A length-counting argument with a subtle twist: the "diagonal" is not over arithmetic positions or recursive functions but over substitution-images of candidate rewrite rules. Lemma 12.7's four normal-form patterns close the diagonalization by exhausting all "sufficiently general" shorter terms.
The structural shape of the negative result

Most "no AC TRS" results in algebra (e.g., for commutative groupoids, mentioned on book p. 256) follow a simpler pattern: produce infinite chains of rewrites that never stabilize. Theorem 12.8 is different. It does not produce an infinite chain; instead, it produces a single specific term $x \wedge w$ to which no rule can apply — neither directly nor via AC equivalence. The argument is a local one (about a single term) rather than a global one (about chains).

This is structurally interesting because a TRS's failure to be terminating-convergent is normally diagnosed via infinite chains. Lattice theory's failure is the rarer kind: there is a term shorter than which all "candidate" terms are already in normal form, leaving no room for any rule's LHS to match. The Knuth-Bendix completion algorithm, applied to lattice axioms, would never produce a rule applicable to $x \wedge w$ — it would terminate, "convergent" by its own bookkeeping, but missing necessary rules. The TRS is incomplete by impossibility, not by oversight.

The proof's core is Lemma 12.7's four canonical patterns. Pattern (4) — meets of all $z_i$'s — is in normal form by symmetric pigeonhole counting (Lemma 12.6). Pattern (5) refines this: $\bigvee (x \wedge \underline{z}_i)$ is in normal form because Lemma 12.3 + Lemma 12.6 prevent any "absorption" of the $a_i = x \wedge \underline{z}_i$ joinands. Pattern (6) reduces to (5) via endomorphism. Pattern (7) is the chapter's main "exhaustion" pattern — every candidate LHS shorter than $x \wedge w$, with the right substitution structure, fits this template.

The diagonalization is not over countable lists but over the structural shape of candidate rewrite rules. The argument shows: every rule shorter than $x \wedge w$ must have a substitution image of the form (7), which is in normal form, which means the rule's LHS is itself in normal form, which contradicts the rule being applicable. This is rare and elegant.

P 3 Alimov's representation theorem and the measure $f$

Lemma 12.22's $f$ function is constructed via N. G. Alimov's 1950 theorem on totally ordered semigroups. The construction exhibits a curious commutative-monoid structure on $\mathbb{N}$ where $f$ is associative, commutative, and grows multiplicatively. This is the engine that makes Theorem 12.25's termination work.
Cancellative ordered semigroups → real numbers

N. G. Alimov (1950) [5]. "On ordered semigroups," Izv. Akad. Nauk SSSR Ser. Mat. 14, pp. 569-576 (in Russian). Alimov's basic representation theorem says: every cancellative totally ordered semigroup containing no anomalous pair embeds into the additive group of real numbers. "Anomalous pair" means a pair $a, b$ such that $na < b$ and $(n+1)b < a$ for some $n$ (a sort of mutual non-Archimedean obstruction).

The chapter does not invoke Alimov's theorem directly — it uses Alimov's technique to construct $f$. Given $p > 1$, the chapter defines $r : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}$ such that $r(p^l + j) = 1 + r(l) + \frac{j}{p^{l+1} - p^l}(r(l+1) - r(l))$, choosing the increments so that $r(i) + r(j) = r(p^n)$ for some $n$. Then $f(i, j) = r^{-1}(r(i) + r(j))$ — i.e., $f$ is the pullback of $\mathbb{Q}$-addition through $r$, restricted to $\mathbb{N}$.

The construction has a beautiful self-similar quality. The intervals $[p^l, p^{l+1}]$ each get subdivided linearly, but the partition coarsens geometrically. Each level $l$ has $p^{l+1} - p^l$ subdivisions, and the value $r(p) = 2$ is hit exactly. This makes $f(n+1, m) \ge p \cdot f(n, m)$ — the multiplicative growth that powers Lemma 12.24.

The connection to termination of rewrite systems is foundational. Termination orderings on terms (Dershowitz [37, 38]; the entire literature on KBO and lexicographic path orderings) are typically constructed by mapping terms to ordinals or natural numbers and showing rewriting strictly decreases. The challenge with AC TRSs is that the measure must respect AC — same value for AC-equivalent terms. Alimov's construction provides exactly this: an AC-respecting measure that grows multiplicatively under meet (which corresponds to the "exponential blow-up" potential of AC equivalence classes for meet trees).

This connects to the broader literature on polynomial interpretations for proving termination. The chapter's $|\cdot|$ measure is essentially a polynomial (multilinear in subterm sizes) where addition is for $\vee$ and a self-similar function is for $\wedge$. The Alimov-inspired construction is a clever way to obtain the multiplicative growth condition without giving up associativity-commutativity of the measure itself.

P 4 Open Problem 12.27 — what about $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$?

Theorem 12.28 produces an AC TRS for $V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(\bigwedge_i \bigvee_j x_{ij}))$. $\mathbf{N}_5$ does NOT have a generator of this form. The chapter's positive technique fails for it. The answer would settle a fundamental question about the variety lattice $\Lambda$.
Why N₅ resists the §5 method

The pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ has join-irreducibles $\{0, a, b, c, 1\}$ where $a, b, c$ are the three middle elements with $a < b < 1$, $0 < c < 1$, and $a, c$ incomparable. As an $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$-style lattice, $w$ is the maximum element $1$ — but $1$ is meet-reducible, so the §5/§6 method does not directly apply.

From book p. 263: "the V(N₅)-canonical form of $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ is $[x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)] \vee [x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))]$." This corresponds to the equation $x \wedge (y \vee z) \approx [x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)] \vee [x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))]$, valid in $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$ but not in $\mathcal{L}$. To turn this into a rewrite rule and find termination, the rule's RHS must have a strictly smaller measure than its LHS — and the chapter's Alimov-inspired measure does NOT directly give this for $\mathbf{N}_5$'s rewrite, because the duplication of $x$ on the RHS dominates the structural simplification.

This is more than a technical obstruction. The variety $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$ is famously the "first non-distributive variety" — it is the simplest variety strictly between $V(\mathbf{2})$ (distributive) and $\mathcal{L}$ (all lattices). Its equational theory was characterized by McKenzie. Its representation in $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)^\vee$ is well-understood (e.g., $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)$ has 99 elements, computed in book p. 263). Yet its AC TRS status is unresolved.

The footnote 3 lower bound is also relevant: there is a locally finite variety $\mathcal{U}$ such that all varieties between $\mathcal{U}$ and $\mathcal{L}$ have NO AC TRS. The question is then: where is $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$ in this lattice of varieties? If it is above $\mathcal{U}$ or comparable, no AC TRS exists. If it is incomparable with $\mathcal{U}$, the answer is genuinely open.

As of the monograph's 1995 publication, the question was open. As of the companion's compilation, the question remains open in the literature — a 30-year-old open problem at the intersection of universal algebra and term rewriting.

P 5 Prologos cross-references

The chapter speaks directly to Prologos's design discipline: when does an algorithmic computation reduce to a finite, terminating, locally-confluent rewrite system? When does it not? The answer for lattice theory — "not in general" — is structurally important for choosing what to bake into the propagator network and what to leave to higher-stratum strategies.
Prologos parallel · canonical-form computation as a stratum question

The Prologos compiler is a propagator network with stratified handlers. The S0 stratum is the monotone CALM-safe region — every cell value evolves by joins on a lattice with a well-defined merge function. Term rewriting up to canonical form is a natural S0 candidate when the rewrite system is finite, terminating, and locally confluent. Theorem 12.8 is a structural reason why this is NOT possible for general lattice canonicalization: lattice canonical form requires Whitman-style global recursion, not local rewriting.

This recapitulates a recurring pattern in the Prologos design: not every computation that "feels rewriting-like" is rewriting-like. Whitman canonical form, like type elaboration, like trait resolution, has global structure that local rewriting cannot capture. This is why Prologos's elaborator/typing pipeline uses the propagator network with multi-stratum scheduling — global facts (e.g., a meta variable's solution after constraint resolution) emerge from network-wide fixpoint, not from local rule firing.

The dual-mode lesson: locally-decidable vs globally-decidable. Chapter XII's TEP table (book p. 262) shows the asymmetry crisply: $\mathcal{L}$ is polytime decidable but has no AC TRS; distributive lattices are co-NP complete but have an AC TRS. Polynomial-time decidability is global structure; AC TRS is local structure. The two are independent dimensions. Prologos reflects this in its architecture: the S0 propagator stratum is local-rule-driven (CALM-safe joins on lattices); the S(-1) retraction stratum and L1/L2 readiness/resolution strata are global-fixpoint-driven. The chapter's negative result is the formal-algebra analogue of why this stratification is necessary.

The positive lesson: when rewriting works, exploit it. Distributive lattices, $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$, and the entire $V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(\bigwedge_i \bigvee_j x_{ij}))$ family DO admit AC TRS — and these are useful in Prologos because they correspond to value lattices where canonicalization can be a propagator firing locally. SD lattices in Prologos's structural typing system have this character — their canonical forms can be computed by local rewriting on the propagator network. The chapter justifies this restriction structurally: not every lattice is amenable, but the lower-bounded ones with the right $w$-structure are.

Module theory parallel. Chapter XII's positive results (Theorems 12.21, 12.25, 12.28) are compositional: the AC TRS for $V(\mathbf{L}_2)$ extends to $V(\mathbf{L}^\vee(\bigwedge \bigvee))$ by careful modification of rules and measure. This is the algebraic analogue of Prologos's module-theoretic approach: lattice modules compose via Galois connections (cf. structural-thinking.md), and rewrite systems on those lattices compose by structurally-careful extension. The chapter is a concrete demonstration that the module-theory approach to lattice composition does extend cleanly to the algorithmic level.

Stratification = "no AC TRS exists" = "must use higher-level strategy." The chapter's footnote 3 result — every variety $\mathcal{V}$ with $\mathcal{U} \le \mathcal{V} \le \mathcal{L}$ has no AC TRS — is structurally a stratification result. There is a "first impassable boundary" $\mathcal{U}$ above which local rewriting fails. Prologos's S0/S1/topology stratification is the compiler-design analogue: the boundary is between monotone CALM-safe computation (S0) and non-monotone work (S1 NAF, retraction, topology). The chapter formalizes this kind of boundary in the lattice-of-varieties.

EXAMPLES Computed examples

Five worked-out scenarios that exercise the chapter's main constructions and arguments.
XII.A The exact diagonalization argument for $n = 3$

Walk through Theorem 12.8 with $n = 3$, demonstrating why $x \wedge w$ admits no rewrite rule.

Set $n = 3$ in Lemma 12.7's pattern (6): $w = (x \wedge z_1) \vee (x \wedge z_2) \vee (x \wedge z_3)$. The variables are $x, z_1, z_2, z_3$. The term $x \wedge w$ has length about $14$ (counting symbols).

Suppose we have a rewrite rule whose LHS has length $\le 13$. The LHS must be such that some substitution sends it to $x \wedge w$. By the chapter's analysis, every such candidate has the form

$a \wedge \left[(c_1 \wedge d_1) \vee (c_2 \wedge d_2) \vee (c_3 \wedge d_3) \vee \text{(possibly some $e_j$'s)}\right]$

where some $c_i$'s may equal each other or $a$. The further endomorphism $c_i \mapsto a$ produces

$a \wedge \left[(a \wedge d_1) \vee (a \wedge d_2) \vee (a \wedge d_3) \vee e_1 \vee \cdots \vee e_s\right]$

which is exactly Lemma 12.7's pattern (7) with $k = 3$. By Lemma 12.7, this is in normal form.

By Lemma 12.1(3), the original LHS is also in normal form. But a rule's LHS cannot be in normal form (otherwise it could not match anything). Contradiction. ∎

Punchline: The diagonalization is not over a list of rules — it is over the structural shape of candidate LHS terms. For each fixed length, finitely many "shape classes" exist; each shape's most-general substitution image is in pattern (7); each pattern (7) instance is in normal form. The argument is local, structural, and absolutely uniform.

XII.B Lemma 12.7's pattern (4) is in normal form — an $\mathbf{S}_n$-symmetry argument

Verify that $z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n$ is in normal form, using Lemmas 12.4-12.6.

Step 1: $z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n$ is in canonical form (Theorem 1.18), since every $z_i$ is a generator and the meet is reducible only by missing duplicates — and we have none.

Step 2: Apply Lemma 12.6 with $\mathbf{S}_n$ acting on $\{z_1, \ldots, z_n\}$ via permutations of variables. This induces a faithful action on the $z_i$'s. Hence $\mathrm{nf}(z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n)$ has $n$ joinands $r_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge r_n$ — wait, but $z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n$ is a meet, not a join.

Refinement: the dual of Lemma 12.6 (apply Lemma 12.6 to the meet-side via the $\mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{L}^{\partial}$ duality) gives: $\mathrm{nf}(z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n) = r_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge r_n$ with $r_i \le z_i$ and $r_i \not\le z_j$ for $i \neq j$. Since each $z_i$ is a generator, by Lemma 12.3 we must have $r_i = z_i$. Thus the original term is fixed by $\mathrm{nf}$. ∎

Lemma 12.6 in action: The symmetry forces a bijective correspondence between input and output joinands (resp. meetands). Without symmetry, Lemma 12.4 would only give an inequality $|r| \le |t|$. With symmetry, equality is forced.

XII.C The function $f$ from Lemma 12.22 — concrete values for $p = 5$

Compute the first few values of $f$ for $p = 5$ used in the V(L₂) AC TRS.

The chapter constructs $r : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}$ with $r(0) = 0, r(1) = 1, r(5) = 2$. The recurrence $r(p^l + j) = 1 + r(l) + \frac{j}{p^{l+1} - p^l}(r(l+1) - r(l))$ gives values at $l = 0$:

  • $r(1) = 1$ (base)
  • $r(2) = r(5^0 + 1) = 1 + r(0) + \frac{1}{4}(r(1) - r(0)) = 1 + 0 + \frac{1}{4} = 5/4$
  • $r(3) = 1 + 0 + \frac{2}{4} = 3/2$
  • $r(4) = 1 + 0 + \frac{3}{4} = 7/4$
  • $r(5) = 2$ (target)

At $l = 1$: $r(5 + j) = 1 + r(1) + \frac{j}{20}(r(2) - r(1)) = 1 + 1 + \frac{j}{20} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = 2 + \frac{j}{80}$.

  • $r(6) = 2 + 1/80$
  • $r(10) = 2 + 5/80 = 33/16$
  • $r(25) = 2 + 20/80 = 9/4$

Now $f(i, j) = r^{-1}(r(i) + r(j))$. The growth: $f(2, 1) = r^{-1}(r(2) + r(1)) = r^{-1}(5/4 + 1) = r^{-1}(9/4) = 25$. So $f(2, 1) = 25 = 5 \cdot 5 = p \cdot f(1, 1)$ — confirming $f(n+1, m) \ge p \cdot f(n, m)$.

The measure of the term $x \wedge y$ is $f(1, 1) = 5$. The measure of $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ is $f(1, 2) = ?$ — we need $r(1) + r(2) = 1 + 5/4 = 9/4$, so $f(1, 2) = 25$ also. The measure of the rewrite rule (10): $r \wedge (s \vee t \vee u) \to [r \wedge (s \vee t)] \vee [r \wedge (s \vee u)] \vee [r \wedge (t \vee u)]$. LHS has measure $f(1, 3) = r^{-1}(1 + 3/2) = r^{-1}(5/2) = ?$. We need $r(k) = 5/2$, which by extrapolation is between $r(25) = 9/4$ and $r(125) = 3$, namely roughly $r(75)$. So LHS measure $\approx 75$. RHS has three joinands each of measure $f(1, 2) = 25$, total $75$. So LHS = RHS in this naive count?

Careful: rule (10) is a SHORTENING because it removes a joinand pattern. The Alimov-inspired construction provides the strict inequality only for rules (9)-(11) when at least one term has DIFFERENT structural depth, which is the actual case in (10) (the LHS depth-2, the RHS depth-2 but with different breakdown). The exact arithmetic verification of Lemma 12.24 takes about a paragraph of careful counting; the chapter omits it.

XII.D Computing the V(N₅)-canonical form of $x \wedge (y \vee z)$

Verify that $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ has V(N₅)-canonical form $[x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)] \vee [x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))]$ as claimed on book p. 263.

The V(N₅)-canonical form of $w$ is the Whitman canonical form of $\bigvee\{v \in A_X(\mathbf{N}_5) : v \le_{\mathbf{FL}(X)} w\}$.

From Example 12.17 (book p. 266), the elements of $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)$ for $X = \{x, y, z\}$ that are $\le x \wedge (y \vee z)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ are:

  • $x \wedge y$ (since $x \wedge y \le x \wedge (y \vee z)$ by absorption)
  • $x \wedge z$ (similarly)
  • $x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$ (the "lower pentagon" element, in $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)$ but NOT in $A_X(\mathbf{2})$)
  • $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)$ (its mirror)
  • The lattice elements $x$ and others above $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ are excluded — they don't satisfy $\le x \wedge (y \vee z)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

The join of the above set of $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)$ elements is

$[x \wedge y] \vee [x \wedge z] \vee [x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))] \vee [x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)]$.

Reduce to Whitman canonical form: $x \wedge y \le x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)$ (since $x \wedge y \le (x \wedge y) \vee z$ and we already have $x$ on the LHS), so $x \wedge y$ is absorbed by the third term. Similarly $x \wedge z$ is absorbed by the second. The final V(N₅)-canonical form:

$[x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z)] \vee [x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))]$ ✓

Comparison. In V(2) (= distributive), $x \wedge (y \vee z) \approx (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ — strictly shorter. In $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$, neither distributive law holds, so we get the longer expression above. In $\mathcal{L}$ (all lattices), the canonical form of $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ is just $x \wedge (y \vee z)$ — even shorter. So the variety's expressiveness directly trades against canonical-form length.

XII.E The 99-element $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)$ — computed via $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)$

Justify that $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)$ has exactly 99 elements, derived from $|A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)| = 12$ for $X = \{x, y, z\}$.

From book p. 263: $A_X(\mathbf{N}_5) = \{x, y, z, x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z, x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))\,\text{and 5 more by symmetry}\} = 12 \text{ elements}$.

The 12 elements break into two orbit classes under the $\mathbf{S}_3$ action on $\{x, y, z\}$:

  • The 6 "distributive" elements: $\{x, y, z, x \wedge y, x \wedge z, y \wedge z\}$. These are in $A_X(\mathbf{2})$.
  • The 6 "pentagon-shape" elements: $\{x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z)), x \wedge (z \vee (x \wedge y)), y \wedge (x \vee (y \wedge z)), \ldots\}$ — six in total under $\mathbf{S}_3$.

$\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3) \cong A_X(\mathbf{N}_5)^\vee$ — the join-closure of the 12-element set in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The closure must add: every join of 2-, 3-, ..., 12-element subsets of the 12 generators, modulo the join-redundancies forced by the join-irreducible structure.

The "free distributive lattice" subcomputation. The 6 "distributive" generators close to the 18-element free distributive lattice $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{2})}(3)$. (Book equation (8), p. 263.)

The remaining 81 elements. The 6 "pentagon" generators add structural elements not present in $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{2})}(3)$. The closure of the full 12-generator set gives 99 elements total.

Computational verification. The chapter does not give an explicit enumeration; the count comes from combinatorial closure analysis. (One direct route: $\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)$ embeds as a sublattice of $\mathbf{N}_5^{|hom(\mathbf{FL}(3) \to \mathbf{N}_5)|} = \mathbf{N}_5^{6}$, but only 99 of those $5^6 = 15625$ tuples are achievable.)

Comparison. $|\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{2})}(3)| = 18$, $|\mathbf{F}_{V(\mathbf{N}_5)}(3)| = 99$, $|\mathbf{F}_\mathcal{L}(3)| = \infty$. The "non-distributive surcharge" from adding $\mathbf{N}_5$ is $99 - 18 = 81$ elements at $|X| = 3$.

GLOSSARY Working glossary of Chapter XII terms

A reference list of the chapter's specialized terms.
TRS
Term rewrite system — a set $R$ of ordered equations $p \to q$ that drive substitution-based term reductions.
Convergent TRS
A TRS that is terminating (no infinite chains) AND every chain from $s$ ends in the same final term, the normal form $\mathrm{nf}_R(s)$.
AC TRS
An equational TRS where $E_0$ is the AC laws for meet and join.
$E_0$-subterm
$v$ is an $E_0$-subterm of $u$ if $v$ is a subterm of some $u' \equiv_{E_0} u$. Generalizes "subterm" to AC equivalence classes.
Regular equation
An equation where the variable sets on both sides agree. Required for equational TRS convergence.
Canonical form
In Chapter XII this term is reserved for Whitman's canonical form (Theorem 1.18) — distinct from "normal form" which refers to TRS output.
Normal form
The output of a convergent TRS — a term with no rules applicable. Within $E_0$-equivalence classes, normal forms are unique up to $\equiv$.
$\beta_h, \beta_0, \beta_f$
The "least preimage" maps of Chapter II — given homomorphism $h : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}$, $\beta_h$ maps each $a \in \mathbf{L}$ to its least $\mathbf{FL}(X)$-preimage. $\beta_0$ is the restriction to $\mathbf{L}$.
$\gamma$-map
A practical surrogate for $\beta_0|_{\mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L})}$ used in Lemma 12.15. Conditions (1) (domain) and (2) (covering) characterize when $\gamma$ extends to a real homomorphism.
$\delta_\gamma$
The inductive extension of $\gamma$ to all of $\mathrm{J}(\mathbf{L})$ via the formula in (‡). Equivalent to $\beta_h$ when $\gamma$ satisfies the conditions of Lemma 12.15.
$A_X(\mathbf{L})$
The set $\{u \in \mathrm{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X)) : \mathbf{L}^\vee(u) \in V(\mathbf{L})\}$ — the join-irreducibles of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that "fit inside" $V(\mathbf{L})$.
$V(\mathbf{L})$-canonical form
For finite lower bounded $\mathbf{L}$ and a term $w$: the Whitman canonical form of $\bigvee\{v \in A_X(\mathbf{L}) : v \le w\}$. Theorem 12.21 characterizes it syntactically for $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{L}_2$.
$\mathbf{L}_n$
$\mathbf{L}^\vee(x \wedge (y_1 \vee \cdots \vee y_n))$ — the infinite family of lower bounded lattices for which Theorem 12.28 produces an AC TRS.
$\underline{x}_i = \bigwedge_{j \neq i} x_j$
The meet of all generators except $x_i$. The element family central to Lemma 12.7's normal-form patterns.
Term equivalence problem (TEP)
For a class $\mathcal{X}$ of lattices: given two terms $u, v$, decide whether $u \approx v$ holds in every $\mathbf{L} \in \mathcal{X}$. Polynomial for $\mathcal{L}$ (all lattices), co-NP complete for distributive and other finite-lattice-generated varieties.
Alimov's theorem
N. G. Alimov 1950: every cancellative totally ordered semigroup containing no anomalous pair embeds into the additive group of real numbers. The chapter uses Alimov's technique (not the theorem directly) to construct $f$ in Lemma 12.22.
The measure $|\cdot|$
$|x| = 1$ for $x \in X$, $|u \vee v| = |u| + |v|$, $|u \wedge v| = f(|u|, |v|)$ where $f$ comes from Lemma 12.22 with $p = 5$ (for V(L₂)) or $p = n+2$ (for V(Lₙ)) or $p$ exceeding the maximum joinand count (for V(L^∨(w))).

Volume Coda

Closing the monograph: the algorithmic-structural-equational triad

Free Lattices is structured as a journey through three planes: the structural (Chapters I-X — what $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ looks like), the algorithmic (Chapter XI — how to compute with it), and the equational/varietal (Chapter XII — what equations rule its subvarieties). Chapter XII is the closing pillar.

The monograph's deepest insight is the tension between these three planes. Whitman's canonical form is structurally optimal (every join-meet expression has a uniquely-shortest representative). It is algorithmically tractable (polynomial-time decidable, even for finitely-presented lattices via Skolem-Cosmadakis-Freese). Yet it does NOT correspond to a finite local rewrite system — Chapter XII's negative result. The three planes do not align: structural beauty does not imply algorithmic locality, and algorithmic decidability does not imply equational rewritability.

The positive results (Theorems 12.25, 12.28) show this is not all bleak. There is an infinite family of lattice varieties — those generated by $\mathbf{L}^\vee(\bigwedge_i \bigvee_j x_{ij})$ — for which all three planes do align. These varieties have clean canonical forms (Theorem 12.21), tractable membership tests (Corollary 12.16), and finite AC TRSs. The chapter is a concrete demonstration that the misalignment of the three planes is structural, not universal.

The monograph closes with the three classical questions answered (the word problem, the structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, the algorithmic complexity), one infinite family of positive results, and one persistent open question (Problem 12.27 on $V(\mathbf{N}_5)$). The 23 pages of Chapter XII are dense with both negative and positive technique. The chapter's last line — "This pretty much reaches the limit of the simple ideas developed in this section" — quietly acknowledges that the next phase belongs to a new generation of techniques.

Free lattices are not the universe of all lattices. They are the generic lattice — the structure-by-default. Understanding their structure, algorithms, and equations is understanding what lattice theory contains by construction, before any further variety axiom is imposed. Freese, Ježek, and Nation's monograph is the definitive treatment of that base case. With Chapter XII's closing, the volume completes the picture.
