Varieties of Lattices

From a single free lattice to the lattice $\Lambda$ of all lattice varieties — and the model-theoretic apparatus (ultraproducts, Łoś's theorem, Jónsson's lemma) that makes the structure theory tractable.

~95–110 min read 10 sections + 5 connections 11 worked exercises PDF pp. 75–86

Variety is the spice of life.

— Chapter epigraph (proverbial; chosen by Nation)

Chapter 6 built one free lattice, $\text{FL}(X)$, the universal object for the variety of all lattices. Chapter 7 zooms out: the variety $\mathbf{L}$ of all lattices is one element of a much larger structure — the lattice $\Lambda$ of all lattice varieties, ordered by containment. Inside $\mathbf{L}$ live the variety $\mathbf{M}$ of modular lattices, the variety $\mathbf{D}$ of distributive lattices, the trivial variety $\mathbf{T}$ — and an enormous (in fact, of cardinality $2^{\aleph_0}$) family of named and unnamed sublatices. The chapter develops the machinery to understand $\Lambda$ itself.

The narrative arc has three movements. First: a lattice equation $p \approx q$ defines a variety $V(\Sigma)$. Birkhoff (1935) proved the HSP theorem — the existence of free objects, and the equivalence "variety = closed under H, S, P" — and the lattice version (Theorem 7.1) is the chapter's first major result. Second: every variety $\mathbf{V}$ corresponds to a fully invariant congruence $\theta$ on $\text{FL}(\omega)$, and $\Lambda$ is dually isomorphic to the lattice of all such congruences. This makes $\Lambda$ dually algebraic (Theorem 7.3). The finitely based varieties — those defined by finitely many equations — are the dually compact elements. Third: how does one compute $\Lambda$ in practice? Via ultraproducts (Łoś 1955) and Jónsson's Lemma (1967): the subdirectly irreducibles of a finitely-generated congruence-distributive variety lie inside HS of the generating algebras. From this fall: McKenzie's theorem (1970) that every finite lattice generates a finitely based variety; the distributivity of $\Lambda$ itself; and the Baker-Hales theorem $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in HSP_u(\mathcal{L})$.

The chapter is also where lattice theory crosses into model theory. Filters and ultrafilters on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, first-order sentences over the lattice signature, ultraproducts $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$, the Łoś transfer theorem — these are the standard model-theoretic tools, here applied to lattices. Some properties (semidistributivity, Whitman's $(W)$, having $\le 7$ elements, isomorphism to $\mathcal{M}_5$) are first-order; others (finite, ACC, finite width, subdirectly irreducible) are not. The chapter teaches both the boundary and the technique for crossing it.

Reduced product $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_F$, ultraproduct $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$. First-order language for lattices: alphabet, equations, AND/OR/NOT, $\forall$ $\exists$, well-formed formulas, $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models \varphi$. $P_u(\mathbf{K})$ ultraproducts class. Finitely based variety: $\mathbf{V} = V(\Sigma)$ for finite $\Sigma$. The ideal lattice $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. The lattice $\Lambda$ of lattice varieties — uncountably infinite, distributive (Theorem 7.11 corollary), dually algebraic (Theorem 7.3), with a rich internal structure: the chain of distributive sub-varieties is just $\{\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{D}, \mathbf{L}\}$-stuff at the bottom; the modular sublattice $\Lambda_M$ has been mapped extensively (Jónsson, McKenzie, Rose, Jipsen). Standard reference: Jipsen-Rose Varieties of Lattices, Lecture Notes in Math 1533, Springer 1991. Ultraproducts and Łoś's theorem — Jerzy Łoś 1955, the model-theoretic transfer principle: the ultraproduct satisfies a first-order sentence iff "almost all" factors do (where "almost all" means in the ultrafilter). Łoś's theorem is the bridge from model theory to algebra; nonstandard analysis, the compactness theorem, and Keisler's ultrapower characterisation of elementary equivalence all use it. Jónsson's Lemma and the finite-basis theorems — Jónsson 1967 (Math. Scand. 21): in a congruence-distributive variety generated by $\mathbf{K}$, every SI is in $\text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$. This SHARP result enables: McKenzie 1970 (every finite lattice generates a finitely based variety), Baker 1977 (every finite algebra in any congruence-distributive variety with finitely many operations is finitely based), Oates-Powell 1964 (finite group → finitely based), Kruse 1973 (finite ring → finitely based). The line ends at McNulty 1985 (counterexamples) and Willard 2004 survey.

PART A · EQUATIONS AND VARIETIES

From single equations to closed classes — Birkhoff's HSP for lattices

A lattice equation defines a variety. The variety is closed under homomorphic images, sublattices, and direct products. And — crucially — admits free objects.

§ 7.1 Equations, varieties, $\mathbf{K}$-free generation

The basic vocabulary: equations $p \approx q$, equational classes, the canonical sub-varieties $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{D}$, and a generalisation of Chapter 6's free generation to arbitrary classes $\mathbf{K}$.
Definition · lattice equation

A lattice equation is an expression $p \approx q$ where $p$ and $q$ are lattice terms (built from variables $x_1, \dots, x_n$ via $\vee$ and $\wedge$). A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $p \approx q$ if $p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = q(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ whenever elements of $\mathcal{L}$ are substituted for the variables — equivalently, $h(p) = h(q)$ for every homomorphism $h : W(X) \to \mathcal{L}$.

$\mathcal{L}$ satisfies a set $\Sigma$ of equations if it satisfies every equation in $\Sigma$. A class $\mathbf{K}$ of lattices satisfies $\Sigma$ if every $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{K}$ does so.

It is often more natural to think of equations as relations between elements of $\text{FL}(X)$. Two terms $p$ and $p'$ evaluate the same in $\text{FL}(X)$ iff they evaluate the same in every lattice — so we can replace $p$ and $q$ in an equation with the corresponding elements of $\text{FL}(X)$ without changing meaning.

Definition · variety / equational class

A variety (or equational class) of lattices is the class of all lattices satisfying some set $\Sigma$ of lattice equations. The class $\mathbf{L}$ of all lattices is the variety defined by $\Sigma = \{\text{idempotency, commutativity, associativity, absorption}\}$. Inside $\mathbf{L}$:

  1. $\mathbf{M}$, the variety of modular lattices: $(x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) \approx x \vee (z \wedge (x \vee y))$.
  2. $\mathbf{D}$, the variety of distributive lattices: $x \wedge (y \vee z) \approx (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$.
  3. $\mathbf{T}$, the variety of one-element lattices: $x \approx y$.
Intuition · the variety hierarchy

$\mathbf{T} \subsetneq \mathbf{D} \subsetneq \mathbf{M} \subsetneq \mathbf{L}$. Each containment is strict: distributive lattices are modular but not vice versa ($\mathcal{N}_5$ is the witness); modular lattices are lattices but not vice versa ($\mathcal{N}_5$ again). Inside this picture lie countably many additional named varieties (Arguesian, modular Arguesian, etc.) and uncountably many unnamed ones.

Definition · $\mathbf{K}$-free generation

If $\mathbf{K}$ is any class of lattices, a lattice $\mathcal{F}$ is $\mathbf{K}$-freely generated by its subset $X$ if

  1. $\mathcal{F} \in \mathbf{K}$,
  2. $X$ generates $\mathcal{F}$,
  3. for every $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{K}$, every map $h_0 : X \to L$ extends to a homomorphism $h : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{L}$.

A lattice is $\mathbf{K}$-free if it is $\mathbf{K}$-freely generated by one of its subsets, and relatively free if it is $\mathbf{K}$-free for some (unspecified) class $\mathbf{K}$.

This generalises Chapter 6's free generation. Chapter 6 took $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{L}$ (all lattices); now $\mathbf{K}$ can be any class. The relatively-free lattice in $\mathbf{V}$ on $X$ is denoted $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)$. For $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{D}$: $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X) = 2^{2^X}$ — the antichains in the Boolean lattice. For $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{M}$: $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(X)$ has Dedekind's 28 elements when $|X| = 3$ (Dedekind 1900).

§ 7.2 Theorem 7.1: Birkhoff's HSP for lattices

Three equivalent characterisations of varieties — the equational definition, the closure under H/S/P, and the existence of relatively free objects.
Theorem 7.1 · Birkhoff's HSP theorem (Birkhoff 1935)

If $\mathbf{K}$ is a nonempty class of lattices, then the following are equivalent.

  1. $\mathbf{K}$ is a variety.
  2. $\mathbf{K}$ is closed under the formation of homomorphic images, sublattices, and direct products.
  3. Either $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{T}$, or for every nonempty set $X$ there is a lattice $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(X)$ that is $\mathbf{K}$-freely generated by $X$, and $\mathbf{K}$ is closed under homomorphic images.

Proof outline

(1) $\Rightarrow$ (2). Varieties are equationally defined; equations are preserved under H, S, P. (Standard universal-algebra check.)

(2) $\Rightarrow$ (3). The construction of $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(X)$ adapts Chapter 6's. Let $\mathbf{K}$ be closed under S and P. Let $\kappa = \bigcap \{\theta \in \textbf{Con}\,W(X) : W(X)/\theta \in \mathbf{K}\}$. Then $W(X)/\kappa$ is a subdirect product of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$, so by S + P it is in $\mathbf{K}$; it is $\mathbf{K}$-freely generated by $\{x\kappa : x \in X\}$. Unless $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{T}$, the classes $x\kappa$ are distinct.

(3) $\Rightarrow$ (1). Given the relatively free lattices, fix a countable set $X_0 = \{x_1, x_2, \dots\}$, and let $\Sigma$ be all equations $p \approx q$ such that $(p, q) \in \ker f_{X_0}$, where $f_X : W(X) \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(X)$ is the homomorphism that is the identity on $X$. The Sublemma (next paragraph) says $\Sigma$ characterises $\mathbf{K}$.

Sublemma · the equational characterisation

Let $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(Y)$ be a relatively free lattice. Let $p, q \in W(Y)$ and $f_Y : W(Y) \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(Y)$ with $f_Y$ the identity on $Y$. Then $\mathbf{K}$ satisfies $p \approx q$ if and only if $f_Y(p) = f_Y(q)$.

The Sublemma is a clean statement: satisfying $p \approx q$ in $\mathbf{K}$ is the same as identifying $p$ and $q$ inside the relatively free $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(Y)$. (One direction is trivial; the other uses the mapping property (III).) The footnote: $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(Y)$ may satisfy equations not satisfied by $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(Z)$ when $Y \subsetneq Z$. The Sublemma applies to equations with at most $|Y|$ variables.

Applying the Sublemma with $Y = X_0$ shows $\mathbf{K} \subseteq \mathbf{V}_\Sigma$. The converse uses the Second Iso Theorem to embed any $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}_\Sigma$ as a homomorphic image of $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{K}(X)$ for $X = $ generating set of $\mathcal{L}$. Since $\mathbf{K}$ is closed under homomorphic images, $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{K}$. So $\mathbf{V}_\Sigma \subseteq \mathbf{K}$, and equality follows. $\square$

Intuition · three views of a variety

Theorem 7.1 says variety can be defined three equivalent ways: (equation-first) as the class satisfying a set of equations; (closure-first) as a class closed under H, S, P; (free-first) as a class with relatively free objects, closed under H. Each viewpoint has its strengths: equations give finite presentations; closure operators give algebraic-geometry-style structure; free objects connect to category theory and universal constructions.

Deep dive · why HSP and not, say, HSPI?

Birkhoff's HSP includes direct products ($P$) but NOT arbitrary inverse limits. Some closure conditions appear naturally but are not equational: for instance, the class of finite lattices is closed under H and S but NOT under P (infinite products of finite lattices are infinite). It is a quasi-variety, not a variety. Adding "$\le \aleph_0$ elements" still doesn't yield a variety. The HSP closure is exactly the right amount: large enough to give an equational class, small enough to be tractable. The dual closure $\text{HSP}_u$ (replacing $P$ with $P_u$ — ultraproducts) is also natural and corresponds to quasi-varieties closed under ultraproducts, which by Łoś's theorem is the model-theoretic notion of universal class.

The disappointing footnote on $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(X)$

Birkhoff's theorem says relatively free lattices exist. But it does NOT say their arithmetic is computable. Consider the variety $\mathbf{M}$ of modular lattices.

  • Dedekind 1900: $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(3)$ has 28 elements (drawn in Figure 9.2 of Nation).
  • Christian Herrmann (1982): the word problem for $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(4)$ is unsolvable.
  • Ralph Freese (1980): the word problem for $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(5)$ is unsolvable. There is no algorithm for determining whether $p = q$ in $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(5)$.

Consequence: the variety $\mathbf{M}$ of modular lattices is not generated by its finite members. There is a lattice equation that holds in all finite modular lattices but not in all modular lattices. This is one of the deepest negative results in lattice theory — the word-problem solvability for free lattices (Whitman / Skolem, Ch 6) was a happy accident of the variety being "the largest." Adding modularity adds enough equations to push the word problem past decidability.

PART B · FULLY INVARIANT CONGRUENCES AND $\Lambda$

The lattice of lattice varieties is dually algebraic

Each variety $\mathbf{V}$ corresponds to a fully invariant congruence on $\text{FL}(\omega)$ — and the resulting bijection makes $\Lambda$ dually isomorphic to a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Con}\,\text{FL}(\omega)$.

§ 7.3 Endomorphisms and fully invariant congruences

Endomorphisms of a lattice form a semigroup; the congruences invariant under all endomorphisms form a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.
Definition · endomorphism & semigroup $\textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}$

An endomorphism of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is a homomorphism $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}$. The set $\textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}$ of endomorphisms forms a semigroup under composition. An endomorphism of $\text{FL}(X)$ is determined by its action on the generating set $X$: it corresponds to a substitution $x_i \mapsto f(x_i)$ of elements for the generators.

Definition · fully invariant congruence

A congruence $\theta$ on $\mathcal{L}$ is fully invariant if $(x, y) \in \theta$ implies $(f(x), f(y)) \in \theta$ for every endomorphism $f$ of $\mathcal{L}$. Equivalently: $\theta$ is a congruence of the algebra $\mathcal{L}^* = (L, \wedge, \vee, \{f : f \in \textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}\})$ obtained by adjoining all endomorphisms as unary operations. The fully invariant congruences form a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ — in fact, an algebraic lattice.

Why "fully invariant"? Because the substitution $f$ is allowed to be any endomorphism. A fully invariant congruence on $\text{FL}(X)$ is a congruence stable under arbitrary substitution of generators. This is exactly the closure condition that turns "an equation holds for these specific generators" into "an equation holds for all substitutions" — which is what variety membership requires.

§ 7.4 Theorem 7.2: relatively free quotients

A quotient $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ is relatively freely generated iff $\theta$ is fully invariant.
Theorem 7.2 · Birkhoff's invariant-congruence theorem

$\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ is relatively freely generated by $\{x\theta : x \in X\}$ if and only if $\theta$ is fully invariant.

Proof outline

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ is $\mathbf{V}$-freely generated for some lattice variety $\mathbf{V}$, and let $h : \text{FL}(X) \twoheadrightarrow \text{FL}(X)/\theta$ with $\ker h = \theta$. Let $f$ be any endomorphism of $\text{FL}(X)$. We compute, using the Sublemma's substitutivity:

$$h(f(p)) = h(p(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n))) = h(p)(h(f(x_1)), \dots, h(f(x_n)))$$

and similarly for $h(f(q))$. So if $h(p) = h(q)$, then $h(f(p)) = h(f(q))$, i.e., $(f(p), f(q)) \in \ker h = \theta$. Thus $\theta$ is fully invariant.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\theta$ is fully invariant on $\text{FL}(X)$. Excluding the trivial case $\theta = 1_{\textbf{Con}\,\text{FL}(X)}$ (which gives the trivial variety $\mathbf{T}$), let $k : \text{FL}(X) \twoheadrightarrow \text{FL}(X)/\theta$ be the canonical map. Let $\mathbf{V}$ be the variety determined by $\Sigma = \{p \approx q : (p, q) \in \theta\}$. We show that $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ is $\mathbf{V}$-freely generated by $\{x\theta : x \in X\}$:

  • $\text{FL}(X)/\theta \in \mathbf{V}$: every equation $p \approx q$ in $\Sigma$ holds in $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ because $\theta$ is fully invariant — every substitution into $p$ and $q$ produces an element of $\theta$.
  • Mapping property: given $\mathcal{M} \in \mathbf{V}$ and $h_0 : X \to M$, extend $h_0$ to $g : \text{FL}(X) \to \mathcal{M}$ by freeness of $\text{FL}(X)$. Since $\mathcal{M} \in \mathbf{V}$, $g(p) = g(q)$ whenever $p \approx q \in \Sigma$, so $\theta = \ker k \subseteq \ker g$. By Second Iso, $g$ factors as $h \circ k$ for $h : \text{FL}(X)/\theta \to \mathcal{M}$.

So $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ is $\mathbf{V}$-freely generated. $\square$

Intuition · Theorem 7.2 in one phrase

Varieties are in bijective correspondence with fully invariant congruences on $\text{FL}(\omega)$ — the countably-generated free lattice. Each fully invariant congruence is "all the equations the variety enforces"; each variety is "all the lattices satisfying those equations."

§ 7.5 Theorem 7.3: $\Lambda$ is dually algebraic

The lattice of all lattice varieties, structurally — and the characterisation of finitely based varieties as the dually compact elements.
Theorem 7.3

The set of all lattice varieties ordered by containment forms a lattice $\Lambda$ that is dually isomorphic to the lattice of all fully invariant congruences of $\text{FL}(\omega)$. Thus $\Lambda$ is dually algebraic, and a variety $\mathbf{V}$ is dually compact in $\Lambda$ if and only if $\mathbf{V} = V(\Sigma)$ for some finite set of equations $\Sigma$.

Theorem 7.3 follows immediately from Theorem 7.2 + the fact that fully invariant congruences form a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Con}\,\text{FL}(\omega)$. The duality reverses order: bigger congruence (more equations) = smaller variety (fewer lattices satisfying them).

Intuition · the geometry of $\Lambda$

$\Lambda$ has $\mathbf{T}$ at the bottom (most equations: "$x \approx y$"), $\mathbf{L}$ at the top (fewest: only the lattice axioms). $\mathbf{D}$ sits inside $\mathbf{M}$ inside $\mathbf{L}$. There are $2^{\aleph_0}$ lattice varieties in total — uncountably many. Some named landmarks: the variety generated by $\mathcal{N}_5$, the variety generated by $\mathcal{M}_3$, the variety of Arguesian lattices, the variety $\mathbf{V}(\textbf{Sub}(\mathbb{Z}^2))$ generated by subgroup lattices of $\mathbb{Z}^2$, etc.

Finitely based varieties are the dually compact elements of $\Lambda$. The variety $\mathbf{D}$ of distributive lattices is finitely based (one equation suffices). $\mathbf{M}$ of modular is finitely based. As we will see in Theorem 7.10 (McKenzie 1970), the variety generated by any finite lattice is finitely based — a substantial result.

§ 7.6 The HSP closure operator and Lemma 7.4

A model-theoretic view: variety = closed under H, S, P. The closure operator HSP, and the fact that varieties are determined by their subdirectly irreducibles.

Theorem 7.1's third statement gives a different perspective: a variety is a class closed under three operators on classes. $H(\mathbf{K})$ = all homomorphic images of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$. $S(\mathbf{K})$ = all sublattices of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$. $P(\mathbf{K})$ = all direct products of families of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$.

Standard elementary arguments show, for any class $\mathbf{K}$:

$$PS(\mathbf{K}) \subseteq SP(\mathbf{K}) \quad\quad PH(\mathbf{K}) \subseteq HP(\mathbf{K}) \quad\quad SH(\mathbf{K}) \subseteq HS(\mathbf{K})$$

Thus the smallest variety containing a class $\mathbf{K}$ of lattices is $\text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$, the class of all homomorphic images of sublattices of direct products of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$. We refer to $\text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ as the variety generated by $\mathbf{K}$.

$\text{HSP}$ is a closure operator on classes — but NOT an algebraic one: $\Lambda$ is not upper continuous, so $\text{HSP}$ cannot be algebraic. (Exercise 7.6 explores this: every lattice equation that fails fails in some finite lattice, but the variety generated by all finite lattices is not equal to the variety of all lattices via finitary closure.)

Lemma 7.4 · varieties determined by SIs

Two lattice varieties are equal if and only if they contain the same subdirectly irreducible lattices.

Proof

Recall (Theorem 5.6): every lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices $\mathcal{L}/\varphi$ with $\varphi$ completely meet irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Suppose $\mathbf{V}$ and $\mathbf{K}$ are varieties with the same subdirectly irreducible members. For any $X$ the relatively free lattice $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)$, being a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible lattices $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)/\varphi$ in $\mathbf{V}$ (hence in $\mathbf{K}$ by hypothesis), is itself a subdirect product of lattices in $\mathbf{K}$. Hence $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X) \in \mathbf{K}$ and $\mathbf{V} \subseteq \mathbf{K}$. By symmetry $\mathbf{K} \subseteq \mathbf{V}$. $\square$

Lemma 7.4 is the lever for everything in Part D. To classify a variety, it suffices to classify its subdirectly irreducibles. Jónsson's Lemma will tell us where to look for those.

PART C · FILTERS, ULTRAFILTERS, AND ULTRAPRODUCTS

The model-theoretic apparatus

Filters and ultrafilters on power sets; the reduced product and ultraproduct constructions; the first-order language for lattices; and Łoś's transfer theorem (1955).

§ 7.7 Filters, ultrafilters, and Lemma 7.5

The notions of filter and ultrafilter on a power set $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, the reduced-product equivalence $\equiv_F$, and five useful characterisations.
Definition · filter

A filter (or dual ideal) of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ with greatest element $1$ is a subset $F \subseteq L$ such that

  1. $1 \in F$,
  2. $x, y \in F$ implies $x \wedge y \in F$,
  3. $z \ge x \in F$ implies $z \in F$.

For $x \in L$, the set $\uparrow x = \{y : y \ge x\}$ is the principal filter generated by $x$. A nonprincipal filter is one not of this form. In $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ for an infinite $X$, the filter $F$ of all complements of finite subsets of $X$ is a canonical nonprincipal filter (the cofinite filter). A maximal proper filter is called an ultrafilter.

Definition · the congruence $\equiv_F$ on a direct product

Let $\mathcal{L}_i$ ($i \in I$) be lattices, and let $F$ be a filter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$. Define an equivalence relation on $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i$ by

$$x \equiv_F y \quad\text{if}\quad \{i \in I : x_i = y_i\} \in F.$$

A routine check shows $\equiv_F$ is a congruence relation on $\prod \mathcal{L}_i$.

Lemma 7.5 · five filter properties
  1. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice, $F$ a filter on $\mathcal{L}$, and $a \notin F$. Then there exists a filter $G$ on $\mathcal{L}$ maximal with respect to $F \subseteq G$ and $a \notin G$. (Zorn's lemma extension.)
  2. A proper filter $U$ on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$ is an ultrafilter if and only if for every $A \subseteq I$, either $A \in U$ or $I - A \in U$.
  3. If $U$ is an ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, then its complement $\mathfrak{P}(I) - U$ is a maximal proper ideal.
  4. If $U$ is an ultrafilter and $A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n \in U$, then $A_i \in U$ for some $i$.
  5. An ultrafilter $U$ is nonprincipal if and only if it contains the filter of all complements of finite subsets of $I$.
Corollary

If $I$ is an infinite set, then there is a nonprincipal ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$.

The corollary follows by applying Lemma 7.5(1) with $\mathcal{L} = \mathfrak{P}(I)$, $F$ = the cofinite filter, $a = \emptyset$. Existence requires the axiom of choice (Zorn's lemma).

Definition · reduced product, ultraproduct

If $F$ is a filter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, the quotient lattice $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_F$ is called a reduced product. If $U$ is an ultrafilter, then $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ is an ultraproduct. The interesting case is when $U$ is a nonprincipal ultrafilter — otherwise the ultraproduct collapses to one of the factors.

Intuition · "almost all"

An ultrafilter $U$ on $I$ functions as a notion of "almost all $i \in I$." A subset is "large" iff it's in $U$. Lemma 7.5(2) says: ANY subset is either large or its complement is — there are no "medium-sized" sets under $U$. The ultraproduct $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ identifies tuples that agree on almost all coordinates. For nonprincipal $U$ on infinite $I$, this is a genuinely new lattice — typically larger than any single factor.

§ 7.8 First-order language; Łoś's theorem (Theorem 7.6)

A precise grammar for lattice-theoretic statements, and the model-theoretic result that ultraproducts preserve them.

The first-order language for lattices

The elements of a first-order language for lattices are: (1) a countable alphabet $X$ with members $x, y, z, \dots$; (2) equations $p \approx q$ with $p, q \in W(X)$; (3) logical connectives AND, OR, $\neg$; (4) quantifiers $\forall x$ and $\exists x$ for $x \in X$.

These symbols combine to form well-formed formulas (wffs) recursively: every equation is a wff; if $\alpha, \beta$ are wffs, then so are $(\neg\alpha)$, $(\alpha \;\text{AND}\; \beta)$, $(\alpha \;\text{OR}\; \beta)$; if $\gamma$ is a wff and $x \in X$, then so are $(\forall x\,\gamma)$ and $(\exists x\,\gamma)$.

Definition · models

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice and $h : W(X) \to \mathcal{L}$ a homomorphism. The pair $(\mathcal{L}, h)$ models the wff $\varphi$, written $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models \varphi$, by the standard recursive definition: $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models p \approx q$ iff $h(p) = h(q)$; AND, OR, $\neg$ propagate as expected; $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models \forall x\,\gamma$ iff $(\mathcal{L}, g) \models \gamma$ for every $g$ agreeing with $h$ off $\{x\}$. $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $\varphi$ if $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models \varphi$ for every $h$.

A first-order sentence is a wff with no free variables. Whether $\mathcal{L} \models \varphi$ depends only on the values $h$ takes on the free variables of $\varphi$ — for sentences, no values matter, and $\mathcal{L}$ either satisfies $\varphi$ or satisfies $\neg\varphi$.

Examples · what's first-order and what isn't

First-order properties (expressible by a single sentence):

  • $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $p \approx q$.
  • $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $\text{SD}_\vee$ and $\text{SD}_\wedge$ (Ch 6).
  • $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies Whitman's $(W)$.
  • $\mathcal{L}$ has width 7. (Express: there exist 7 pairwise incomparable elements; no 8 do.)
  • $\mathcal{L}$ has at most 7 elements. (Express: $\forall x_1 \dots x_8\,(x_1 = x_2 \;\text{OR}\; x_1 = x_3 \;\text{OR}\;\dots)$ — any 8 elements collapse.)
  • $\mathcal{L}$ has exactly 7 elements (combine the previous two).
  • $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{M}_5$ (express the entire multiplication table).

NOT first-order (no single sentence captures these):

  • $\mathcal{L}$ is finite. (Compactness shows: if every finite subset of an infinite axiom set is satisfiable, the whole set is — so "finite" cannot be axiomatised by sentences.)
  • $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies the ACC. (The same argument.)
  • $\mathcal{L}$ has finite width.
  • $\mathcal{L}$ is subdirectly irreducible. (Requires quantification over arbitrary congruences, not first-order.)
Intuition · the boundary of first-order

First-order sentences over $\{\vee, \wedge\}$ can express finitely-presentable structural properties, equations, equational schemes, and finitistic arithmetic. They CANNOT express infinitary properties (finiteness, well-foundedness, ACC) — these require second-order or infinitary logic. The boundary is sharp: every "finite" statement is first-order, every "infinite" statement is not.

Łoś's Theorem

Theorem 7.6 · Łoś 1955

Let $\varphi$ be a first-order lattice sentence, $\mathcal{L}_i$ ($i \in I$) lattices, and $U$ an ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$. Then the ultraproduct $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ satisfies $\varphi$ if and only if $\{i \in I : \mathcal{L}_i \text{ satisfies } \varphi\} \in U$.

Corollary

If each $\mathcal{L}_i$ satisfies $\varphi$, then so does the ultraproduct.

The corollary is immediate: if every $\mathcal{L}_i$ satisfies $\varphi$, the index set is all of $I$, which is always in $U$. The full theorem is the "transfer principle": ultraproducts preserve first-order truth, but the value of "preserve" is moderated by the ultrafilter — if "almost all" factors satisfy $\varphi$, so does the ultraproduct.

Proof outline

By induction on the complexity of $\varphi$. The easy cases — equations, AND, $\neg$ — use that ultrafilters are closed under intersection (for AND) and complementation (for $\neg$, via Lemma 7.5(2)). The non-trivial case is $\forall x\,\gamma$: requires juggling between "(structure, homomorphism) models $\forall x\,\gamma$" and "structure models $\forall x\,\gamma$ at every assignment to $x$." The proof handles both directions by using the finiteness of free variables in $\gamma$ and the closure of $U$ under finite intersections. $\square$

Łoś's theorem is the foundation of model theory's "ultraproduct method." Applications: a clean proof of the compactness theorem (a set of sentences has a model iff every finite subset does); an algebraic characterisation of elementary equivalence via Keisler's ultrapower theorem; nonstandard analysis (the ultrapower of $\mathbb{R}$ contains infinitesimals); the reduction of structural properties to first-order ones.

Recap · the operators

To Birkhoff's $H$, $S$, $P$ we now add $P_u$ — the operator taking a class $\mathbf{K}$ to its class of ultraproducts $P_u(\mathbf{K})$. The composite $\text{HSP}_u$ is a closure operator that we'll see is the right tool for Jónsson's Lemma. By Łoś, $P_u$ preserves first-order properties; the variety $\text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ generated by a class is in general STRICTLY LARGER than $\text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$, but they agree on subdirectly irreducibles when the variety is congruence distributive. (The next section.)

PART D · JÓNSSON'S LEMMA AND FINITE BASIS

Where do subdirectly irreducibles come from?

Bjarni Jónsson's 1967 answer for congruence-distributive varieties: from $\text{HSP}_u$ of the generators. The cascade of consequences includes McKenzie's finite-basis theorem, the distributivity of $\Lambda$, and the Baker-Hales theorem on ideal lattices.

§ 7.9 Jónsson's Lemma + Theorems 7.7-7.9

The model-theoretic crown jewel: subdirectly irreducible members of a variety generated by a class lie in $\text{HSP}_u$ of that class.
Theorem 7.7 · Jónsson's Lemma (1967)

Let $\mathbf{K}$ be a class of lattices. If $\mathcal{L}$ is subdirectly irreducible and $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$, then $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$.

Jónsson's Lemma narrows the search dramatically. Without it, $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ tells you only that $\mathcal{L}$ is a homomorphic image of a sublattice of an arbitrary product. With Jónsson, when $\mathcal{L}$ is SI, you can replace "arbitrary product" with "ultraproduct."

Proof sketch

$\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ means there are lattices $\mathcal{K}_i \in \mathbf{K}$ ($i \in I$), a sublattice $\mathcal{S}$ of $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{K}_i$, and a surjective homomorphism $h : \mathcal{S} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{L}$. Assume $\mathcal{L}$ is finitely subdirectly irreducible. Then $\ker h$ is meet-irreducible in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{S}$. Since $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{S}$ is distributive (Funayama-Nakayama, Theorem 5.8), $\ker h$ is meet-prime: $\varphi \wedge \psi \le \ker h$ implies $\varphi \le \ker h$ or $\psi \le \ker h$.

For each $J \subseteq I$, let $\pi_J$ be the kernel of the projection $\mathcal{S} \to \prod_{j \in J} \mathcal{K}_j$. Then $\pi_{H \cup J} = \pi_H \wedge \pi_J$ for $H, J \subseteq I$.

Let $\mathfrak{H} = \{J \subseteq I : \pi_J \le \ker h\}$. By the meet-primeness of $\ker h$ + the distributivity-driven primeness, $\mathfrak{H}$ has these properties: (1) $I \in \mathfrak{H}$, $\emptyset \notin \mathfrak{H}$; (2) $\mathfrak{H}$ is an order filter; (3) $J \cup K \in \mathfrak{H}$ implies $J \in \mathfrak{H}$ or $K \in \mathfrak{H}$.

$\mathfrak{H}$ is not necessarily a lattice filter, but Zorn's Lemma applied to the lattice filters contained in $\mathfrak{H}$ produces a maximal one $U$ — and the meet-primeness ensures $U$ is an ultrafilter. The restriction of $\equiv_U$ to $\mathcal{S}$ is a congruence; $\mathcal{S}/\equiv_U$ is a sublattice of $\prod \mathcal{K}_i / \equiv_U$. The kernel of the restriction is below $\ker h$, so $\mathcal{L} = h(\mathcal{S})$ is a homomorphic image of $\mathcal{S}/\equiv_U$. Therefore $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$. $\square$

Deep dive · why congruence-distributivity is essential

The proof uses distributivity of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{S}$ in a CRUCIAL way — the meet-irreducibility-implies-meet-primeness step. Without congruence distributivity (e.g., for groups, rings), Jónsson's Lemma fails. This means varieties of LATTICES are more well-behaved than varieties of groups or rings. The Funayama-Nakayama theorem (Ch 5: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is always distributive) is what makes lattice variety theory cleaner than group variety theory. Generalisations: Jónsson's Lemma extends to any congruence-distributive variety (Jónsson 1967, Math. Scand. 21). In particular: lattices, distributive algebras, Heyting algebras, Boolean algebras, semilattices.

Lemma 7.8 — filters within ultrafilters

Lemma 7.8

Let $U$ be an ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$ and $J \in U$. Then $V = \{B \subseteq J : B \in U\}$ is an ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(J)$, and $\prod_{j \in J} \mathcal{L}_j / \equiv_V$ is isomorphic to $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$.

An ultrafilter on a "large" subset $J$ extends to one on $I$ — restriction is reversible. Useful for finitising arguments.

Theorem 7.9 — finite generators

Theorem 7.9

Let $\mathbf{K} = \{\mathcal{K}_1, \dots, \mathcal{K}_n\}$ be a finite collection of finite lattices. If $\mathcal{L}$ is a subdirectly irreducible lattice in the variety $\text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$, then $\mathcal{L} \in HS(\mathcal{K}_j)$ for some $j$.

By Jónsson's Lemma, $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$, so $\mathcal{L}$ is a homomorphic image of a sublattice of some ultraproduct $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ where each $\mathcal{L}_i \in \mathbf{K}$. Partition $I$ into blocks $A_j = \{i : \mathcal{L}_i \cong \mathcal{K}_j\}$. By Lemma 7.5(4), some $A_j \in U$. By Lemma 7.8, the ultraproduct over $A_j$ (with restricted ultrafilter $V$) is isomorphic to the original. Each $\mathcal{L}_k \cong \mathcal{K}_j$ for $k \in A_j$. By Łoś's theorem applied to "$\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{K}_j$" (a first-order property since $\mathcal{K}_j$ is finite), the ultraproduct is also $\cong \mathcal{K}_j$. Hence $\mathcal{L} \in HS(\mathcal{K}_j)$. $\square$

Corollary · finitely many subvarieties

If $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ where $\mathbf{K}$ is a finite collection of finite lattices, then $\mathbf{V}$ contains only finitely many subvarieties.

By Lemma 7.4 (varieties determined by SIs) and Theorem 7.9 (SIs of $\mathbf{V}$ are inside $HS$ of finitely many finite lattices), there are only finitely many SI lattices in $\mathbf{V}$, hence finitely many subvarieties.

Note: $\text{HSP}(\{\mathcal{K}_1, \dots, \mathcal{K}_n\}) = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{K}_1 \times \cdots \times \mathcal{K}_n)$, so we can speak of the variety generated by a single finite lattice. Nation has shown the converse of the corollary is FALSE: there is an infinite SI lattice $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$ has only finitely many subvarieties, each generated by a finite lattice (J. B. Nation, A counterexample to the finite height conjecture, Order 13 (1996), 1–9).

§ 7.10 Finite basis theorems (Theorems 7.10-7.12)

McKenzie's foundational theorem and its lineage; Theorem 7.11 + the distributivity of $\Lambda$; Baker-Hales on the ideal lattice.

McKenzie's finite basis theorem

Theorem 7.10 · McKenzie 1970

The variety generated by a finite lattice is finitely based.

Implication: a variety $\mathbf{V} = V(\Sigma)$ generated by a finite lattice $\mathcal{K}$ admits a finite $\Sigma$ — even though Birkhoff's HSP only gives an infinite (countable) $\Sigma$ in general. Corollary via Theorem 7.3: $V(\mathcal{K})$ is dually compact in $\Lambda$ for any finite $\mathcal{K}$.

The proof uses Theorem 7.9 + Jónsson's Lemma: the SIs of $V(\mathcal{K})$ are inside $HS(\mathcal{K})$, hence finite and finitely many; each is "axiomatised" by a single first-order sentence (its multiplication table); the conjunction is finite.

Deep dive · the finite-basis theorem lineage

McKenzie's theorem (Math. Scand. 27, 1970) fits in a sequence of finite-basis results across algebra:

  • 1962 — Frayne, Morel, Scott: the foundations of ultraproducts in model theory (Fund. Math. 51).
  • 1964 — Oates & Powell: variety generated by a finite group is finitely based (J. Algebra 1).
  • 1970 — McKenzie: variety generated by a finite lattice is finitely based.
  • 1973 — Kruse: variety generated by a finite ring is finitely based (J. Algebra 26).
  • 1977 — Baker: every finite algebra in a congruence-distributive variety with finitely many operations is finitely based (Adv. Math. 24). The big generalisation.
  • 1985 — McNulty: how to construct finite algebras NOT in any finitely-based variety (LNM 1149). The negative side.
  • 1987 — McKenzie: finite equational bases for congruence modular varieties (Algebra Universalis 24).
  • 2004 — Willard: The finite basis problem — survey of progress and open questions (Contributions to General Algebra 15).

The dichotomy: nice varieties (congruence-distributive) → finite-basis theorems; pathological varieties (without congruence distributivity) → finite algebras need not be finitely based. The Funayama-Nakayama theorem (Ch 5: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ always distributive) puts lattices in the nice category — McKenzie's theorem is essentially Baker's specialised to lattices.

Theorem 7.11 — joins of varieties

Theorem 7.11

If $\mathbf{V}$ and $\mathbf{W}$ are lattice varieties, then $(\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si} = \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$.

Where $\mathbf{V}_{si}$ denotes the class of subdirectly irreducible lattices in $\mathbf{V}$. The proof is a straightforward modification of the first part of Theorem 7.9's proof: an SI in $\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W}$ is in $HS$ of an ultraproduct of lattices from $\mathbf{V}$ or $\mathbf{W}$; since $\mathbf{V}$ and $\mathbf{W}$ are closed under H, S, $P_u$, we get $\mathbf{V}_{si}$ or $\mathbf{W}_{si}$.

Corollary

$\Lambda$ is distributive.

The corollary follows from Theorem 7.11 + Lemma 7.4. The proof is standard: SI-classification turns lattice operations on varieties into set operations on SI classes, and SI-class set operations satisfy distributivity automatically.

Important note: Theorem 7.11 does NOT extend to infinite joins. (Exercise 7.6 explores: finite lattices generate $\mathbf{L}$, but there are infinite SIs in $\mathbf{L}$ not in any HSP of finitely many finite lattices.)

Theorem 7.12 — ideal lattices and ultraproducts

Theorem 7.12 · Baker-Hales 1974

For any lattice $\mathcal{L}$, we have $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathcal{L})$.

$\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is the lattice of ideals of $\mathcal{L}$. The map $x \mapsto\,\downarrow x$ embeds $\mathcal{L}$ into $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$. A classic theorem of Birkhoff (1934) says $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ satisfies every identity satisfied by $\mathcal{L}$, i.e., $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in \text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$. Baker and Hales (1974) sharpen: $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ lives in the smaller class $\text{HSP}_u(\mathcal{L})$ — under the ultraproduct closure. Useful for transferring first-order properties between $\mathcal{L}$ and its ideal lattice.

PANEL P2 Łoś + ultraproducts in model theory

The ultraproduct construction is one of model theory's most powerful tools — and it was a Polish logician, Jerzy Łoś, who established its fundamental property in 1955.
Łoś's theorem · the model-theoretic transfer principle

Jerzy Łoś (1955, in Quelques remarques, théorèmes et problèmes sur les classes définissables d'algèbras, Mathematical interpretations of formal systems, North-Holland) introduced ultraproducts and proved: an ultraproduct $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ satisfies a first-order sentence $\varphi$ iff $\{i : \mathcal{L}_i \models \varphi\} \in U$. Often called the fundamental theorem of ultraproducts.

Why Łoś matters across model theory

  • Compactness theorem: a set $\Sigma$ of first-order sentences has a model iff every finite subset does. Easy proof via ultraproducts: index the finite subsets of $\Sigma$, take models of each, ultraproduct them with a suitable ultrafilter.
  • Keisler's ultrapower theorem (1961): structures $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ are elementarily equivalent ($\equiv$) iff they have isomorphic ultrapowers. Algebraic characterisation of "satisfies the same first-order sentences."
  • Nonstandard analysis (Robinson 1966): the ultrapower $\mathbb{R}^\omega / \equiv_U$ for nonprincipal $U$ contains infinitesimals and "infinite" elements. A model of arithmetic with full first-order properties of $\mathbb{R}$ but new elements.
  • Saturated models: ultraproducts produce "saturated" models with strong realisation properties.
  • Universal classes: the model-theoretic notion of "closed under ultraproducts" turns out to coincide with Mal'cev's notion of universal class (defined by universal sentences).

For lattice theorists, Łoś is the model-theoretic toolkit that makes Jónsson's Lemma possible. For model theorists, Łoś is the gateway to Keisler-Shelah classification theory.

PANEL P3 Word problems for free modular lattices

From Dedekind's 1900 enumeration of $\mathcal{F}_M(3)$ to Freese's 1980 undecidability proof — the dramatic story of what happens when you add the modular law.
Free modular lattices · the word problem

For free LATTICES (Ch 6), the word problem is decidable in polynomial time (Skolem 1920, Whitman 1941). Adding the modular law changes everything.

The chronology

  • 1900 — Dedekind (Über die drei Modulen erzeugte Dualgruppe, Math. Annalen 53, 371–403): computed $\mathcal{F}_M(3)$ has 28 elements. Drawn carefully in his paper. Effort foundational to module theory.
  • 1969 — Evans: relations between residual finiteness, finite embeddability, and the word problem (J. London Math. Soc. 2). Set framework.
  • 1980 — Freese (Free modular lattices, Trans. AMS 261): the word problem for $\mathcal{F}_M(5)$ is unsolvable. Proof uses McIntyre's construction of a finitely-presented field with unsolvable word problem; embeds the field problem into modular-lattice equality. Major result.
  • 1982 — Christian Herrmann (Über die von vier Modulen erzeugte Dualgruppe): refines Freese to $\mathcal{F}_M(4)$. Lower bound matches Dedekind's $\mathcal{F}_M(3)$ being decidable (28 elements).
  • 2024+: ongoing work on tighter bounds and connections to projective geometry's coordinatisation.

Implications

  • $\mathbf{M}$ is not generated by its finite members. If it were, the word problem in $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{M}(\omega)$ would be decidable (since each test would reduce to checking finite lattices). Freese's theorem rules this out.
  • There exists a lattice equation that holds in all finite modular lattices but not in all modular lattices. Concrete witness sought; partial answers in subsequent work.
  • Contrast with Theorem 7.10 (McKenzie): the variety generated by a SINGLE finite lattice is finitely based. Going from a single finite lattice (finite-based) to ALL finite lattices (not even generating $\mathbf{M}$) is a big jump.

The undecidability of the modular-lattice word problem is one of the deepest negative results in algebra. It means: there is no general decision procedure for "does this equation hold in all modular lattices" — case analysis must be done variety by variety, often via ad-hoc arguments.

PANEL P4 Finite-basis theorems lineage

From McKenzie 1970 to Willard 2004 — the program of identifying which finite algebras generate finitely-based varieties.
Finite-basis theorems · half a century of results

The motivating problem: given a finite algebra $\mathcal{A}$, is the variety $V(\mathcal{A})$ finitely based — i.e., presented by finitely many equations? "Yes" results are theorems; "no" results are counter-examples.

Positive theorems

  • 1964 — Oates & Powell: every finite group is finitely based (J. Algebra 1).
  • 1970 — McKenzie: every finite lattice is finitely based (Math. Scand. 27). The chapter's Theorem 7.10.
  • 1973 — Kruse: every finite ring is finitely based (J. Algebra 26).
  • 1977 — Kirby Baker: any finite algebra in a congruence-distributive variety with finitely many operations is finitely based (Adv. Math. 24, 207-243). The umbrella theorem subsuming McKenzie + many others.
  • 1987 — McKenzie: finite equational bases for congruence MODULAR varieties (Algebra Universalis 24). Extends Baker beyond congruence-distributive.

Counter-examples

  • 1985 — McNulty: how to construct finite algebras that are NOT in any finitely-based variety (LNM 1149).
  • Various small examples in subsequent work: a 7-element groupoid (Murskii); a 6-element binar (Sapir-Volkov).

Survey + open

  • 2004 — Willard: The finite basis problem (Contributions to General Algebra 15). Survey: open problems, classification by structure type, conjecture status.
  • Open: complete characterisation of finite algebras with finitely-based varieties remains.

The pattern

Congruence-modularity (and a fortiori distributivity) implies finite-basis. The reason: Jónsson's Lemma and its analogues for modular varieties (Day's commutator theory) constrain the SIs sharply. Without congruence modularity, anything goes — small finite algebras can be infinite-base. Lattices benefit from Funayama-Nakayama: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is always distributive, so all lattice varieties are congruence-distributive, hence McKenzie 1970 applies universally.

PANEL P5 Prologos cross-references

Equational logic, varieties, and Łoś-style transfer in Prologos's type system and effect machinery.
Prologos · varieties as type-class hierarchies

Prologos's traits (Eq, Ord, Add, Sub, Mul, etc., from `prologos.core.*`) are equational classes in Birkhoff's sense. Each trait declares a signature (the operations) and laws (the equations). When a type implements a trait, the implementation is checked against the laws — by structural-unification propagators (PUnify) plus user-provided proof obligations.

The trait hierarchy is exactly the variety lattice $\Lambda$ for the trait's signature. Bundles (e.g., `bundle Num := (Add Sub Mul)`) are intersections of varieties — taking the conjunction of multiple sets of equations. The lattice of all trait bundles is dually algebraic in Prologos's design (per Theorem 7.3 here): finitely-based bundles are dually compact, infinite intersections require infinite bundles. The Prologos compiler uses this structure for trait dispatch resolution: given a context constraint $\text{Num}\,?A$, find the smallest variety containing $?A$ that satisfies the constraint.

Łoś-style transfer in propagator semantics

Prologos's propagator network uses BSP (Bulk Synchronous Parallel) scheduling: each round fires propagators in parallel, then merges via lattice joins. Łoś-style transfer applies in the limit: the semantic content of the network at quiescence is the JOIN of all individual propagator outputs, modulo the equivalence "almost all rounds agree." For monotone-set lattices (used in fan-in readiness, per `propagator-design.md`), this is literally an ultrafilter-style argument: the readiness signal $\bigcup_i R_i$ corresponds to "almost all input sources have signaled."

First-order language for type assertions

Prologos's surface syntax includes spec (function signatures) and defn (implementations); both compile to first-order assertions over the type signature's algebra. Type-class membership is a first-order predicate. Trait-method satisfaction $h(\text{eq?}\,a\,b) = \text{true}$ is a first-order equation. The trait dispatch resolver is essentially a model-checker: given assumptions $\Sigma$ and goal $\varphi$, decide $\Sigma \models \varphi$. By analogy with Whitman's algorithm: for trait languages over decidable signatures (which Prologos's primary traits are), the satisfaction check is decidable.

Word problem for free modular lattices · cautionary tale

Freese 1980 (word problem for $\mathcal{F}_M(5)$ unsolvable) is a sharp warning for Prologos's design: do not impose modularity on user-defined trait lattices unless absolutely necessary. The system's structural-unification machinery (PUnify) operates on a free-lattice-like substrate; if user code introduces modularity equations, the trait-resolution procedure may become undecidable. This shapes Prologos's choice of semidistributive (Ch 6) lattices for the type-domain rather than modular: SD is decidable + structural; modular is undecidable beyond 3 generators. The chapter's contrast — free modular lattices undecidable, free lattices decidable — reinforces the design choice.

Ex. 7.1Fully invariant congruences form a complete sublattice
Show that fully invariant congruences form a complete sublattice of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$.

Solution. A complete sublattice means the join and meet (over arbitrary subsets) of fully invariant congruences are again fully invariant.

Meets: If $\{\theta_i\}$ are fully invariant, then $\bigwedge \theta_i$ is the intersection (set-theoretically). For any $f \in \textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}$ and $(x, y) \in \bigwedge \theta_i$: $(x, y) \in \theta_i$ for all $i$, so $(f(x), f(y)) \in \theta_i$ for all $i$ (each is fully invariant), hence $(f(x), f(y)) \in \bigwedge \theta_i$.

Joins: The join $\bigvee \theta_i$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is the smallest congruence containing all $\theta_i$. For any $f \in \textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}$, the image $\{(f(x), f(y)) : (x, y) \in \bigvee \theta_i\}$ is contained in the smallest congruence containing $\{f(\theta_i)\}$. Since each $f(\theta_i) \subseteq \theta_i$ (as $\theta_i$ is fully invariant), this smallest congruence is below $\bigvee \theta_i$. Hence $\bigvee \theta_i$ is fully invariant.

So fully invariant congruences are closed under arbitrary meets and arbitrary joins, hence form a complete sublattice.

Ex. 7.2Minimum congruence $\rho_\mathbf{V}$ for variety membership
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice and $\mathbf{V}$ a lattice variety. Show that there is a unique minimum congruence $\rho_\mathbf{V}$ on $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\mathcal{L}/\rho_\mathbf{V} \in \mathbf{V}$.

Solution. Let $\Sigma$ be the equations defining $\mathbf{V}$. Define $\rho_\mathbf{V}$ as the smallest congruence on $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\mathcal{L}/\rho_\mathbf{V}$ satisfies $\Sigma$. Equivalently:

$$\rho_\mathbf{V} = \bigvee \{\,(p(\vec a), q(\vec a)) : (p \approx q) \in \Sigma, \vec a \in L\,\}.$$

Why this works: $\mathcal{L}/\rho_\mathbf{V}$ collapses every pair $(p(\vec a), q(\vec a))$ where $p \approx q$ is a $\mathbf{V}$-equation, hence satisfies $\Sigma$. Any other congruence $\theta$ with $\mathcal{L}/\theta \in \mathbf{V}$ must also collapse all such pairs (since $\mathbf{V}$-membership requires $\Sigma$ to hold), hence $\rho_\mathbf{V} \le \theta$. So $\rho_\mathbf{V}$ is the unique minimum.

Uniqueness: If $\rho$ and $\rho'$ both have the property, then by minimality $\rho \le \rho'$ and $\rho' \le \rho$, so $\rho = \rho'$.

Ex. 7.3SI ⟹ HSP join-irreducible in $\Lambda$
(a) Prove that if $\mathcal{L}$ is a subdirectly irreducible lattice, then $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$ is (finitely) join irreducible in the lattice $\Lambda$ of lattice varieties. (b) Prove that if a variety $\mathbf{V}$ is completely join irreducible in $\Lambda$, then $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{K})$ for some finitely generated, subdirectly irreducible lattice $\mathcal{K}$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Suppose $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W}$ for varieties $\mathbf{V}$, $\mathbf{W}$. By Theorem 7.11, $(\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si} = \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$. The SI lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is in $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W}$, hence in $(\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si}$, so $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$, i.e., $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}$ or $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{W}$. Without loss, $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}$, hence $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L}) \subseteq \mathbf{V} \subseteq \mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W} = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$. So $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$, i.e., $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$ is finitely join-irreducible.
  • (b) If $\mathbf{V}$ is completely join-irreducible in $\Lambda$, then for the chain of all $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{K})$ with $\mathcal{K}$ finitely-generated SI in $\mathbf{V}$, their join (over all such $\mathcal{K}$) is at most $\mathbf{V}$ (each is in $\mathbf{V}$). By Lemma 7.4 (varieties determined by SIs), the join contains all SIs of $\mathbf{V}$. Hence the join equals $\mathbf{V}$. By complete join-irreducibility, $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{K})$ for some single $\mathcal{K}$ — a finitely-generated SI lattice.
Ex. 7.4$\equiv_F$ is a congruence on the direct product
Show that if $F$ is a filter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, then $\equiv_F$ is a congruence relation on $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i$.

Solution. Recall $x \equiv_F y$ iff $\{i : x_i = y_i\} \in F$. Need to show: equivalence relation + congruence (preserved by $\vee$ and $\wedge$).

  • Reflexivity: $x \equiv_F x$ since $\{i : x_i = x_i\} = I \in F$.
  • Symmetry: $\{i : x_i = y_i\} = \{i : y_i = x_i\}$.
  • Transitivity: $x \equiv_F y$ and $y \equiv_F z$ ⟹ $A := \{i : x_i = y_i\} \in F$ and $B := \{i : y_i = z_i\} \in F$. Then $A \cap B \in F$ (filter closed under finite intersections), and $A \cap B \subseteq \{i : x_i = z_i\}$ (where $x_i = y_i = z_i$ on $A \cap B$). By upward closure, $\{i : x_i = z_i\} \in F$. So $x \equiv_F z$.
  • Congruence: Suppose $x \equiv_F x'$ and $y \equiv_F y'$. Then $A := \{i : x_i = x'_i\} \in F$ and $B := \{i : y_i = y'_i\} \in F$. So $A \cap B \in F$, and on $A \cap B$, $(x \vee y)_i = x_i \vee y_i = x'_i \vee y'_i = (x' \vee y')_i$. Thus $\{i : (x \vee y)_i = (x' \vee y')_i\} \supseteq A \cap B \in F$, so $x \vee y \equiv_F x' \vee y'$. Similarly for $\wedge$.

Hence $\equiv_F$ is a congruence on $\prod \mathcal{L}_i$.

Ex. 7.5Filters as intersections of ultrafilters
(a) Show that if $F$ is a filter on $\mathfrak{P}(I)$, then $F$ is the intersection of the ultrafilters $U$ such that $U \supseteq F$. (b) Then prove that the congruence $\equiv_F$ on $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i$ is the intersection of the congruences $\equiv_U$ with $U$ an ultrafilter. (c) Conclude that the reduced product $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_F$ is a subdirect product of ultraproducts $\prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Clearly $F \subseteq \bigcap \{U : U \supseteq F, U \text{ ultrafilter}\}$. Conversely, for any $A \notin F$: by Lemma 7.5(1), there is an ultrafilter $U \supseteq F$ with $A \notin U$. So $A \notin \bigcap \{U : U \supseteq F\}$. Hence the equality.
  • (b) $x \equiv_F y$ iff $\{i : x_i = y_i\} \in F$ iff $\{i : x_i = y_i\} \in U$ for every ultrafilter $U \supseteq F$ (by part (a)) iff $x \equiv_U y$ for every such $U$. Hence $\equiv_F = \bigcap_{U \supseteq F} \equiv_U$.
  • (c) By Birkhoff's subdirect representation (Theorem 5.6), if $\equiv_F = \bigcap \equiv_U$, then $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_F$ embeds subdirectly into $\prod_U (\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U)$. The right side is a product of ultraproducts; the left side is the reduced product. Hence the reduced product is a subdirect product of ultraproducts.
Ex. 7.6Every failing equation fails in a finite lattice
Prove that every lattice equation that does not hold in all lattices fails in some finite lattice. (Let $p \neq q$ in $\text{FL}(X)$. Then there exist a finite join subsemilattice $\mathcal{S}$ of $\text{FL}(X)$ containing $p, q$ and $0 = \bigwedge X$, and a lattice homomorphism $h : \text{FL}(X) \to \mathcal{S}$, such that $h(p) = p$ and $h(q) = q$.)

Solution. Suppose $p \approx q$ fails in some lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Then $p \neq q$ in $\text{FL}(X)$ (since $\text{FL}(X)$ is the most general; if $p = q$ in $\text{FL}(X)$, $p = q$ in every lattice). Following the hint:

Construct $\mathcal{S}$ inductively. Start with $S_0 = X \cup \{p, q, \bigwedge X\}$ (finite). To make this closed under $\vee$ and $\wedge$, take pairwise joins and meets — but cap them at "limits" given by lattice term complexity. Since $p, q$ are specific elements of $\text{FL}(X)$, only finitely many join-irreducibles below them are needed. Take $\mathcal{S}$ to be the (finite) sublattice generated by $S_0$ within $\text{FL}(X)$.

The standard construction: $\mathcal{S}$ turns out to be lower bounded (Exercise 11 of Chapter 6), so it satisfies $\text{SD}_\vee$. The homomorphism $h$ from $\text{FL}(X)$ to $\mathcal{S}$ is the identity on $\mathcal{S}$ and projects elements outside $\mathcal{S}$ down to their "best lower bound" in $\mathcal{S}$. By construction $h(p) = p$ and $h(q) = q$ in $\mathcal{S}$, but $p \neq q$ in $\mathcal{S}$. So $p \approx q$ fails in $\mathcal{S}$, a finite lattice.

The standard solution uses lattices that are lower bounded (Exercise 11 of Ch 6); they satisfy $\text{SD}_\vee$. Hence any finite collection of them generates a variety NOT containing $\mathcal{M}_3$, while all together they generate the variety of all lattices. The variety generated by $\mathcal{M}_3$ contains only $\mathbf{D}$ (generated by $\mathbf{2}$) and $\mathbf{T}$. This shows $\Lambda$ is not join-continuous.

Ex. 7.7First-order sentences for atomicity properties
Give a first order sentence characterising each of the following properties of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ (i.e., $\mathcal{L}$ has the property iff $\mathcal{L} \models \varphi$). (a) $\mathcal{L}$ has a least element. (b) $\mathcal{L}$ is atomic. (c) $\mathcal{L}$ is strongly atomic. (d) $\mathcal{L}$ is weakly atomic. (e) $\mathcal{L}$ has no covering relations.

Solutions.

  • (a) Least element: $\exists z\,\forall x\,(z \le x)$, where $z \le x$ abbreviates $z \vee x = x$.
  • (b) Atomic: "Every $a > 0$ is above some atom." Atom: covers $0$. So: $\forall a\,(\exists z\,(z \le a) \;\text{AND}\; (a > z) \Rightarrow \exists u\,(u > z) \wedge (u \le a) \wedge (\forall v\,((u > v) \;\text{AND}\; (v \ge z) \Rightarrow v = z)))$. With abbreviations for $\le, <, \prec$.
  • (c) Strongly atomic: "$a > b$ implies $\exists u$ with $u \succ b$ and $a \ge u$." A first-order sentence: $\forall a, b\,((a > b) \Rightarrow \exists u\,((u \succ b) \;\text{AND}\; (u \le a)))$, where $u \succ b$ abbreviates $u > b \wedge \neg \exists v\,(u > v > b)$.
  • (d) Weakly atomic: "$a > b$ implies $\exists u, v$ with $a \ge u \succ v \ge b$." Like (c) but with $u, v$ free between $a$ and $b$. $\forall a, b\,((a > b) \Rightarrow \exists u, v\,((u \succ v) \;\text{AND}\; (a \ge u) \;\text{AND}\; (v \ge b)))$.
  • (e) No covering relations: $\forall a, b\,(a > b \Rightarrow \exists c\,(a > c > b))$, i.e., between any two strictly comparable elements there is a third.
Ex. 7.8Breadth $n$
A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has breadth $n$ if $L$ contains $n$ elements whose join is irredundant, but every join of $n+1$ elements of $L$ is redundant. (a) Give a first order sentence characterising lattices of breadth $n$ (for a fixed finite integer $n \ge 1$). (b) Show that the class of lattices of breadth $\le n$ is not a variety. (c) Show that a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ and its dual $\mathcal{L}^d$ have the same breadth.

Solutions.

  • (a) "Breadth $\le n$" + "breadth $\ge n$":
    $\le n$: $\forall x_1, \dots, x_{n+1}\,\bigvee_i (x_i \le \bigvee_{j \neq i} x_j)$ — at least one is below the join of the others.
    $\ge n$: $\exists x_1, \dots, x_n\,\bigwedge_i (x_i \not\le \bigvee_{j \neq i} x_j)$ — irredundant join exists.
    Conjunction yields exactly breadth $n$.
  • (b) Breadth $\le n$ is closed under sublattices and homomorphic images, but NOT under direct products: if $\mathcal{L}$ has breadth $n$, then $\mathcal{L} \times \mathcal{L}$ generally has breadth $\ge 2n$. Hence not a variety (varieties closed under P).
  • (c) $\mathcal{L}^d$ swaps $\vee$ and $\wedge$. The dual notion of "breadth via joins" becomes "breadth via meets." But for $\mathcal{L}$, the same elements that give an irredundant meet can be permuted to give an irredundant join (and vice versa) — at least for finite breadth analysis. Concretely: in a finite lattice $\mathcal{L}$ with breadth $n$ via joins, the dual $\mathcal{L}^d$ has the same breadth $n$ via meets, which (in $\mathcal{L}^d$) is the breadth via joins. Hence $\mathcal{L}$ and $\mathcal{L}^d$ have the same breadth.
Ex. 7.9$\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$ first-order sentence
Give a first order sentence $\varphi$ such that a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies $\varphi$ if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the four element lattice $\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$.

Solution. $\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$ is the diamond minus center: 4 elements $\{0, a, b, 1\}$ with $a, b$ incomparable, $0 < a, b < 1$. The sentence $\varphi$:

$\exists x_0, x_a, x_b, x_1$ such that:
(i) $x_0 < x_a < x_1$ AND $x_0 < x_b < x_1$ (the structure)
(ii) $x_a$ AND $x_b$ are incomparable: $x_a \not\le x_b$ AND $x_b \not\le x_a$
(iii) $x_a \wedge x_b = x_0$ AND $x_a \vee x_b = x_1$ (joins/meets correct)
(iv) $\forall y\,(y = x_0 \;\text{OR}\; y = x_a \;\text{OR}\; y = x_b \;\text{OR}\; y = x_1)$ (exactly 4 elements)

Conjunction of (i)-(iv) is a first-order sentence; $\mathcal{L} \models \varphi$ iff $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{2}$.

Ex. 7.10Prove Theorem 7.11
Prove Theorem 7.11: $(\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si} = \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$.

Solution. ($\supseteq$) Trivially: $\mathbf{V}_{si} \subseteq (\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si}$ since $\mathbf{V} \subseteq \mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W}$ and SI is downward-preserved. Similarly $\mathbf{W}_{si} \subseteq (\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si}$.

($\subseteq$) Let $\mathcal{L}$ be SI in $\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W}$. By Jónsson's Lemma (Theorem 7.7), $\mathcal{L} \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{V} \cup \mathbf{W})$. Hence $\mathcal{L}$ is a homomorphic image of a sublattice of an ultraproduct $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$ where each $\mathcal{L}_i \in \mathbf{V} \cup \mathbf{W}$.

Partition $I$: $A_V = \{i : \mathcal{L}_i \in \mathbf{V}\}$ and $A_W = \{i : \mathcal{L}_i \in \mathbf{W}\}$. Then $A_V \cup A_W = I \in U$. By Lemma 7.5(4), $A_V \in U$ or $A_W \in U$.

If $A_V \in U$: by Lemma 7.8, the ultraproduct restricts to $\prod_{i \in A_V} \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_V$ where $V$ is an ultrafilter on $\mathfrak{P}(A_V)$. All $\mathcal{L}_i \in \mathbf{V}$ for $i \in A_V$, so the ultraproduct is in $\text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{V}) \subseteq \mathbf{V}$. Hence $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}$.

Similarly if $A_W \in U$, then $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{W}$. So $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$. $\square$

Ex. 7.11Distributivity / modularity of ideal lattices
Prove that $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is distributive if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive. Similarly, show that $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is modular if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ is modular.

Solution sketches.

Distributive case.

  • ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is distributive. Embed $\mathcal{L}$ into $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ via $x \mapsto\,\downarrow x$. Sublattices of distributive lattices are distributive. So $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive.
  • ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive. By Birkhoff (1934), $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ satisfies every identity satisfied by $\mathcal{L}$ — in particular, the distributive law. So $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is distributive.

Modular case. Same arguments. ($\Rightarrow$): sublattices of modular lattices are modular. ($\Leftarrow$): Birkhoff's identity transfer.

The modular case has more bite: Birkhoff's theorem ($\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in \text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$) tells us $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ inherits all equational properties of $\mathcal{L}$, but the Baker-Hales theorem (Theorem 7.12) says $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathcal{L})$ — the much sharper containment. The distributivity / modularity of $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L})$ is one face of this transfer.
