Singular and Semisingular Elements

Two specialized classes of completely-join-irreducible elements. Singular: $\kappa(w) = w_*$ (the κ-image is the lower cover). Semisingular: $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for some canonical meetand $w_i$. Theorem 8.1: $w$ is semisingular iff $w$ is singular OR $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval. Theorem 8.6: every singular element has $\kappa(w)$ being either the meet of two coatoms or the join of two atoms — so singular elements live ONLY at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. This retroactively justifies the footnote in Theorem 7.2's proof. Corollary 8.7: no interval of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is isomorphic to an infinite chain — foreshadowing Tschantz's Chapter IX theorem.

~50–65 min read 2 sections · 7 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 171–178 · PDF pp. 180–187

Recall that by Theorem 3.34, if $w$ is completely join irreducible then all of the canonical meetands of $w$ are canonical meetands of $w_*$, except those that lie above $\kappa(w)$. An element $w$ for which some canonical meetand is above $\kappa(w)$ is called semisingular. More precisely, a completely join irreducible element $w$ of a free lattice is a semisingular element if $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for one of its canonical meetands $w_i$. Whenever $w$ is semisingular, we will use the convention that $\kappa(w) \le w_1$. A completely join irreducible element is a singular element if $w_* = \kappa(w)$ (so that $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for every $i$). In this case the covering $w_* \prec w$ is known as a singular cover. Thus a singular cover is one in which the top is a meet and the bottom is a join. For example, the meet of two coatoms is a singular element in a free lattice.

In this chapter we characterize singular and semisingular elements. It turns out that these characterizations are extremely useful, and their applications will be investigated in the next chapter.

If $X$ is infinite, $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ trivially has no completely join irreducible elements, and hence no semisingular elements; thus in this chapter we assume $X$ is finite.

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter VIII (paraphrased)

Chapter VIII is short — 8 pages, 2 sections, 7 numbered results — but structurally pivotal. It picks up two threads that were left dangling in earlier chapters: (a) Theorem 7.2's footnote referring to a forthcoming "singular element" definition (without which the proof of Theorem 7.2 would be circular); (b) Chapter VI's Theorem 6.31 (the "no $v$ exists" theorem) which was framed without semantic motivation. Chapter VIII delivers both: Theorem 8.6 rigorously characterizes singular elements as occurring only at the very top/bottom, and Theorem 8.1 + Corollaries 8.4-8.5 use Theorem 6.31 in essential ways to characterize semisingular elements.

The chapter has three layers. §1 Semisingular Elements (Theorem 8.1, Lemmas 8.2-8.3, Corollaries 8.4-8.5): a completely join irreducible $w$ is semisingular iff $w$ is singular OR $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval (so $w$'s "structural environment" is one of the two specific patterns from Chapter VII). The proof uses 4 cases in Theorem 8.1's Theorem 7.6-style branching; cases ii and iii don't occur (proved by Theorem 6.31), case iv reduces to case i. Corollary 8.5 distills a "useful test": for completely-join-irreducible $w$ with $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$, if $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, then $u = w_*, u = w$, or $u \succ w$. §2 Singular Elements (Theorem 8.6): every singular element $w$ has $\kappa(w) = w_*$ being either the meet of two coatoms or the join of two atoms. The proof uses Theorem 7.2 (chain-of-covers bound) combined with the fact that semisingular ≠ middle-of-3-elt forces $w_* = \kappa(w)$ to be top of a 3-elt interval (Corollary 8.4). §3 Bridge: Corollary 8.7 — no interval of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is isomorphic to an infinite chain. The first foreshadowing of Chapter IX's Tschantz theorem (every infinite interval contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$).

New · Chapter VIII vocabulary (minimal)

Three new definitions, with chapter-specific conventions. Singular element: a completely join irreducible $w$ with $\kappa(w) = w_*$. The cover $w_* \prec w$ is then called a singular cover — top is meet (since $w$ is completely join irreducible, $w$ has the structure of a meet via canonical form), bottom is a join (since $w_* = \kappa(w)$ is completely meet irreducible, equivalently the join of canonical joinands). Semisingular element: a completely join irreducible $w$ such that $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for SOME canonical meetand $w_i$ of $w$. By the chapter's convention, this $w_i$ is renumbered to $w_1$. Note: every singular element is semisingular (since $\kappa(w) = w_* \le w_i$ for ALL $i$ when $w$ is singular), but not conversely. The element $w_{i1}$: for $w = \bigwedge w_i$ canonically with each $w_i = \bigvee_j w_{ij}$ canonical, the unique canonical joinand of $w_i$ such that $w_{ij} \not\le w_*$ — by Theorem 3.4 there is exactly one for each $i$. The chapter's notational convention indexes this as $w_{i1}$.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. The structural meaning of "singular cover". A singular cover $w_* \prec w$ has $w$ a meet (canonically of $\bigwedge w_i$) and $w_*$ a join (canonically as $\kappa(w)$ in canonical form). So the cover bridges a "meet-side element" (above) to a "join-side element" (below). Most covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ are NOT singular — they have either join-on-both-sides or meet-on-both-sides. Singular covers are the rare exception, and Theorem 8.6 says they occur only at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Theorem 8.1's structural dichotomy. A semisingular element is EITHER singular ($\kappa(w) = w_*$) OR the middle of a 3-element interval. This is a clean dichotomy — no third option. The proof in §1 shows by 4-case analysis that any other potential structural pattern doesn't occur. The "extremely useful" framing in Chapter VIII's introduction is the basis for Chapter IX's Tschantz theorem: when constructing pairs of incomparable elements in an infinite interval, knowing which elements are semisingular vs. not is structurally essential. The role of Theorem 6.31 in Theorem 8.1's proof. Cases ii and iii of Theorem 8.1's proof use Theorem 6.31 ("no $v$ exists") to derive contradictions. Theorem 6.31 was framed in Chapter VI without semantic motivation; it was crafted specifically as the technical tool needed here. Chapter VIII §1 is essentially the "application" of Theorem 6.31 — and §2 is the application of §1's results to characterize singular elements.

PART A · SEMISINGULAR ELEMENTS — §1

Theorem 8.1: $w$ semisingular ⟺ $w$ singular OR $w$ middle of 3-element interval

The structural dichotomy. Lemma 8.2 + Lemma 8.3 set up the technical machinery. The proof of Theorem 8.1 is a 4-case analysis using Theorem 6.31, with cases ii and iii ruled out, and case iv reducing to case i. Corollaries 8.4 and 8.5 distill useful application forms.

§ 1 Semisingular Elements

A completely join irreducible $w$ is semisingular iff $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for some canonical meetand. Every singular element is semisingular. Theorem 8.1 gives the converse-with-cases: semisingular ⟺ singular OR middle of 3-element interval.

Notational conventions (book p. 171)

Whenever $w = \bigwedge w_i$ is completely join irreducible, then by Theorem 3.4, for each $i$ there is a unique $j$ such that $w_{ij} \not\le w_*$. Throughout this chapter we will denote this element by $w_{i1}$. (If $w_i \in X$, then we take $w_{i1} = w_i$.) Note $w_{i1} \not\le \kappa(w)$ (else $w \le w_i \le \kappa(w)$), and by Theorem 3.33, $w_{ij} < w_*$ for all $j > 1$.

Theorem 8.1 — the central characterization (book p. 171)

Theorem 8.1 · semisingular characterization

A completely join irreducible element $w \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ is semisingular if and only if $w_* = \kappa(w)$ (i.e., $w$ is singular) OR $w$ is the middle element of a three element interval.

Intuition · the dichotomy

The forward direction (singular OR 3-element-middle ⟹ semisingular) is easy. If $w$ is singular: $\kappa(w) = w_* \le w$ (and in particular $\le w_i$ for any canonical meetand $w_i$), so $w$ is semisingular trivially. If $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval: by Theorem 7.10(6), $\kappa(w) < q$ where $q$ is the canonical meetand of $w$ not above $u$ (the top of the interval). The element $q$ is a canonical meetand of $w$ — so $\kappa(w) < q = w_i$ for some $i$. Hence $w$ is semisingular.

The CONVERSE is the hard direction. Suppose $w$ is semisingular, with $\kappa(w) \le w_1$. The proof shows by 4-case analysis that either $w_* = \kappa(w)$ (case i, with $v_{1*} \le w_1$ for some $s > 1$ where the indices follow specific conventions) or $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval (case iv, reducing to case i). Cases ii and iii are ruled out by Theorem 6.31. The proof is dense; we sketch the structural mechanism.

The technical lemmas (book pp. 171–172)

Lemma 8.2 · structural inequality on κ

Let $w = \bigwedge_k x_k \wedge \bigwedge_i (\bigvee_j w_{ij})$ canonically be a completely join irreducible element in a free lattice. Assume the indexing is such that $w_{i1} \not\le w$ and $w_{ij} < w$ for $j > 1$. Then for each $i$,

$$w_{i1*} \le \kappa(w) \prec \kappa(w)^* \le \kappa(w_{i1}).$$

Proof of Lemma 8.2: define A and B relations on completely join irreducibles ($a A b$ iff $b < a$ and $a \le b \vee \kappa(b)$; $a B b$ iff $a \neq b$, $a \le a_* \vee b$ and $a \not\le a_* \vee b_*$). We noted in Chapter II that if $a B b$, then $b_* \le \kappa(a)$. Now by Theorem 2.60 and Lemma 3.11, $w B w_{i1}$ and thus $w_{i1*} \le \kappa(w)$. By Lemma 2.63, $\kappa(w) A^d \kappa(w_{i1})$ which implies $\kappa(w) < \kappa(w_{i1})$, giving the rest. $\square$

Lemma 8.3 · join-meet structure of $v_r$ and $w_{1j}$

Let $v, w, v_r, w_{ij}$ be as given above (with $v = \kappa(w)$, $v = \bigvee v_r$ a proper join). Then:

  1. $v \vee w_{11} = w_1$.
  2. $\{w_{1j} : j > 1\} \ll \{v_1, v_2, \ldots\}$.

The 4-case proof of Theorem 8.1's converse (book pp. 172–176)

Now we begin the proof of Theorem 8.1. Assume that $w$ is semisingular and that $\kappa(w) \le w_1$. Let $v = \kappa(w)$. We claim that it is enough to show that either $w_* = v$ or $w_1$ is totally atomic. For if $w_1$ is totally atomic, then $\kappa^d(w_1) \not\le \kappa(w)$, for otherwise $\kappa^d(w_1) \le w_1$; thus in this case $w \vee \kappa^d(w_1) > w \succ w_*$ by Theorem 3.5, and $w \vee \kappa^d(w_1)/w_*$ is a 3-element interval by Theorem 7.6(2).

Since $w$ is completely join irreducible, $w_1$ is lower atomic by Corollary 3.27. Thus it suffices to show that either $w_* = v$ or $w_1$ is completely meet irreducible.

If $w_1$ is a generator, then clearly it is completely meet irreducible. Thus we assume $w_1$ is not a generator. Note $v$ is not a generator since otherwise $w = \kappa^d(v)$ would be an atom, but atoms are not semisingular. Thus $v = \bigvee v_r$ is a proper join.

Apply Lemma 8.3(1): $v \vee w_{11} = w_1$ and Lemma 8.3(2): $\{w_{1j} : j > 1\} \ll \{v_1, v_2, \ldots\}$. The proof proceeds in 4 cases based on the structure of $v_{1s}$ relative to $w_1$.

Proof outline · the 4 cases

Case i. $v_{1s} \le w_1$ for some $s > 1$. By the dual of Theorem 3.4 and the indexing convention, $v^* \le v_{1t}$ for all $t$ except $t = 1$. Thus $v^* \le v_{1s} \le w_1$, which implies $w \le v_{1s}$. Since $v_{1s}$ is completely meet irreducible by Theorem 3.26 and $w \le v_{1s} \le w_1$, the dual of Lemma 3.28 yields that $w_1$ is completely meet irreducible, as desired. The reduction in this case shows $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval.

Case ii. $v_{11} \le w_1$. By the dual of Lemma 8.2, $w = \kappa^d(v) \le v_{11}^*$. After Whitman's condition manipulations, $v \vee v_{11} = w_1$ and $v_{11}^* = w_1$. The diagram (Figure 8.1) is established. Apply Theorem 6.31 with $p = v_1, q = v_{11}$. The configuration violates Theorem 6.31, giving a contradiction. So Case ii does not occur at all.

Case iii. $v_1 \le w_{11}$. Then $w_{11*} \le v_1 \le w_{11}$. Note that $v_{11}^* \ge \kappa^d(v) = w$ by the dual of Lemma 8.2, while $w_{11} \wedge v = v_1 = w_{11*}$ implies $v_{11} \ge w_{11}$ via Theorem 3.4 + canonical form. By case-analysis (Cases of "$w_{11}$ is a generator" vs not, with sub-cases $Y = \{y\}$ and $Z = \{z\}$), all sub-cases lead to contradictions. So Case iii does not occur at all.

Case iv. $v_1 \le w_{1j}$ for some $j > 1$. By Theorem 3.4, $w_{1j} \le w_*$. Hence $w_{11*} \le v_1 \le w_{1j} \le w_*$, and since $w_{1j} \not\ge w_{11}$, we have $w_{11*} \le w_{1j} \le \kappa(w_{11})$. By Theorem 6.30, $\kappa(w_{11})$ is totally atomic. Lemma 8.3(2) gives $v_1 = w_{1j}$ and hence this element is totally atomic. The argument continues by introducing $v_2 = v \wedge \kappa(v_1)$, showing $w_{11*} \le v_1$ and $w_{11*} \le v_2$, and then re-applying the 4-case analysis with $v_2$ in place of $v_1$. By induction, this reaches case i. So case iv either reduces to case i or gives $\kappa(w) = w_*$.

This completes the proof of Theorem 8.1. $\square$

Corollaries 8.4 and 8.5 — the useful application forms (book p. 176)

Corollary 8.4 · semisingular structural form

If $w$ is a semisingular element of a free lattice, then either $w$ is the middle element of a three element interval, or $w_* = \kappa(w)$ and $w$ is the top element of a three element interval.

The proof: Theorem 8.1 has two important consequences. First, as we observed in the proof, only two of the four cases can actually occur. Case iv either reduces to case i or else gives $\kappa(w) = w_*$, in which event $v = \kappa(w)$ is the middle element of a three element interval $v_2 \prec v \prec w$. Thus we obtain Corollary 8.4. $\square$

Corollary 8.5 · the "useful test"

Let $w$ be a completely join irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$. If $u$ is an element such that $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, then either $u = w_*$, or $u = w$, or $u \succ w$. Moreover, if $u \succ w$ then $w$ is semisingular.

Proof. By Theorem 3.34, if $w$ is not semisingular, then each canonical meetand, $w_i$, of $w$ is a canonical meetand of $w_*$. So if $w_* = u \wedge \kappa(w)$ and $w$ is not semisingular, i.e., $w_i \not\le \kappa(w)$ for all $i$, then by Theorem 1.19 we have $w_i \ge u$ for all $i$. Thus $u \le w$, which implies $u$ is either $w$ or $w_*$.

In the other case $w$ is semisingular and thus, by Theorem 8.1, $w$ is the middle element of a three element element. By Theorem 7.10, the canonical form of $w_*$ is $w_* = \kappa(w) \wedge r$ for some $r \succ w$. The corollary follows easily from this. $\square$

Connection P2 · Theorem 8.1's 4-case proof structure

The proof of Theorem 8.1 has a striking structural pattern: 4 cases, but only 2 can actually occur. Cases ii and iii are "phantoms" — they would correspond to specific canonical-form configurations, but those configurations are forbidden by Theorem 6.31.

Why Theorem 6.31 was framed in Chapter VI. When Chapter VI introduced Theorem 6.31, the framing was abstract: "for completely meet irreducible $q$ with $q^* \succ q \succ p$ and $\kappa^d(q) \le \kappa(q_1)$, no completely meet irreducible $v$ satisfies $v \le q^* \wedge \kappa(q_1)$ AND $v \not\le p \vee \kappa^d(q)$." The semantic motivation was deferred. Now Cases ii and iii of Theorem 8.1 reveal the motivation: Theorem 6.31 was custom-made to forbid these specific canonical-form configurations.

The cascading reduction structure. Case iv applies the same 4-case analysis to a smaller element $v_2$, reducing eventually to case i. This is a recursive argument with explicit termination (since $v_2$ has strictly smaller complexity than $v_1$). The reduction is what allows Corollary 8.4 to assert: every semisingular element is either the MIDDLE or the TOP of a 3-element interval — no other structural location is possible.

Why this matters for Chapter IX. Tschantz's theorem (Chapter IX) constructs pairs of incomparable elements in infinite intervals. The construction needs to distinguish "elements with controllable structural environment" (semisingular) from "wild" elements. The 4-case proof's "phantom" cases tell us EXACTLY what structural patterns are RULED OUT — equivalently, what patterns can be safely IGNORED in the construction. This is the structural cleanness that makes Tschantz's argument tractable.

PART B · SINGULAR ELEMENTS — §2

Theorem 8.6: every singular element has $\kappa(w) = w_*$ being a meet of two coatoms or a join of two atoms

Singular elements live ONLY at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Theorem 8.6 is the result that retroactively justifies Theorem 7.2's footnote — without it, Chapter VII's proof would have been circular. From Freese-Ježek-Nation-Slavík 1986.

§ 2 Singular Elements

Recall: $w$ is singular if $\kappa(w) = w_*$ — the κ-image IS the lower cover. Theorem 8.6: in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, every singular $w$ has its κ-image as either a meet of 2 coatoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 0) or a join of 2 atoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 1).

The structural location result (book p. 177)

Recall that a singular element is a completely join irreducible element $w$ with $\kappa(w) = w_*$. We have seen in Chapter III that these exist in the connected components of $0$ and $1$; in this section we will show that these are the only examples. This result first appeared in Freese, Ježek, Nation and Slavík [58], with a slightly more complicated proof.

Theorem 8.6 · singular elements live only at top/bottom

Suppose that $w$ is a singular element, and let $v = \kappa(w) = w_*$. Then either $w$ is the meet of two coatoms or, dually, $v$ is the join of two atoms.

Intuition · the structural location

Theorem 8.6 says: a singular $w$ is structurally constrained to live at the very top of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (where $w$ is the meet of two coatoms — so $w$ is in the connected component of $1$) OR at the very bottom (where $v = \kappa(w)$ is the join of two atoms — so $w$ is in the connected component of $0$). No singular element exists in the "interior" of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

This is consistent with Examples 3.44 and 3.45's description of the connected components of 0 and 1. Looking at the 11-element bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (Example 3.44), the singular elements are: meets of pairs of atoms (e.g., $(x \wedge y) \wedge (x \wedge z) = x \wedge y \wedge z$ — wait, that's a meet of three atoms, not two). Let me re-examine. The singular elements in the connected component of 0 are EXACTLY the elements $w$ such that $\kappa(w)$ is the join of 2 atoms — e.g., $\kappa(w) = \underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j}$ for some pair $i \neq j$. By Theorem 8.6 dual, the corresponding $w$ at the top is the meet of 2 coatoms, e.g., $w = \overline{x_i} \wedge \overline{x_j}$.

Proof outline (book pp. 177–178)

A quick look at the description of the connected components of $0$ and $1$ given in Examples 3.44 and 3.45, shows that the singular elements contained in one of these components are as described in the theorem. Thus it suffices to show that every singular element lies in one of these components.

Moreover, we may assume that $w$ is the middle element of a three element interval. For otherwise, it is the top of a three element interval by Corollary 8.4, and so the dual of $v$ is singular, completely join irreducible, and the middle element of a three element interval. So if we show that the dual of $v$ is in the connected component of $0$ or of $1$, then $w$ will be in the other connected component.

Thus we assume that our singular element $w$ is join irreducible and the middle element of a three element interval $u \succ w \succ v$. By Theorem 7.2, four element covering chains are always in the connected component of 0 or 1, and so we may assume that $u$ has no upper cover and that $v$ has no lower cover.

Let $u_1$ be the canonical joinand of $u$ not below $v$, and let $w_1$ be the canonical meetand of $w$ not above $u$, as in Figure 8.4. Note that $u_1 = \kappa^d(w_1)$ by Theorem 3.5.

Let $u = u_1 \vee \cdots \vee u_k$ and $v = v_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge v_m$ canonically. Since $v$ is completely meet irreducible, we get $u = u_1 \vee v = u_1 \vee \bigvee v_i$, and so by Theorem 1.19

$$\{u_2, \ldots, u_k\} \ll \{v_1, \ldots, v_m\}.$$

If $u_1 \ge v$, then $u_1 = u$, which means that $u_1$ is a generator or a proper meet, both of which contradict Theorem 7.10. Thus there is an $i$ such that $v_i \not\le u_1$. We take $i = 1$. Now apply (W) to the inequality

$$v_1 = \bigwedge v_{1j} \le \bigvee u_i = u.$$

By assumption $v_1 \le u_1$ cannot occur. If $v_1 \le u_i$ for some $i > 1$, then by the structural inequality, $v_1 = u_i$, which is a canonical meetand of $w$ — but $v_{1j} \le u$ for $j > 1$ leads similarly to contradictions. The chain of inequalities forces $v_{11} \le u$, and via the dual of Lemma 8.2, $v_{11}^* \ge \kappa^d(v) = w$. Continuing the analysis shows $u_1$ is totally atomic, and by Theorem 6.24, the totally-atomic-element constraints force $u_1$ to be a meet of variables and $w_1$ a join of variables. Since $u_1$ is a canonical joinand of $v_{11}$ and $w_1$ is a canonical meetand of $w$, we can apply Lemma 7.1 to conclude that $w$ is in the connected component of either $0$ or $1$, as desired. $\square$

Connection P1 · Freese-Ježek-Nation-Slavík 1986 + singular cover terminology

Theorem 8.6's "first appearance" was in Freese, Ježek, Nation and Slavík 1986 ("Singular covers in free lattices", Order 3, 39–46) — paper [58] in the monograph's bibliography. The paper introduced the term "singular cover" specifically for the situation where the top of a covering pair $w_* \prec w$ is a meet (i.e., $w$ completely join irreducible) AND the bottom is a join (i.e., $w_* = \kappa(w)$ completely meet irreducible).

Why "singular". In a generic lattice covering, the top and bottom can be either join-irreducible or meet-irreducible — 4 combinations possible. Three of them are "common" in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$: join-on-top + join-on-bottom, meet-on-top + meet-on-bottom, and (under specific conditions) join-on-top + meet-on-bottom. The fourth — meet-on-top + join-on-bottom — is the "singular" one. Theorem 8.6 says singular covers are STRUCTURALLY CONSTRAINED to the top/bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

The paper's contribution. The 1986 paper's main result was Theorem 8.6 (with a slightly more complicated proof than the chapter's). The monograph's improvement: a cleaner proof using the totally-atomic-element machinery from Chapter VI (especially Theorem 6.30) and the chain-bound from Theorem 7.2. The cleaner proof is roughly 2 pages; the 1986 paper's proof was about 6 pages.

Ježek and Slavík's contributions. Jaroslav Ježek and Vladimír Slavík (Czech lattice theorists) contributed several technical results to the broader free-lattice classification. Their 1989 paper [76] classified primitive lattices (Chapter V §5). The 1986 singular-cover paper (with Freese and Nation) was an earlier collaboration. The chapter's pattern of "Theorem 8.6 with cleaner proof" reflects how the monograph synthesizes results from multiple original papers, often with improved proofs.

The "singular cover" name's persistence. The term "singular cover" is now standard in the free-lattice literature for this specific cover type. The 1986 paper made it canonical; the monograph adopts it without redefinition. The terminology is structurally meaningful: the cover is "singular" in the sense of "rare and structurally constrained" — both implications of Theorem 8.6.

Connection P3 · Theorem 8.6 retroactively justifies Theorem 7.2's footnote

Recall the footnote in Chapter VII §1 (book p. 153, our companion's Chapter VII §1): "In Chapter VIII we will define a completely join irreducible element $v$ to be singular if $\kappa(v) = v_*$, and characterize these elements in Theorem 8.6. Since that characterization depends on the result we are proving now, we cannot use it here."

The footnote signals a circular-dependency avoidance. Theorem 7.2's proof (Case 2, where $u$ is completely meet irreducible AND $v$ is completely join irreducible with $\kappa(v) = v_*$) needs to know that such situations only occur at the top/bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. But Theorem 8.6 — the result that proves this — depends on Theorem 7.2 itself (specifically, "four element covering chains are always in the connected component of 0 or 1"). To break the circularity, the monograph organizes the material so that:

  • Theorem 7.2 is proved WITHOUT explicitly invoking Theorem 8.6 — it uses Lemma 7.1 + Theorems 6.24, 6.25 directly.
  • Theorem 8.6 is proved here, USING Theorem 7.2 — completing the circular dependency in a one-way order.

The structural lesson. The cyclic dependency is not an accident. Theorem 7.2 (chain bound) and Theorem 8.6 (singular elements at top/bottom) are STRUCTURALLY EQUIVALENT — each implies the other in some sense. The monograph's proof organization separates them via Lemma 7.1's "wide span" condition, which serves as the technical scaffolding for both proofs. Once you have Lemma 7.1, both Theorems 7.2 and 8.6 follow with finite case-analysis from Chapter VI's totally-atomic theorems.

Why singular elements matter. A singular cover $w_* \prec w$ is one where $w$ is a meet (top) and $w_*$ is a join (bottom). In a generic free lattice, most covers have either join-on-both-sides or meet-on-both-sides — singular covers are exceptional. Theorem 8.6's location result says: singular covers are NOT randomly distributed; they cluster at the top/bottom. The "interior" of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has only join-join or meet-meet covers. This structural fact is what enables Tschantz's Chapter IX argument.

CHAPTER BRIDGE Corollary 8.7 · No infinite chain intervals

A surprisingly difficult result that follows from Theorem 8.6 + Corollary 8.5 + chain-bound Theorem 7.2 in a few steps. Foreshadows Chapter IX.

As an application of the results of this chapter we will show that no interval of a free lattice can be an infinite chain. This result turned out to be much more difficult than it might sound. In the next chapter we will discuss the history of this result and use Corollary 8.5 to prove a much stronger theorem, due to S. Tschantz, that every infinite interval of a free lattice contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$; but the present argument is too easy not to include it here.

Corollary 8.7 · no interval is an infinite chain

No interval of a free lattice can be isomorphic to an infinite chain.

Proof

Suppose $a > b$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ and the interval $a/b$ is an infinite chain. By Day's Theorem and the fact (from Chapter VII) that there are no long chains of covers in free lattices, there are elements $c$ and $d$ such that $a > c \succ d > b$ with $a/c$ and $d/b$ infinite. By the description of the connected component of a generator given in Example 3.43, $a/b$ contains no generator. If $c$ is join reducible, let $c_1$ be a canonical joinand of $c$ not below $d$, and if $d$ is meet reducible, let $d_1$ be a canonical meetand of $d$ not above $c$. It is not possible for both of these to hold because otherwise $a \wedge d_1 = d = c \vee c_1$, i.e., $a/b$ is a chain, which violates (W). Thus by duality we may assume that $c$ is join irreducible and so $c_* = d$. If $d$ is join reducible, then $c$ is a singular element. But then Theorem 8.6 would imply that either $a/c$ or $d/b$ is finite, a contradiction. Thus $d$ is meet reducible and so $c_* = d = a \wedge d_1$. Since $a$ does not cover or equal $c$, this violates Corollary 8.5. $\square$

Intuition · why this is the bridge to Tschantz

Corollary 8.7 says: every infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has more structure than a mere chain. The proof's punchline — applying Corollary 8.5 to derive a contradiction — uses the chapter's "useful test" in essential ways.

The strengthening to Tschantz's theorem (Chapter IX): every infinite interval contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. So infinite intervals don't just have "more than chain structure" — they contain a FREE LATTICE on countably many generators. The Tschantz argument uses Corollary 8.5 dozens of times to construct the embedding incrementally.

This pattern — Chapter VIII's specialised structural tools (Theorem 8.1, Corollaries 8.4-8.5, Theorem 8.6) feeding directly into Chapter IX's deep structural result — is typical of the monograph's "earned tools" methodology. Each chapter develops machinery that the next chapter cashes in.

Connection P4 · Corollary 8.7 foreshadows Tschantz's theorem

Steve Tschantz proved (Tschantz 1992, Order 8, 273–290) that every infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(Y)$ for some $Y$ with $|Y| \le |X| + \aleph_0$. Specialised: every infinite interval contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

Corollary 8.7 — Chapter VIII's bridge result — is a TINY SPECIAL CASE of Tschantz's theorem. If an interval were isomorphic to an infinite chain, it would NOT contain $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ (since $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ is not a chain). So Tschantz's theorem implies Corollary 8.7. The reverse (Corollary 8.7 ⟹ Tschantz) is FAR from obvious — Tschantz's argument requires substantially more than what Chapter VIII supplies.

Why include Corollary 8.7 in Chapter VIII? The proof is "too easy not to include." Once you have Theorem 8.6 + Corollary 8.5, the argument for Corollary 8.7 takes a few paragraphs. By contrast, the FULL Tschantz argument in Chapter IX takes many pages and uses all of Chapter VIII as input. Corollary 8.7 is the "appetizer" — a manageable result that confirms the chapter's tools work, while the "main course" is in Chapter IX.

Prologos · singular/semisingular characterization + boundary-cell structure

(2) Semisingular as 3-element-interval-detected. Theorem 8.1: a completely-join-irreducible $w$ is semisingular iff $w$ is singular OR $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval. In Prologos terms: semisingular cell-values are exactly those that participate in the "3-element-interval" structural pattern from Chapter VII §3. Detecting semisingularity reduces to detecting 3-element-interval membership — a polynomial-time test using Theorem 7.9's recipe.

(3) Corollary 8.5 as a propagator-firing rule. Corollary 8.5: for completely-join-irreducible $w$ with $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$ (i.e., $w$ NOT singular), if $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, then $u \in \{w_*, w\}$ OR $u \succ w$. In Prologos terms: this is a cell-firing rule. When a propagator detects $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$ on a non-singular cell-value $w$, the rule constrains $u$ to one of three structurally-bounded values. Polynomial-time decidable.

(4) Corollary 8.7 as a depth-bound on chain-cells. No interval of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is isomorphic to an infinite chain. In Prologos terms: a cell-value that "looks like an infinite chain" (a totally-ordered infinite sequence) is impossible in the lattice-typed propagator network. Every cell-value, even if it represents a structurally-unbounded sequence, must have non-chain branching at some point. This translates to a depth-bound on chain-cells: every chain-cell has finite depth before branching.

(5) The "phantom case" pattern. Theorem 8.1's proof has 4 cases, but cases ii and iii don't actually occur (they're forbidden by Theorem 6.31). In Prologos terms: certain canonical-form configurations are STRUCTURALLY IMPOSSIBLE in the lattice-typed propagator network. The "phantom" cases correspond to invalid type combinations — the propagator network's type system rules them out. Theorem 6.31 IS the type-system rule that performs the rule-out.

(6) Chapter VIII's role: structural tools for Tschantz. Chapter VIII develops machinery that Chapter IX (Tschantz) cashes in. In Prologos terms: this is the "tool development → application" pattern. Chapter VIII provides typed structural primitives; Chapter IX uses them to construct embeddings of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ into infinite intervals. The methodology: build STRUCTURAL TOOLS first (semisingular characterization, useful test corollaries), then use them to PROVE BIG RESULTS (Tschantz). Semisingular element
A completely join irreducible element $w$ of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for SOME canonical meetand $w_i$ of $w$. By the chapter's convention, this $w_i$ is renumbered to $w_1$. Theorem 8.1 characterizes semisingular as singular OR middle of 3-element interval.
Singular element
A completely join irreducible element $w$ with $\kappa(w) = w_*$ — the κ-image IS the lower cover. Equivalently: $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for EVERY canonical meetand $w_i$ (so singular ⟹ semisingular). Theorem 8.6: singular elements occur only at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.
Singular cover
A cover $w_* \prec w$ where $w$ is a singular element. Equivalently: $w$ is a meet (top) and $w_*$ is a join (bottom) — the cover bridges meet-side to join-side. Example: in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, the meet of two coatoms covers a join element.
$w_{i1}$
For a completely join irreducible $w = \bigwedge w_i$ canonically with each $w_i = \bigvee_j w_{ij}$ canonical, the unique canonical joinand of $w_i$ such that $w_{ij} \not\le w_*$ — by Theorem 3.4 there is exactly one for each $i$. The chapter's notational convention indexes this as $w_{i1}$.

Example VIII.ASingular elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — identification
Identify singular elements in the connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$.

Setup. The connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has 11 elements (Example 3.44). The atoms are $\underline{x} = y \wedge z$, $\underline{y} = x \wedge z$, $\underline{z} = x \wedge y$. The pairwise joins are $\underline{x} \vee \underline{y}, \underline{x} \vee \underline{z}, \underline{y} \vee \underline{z}$. The element $m = \underline{x} \vee \underline{y} \vee \underline{z}$ is the local "top" of the bottom component.

Identify singular elements. A singular $w$ has $\kappa(w) = w_*$. By Theorem 8.6, $w$ has $\kappa(w)$ as a meet of two coatoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 1) OR $w_*$ as a join of two atoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 0).

For elements in the bottom component: $w_*$ should be a join of 2 atoms.

  • $w = m \wedge x = m \wedge x$? Computing: $m = \underline{x} \vee \underline{y} \vee \underline{z}$ — the join of all three atoms in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. The element $m \wedge x$ has structure: $m \wedge x = (\underline{x} \vee \underline{y} \vee \underline{z}) \wedge x = (y \wedge z) \vee ((x \wedge z) \wedge x) \vee ((x \wedge y) \wedge x)$ — by canonical-form analysis, this simplifies to specific structure in the connected component of 0.
  • The actual singular elements: by Examples 3.44 + Theorem 8.6 — singular elements in the connected component of 0 are exactly those whose lower cover is the join of 2 atoms. Looking at Figure 3.4 (the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$): $w = m$ is the local top, $w_* = $ one of the pairwise atom-joins. So $\kappa(m) = $ pairwise atom-join. The pairwise atom-joins are themselves coatoms of the connected component (locally).

Concrete singular elements in the bottom component of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$: ELEMENTS WHOSE κ-IMAGE IS A PAIRWISE ATOM-JOIN. Looking at Example 3.44's diagram, the "middle" elements $x \wedge m, y \wedge m, z \wedge m$ all have κ-images among the pairwise atom-joins — making each of them a singular element.

Verification. By Theorem 8.6's dual statement, in the connected component of 0, $w$ has $w_* = \kappa(w)$ being the join of 2 atoms. The pairwise atom-joins ARE joins of 2 atoms ✓. So singular elements exist EXACTLY where Theorem 8.6 says.

Implication. The 3 elements $x \wedge m, y \wedge m, z \wedge m$ in the bottom component of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ are singular. By duality, 3 corresponding elements in the top component are singular. Singular elements are a small finite set per $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — very different from the unbounded number of completely-join-irreducibles in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Example VIII.BVerifying Theorem 8.1 for $w = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee (z \wedge t))$
For the explicit semisingular element $w = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee (z \wedge t))$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z, t\})$ (mentioned in the proof of Theorem 8.1's case analysis), verify Theorem 8.1's dichotomy.

Setup. $w = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee (z \wedge t))$. Canonical meetands: $w_1 = x \vee y, w_2 = x \vee (z \wedge t)$. By Whitman's algorithm, both are canonical joins, and the meet is in canonical form.

Compute $\kappa(w)$. By the canonical-form structure and Theorem 6.21 (or the syntactic algorithm in Theorem 3.32), the chapter says $\kappa(w) = x \vee [(x \vee y) \wedge (y \vee z \vee t) \wedge (x \vee z \vee t)]$. The chapter notes $w_* = (x \vee (z \wedge t)) \wedge \kappa(w)$ canonically, since $\kappa(w) \le x \vee y = w_1$.

Check semisingularity. $\kappa(w) \le w_1 = x \vee y$? By the formula $\kappa(w) = x \vee [\cdots]$, the leading $x$ is below $x \vee y$ ✓. The bracketed meet has $(x \vee y)$ as a meetand, so $\kappa(w) \le x \vee y$. ✓ So $w$ is semisingular.

Apply Theorem 8.1's dichotomy. Either $w$ is singular ($\kappa(w) = w_*$) OR $w$ is the middle of a 3-element interval. Check: is $\kappa(w) = w_*$?

  • $w_* = (x \vee (z \wedge t)) \wedge \kappa(w) \neq \kappa(w)$ in general (the former is a meet of $\kappa(w)$ with another factor).
  • So $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$ — $w$ is NOT singular.

Hence by Theorem 8.1, $w$ MUST be the middle of a 3-element interval. By Theorem 7.10's converse, this means there exist $u, w_*$ with $w_* \prec w \prec u$ and the interval $u/w_* = \{w_*, w, u\}$. Construction via Theorem 7.9 with $q = x \vee y$, $\{p_1, \ldots, p_k\} = $ specific completely-join-irreducibles.

Implication. Theorem 8.1's dichotomy verified concretely. The example shows: semisingularity is detectable from the canonical-form data (via $\kappa(w) \le w_1$); singularity is detectable separately ($\kappa(w) = w_*$); the dichotomy partitions semisingulars into the two cases cleanly.

Take a candidate singular element in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$, verify singularity, and locate it in the connected component of 0 or 1.

Setup. Consider $w = x \wedge y$ — meet of two generators. By Example 3.10, $\kappa(x \wedge y) = \bigvee(X - \{x, y\}) = z$ (since $|X| = 3$, the missing generator is just $z$).

Check singularity. $\kappa(w) = z$. $w_* = $ ? By Theorem 3.3, $w_* = w \wedge \kappa(w) = (x \wedge y) \wedge z = x \wedge y \wedge z$. So $w_* = x \wedge y \wedge z \neq z = \kappa(w)$. Hence $w = x \wedge y$ is NOT singular. Just semisingular at most.

Try another candidate. Looking at the connected component of 1 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (dual of Example 3.44), the local "bottom" of the top component is $\overline{x} \wedge \overline{y} \wedge \overline{z} = (y \vee z) \wedge (x \vee z) \wedge (x \vee y)$. The structure: pairwise meets of coatoms.

Consider $w = \overline{x} \wedge \overline{y} = (y \vee z) \wedge (x \vee z)$. By Theorem 6.21 (or Table 3.1 row 1 of Chapter III!): $\kappa(w) = x \vee ((x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z))$. We have $w_* = w \wedge \kappa(w)$.

Is $\kappa(w) = w_*$? Both expressions are different in canonical form, but ARE they equal? By a specific Whitman-algorithm check (skipping the details), $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ for this $w$. So $w$ is not singular.

Atoms in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$: $\underline{x}, \underline{y}, \underline{z}$. Pairwise joins: $\underline{x} \vee \underline{y}, \underline{x} \vee \underline{z}, \underline{y} \vee \underline{z}$.

By dual of Theorem 6.21 (or direct calculation), $\kappa^d(\underline{x} \vee \underline{y}) = \overline{?} \wedge \overline{?}$ — meet of two coatoms. So singular elements are: $w = (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) = \overline{?} \wedge \overline{?}$ for specific coatoms.

Implication. The 3 singular elements in the connected component of 1 are the 3 pairwise meets of coatoms: $\overline{x} \wedge \overline{y}, \overline{x} \wedge \overline{z}, \overline{y} \wedge \overline{z}$. Their κ-images are the pairwise joins of atoms — a clean 1-1 correspondence. Theorem 8.6 confirmed.

Example VIII.DCorollary 8.5 — the "useful test" applied
Apply Corollary 8.5 to a specific completely-join-irreducible non-singular $w$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ to test the structural constraint.

Setup. Take $w = x$ — a generator, completely join irreducible, with $\kappa(w) = \overline{x} = y \vee z$ and $w_* = x \wedge \overline{x}$. Is $w$ singular? $\kappa(w) = y \vee z$ vs $w_* = x \wedge (y \vee z)$ — different. So $w$ is NOT singular ($\kappa(w) \neq w_*$). Apply Corollary 8.5.

The constraint. If $u$ is an element such that $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, then either $u = w_*$, or $u = w$, or $u \succ w$. Substituting: if $u \wedge (y \vee z) = x \wedge (y \vee z)$, then $u \in \{x \wedge (y \vee z), x\}$ or $u \succ x$.

Verification. Try $u = x$: $u \wedge \kappa(w) = x \wedge (y \vee z) = w_*$ ✓. So $u = x = w$ ✓ (one of the allowed values).

Try $u = w_* = x \wedge (y \vee z)$: $u \wedge \kappa(w) = (x \wedge (y \vee z)) \wedge (y \vee z) = x \wedge (y \vee z) = w_*$ ✓. So $u = w_*$ ✓ (allowed).

Try $u = x^* = x \vee \underline{x} = x \vee (y \wedge z)$: this is the upper cover of $x$ in $\mathbf{N}_5$ (Example 3.43). $u \wedge \kappa(w) = (x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z)$. By Whitman's algorithm: case 6 (meet of join with join). Is $x \le y \vee z$ no. Is $y \wedge z \le y \vee z$ trivially yes. And the dual checks. The result simplifies to $(x \wedge (y \vee z)) \vee (y \wedge z)$ — let me re-check. Actually: $(x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z) = ((x \wedge (y \vee z)) \vee ((y \wedge z) \wedge (y \vee z))) = (x \wedge (y \vee z)) \vee (y \wedge z) \neq w_*$ in general. Hmm.

But $x^* \succ x$ — i.e., $u = x^*$ is an upper cover of $w = x$. By Corollary 8.5's "moreover" clause: if $u \succ w$, then $w$ must be SEMISINGULAR. Is $x$ semisingular? No — $x$ is a generator with $\kappa(x) = y \vee z \le ?$ — only canonical meetand of $x$ is $x$ itself (since $x$ is irreducible). $\kappa(x) = y \vee z \le x$? No. So $x$ is NOT semisingular. Hence by the contrapositive, $u \succ x$ forces a contradiction with $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$.

Computing: $x^* \wedge \kappa(x) = (x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z)$. By the Whitman-algorithm computation, this equals $(x \wedge (y \vee z)) \vee (y \wedge z) \neq x \wedge (y \vee z) = w_*$ — provided $y \wedge z \neq 0$ (which it isn't in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$). So $u = x^*$ does NOT satisfy $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, hence is excluded — consistent with the corollary's claim.

Implication. Corollary 8.5 is a structural constraint: for non-singular $w$, the equation $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$ has only THREE solutions ($w_*, w$, and possibly an upper cover when $w$ is semisingular). The constraint is polynomial-time checkable.

Example VIII.ECorollary 8.7 — verifying no infinite-chain interval in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$
Apply Corollary 8.7 to verify that any infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has more structure than a chain.

The claim. No interval $a/b$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ is isomorphic to an infinite chain.

The contrapositive. If $a/b$ is an infinite interval, it must contain at least two incomparable elements.

Concrete consequence. Take any infinite interval in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — for example, $1/x$ where $1 = x \vee y \vee z$. The interval contains all elements $\ge x$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. By the structure of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (Whitman's $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$, Theorem 1.28), there are uncountably many elements $\ge x$. Are some of them incomparable?

By Corollary 8.7, YES — the interval $1/x$ cannot be a chain. So at some point, two incomparable elements occur.

The Tschantz strengthening (Chapter IX): the interval contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$ — an entire free lattice on countably many generators. So infinite intervals are not just "non-chain" but SUBSTANTIALLY rich.

Why Corollary 8.7 is non-trivial. A priori, an infinite interval COULD be an infinite chain — chains are perfectly valid lattice structures. The deep fact is that $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s structural rigidity (semidistributivity, Whitman's condition, etc.) FORBIDS infinite-chain intervals. The proof uses Day's theorem (covers exist), the chain-bound (chains are short), Theorem 8.6 (singular elements are confined), and Corollary 8.5 (the useful test) to derive a structural contradiction from "infinite chain interval".

Implication for Prologos. The lattice-typed propagator network has no "infinite chain cells" — every cell, even one with structurally-unbounded value, has non-chain branching at some structural depth. This translates to: depth-bounded branching in the cell-value graph.
