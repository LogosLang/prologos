What this is. A pedagogical companion to J. B. Nation's Notes on Lattice Theory, published as free PDF lecture notes by the University of Hawaii. Each chapter of Nation's notes gets a companion HTML file that preserves the book's numbering (Theorem 1.1, Lemma 1.2, Theorem 1.4, etc.) so you can hold the notes open beside it and cross-reference. Where Nation is terse, the companion expands with intuition, diagrams, and worked-out reasoning. Where Nation is concrete, the companion connects outward — to combinatorics (Dilworth's theorem), logic (compactness theorem), order theory at large, and the Prologos propagator network.

How to use it. Read Nation's notes first. When stuck on a definition or proof, switch to the companion's matching section for prose, intuition, and a fuller reading. End-of-chapter exercises are worked in collapsible blocks — open them after attempting the exercise yourself.

Sister companion. The Călugăreanu Companion covers Călugăreanu's Lattice Concepts of Module Theory (Springer 2000) — a parallel volume going deeper into the module-theoretic side of lattice theory (radicals, supplemented, Goldie / Krull / Gabriel dimensions). The two books cover overlapping foundational vocabulary but diverge sharply in scope: Nation goes broader (free lattices, varieties, geometric lattices); Călugăreanu goes deeper into modules. Reading both companions gives the full landscape.

Ordered Sets

Posets, Hasse diagrams, the Cayley-style representation theorem, Dilworth's theorem, Axiom of Choice equivalents, the compactness theorem, and the three invariants $w, c, d$ on ordered sets.

Nation's foundational chapter. Posets defined via reflexivity, antisymmetry, transitivity. Examples: power set $\mathfrak{P}(X)$, subgroup lattice $\mathbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$, the reals, chains, antichains. Cover relation $\prec$, Hasse diagrams, order-preserving maps, isomorphism. Theorem 1.1 — every ordered set $\mathcal{Q}$ embeds in $\mathfrak{P}(\mathcal{Q})$ via $\phi(x) = \{y : y \le x\}$ (the Cayley analogue for orders). Order ideals $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$, dual $\mathcal{P}^d$, principal $\downarrow x$, $\uparrow x$. Maximum vs maximal. Lemma 1.2 (3-way DCC). Lemma 1.3 (induction principle for DCC). Three invariants: width $w(\mathcal{P})$, chain covering $c(\mathcal{P})$, dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$. Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth, 1950): $w(\mathcal{P}) = c(\mathcal{P})$ in finite case. Proof in finite case (Tverberg-Freese); infinite case via compactness. Theorem 1.5: AoC ⟺ Zermelo well-ordering ⟺ Hausdorff maximality ⟺ Zorn's Lemma ⟺ LUB-chain → maximal. Theorem 1.6 (Gödel compactness theorem): a set of WFFs is satisfiable iff every finite subset is. Theorem 1.7 (Szpilrajn, 1930): every partial order is the intersection of total orders. Theorem 1.9: $d(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$, with $d(\mathcal{P}) = $ smallest $\kappa$ such that $\mathcal{P}$ embeds in product of $\kappa$ chains. Connection panels: Dilworth in combinatorics · Cayley-style representation theorems · the compactness theorem from logic + Jónsson's method · order dimension in scheduling · Prologos.

Semilattices, Lattices and Complete Lattices

Where the lattice operations enter. Semilattices as idempotent commutative semigroups; lattices via algebra ↔ order; the trinity of closure systems / operators / rules; the order-ideal and MacNeille completions; the Knaster-Tarski-Davis fixed-point theorem.

Five movements through Nation's foundational chapter on the algebraic side of order theory. (A) Semilattices. Theorem 2.1 (algebra ↔ poset with all glbs); Theorem 2.2 (Cayley-style: every meet semilattice embeds in $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S})$). (B) Lattices. Theorem 2.3 (algebra ↔ poset with both glbs and lubs); $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$ as obstructions to power-set embedding; Theorem 2.4 (finite test: top + all meets ⟹ lattice); Theorem 2.5 (complete meet semilattices are complete lattices). (C) The closure trinity. Theorem 2.6 (three-way correspondence: closure systems = closure operators = closure rules); Theorem 2.7 (every complete lattice IS a closure system on itself). (D) Join irreducibles. Lemma 2.8 (DCC ⟹ each element is a finite join of join irreducibles); Theorem 2.9 (ACC + DCC lattices ≅ closure-rule lattice on $J(\mathcal{L})$). (E) Completions. Order-ideal completion $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$, MacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P}) = (A^u)^\ell$ (the Dedekind-cut generalisation, MacNeille 1937); Theorem 2.10 (join-dense completions ↔ closure operators on $P$); Theorem 2.12 ($\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ has $\mathcal{O}$ at bottom, $\mathcal{M}$ at top); Theorem 2.13 (Knaster-Tarski-Davis): a lattice is complete ⟺ every order-preserving self-map has a fixed point. Algebraic Lattices

The algebraic closure operator (closure determined by finite witnesses), the algebraic (= compactly generated) lattice as the unifying structural object, and the Birkhoff-Frink theorem (1948) certifying that algebraic lattices ARE the subalgebra lattices of universal algebras.

Five conceptual movements. (A) Algebraic closure operators. $\Gamma$ is algebraic if $\Gamma(B) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F$ finite$\}$. Six examples (subalgebras, congruences, order ideals, ideals of $\vee$-semilattices with/without 0, ideals of lattices); two NON-examples (topology and MacNeille). Theorem 3.1 (3-way: algebraic ⟺ up-directed unions closed ⟺ chain unions closed). (B) Algebraic lattices. Compact element $x$ ($x \le \bigvee A$ ⟹ $x \le \bigvee F$ finite); $\mathcal{L}^c$ as join semilattice with 0; algebraic = compactly generated. Theorem 3.2 (closure operator algebraic ⟺ generated by finitary rules). Theorem 3.3 ($\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$ — the canonical ideal-lattice representation). (C) Birkhoff-Frink. Theorem 3.4 (1948): every algebraic closure operator $\Gamma$ on $X$ is realised by a universal algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on $X$. Corollary: every algebraic lattice IS a subalgebra lattice. Theorem 3.5 (Bill Hanf): countable compact-counts ⟺ countable algebra ⟺ algebra with one binary operation. Plus Grätzer-Schmidt + Lampe sister theorems for congruence lattices. (D) Structural properties. Theorem 3.6 (algebraic ⟹ weakly atomic). Theorem 3.7 (algebraic ⟹ upper continuous). Theorem 3.8 (3 forms of upper continuity). (E) Decompositions. $M^*(\mathcal{L})$ — completely meet irreducibles. Theorem 3.9 (3-way characterisation). Theorem 3.10 (Birkhoff: $M^*$ meet dense in algebraic). Theorem 3.11 (Crawley: strongly atomic algebraic ⟹ irredundant decompositions exist). Theorem 3.12 (distributive ⟹ unique). Connection panels: Birkhoff-Frink history · compactness in lattice/topology/logic/domain theory · subalgebra lattices and varieties · Crawley-Dilworth decomposition theory · Prologos.

Representation by Equivalence Relations

The Cayley analogue for lattices: every lattice embeds in the partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for some set $X$ (Whitman 1946; simplified by Jónsson 1953). The proof is a transfinite recursion. Then climbs to modular ⟺ type 2 representation, Arguesian ← type 1.

Three conceptual movements. (A) Equivalence relations + Eq X. Equivalence relations as binary relations satisfying R, S, T. Kernel of a function $f$. Congruence relation. $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ as algebraic, strongly atomic, semimodular, relatively complemented, simple lattice. Relative product $R \circ S$. Three types of representations: type 1 ($F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y)$), type 2 ($F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x)$), type 3 ($F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x) \circ F(y)$). (B) Whitman's theorem. Theorem 4.1 (Whitman 1946 / Jónsson 1953): every lattice has a type 3 representation. Proof via weak representation + Sublemma 1 (extension by 3 new points) + Sublemma 2 (limit-ordinal continuity) + $\omega$-iterated fix-up. Theorem 4.2 (Pudlák-Tůma 1980): every finite lattice has a finite representation — a celebrated 20th-century result. Theorem 4.3: every lattice embeds in $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$ for some group. (C) Modular and Arguesian. Lemma 4.4: type 2 ⟹ modular law (Dedekind 1890s). Theorem 4.5 (Jónsson): lattice has type 2 representation ⟺ modular. Arguesian law $(A)$: $(a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \le a_2 \vee b_2 \Rightarrow c_2 \le c_0 \vee c_1$ — modeled after Desargues' Law (Schützenberger 1945). Theorem 4.6: type 1 ⟹ Arguesian. Examples (subgroups, abelian, ideals, vector spaces, modules). Haiman 1987/1991: Arguesian ⟹ type 1 FAILS. Pálfy-Szabó 1995: subgroup lattices of abelian groups satisfy a STRICTLY STRONGER identity. Connection panels: Cayley-style theorem lineage · Dedekind's modular law + module theory · Arguesian / Desargues / projective geometry · Pálfy-Szabó + variety theory · Prologos.

Congruence Relations

From arbitrary equivalence relations to congruences: equivalence relations preserved by ALL algebra operations. The quotient $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ inherits the algebraic structure. The lattice $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is always algebraic AND distributive (Funayama–Nakayama 1942).

Four conceptual movements. (A) Quotients + congruences. Theorem 5.1 (First Iso): $\mathcal{L}/\ker f \cong f(\mathcal{L})$. Theorem 5.2: the quotient $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ is a lattice and the natural map is a homomorphism. (B) Con L + subdirect representation. $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L} \subseteq \textbf{Eq}\,L$ is a complete sublattice. Theorem 5.3: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic — finitely generated congruences are compact. Principal congruence $\text{con}(a,b)$. Theorem 5.4 (Second Iso): correspondence between congruences on $\mathcal{L}/\theta$ and congruences on $\mathcal{L}$ above $\theta$. Subdirect product, subdirectly irreducible (SI), monolith $\mu$ as smallest non-zero congruence. Theorem 5.5–5.7 (Birkhoff 1944): every algebra is a subdirect product of subdirectly irreducible quotients. (C) Distributivity. Theorem 5.8 (Funayama–Nakayama 1942): $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is DISTRIBUTIVE for every lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Proof via Pixley's majority polynomial $m(x,y,z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$ and the Mal'cev-style 3-permutability. The congruence lattice problem (Dilworth: which distributive algebraic lattices arise as $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$?). Wehrung 2007 + Růžička 2008: counterexample of cardinality $\aleph_2$. (D) Weaving polynomials + completely join prime. Theorem 5.9: characterisation of $\text{con}(a,b)$ via weaving polynomials (alternating $\vee$ and $\wedge$ chains). Theorems 5.10–5.12: in finite distributive lattices, completely join prime = join prime = join irreducible.

Free Lattices

From abstract universal-algebra construction to concrete combinatorial structure: the free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$, Whitman's algorithm for the word problem, and the surprising appearance of the semidistributive laws — all coordinated by Day's 1970 doubling construction.

Four conceptual movements. (A) Generators, free generation, existence. Definition of free generation by $X$ (conditions I/II/III). Theorem 6.1: existence of $\text{FL}(X)$ via three "Basic Principles" (universal-algebra forms of Theorems 5.1, 5.4, 5.5). Word algebra $W(X)$, $\Lambda$, $\lambda$. Proof works for any nontrivial variety (Birkhoff 1935 HSP). (B) Whitman's solution. Theorem 6.2 (Whitman 1941): six conditions for $\text{FL}(X)$, including $(W) : p_1 \wedge p_2 \le q_1 \vee q_2 \iff$ four trivial reasons. Skolem 1920 priority (rediscovered by Cosmadakis-Freese late 1980s, Burris 1995). Alan Day's 1970 doubling construction $\mathcal{L}[I]$: slick proof of $(W)$. Word-problem algorithm (polynomial-time per Skolem; exponential per Whitman). (C) Canonical form. Theorem 6.3: characterisation of free generation. Lemmas 6.4–6.6: minimal-length terms + refinement quasiorder $\ll$. Theorem 6.7: canonical form unique up to AC. Canonical joinands / meetands. (D) Structural consequences. Theorem 6.8 (Jónsson 1961): $\text{FL}(X)$ satisfies $\text{SD}_\vee$ and $\text{SD}_\wedge$. Theorem 6.9 (Nation 1982): a finite lattice embeds in a free lattice $\iff$ it satisfies $(W) + \text{SD}_\vee + \text{SD}_\wedge$ — confirming Jónsson's conjecture. Theorem 6.10: every countable poset embeds in $\text{FL}(3)$, but no uncountable chain embeds in any free lattice. Theorem 6.11: $\text{FL}(X)$ weakly atomic for finite $X$. Reference: Freese-Ježek-Nation, Free Lattices, AMS Surveys & Monographs 42 (1995).

Varieties of Lattices

From a single free lattice to the lattice $\Lambda$ of all lattice varieties — and the model-theoretic apparatus (filters, ultrafilters, ultraproducts, Łoś's theorem 1955, Jónsson's lemma 1967) that makes its structure theory tractable.

Four conceptual movements. (A) Equations + varieties. Lattice equation $p \approx q$, variety / equational class, the standard sub-varieties $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{M}, \mathbf{D}, \mathbf{T}$. $\mathbf{K}$-free generation generalising Ch 6. Theorem 7.1 (Birkhoff HSP, 1935): variety $\iff$ closed under H, S, P $\iff$ admits relatively-free objects + closed under H. The disappointing footnote: word problem for $\mathcal{F}_M(5)$ unsolvable (Freese 1980), $\mathcal{F}_M(4)$ also (Herrmann 1982) — $\mathbf{M}$ is not generated by its finite members. (B) Fully invariant congruences + $\Lambda$. Endomorphism semigroup $\textbf{End}\,\mathcal{L}$, fully invariant congruence. Theorem 7.2: $\text{FL}(X)/\theta$ relatively free $\iff$ $\theta$ fully invariant. Theorem 7.3: $\Lambda$ dually isomorphic to fully invariant congruences on $\text{FL}(\omega)$; dually algebraic; finitely-based varieties = dually compact. HSP closure operator. Lemma 7.4: varieties determined by their SIs. (C) Filters, ultrafilters, ultraproducts. Filter, principal/nonprincipal, ultrafilter. Lemma 7.5 (5 properties: Zorn extension, ultrafilter characterisation, complement is maximal ideal, finite-cover principle, nonprincipal ⟺ contains cofinite). Reduced product, ultraproduct $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U$. First-order language for lattices: alphabet, equations, AND/OR/NOT, $\forall \exists$, wffs, $(\mathcal{L}, h) \models \varphi$. First-order: SD, $(W)$, width 7, $\le 7$ elements, $\cong \mathcal{M}_5$. Not first-order: finite, ACC, finite width, subdirectly irreducible. Theorem 7.6 (Łoś 1955): $\prod \mathcal{L}_i / \equiv_U \models \varphi$ $\iff$ $\{i : \mathcal{L}_i \models \varphi\} \in U$. (D) Jónsson + finite basis. Theorem 7.7 (Jónsson's Lemma 1967, Math. Scand. 21): SI in $\text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$ $\Rightarrow$ SI in $\text{HSP}_u(\mathbf{K})$. Crucial use of Funayama-Nakayama (Ch 5). Lemma 7.8 (filter restriction). Theorem 7.9: SI in HSP of finite collection of finite lattices is in HS of one of them. Theorem 7.10 (McKenzie 1970): variety generated by a finite lattice is finitely based. Generalisation lineage: Oates-Powell 1964 (groups), Kruse 1973 (rings), Baker 1977 (any congruence-distributive variety). Theorem 7.11: $(\mathbf{V} \vee \mathbf{W})_{si} = \mathbf{V}_{si} \cup \mathbf{W}_{si}$. Corollary: $\Lambda$ distributive. Theorem 7.12 (Baker-Hales 1974): $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}) \in \text{HSP}_u(\mathcal{L})$.

Distributive Lattices

The most tractable lattice variety: a single subdirectly irreducible $\mathbf{2}$, the unique-up-to-isomorphism set-representation, finite Birkhoff duality with posets, free distributive lattices and Dedekind's problem (9th value computed in April 2023!), Stone's representation of Boolean algebras (1936), and Dilworth's stunning 1945 disproof of Huntington's 65-year conjecture on uniquely complemented lattices.

Four conceptual movements. (A) Local finiteness + forms of distributivity. Theorem 8.1 (HSP of finite is locally finite, with cardinality bound $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(n)| \le |L|^{|L|^n}$). Lemma 8.2: three equivalent forms of distributivity, including the self-dual median identity $(x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$. Lemma 8.3: surjective $h : \mathcal{L} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{2}$ ↔ ideal/filter partition. (B) Birkhoff Prime Ideal Theorem. Prime ideal/filter; complement of prime ideal = prime filter. Theorem 8.4 (Birkhoff Prime Ideal Theorem): in distributive D, $a \not\le b$ ⟹ exists prime filter separating them. Cascade of corollaries: $\mathbf{2}$ is the only SI distributive lattice; $\mathbf{D} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{2})$; $\mathbf{D}$ is locally finite. (C) Set representation + finite Birkhoff. Theorem 8.5 (every distributive lattice embeds in $\mathfrak{P}(S)$). Join prime element. Theorem 8.6 (Birkhoff 1933 finite representation): finite $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$ — finite distributive lattices ⟺ finite posets. Free distributive lattice $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)$ as the order ideals of antichain-poset on subsets. Dedekind's problem: counting $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)|$. Sequence: 2, 3, 6, 20, 168, 7581, 7M, 2.4T, 56 sextillion, ... $M(9)$ computed in April 2023 by Christian Jäkel (TU Dresden, 5311 GPU-hours) AND independently by Van Hirtum, De Causmaecker et al. (KU Leuven/Paderborn, FPGAs). $M(10)$ remains computationally infeasible. Kleitman's asymptotic: $\log_2 M(n) \sim \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$. (D) Boolean + Stone + uniquely complemented. Theorem 8.7 (Tarski-Papert, 1959): complete embedding into $\mathcal{P}(X)$ ⟺ completely-join-prime elements separate. Boolean algebra = complemented distributive. Free Boolean $\text{FBA}(X) = \mathfrak{P}(\mathfrak{P}(X))$ for finite $X$; $\text{FBA}(\aleph_0)$ is the unique countable atomless Boolean algebra. Theorem 8.8 (Stone Representation, 1936): every Boolean algebra $\cong$ clopen subsets of compact totally disconnected Hausdorff space. Sublemmas A and B. Priestley duality (1970) generalises to all distributive lattices via ordered Stone spaces; Esakia duality (1974) for Heyting. Peirce 1880 + Huntington 1904 conjecture. Theorem 8.9 (Birkhoff-Ward 1939): complete + atomic + uniquely complemented ⟹ Boolean. Dilworth 1945 disproof: every lattice embeds in a uniquely complemented one — Huntington's conjecture is dramatically false.

Modular and Semimodular Lattices

From Dedekind's 1900 modular law for normal subgroup lattices to the lattice-theoretic Jordan-Hölder theorem (Wielandt 1939, Grätzer-Nation 2011, Czedli-Schmidt 2011), with semimodularity, projectivity, the Kurosh-Ore replacement theorem, and Dilworth's 1954 covering bijection along the way.

Four conceptual movements. (A) Forbidden sublattices. Theorem 9.1 (Dedekind 1900): a lattice is modular iff it does not contain $\mathcal{N}_5$ (the pentagon) as a sublattice. Theorem 9.2 (Birkhoff): a modular lattice is distributive iff it does not contain $\mathcal{M}_3$ (the diamond). Corollary: distributive iff neither $\mathcal{N}_5$ nor $\mathcal{M}_3$ — the most-cited structural theorem of lattice theory. Bottom of variety lattice $\Lambda$: $\mathbf{T} \prec \mathbf{D} \prec \{\text{HSP}(\mathcal{N}_5), \text{HSP}(\mathcal{M}_3)\}$. Grätzer-Jónsson 1966-68: $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{M}_3)$ has 2 additional covers. Jónsson-Rival 1979 (Pacific J. Math. 82): $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{N}_5)$ has EXACTLY 15 other covers. Nation 1996: counter-example to the finite height conjecture beyond. (B) Dedekind's transposition + semimodularity. Natural maps $\mu_a, \nu_b$ between transposed intervals. Theorem 9.3 (Dedekind): in modular lattice, mutually inverse isomorphisms; $(a \vee b)/b \cong a/(a \wedge b)$. Corollary: $a \succ a \wedge b$ iff $a \vee b \succ b$. Definition of (upper) semimodular and lower semimodular. Modular = both (with strong atomicity, Crawley-Dilworth). Theorem 9.4 (Dedekind 1900 generalisation): finite maximal chain ⟹ all chains finite, all maximal chains same length. Dimension function $\delta$. Theorem 9.5 (semimodular: 4 properties); Theorem 9.6 (modular case: equality $\delta(x \vee y) + \delta(x \wedge y) = \delta(x) + \delta(y)$). Quotient transposition + projectivity. Theorem 9.7 (Grätzer-Nation 2011, Algebra Universalis 64): two maximal chains have prime intervals projective in two steps with permutation. (C) Group-theoretic applications. Chief series in $\mathcal{N}(\mathcal{G})$. Subnormal subgroups $H \triangleleft\triangleleft \mathcal{G}$. Theorem 9.8 (Wielandt 1939, Math. Zeit. 45): $\mathcal{SN}(\mathcal{G})$ is a lower semimodular sublattice of $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$. Composition series. Corollary: Jordan-Hölder theorem via duality of Theorem 9.7. Historical lineage: Jordan 1870 → Hölder 1889 → Dedekind 1900 → Schreier 1928 → Zassenhaus 1934 → Wielandt 1939 → Grätzer-Nation 2011 → Czedli-Schmidt 2011 (uniqueness via projectivity bijection). Stern 1999 monograph. (D) Decompositions + Dilworth's bijection. Theorem 9.9 (Kurosh-Ore Replacement Theorem): in modular lattices, two finite irredundant decompositions of $a$ have the same cardinality with replacement. Theorem 9.10 (simultaneous exchange — Crawley-Dilworth). Theorem 9.11 (algebraic modular case). Application to congruence-modular algebras. Theorem 9.12 (Dilworth 1954, Ann. of Math. 60): in finite modular lattice, $|J_k(\mathcal{L})| = |M_k(\mathcal{L})|$ for every $k$. Kung-Reuter bijection: explicit $m : J(\mathcal{L}) \cup \{0\} \to M(\mathcal{L}) \cup \{1\}$ with $x \le m(x)$.

Finite Lattices and their Congruence Lattices

A closure-operator representation theory for finite (and "principally chain finite") lattices, recovering $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ from the join-irreducibles modulo the $\underline{D}$ relation, and answering Dilworth's congruence lattice problem in the finite case (Grätzer-Schmidt 1962) — recapping Wehrung 2007's negative answer for $\aleph_2$ and beyond.

Four conceptual movements. (A) PCF + closure operator $\Gamma$. Principally chain finite (PCF) lattices: every $\downarrow x$ satisfies ACC + DCC. The $\underline{D}$ relation on $J(\mathcal{L})$: $p \,\underline{D}\, q$ iff $\exists x$ with $p \le q \vee x$ but $p \not\le q_* \vee x$. Lemma 10.1 (3 properties: separation by join-irreducibles, refinement to minimal join expressions/covers, $\underline{D}$ via minimal join covers). Algebraic closure operator $\Gamma(S) = \{p : p \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq S\}$. (B) Representation Theorems 10.2-10.5. Theorem 10.2: $\mathcal{L}$ ≅ compact $\Gamma$-closed subsets of $J(\mathcal{L})$. Theorem 10.3: $\Gamma$-closed iff order ideal + closed under minimal join covers. The $\sigma$ map: $\sigma(\theta) = \{p \in J : p \,\theta\, p_*\}$. Theorem 10.4: $\sigma$ is a one-to-one complete lattice homomorphism. Theorem 10.5: range characterised by closure under reverse $\underline{D}$. The $\trianglelefteq$ quasiorder + $Q_\mathcal{L} = J(\mathcal{L})/\equiv$. Corollary: $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{O}(Q_\mathcal{L})$ — polynomial-time congruence-lattice algorithm. Corollary: SI iff $Q_\mathcal{L}$ has least element. (C) Dilworth's CLP. Recap: Wehrung 2007 negative for $\aleph_2$+. Lemma 10.6: distributive algebraic $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ iff every element is join of completely-join-primes iff every compact element is join of join-irreducible compacts. Theorem 10.7: principal congruences $\text{con}(p, p_*)$ are the join-irreducible compacts of $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$. Theorem 10.8 (Dilworth 1940s, Grätzer-Schmidt 1962, Acta Math. Acad. Sci. Hungar. 13): every distributive algebraic $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ for some PCF lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Construction with $J = P^0 \cup P^1$ (maximal elements identified). (D) Modular/relatively complemented + direct sum. Theorem 10.9: PCF + (modular OR relatively complemented) ⟹ $\underline{D}$ symmetric, $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ Boolean. Theorem 10.10 (Dilworth 1950, Ann. of Math. 51): a relatively complemented PCF lattice is a direct sum of simple ones.

Geometric Lattices

Lattice-theoretic foundations of incidence geometry: algebraic semimodular lattices in which every element is a join of atoms. The meeting point of lattice theory, matroid theory (Whitney 1935 + Birkhoff 1935 + Mac Lane 1938), projective geometry, and modern combinatorics — including the spectacular Adiprasito-Huh-Katz 2018 Hodge-theoretic resolution of the unimodal conjecture.

Four conceptual movements. (A) Geometric lattices. Geometry's incidence axioms (1)-(4) yield: a lattice in which atoms = points, every element is join of atoms, semimodular dimension. Definition: geometric lattice = algebraic semimodular atomistic. Examples: $\mathbb{R}^n$, vector subspaces, $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. Geometric vs lattice dimension. Birkhoff 1930s + Menger-Alt-Schreiber 1936 history. Theorem 11.1: three equivalent characterisations (geometric ⟺ upper-continuous atomistic semimodular ⟺ ideal lattice of atomistic semimodular PCF). (B) Mac Lane's representation + relative complementation. Exchange property of closure operator. Examples: linear span, geometric closure, transcendence-degree closure (Mac Lane 1938 motivation). Theorem 11.2 (Mac Lane 1938, Duke Math. J. 4): geometric ⟺ closed sets of algebraic closure operator with exchange property. Theorem 11.3: every geometric lattice is relatively complemented (uses Zorn + atom-finding). (C) Direct product decomposition. Lemma 11.4: $\mathcal{I}(\sum \mathbf{K}_i) \cong \prod \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$. Theorem 11.5 (Dilworth 1950 finite-dim, Hashimoto 1957 extension): every geometric lattice is direct product of SI geometric lattices; finite-dim case = direct product of simple. Libkin 1995: every atomistic algebraic = direct product of directly indecomposable. (D) Whitney numbers + unimodal + convex. Whitney numbers $w_k = |\{x : \delta(x) = k\}|$. Unimodal conjecture: $w_0 \le \cdots \le w_m \ge \cdots \ge w_n$. Open until 2018 — partial cases by Basterfield-Kelly 1968, Greene 1970, Hsieh-Kleitman 1973, Harper 1974, Dowling-Wilson 1975. RESOLVED in stronger LOG-CONCAVITY form by Adiprasito, Huh & Katz 2018 (Annals of Math 188) via Hodge theory for matroids — June Huh received 2022 Fields Medal partially for this work. Anti-exchange property → convex geometries (Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov 2003 Adv. Math. 173). The matroid / convex-geometry dichotomy.
