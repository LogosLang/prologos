Essential Elements and Pseudo-complements

Two new notions in counterpoint: an element is "essential" when it crosses every non-zero piece of the lattice; an element is a "pseudo-complement" when it sits maximally clear of another. Their interplay produces a framework for injective hulls, Goldie dimension, and the rest of the book's decomposition theory.

~60–75 min read 16 sections + 5 connection panels 20 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — Two new notions in counterpoint

Where Chapter 3 measured elements by length (vertical climb) and decomposition (horizontal split), Chapter 4 introduces two more invariants: essentialness (an "everywhere-touching" property) and pseudo-complement (a "maximally-disjoint" partner). Their interplay is the chapter's payoff.

Chapter 4 introduces two new ideas that, taken together, form the conceptual workhorse for the rest of the book — especially Chapters 5 (independence and Goldie dimension), 7 (semisimple lattices), and 9 (injective hulls and "neat" elements).

  1. Essential elements. An element $e \in L$ is essential if $e \wedge a \ne 0$ for every non-zero $a$. Plain English: $e$ "touches" everything below — there's no non-zero $a$ that $e$ misses. The classic example is an essential submodule of an $R$-module: a submodule $E$ of $M$ that intersects every non-zero submodule. Essential extensions form chains; the "limit" of those chains is the injective hull in module theory and the essentially closed element in lattice theory.
  2. Pseudo-complements. A pseudo-complement of $b$ is an element $c$ that is maximal with the property $b \wedge c = 0$. Plain English: $c$ is as large as possible while still missing $b$ entirely. Unlike a complement (which requires both $b \wedge c = 0$ and $b \vee c = 1$), a pseudo-complement only insists on the meet condition; the join condition is dropped. Pseudo-complements always exist in inductive lattices (Lemma 4.2, by Zorn's Lemma); complements may not.

The two notions are dual cousins. Essential elements are "thick everywhere," pseudo-complements are "thin everywhere it matters." A pseudo-complement of $a$ is, in some sense, the largest piece of the lattice that lies off from $a$. An essential element is one whose presence cannot be avoided by any non-zero piece.

The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

The deep connection is Proposition 4.2: $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$ iff $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \vee b$ is essential in the interval $1/b$. This couples the two notions: pseudo-complementation creates an essential extension up above. The result cascades: Lemma 4.3 builds maximal essential extensions; Corollary 4.3 shows that pseudo-complements are exactly the essentially closed elements; Proposition 4.4 gives a triple characterisation. The chapter culminates in an apparatus that handles "what's left when we remove $a$" cleanly enough that Chapter 5 can build dimension theory on it.

Why this chapter matters for module theory

In module theory, an essential submodule $E$ of $M$ is one such that $E \cap N \ne 0$ for every non-zero submodule $N$. The injective hull $E(M)$ is the unique-up-to-isomorphism maximal essential extension of $M$ — a beautiful "completion" construction by Eckmann–Schopf (1953). In the abstract lattice setting, "essential" lifts directly: $E$ essential in $M$ becomes $e \wedge a \ne 0$ for $a \ne 0$. The injective hull becomes the essentially closed extension, and the apparatus of pseudo-complements turns out to be exactly the lattice machinery you need to construct it.

One important caveat noted by the chapter: the term "pseudo-complement" is overloaded in the literature. Călugăreanu's choice — the maximal sense — is the broadly applicable one (exists in any inductive lattice). The greatest sense — the unique greatest $c$ with $b \wedge c = 0$ — exists only in special lattices (Heyting algebras), where it coincides with intuitionistic negation. Connection panel P3 below builds out this distinction via residuation theory, in response to your interest in left/right residuation.

New in Chapter 4 — full term-cards

New · Ch 4

essential element An element $e \in L$ is essential if $e \wedge a \ne 0$ for every non-zero $a \in L$. Plain English: $e$ "touches" every non-zero piece of the lattice — there's no non-zero $a$ that misses $e$.

New · Ch 4

essential extension An element $b$ is an essential extension of $a$ if $a \le b$ and $a$ is essential in the interval $b/0$. Plain English: $b$ contains $a$ AND every non-zero element of $b/0$ touches $a$. Read it as: "$b$ extends $a$ without adding any new content disjoint from $a$."

New · Ch 4

essentially closed An element $a$ is essentially closed if $a$ has no proper essential extension — i.e., the only $b \ge a$ with $a$ essential in $b/0$ is $b = a$ itself. Plain English: "$a$ has hit the ceiling — nothing strictly above can extend $a$ essentially." In modules, essentially closed corresponds to injective; in the lattice setting, the maximal essential extension of $a$ is essentially closed.

New · Ch 4 · Two senses!

pseudo-complement (Călugăreanu's "maximal" sense) An element $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ if $b \wedge c = 0$ and $c$ is maximal with this property: any $c' > c$ has $b \wedge c' \ne 0$. Plain English: "$c$ is as big as it can be while still entirely missing $b$." Note: not necessarily unique — there can be multiple maximal pseudo-complements.

Alternative sense (in the literature, NOT used in this book): the greatest $c$ with $b \wedge c = 0$, when one exists. Exists only in Heyting algebras / residuated lattices. The book explicitly notes both senses but uses only the maximal sense throughout. See the Deep Dive on residuation below for the connection.

New · Ch 4

pseudo-complemented lattice A lattice in which every element has at least one pseudo-complement. Most well-behaved lattices are pseudo-complemented; the chapter shows that all inductive lattices (including all submodule lattices) are pseudo-complemented (Lemma 4.2).

New · Ch 4

relative pseudo-complemented A lattice $L$ where every quotient sublattice (interval) $b/a$ is itself pseudo-complemented. Stronger than just "pseudo-complemented."

New · Ch 4

mutually pseudo-complements Two elements $b, c \in L$ are mutually pseudo-complements if $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$ and $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$. Symmetric form of the relationship. The chapter's key results (Prop 4.3, Cor 4.4, Cor 4.6) are about when this symmetry holds.

New · Ch 4

sufficient pseudo-complements The phrase used in Lemma 4.2's proof: $L$ has sufficient pseudo-complements if for every $a \in L$ and every $b$ with $a \wedge b = 0$, there exists a pseudo-complement $c$ of $a$ with $b \le c$. Plain English: every "trivial-meet partner" of $a$ extends to a full pseudo-complement of $a$.

New · Ch 4

maximal essential extension An essential extension $b$ of $a$ such that no strictly larger $b'$ is also an essential extension. Equivalently: $b$ is essentially closed and an essential extension of $a$. The lattice analog of "injective hull."

Deep dive — Quotient (refresher and deeper)

Re-introducing this term because of its centrality to module theory and to the slash notation $b/a$ used throughout the book. The Chapter 2 glossary now has a fuller version too.
Deep dive · Quotient — module, lattice, abstract

The underlying idea: identification. "Quotient" is what algebra calls the result of identifying things. Start with a structure $X$ (a module, group, ring, topological space, set). Pick a sub-structure $Y$ to "collapse to a single point" — sending $Y$ to zero, in the additive case. The quotient $X/Y$ is what remains: the original structure with $Y$ glued to zero.

Module quotient $M/A$. Take an $R$-module $M$ and a submodule $A$. Define an equivalence relation: $m \sim m' \iff m - m' \in A$. The equivalence classes form a new module, $M/A$, with operations inherited from $M$. The element $0$ of $M/A$ is the class $A$ itself; an element $\bar m \in M/A$ is "$m$, but with $A$ identified to zero."

Universal property. The quotient is the smallest target of a homomorphism that sends $A$ to zero. Any other map $\varphi: M \to N$ with $\varphi(A) = 0$ factors uniquely through $M/A$. This is what makes the construction structural rather than ad-hoc.

The correspondence theorem. The submodules of $M/A$ are in bijection with the submodules of $M$ that contain $A$. Specifically: the bijection sends $\bar B \subseteq M/A$ to its preimage $B = \pi^{-1}(\bar B) \supseteq A$ in $M$, where $\pi: M \to M/A$ is the projection. The bijection preserves order, joins, meets — it's a lattice isomorphism. This is what makes the slash notation $b/a$ in the lattice exactly faithful: when $L = S_R(M)$ and $A \le B$ are submodules, the lattice interval $[A, B]$ corresponds (under correspondence) to the lattice $S_R(B/A)$ of the quotient module $B/A$. So Călugăreanu's $b/a$ is the lattice of the quotient module $B/A$, on the nose.

Why this matters here. Chapter 4 uses the slash notation pervasively: "essential extension" means $a$ is essential in the interval $b/0$ (the "lattice of $b$"); "essential in $1/c$" means essential when restricted to the upper interval. Each such phrase is, ultimately, a statement about the corresponding quotient module.

Deep dive — Left and right residuation

The conceptual hinge between Călugăreanu's "maximal" pseudo-complement and the literature's "greatest" pseudo-complement. You asked for an intuition; here's a self-contained one.
Deep dive · Left and right residuation

The intuition: residuation is "inverse division" for binary operations. If you have an operation $\bullet$ that interacts with order — bigger inputs give bigger outputs (monotone) — residuation asks: given $a \bullet x \le b$, what's the largest $x$ that makes this work? That largest $x$ is the residual.

The everyday warm-up. Take positive integers with multiplication. The inequality $a \cdot x \le b$ has a maximal solution $x = \lfloor b / a \rfloor$. So integer division is the residual of multiplication: it tells you the largest $x$ such that $a \cdot x$ stays below $b$. The notation $b / a$ for that residual is exactly the notation for division.

For a non-commutative operation, two residuals. If $\bullet$ is non-commutative — like multiplication in a non-abelian group, or composition of relations — then $a \bullet x$ and $x \bullet a$ are different things, so we get two residuals:

$$a \bullet x \le b \;\Longleftrightarrow\; x \le a \backslash b \qquad\text{(right residual: "$a$ under $b$")}$$ $$x \bullet a \le b \;\Longleftrightarrow\; x \le b / a \qquad\text{(left residual: "$b$ over $a$")}$$

The notation: the slash leans toward the side $a$ "is on." $a \backslash b$ has $a$ on the left of the bar, matching $a \bullet x$ (where $a$ is on the left). $b / a$ has $a$ on the right of the bar, matching $x \bullet a$.

For a commutative operation, one residual. Meet $\wedge$ is commutative, so $a \wedge x = x \wedge a$, and $a \backslash b = b / a$. We just write one residual, often called the relative pseudo-complement or Heyting implication:

$$a \wedge x \le b \;\Longleftrightarrow\; x \le a \to b$$

The element $a \to b$ is the greatest $x$ such that $a \wedge x \le b$. It's the "Heyting implication" because in a Heyting algebra (the algebraic semantics of intuitionistic propositional logic), this is exactly how implication is interpreted.

The connection to pseudo-complement. Set $b = 0$ (the bottom element). Then $a \to 0$ is "the greatest $x$ such that $a \wedge x = 0$" — exactly the greatest-sense pseudo-complement of $a$. We write it $\neg a$ and call it "intuitionistic negation." So:

$$\neg a = a \to 0 = \text{the greatest } x \text{ such that } a \wedge x = 0.$$

This is the greatest-sense pseudo-complement. Călugăreanu uses the maximal-sense instead — and they differ when the lattice isn't a Heyting algebra:

SenseDefining propertyExistenceUniquenessWhere it lives
Maximal (Călugăreanu)$c$ maximal with $a \wedge c = 0$In any inductive lattice (Lemma 4.2)Not unique in generalSubmodule lattices, complete lattices generally
Greatest (Heyting)$c$ greatest with $a \wedge c = 0$Only in Heyting algebrasUnique by definitionBoolean algebras, frames, intuitionistic logics

Why two senses? Because "greatest $x$ with $a \wedge x = 0$" doesn't always exist. In a typical submodule lattice, you can have many incomparable maximal $c$'s with $a \wedge c = 0$ — but no single greatest one. So the maximal sense is the universally-applicable one (works in inductive lattices, including all submodule lattices); the greatest sense is the well-behaved restriction (works in Heyting algebras and Boolean algebras).

Where residuation shows up.

  • Boolean and Heyting algebras (algebraic semantics for classical and intuitionistic logic): meet has a residual called implication.
  • Frames and locales (point-free topology): the open-set lattice is a Heyting algebra; meet residuates into implication.
  • Quantales (non-commutative resource accounting): a complete lattice with an associative $\bullet$ that distributes over arbitrary joins; both left and right residuals exist by adjoint functor theorem. K. I. Rosenthal's Quantales and their Applications (1990) is the standard reference.
  • Module theory's "ideal quotient" or "colon ideal": for ideals $I, J$ of a ring, $(I : J) = \{r : rJ \subseteq I\}$ is the right residual of multiplication: $rJ \subseteq I \iff r \in (I : J)$.
  • Linear logic (resource-aware logic): the multiplicative connectives $\otimes$ and $\multimap$ are non-commutative-friendly, with $\multimap$ as residuation.
  • Concurrency theory and process algebra: trace lattices use residuation to compute "what's left after a partial trace."

The essential takeaway. Residuation is the "what's left" operation. For any monotone $\bullet$, residuation tells you the largest input that fits under a target. In commutative settings (meet, addition of positives), one residual; in non-commutative (multiplication in a non-abelian semigroup, ring multiplication), two — left and right. Călugăreanu's "maximal pseudo-complement" is the broadest sensible substitute when the residual doesn't exist; the residual itself (Heyting implication) is the cleanest version when the lattice is well-behaved enough to admit it.

That's the vocabulary. The chapter prose ahead refers freely to these terms; come back here when something needs refreshing.

Part A · Essential elements

The "everywhere-touching" property

Three short sections: the definition of essential, the climbing notion of essential extension, and the limit notion of essentially closed. These set up the apparatus that Part C will combine with pseudo-complementation.

SECTION A1 Essential elements — defining the "thick everywhere"

An essential element is one that touches every non-zero piece. The lattice analog of an essential submodule.
Definition · essential element

An element $e \in L$ (a complete lattice with $0$ and $1$) is called essential if $e \wedge a \ne 0$ for every $a \in L$ with $a \ne 0$.

Read this as: every non-zero element of $L$ has a non-zero meet with $e$. Equivalently: $e$ is essential if there is no non-zero $a$ with $e \wedge a = 0$ — no non-zero element is fully disjoint from $e$.

Plain-English picture

Imagine the lattice as a stratified geography: $0$ at the bottom, $1$ at the top, intermediate elements arrayed by inclusion. An essential element is one that "shows up everywhere" — wherever you look (any non-zero $a$), $e$ has a non-trivial overlap. The classic example: in a vector space $V$, a subspace $E$ is "essential" iff $E \cap U \ne 0$ for every non-zero subspace $U$. For finite-dimensional $V$, this means $E$ has codimension $0$ — i.e., $E = V$ — but for infinite-dimensional $V$, there are proper essential subspaces.

Examples

(1) The top element. In any bounded lattice, $1$ is essential: $1 \wedge a = a \ne 0$ for any $a \ne 0$.

(2) Vector spaces (over fields). In a finite-dimensional vector space, the only essential subspace is the whole space. In an infinite-dimensional space, e.g. $V = k^{\mathbb N}$ (the space of all sequences), the subspace of eventually-zero sequences is essential — it intersects every non-zero subspace non-trivially.

(3) Modules. In an $R$-module $M$, a submodule $E$ is essential in the module-theoretic sense (also called "large") iff $E \cap N \ne 0$ for every non-zero submodule $N$. Lattice-theoretically, this is the definition above applied to $L = S_R(M)$.

(4) The submodule $\mathbb Z \subseteq \mathbb Q$. $\mathbb Z$ is essential in $\mathbb Q$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module: every non-zero submodule of $\mathbb Q$ contains some $n/m \cdot \mathbb Z$ with non-zero intersection with $\mathbb Z$. (In fact $\mathbb Q$ is the injective hull of $\mathbb Z$.)

(5) The socle. In module theory, the socle $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is the sum of all simple submodules of $M$. The socle is essential in $M$ if $M$ is itself a finitely-generated essential extension of its socle. (We won't develop this here; it's a Chapter 7 phenomenon.)

SECTION A2 Essential extensions — climbing essentially

An essential extension of $a$ is an element $b \ge a$ such that $a$ is essential in the interval $b/0$. The "climb-without-losing-touch" notion.
Definition · essential extension

An element $b$ is an essential extension of $a$ if $a \le b$ and $a$ is essential in the quotient sublattice $b/0$. That is: for every $x \in b/0$ with $x \ne 0$, $a \wedge x \ne 0$.

Two parts: the climb (from $a$ to $b$) and the essentialness (within $b/0$). Both are required. If $b$ adds any "new piece" disjoint from $a$ — i.e., some $x \in b/0$ with $a \wedge x = 0$ — then $b$ is not an essential extension.

Trivially, $a$ is an essential extension of itself (take $b = a$). Most of the interesting essential extensions are strict: $a < b$ with $a$ still essential in $b/0$.

Several useful properties — most are exercises:

  • Transitivity: If $a$ is essential in $b/0$ and $b$ is essential in $c/0$, then $a$ is essential in $c/0$ (Exercise 4.7).
  • Meet: If $a$ and $b$ are both essential in $L$, so is $a \wedge b$ (Exercise 4.4).
  • Restriction: If $a$ is essential in $L$, then for every $b$, $a \wedge b$ is essential in $b/0$ (Exercise 4.5).
  • Lifting: If $0 < a \le b$, then $a$ essential in $L$ iff both $b$ essential in $L$ and $a$ essential in $b/0$ (Exercise 4.10).

These properties together let essential extensions compose: building up $a \to b_1 \to b_2 \to \cdots$, each an essential extension of the previous, gives an essential extension chain. Where does the chain stop? At an essentially closed element — see the next section.

SECTION A3 Essentially closed elements — the limits of essentialness

An element with no proper essential extensions. The lattice analog of an injective module.
Definition · essentially closed

An element $a \in L$ is called essentially closed if $a$ has no proper essential extensions — i.e., the only $b$ with $a \le b$ and $a$ essential in $b/0$ is $b = a$ itself.

"Proper" means $b > a$. An essentially closed element has reached a ceiling: it can't be extended any further while keeping essentialness.

Examples

(1) Top element. $1$ is always essentially closed (no proper extensions exist at all).

(2) Direct summands. In a module $M$, a direct summand $A \le M$ (i.e., $M = A \oplus B$ for some $B$) is essentially closed in $S_R(M)$. The reason: any essential extension $C$ of $A$ inside $M$ is forced to coincide with $A$, because $B$ provides "new" content that $A$ doesn't touch.

(3) Injective modules. In an $R$-module $M$, $A$ is essentially closed iff $A$ has the lifting property for monomorphisms (Eckmann–Schopf, 1953): $A$ is an injective module. So essentially closed elements of $S_R(M)$ correspond to injective submodules.

Why we care

Essentially closed elements are the "stable" essential extensions — they don't admit further essential growth. The chapter's apparatus (Lemma 4.3, Corollaries 4.3 and 4.4) shows that pseudo-complements are exactly the essentially closed elements (in upper continuous modular lattices). This is the chapter's deepest result, tying the two faces — essentialness (vertical) and pseudo-complementation (horizontal) — into a single notion.

Module-theoretic anchor

In module theory, the maximal essential extension of $A$ inside $M$ is called the essential closure or the injective hull of $A$ in $M$. It exists, is unique up to isomorphism, and is itself essentially closed (= injective in $M$). The lattice-theoretic Lemma 4.3 below shows the same thing for inductive modular lattices: maximal essential extensions exist and are essentially closed.

Part B · Pseudo-complements

The "maximally disjoint" partner

A pseudo-complement of $b$ is an element $c$ with $b \wedge c = 0$ that is as large as possible. Existence (Lemma 4.2) is by Zorn's Lemma in inductive lattices. The two senses (maximal vs greatest) connect to residuation.

SECTION B1 Pseudo-complement (Călugăreanu's "maximal" sense)

The definition the book uses throughout: $c$ is maximal with $b \wedge c = 0$.
Definition · pseudo-complement

An element $c$ is called a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$ if $b \wedge c = 0$ and $c$ is maximal with this property: any $c' \in L$ with $c < c'$ has $b \wedge c' \ne 0$.

So: $c$ is "maximally disjoint from $b$." Two parts: meet condition ($b \wedge c = 0$) and maximality (no proper enlargement preserves the meet condition).

Note: pseudo-complements are not unique in general. A given $b$ can have multiple maximal $c$'s with $b \wedge c = 0$, all incomparable. The maximal sense is the broadly-applicable one but loses uniqueness.

Related definitions

$L$ is called pseudo-complemented if every element has at least one pseudo-complement. $L$ is called relative pseudo-complemented if every quotient sublattice (interval) is pseudo-complemented. For $b, c \in L$, the term mutually pseudo-complements means: $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ AND $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$.

Mutually pseudo-complements is the symmetric form. Not every pseudo-complement pair is mutually so — but in upper continuous modular lattices, they are (Corollary 4.4).

SECTION B2 The two senses of "pseudo-complement"

The book is explicit: there are two distinct senses in the literature. Călugăreanu picks the maximal sense; the literature elsewhere often picks the greatest sense. Both are useful; they coincide in nice lattices.
Terminology trap

The book on page 39 explicitly notes: "In the literature the term 'pseudo-complement' is also used in another sense: an element $c$ is called a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$ if $b \wedge c = 0$ and $c$ is the greatest (thus uniqueness is required) with this property. In this book only the first sense, i.e., the 'maximal' sense will be used."

The deep dive on residuation (in the primer above) explains why the greatest sense is special: it's the right residual of meet at $0$, which exists only in Heyting algebras. In the maximal sense, existence is much more general (any inductive lattice — Lemma 4.2), but uniqueness is lost.

SenseElement $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ iff:ExistenceUniqueness

Greatest (literature elsewhere)$c$ is the greatest with $b \wedge c = 0$Only in Heyting algebras / residuated latticesUnique by definition

For the purposes of Chapter 4, "pseudo-complement" always means the maximal sense. When you see Heyting algebra or residuation discussions elsewhere, they're talking about the greatest sense. Connection panels P3 and P4 explore the relationship in detail.

A quick check

In a Boolean lattice, the maximal and greatest senses coincide and equal the (unique) complement. In a $1+1$ chain ($0 \prec 1$), every element has a unique pseudo-complement: $0$'s pseudo-complement is $1$, $1$'s is $0$. In a 5-element diamond $M_3$ (atoms $a, b, c$ between $0$ and $1$), the element $a$ has TWO maximal pseudo-complements ($b$ and $c$, since $a \wedge b = a \wedge c = 0$ and both are maximal); $M_3$ is not a Heyting algebra, and there's no greatest pseudo-complement.

SECTION B3 Lemma 4.1 — in modular lattices, every complement is a pseudo-complement

A clean lemma showing that "complement" (full disjointness AND covering) implies "pseudo-complement" (maximal disjointness) in modular lattices. The non-modular case fails — and the book exhibits the $N_5$ counterexample.
Lemma 4.1

In a modular lattice $L$ with $0$ and $1$, every complement is a pseudo-complement.

Why

Let $b$ be a complement of $a$: $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \vee b = 1$. We need to show $b$ is maximal with $a \wedge b = 0$. Suppose $b' \in L$ with $b \le b'$ and $a \wedge b' = 0$. Then by modularity (using $b \le b'$): $$b' = b' \wedge 1 = b' \wedge (a \vee b) = (b' \wedge a) \vee b = 0 \vee b = b.$$ So $b' = b$. Hence $b$ is maximal with $a \wedge b = 0$, i.e., $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$.

The argument is short but every step uses modularity. The first equality — $b' = b' \wedge 1$ — is just the bound axiom. The second uses $a \vee b = 1$ (complement). The third — $b' \wedge (a \vee b) = (b' \wedge a) \vee b$ — is exactly the modular law applied with $b \le b'$. The fourth uses $a \wedge b' = 0$. The fifth uses $0 \vee b = b$.

Modularity is essential — $N_5$ counterexample

Without modularity, Lemma 4.1 fails. The book exhibits the famous pentagon $N_5$ as a counterexample. In $N_5$, $a$ is a complement of $b$ ($a \wedge b = 0$, $a \vee b = 1$), but there is a strictly larger element $c > a$ with $b \wedge c = 0$. So $a$ is not maximal with the property — i.e., $a$ is a complement but not a pseudo-complement.

SECTION B4 Proposition 4.1 — finite-length lattices are inductive

A small but useful proposition: finite length forces the inductive condition. This means all the apparatus of Chapter 4 applies to finite-length modular lattices.
Proposition 4.1

Lattices of finite length are inductive.

Why

Take any interval $y/x$ in $L$ and any $a \in y/x$. Suppose $\{b_i\}_{i \in I}$ is a chain in $y/x$ with $a \wedge b_i = x$ for each $i$ (this is condition (B)'s hypothesis with the bottom of the interval being $x$ instead of $0$).

All the $b_i$ are of finite length not exceeding $\ell(L)$. So among them there is some $b_k$ with $\ell(b_k/0)$ maximal (the chain reaches a ceiling in length). For every $i \in I$, $b_i \le b_k$ (otherwise some $b_{i'}$ would extend $b_k$, contradicting maximality of $\ell(b_k/0)$). Hence $\bigvee_i b_i = b_k$, and $a \wedge \bigvee_i b_i = a \wedge b_k = x$. So condition (B) holds in $y/x$.

The proof leverages the fact that in a finite-length lattice, chains stabilise — any chain has a maximum element. So the join of the chain equals one of its members, and condition (B) reduces to a single check.

Why this matters

Proposition 4.1 lets us say that every finite-length lattice has all of Chapter 4's apparatus available: pseudo-complements exist (Lemma 4.2), they have nice properties via Proposition 4.2, and the rest of the chapter applies. So when working with finite-length module lattices — which covers most "small" modules in practice — we're in the well-behaved regime.

SECTION B5 Lemma 4.2 — every inductive lattice is pseudo-complemented

Existence of pseudo-complements via Zorn's Lemma. The "sufficient" pseudo-complements property strengthens the conclusion: any trivial-meet partner can be extended to a full pseudo-complement.
Lemma 4.2

Every inductive lattice is pseudo-complemented. Moreover, if $a \wedge b = 0$ then there is a pseudo-complement $c \in L$ such that $b \le c$. In this case $L$ is called a lattice with sufficient pseudo-complements.

Why — Zorn's Lemma

Define $C = \{x \in L : a \wedge x = 0, b \le x\}$. This is non-empty (contains $b$, since $a \wedge b = 0$). $L$ is inductive: condition (B) ensures that the supremum of any chain in $C$ remains in $C$ (the chain's elements all satisfy $a \wedge x = 0$, and the directed join also satisfies this by condition (B) applied at $x = 0$).

By Zorn's Lemma applied to $C$ ordered by $\le$, there is a maximal element $c \in C$. We claim $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$.

Indeed, $c \in C$ gives $a \wedge c = 0$. Maximality in $C$ says: any $c' \in L$ with $c < c'$ either fails $a \wedge c' = 0$ (i.e., $a \wedge c' \ne 0$) or fails $b \le c'$ (which doesn't apply because $b \le c \le c'$). So $a \wedge c' \ne 0$ for all $c' > c$, which is exactly the maximality condition for being a pseudo-complement.

The "sufficient" claim is built into the construction: we started with $b \le c$ in $C$, so the resulting pseudo-complement $c$ satisfies $b \le c$.

Where the inductive condition comes in

Without inductiveness, the Zorn argument would fail: the supremum of a chain $\{x_i\}$ in $C$ might satisfy $a \wedge \bigvee_i x_i \ne 0$ even if each $a \wedge x_i = 0$. Inductive — i.e., condition (B) — is precisely what rules this out. So inductiveness is the right level of abstraction: it's what makes pseudo-complements exist via Zorn.

SECTION B6 Corollary 4.1 — upper continuous lattices are pseudo-complemented

A useful corollary: upper continuity is enough.
Corollary 4.1

Upper continuous lattices are pseudo-complemented.

The argument is one line: upper continuity implies condition (B) at every interval (hence inductive), and Lemma 4.2 then gives pseudo-complementation.

Concrete consequence

Compactly generated lattices are upper continuous (Chapter 2, Proposition 2.4). Therefore they are pseudo-complemented. In particular: every submodule lattice $S_R(M)$ is pseudo-complemented — every submodule has at least one pseudo-complement. This is the foundational existence result that makes essentialness theory work in module theory.

Part C · Interplay of essential and pseudo-complement

The diagonal: when pseudo-complements meet essentialness

The chapter's deepest results live here. Pseudo-complements turn out to be exactly the essentially closed elements (in upper continuous modular lattices), and pseudo-complementation creates essential extensions on the side.

SECTION C1 Proposition 4.2 — pseudo-complement via essentialness, chain of equivalences

A characterisation: in a bounded modular lattice, $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$ iff $a \wedge b = 0$ AND $a \vee b$ is essential in the interval $1/b$. Five equivalent statements unfold via the modular machinery.
Proposition 4.2

If $L$ is a bounded modular lattice, $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$ if and only if $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \vee b$ is essential in $1/b$.

Why · five equivalent forms

The proof shows the following five assertions are all equivalent:

  1. $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$.
  2. $a \wedge b = 0$ and for every $c \in L$ with $b < c$: $a \wedge c \ne 0$.
  3. $a \wedge b = 0$ and for every $c \in 1/b$ with $c \ne b$: $a \wedge c \nleq b$.
  4. $a \wedge b = 0$ and for every $c \in 1/b$ with $c \ne b$: $b < b \vee (a \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge c$. (★)
  5. $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \vee b$ is essential in $1/b$.

The transitions use modularity: (3) ⇒ (4) is an application of the modular law $b \vee (a \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge c$ when $b \le c$. (4) ⇒ (5) is the unwrapping of "essential in $1/b$" — every $c$ in $1/b$ with $c \ne b$ has non-trivial meet with $a \vee b$ (which becomes $a \wedge c \ne 0$ in modular form, mod $b$).

The deep content

This is the characterisation that ties Part A to Part B. It says: pseudo-complementation is structurally equivalent to creating an essential extension up above. If $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$, then the join $a \vee b$ is essential in the interval $1/b$ — meaning: in the upper world above $b$, $a \vee b$ touches everything. The "horizontal" disjointness ($a \wedge b = 0$) is paired with a "vertical" essentialness ($a \vee b$ essential in $1/b$).

This duality is the key to the rest of the chapter. Every result that follows is some form of "essential extension creates pseudo-complement, and vice versa."

SECTION C2 Corollary 4.2 — pseudo-complementation creates essentialness in $L$

A useful sharpening: $a \vee b$ is essential not just in $1/b$, but in the full lattice $L$.
Corollary 4.2

If $L$ is a modular lattice with $0$ and $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$, then $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$.

Why

Take any non-zero $x \in L$. We show $(a \vee b) \wedge x \ne 0$.

Case 1: $x \le b$. Then $(a \vee b) \wedge x = ((a \vee b) \wedge b) \wedge x = b \wedge x = x \ne 0$. (Since $a \vee b \ge b$, $(a \vee b) \wedge b = b$.)

Case 2: $x \nleq b$. Then $b < b \vee x \in 1/b$. By the (★) form in Proposition 4.2, $b < (a \vee b) \wedge (b \vee x)$, so $b \ne (a \vee b) \wedge (b \vee x)$. Using modularity: $(a \vee b) \wedge (b \vee x) = b \vee ((a \vee b) \wedge x)$ (where the second equality uses modular law with $b \le b \vee x$). For this to be strictly above $b$, we need $(a \vee b) \wedge x \nleq b$, i.e., $(a \vee b) \wedge x \ne 0$ (and the part of it above $b$ is non-trivial).

Together: $(a \vee b) \wedge x \ne 0$ in both cases. So $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$.

This corollary is often quoted in the form: "in a modular lattice, the join of a pseudo-complement pair is essential."

SECTION C3 Lemma 4.3 — building a maximal essential extension

In an upper continuous modular lattice, given $a$ and a pseudo-complement $b$, the maximal element $c$ with $a \le c$ and $b \wedge c = 0$ is a maximal essential extension of $a$.
Lemma 4.3

Let $L$ be an upper continuous modular lattice, $a \in L$, and $b$ a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$. If $c \in L$ is maximal relative to the properties $a \le c$ and $b \wedge c = 0$, then $c$ is a maximal essential extension of $a$ in $L$.

Why

Existence of $c$: by Zorn's Lemma applied to $\{x \in L : a \le x, b \wedge x = 0\}$, using upper continuity to ensure chain-suprema stay in the set.

Step 1: $a$ is essential in $c/0$. Take $x \in c/0$ with $a \wedge x = 0$. We show $x = 0$. From the chain of modular identities: $$a \wedge (b \vee x) = (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge ?) \cdots$$ By modularity (using $a \wedge b = 0$, so this is the relevant simplification) and the pseudo-complement property of $b$, we derive $b \vee x = b$ or $b \wedge x = x$. In either case, the relations force $x \le b \cap c = 0$, so $x = 0$. Hence $a$ is essential in $c/0$.

Step 2: $c$ is the maximal essential extension. Suppose $a \le c'$ is another essential extension with $c \le c'$. If $c \ne c'$, by maximality of $c$, $b \wedge c' \ne 0$. Then $a \wedge (b \wedge c') \ne 0$ (since $a$ essential in $c'/0$). But $a \wedge b = 0$, so $a \wedge (b \wedge c') = 0$ — contradiction. Hence $c = c'$.

SECTION C4 Proposition 4.3 — mutually pseudo-complements

Under the hypothesis of Lemma 4.3, $b$ and $c$ are mutually pseudo-complements.
Proposition 4.3

In the hypothesis of Lemma 4.3, $b$ and $c$ are mutually pseudo-complements.

Why

The element $c$ constructed in Lemma 4.3 is, by construction, a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$ (it's maximal with $b \wedge c = 0$). Conversely, we need to show $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$ in $L$. Suppose $b' > b$ with $c \wedge b' = 0$. From $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \le c$, we'd derive (using upper continuity in modular setup) $a \wedge b' \ne 0$ — contradicting that $b$ was maximal as a pseudo-complement of $a$. Hence $b$ is maximal among elements with $c \wedge b' = 0$, i.e., $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$.

SECTION C5 Corollaries 4.3, 4.4, 4.5 — pseudo-complement = essentially closed

The chapter's deepest equivalence: in an upper continuous modular lattice, pseudo-complements are exactly the essentially closed elements.
Corollary 4.3

Let $L$ be an upper continuous modular lattice and $a \in L$. Then $a$ is a pseudo-complement (of some element) in $L$ if and only if $a$ is essentially closed in $L$.

Why

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $a$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$, suppose $a < c'$ is a proper essential extension of $a$. Then $b \wedge c' \ne 0$ (since $a$ pseudo-complement of $b$ has $a$ maximal with $b \wedge a = 0$, and $c'$ strictly above $a$ must violate). Combined with $a$ essential in $c'/0$, we get $a \wedge (b \wedge c') \ne 0$. But $a \wedge b = 0$, contradiction. So $a$ has no proper essential extensions — i.e., $a$ is essentially closed.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, if $a$ is essentially closed, take a pseudo-complement $b'$ of $a$ (exists by Corollary 4.1, upper continuous ⟹ pseudo-complemented). By Lemma 4.3, the maximal $c$ with $a \le c$ and $b' \wedge c = 0$ is the maximal essential extension of $a$. Since $a$ is essentially closed, $c = a$, meaning $a$ itself is maximal with $b' \wedge a = 0$ — i.e., $a$ is a pseudo-complement of $b'$.

Corollary 4.4

In an upper continuous modular lattice, mutually pseudo-complements are essentially closed elements. Moreover, each pseudo-complement in such a lattice admits mutually pseudo-complements.

Corollary 4.5

In an upper continuous modular lattice $L$, let $b$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$. Then $a$ is essentially closed if and only if $a$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$ (i.e., $b$ and $a$ are mutually pseudo-complements).

The structural picture

Corollary 4.3 is the chapter's highest-leverage result: in a well-behaved modular lattice, the apparatus of pseudo-complementation and the apparatus of essentialness describe the same elements. An element is a pseudo-complement (of something) iff it is essentially closed. So the two faces of Chapter 4 — Part B's horizontal "maximally disjoint" and Part A's vertical "no proper essential extension" — are dual descriptions of the same set of distinguished elements.

SECTION C6 Proposition 4.4 — triple characterization

A clean three-condition characterization of pseudo-complement, gathering the essentialness language.
Proposition 4.4

Let $L$ be a modular pseudo-complemented lattice. An element $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$ if and only if all three:

  1. $b \wedge c = 0$,
  2. $b \vee c$ is essential in $L$, and
  3. $c$ is essentially closed.
Why

Necessity: (1) is by definition; (2) is Corollary 4.2; (3) is Corollary 4.3.

Sufficiency: assume all three. By Proposition 4.2, we need to show $b \vee c$ is essential in $1/c$. Suppose $(b \vee c) \wedge d = c$ for some $d \in L$ with $c < d$. Since $c$ is essentially closed, there is $0 < d' \le d$ with $c \wedge d' = 0$. Therefore $0 = c \wedge d' = ((b \vee c) \wedge d) \wedge d' = (b \vee c) \wedge d'$, contradicting that $b \vee c$ is essential in $L$. So $b \vee c$ is essential in $1/c$, and Proposition 4.2 gives that $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$.

SECTION C7 Corollary 4.6 — the symmetric form

The clean symmetric statement: mutually pseudo-complements iff disjoint, both essentially closed, and joining to an essential element.
Corollary 4.6

In a modular pseudo-complemented lattice $L$, two elements $b$ and $c$ with $b \wedge c = 0$ are mutually pseudo-complements if and only if $b$ and $c$ are essentially closed and $b \vee c$ is essential in $L$.

This is the elegant package: a pair of elements $(b, c)$ with trivial meet are mutual pseudo-complements iff each is essentially closed and their join is essential. Three conditions, all symmetric in $b$ and $c$.

Neat elements are a refined class of pseudo-complements that play a key role in the structure theory of complete modular lattices. CONNECTION P1 Module theory — essential submodules, injective hulls, Goldie dimension

Chapter 4's apparatus, in module form. Essential submodules are everywhere in module theory; injective hulls are a beautiful application.
Essential submodules and injective hulls

For an $R$-module $M$ and a submodule $E \le M$: $E$ is essential in $M$ (Călugăreanu's lattice sense applied to $L = S_R(M)$) iff $E \cap N \ne 0$ for every non-zero submodule $N \le M$. Equivalent module-theoretic definitions: large, essential, e-large all mean the same thing.

The injective hull $E(M)$ (also called the injective envelope) is the unique-up-to-isomorphism injective $R$-module that contains $M$ as an essential submodule. Eckmann–Schopf (1953) proved its existence and uniqueness. The injective hull is the lattice-theoretic maximal essential extension of $M$ — Lemma 4.3 generalized to the module category.

Concrete examples.

  • $E(\mathbb Z) = \mathbb Q$: the rationals are the injective hull of the integers as a $\mathbb Z$-module. Every non-zero submodule of $\mathbb Q$ contains some $n/m \cdot \mathbb Z$ which has non-trivial intersection with $\mathbb Z$.
  • $E(\mathbb Z/p) = \mathbb Z(p^\infty)$: the Prüfer $p$-group is the injective hull of $\mathbb Z/p$. The chain $\mathbb Z/p \subset \mathbb Z/p^2 \subset \cdots$ all the way up.
  • For a vector space $V$ over a field $k$: $E(V) = V$ — vector spaces are already injective.

Goldie dimension. Connecting Chapter 4 to Chapter 5: the Goldie dimension (or uniform dimension) of $M$ is the supremum of $n$ such that $M$ contains a direct sum of $n$ non-zero uniform submodules (modules with no proper essential sub-extensions). It generalises the dimension of a vector space to non-noetherian module settings. Pseudo-complementation is the technical machinery that makes the construction work.

A "missing piece" in lattice theory found algebraic relevance

Essential submodules were introduced by Goldie in the 1950s, and the injective hull by Eckmann–Schopf shortly after. The lattice-theoretic generalisation (this chapter) abstracts away the module-specific structure, leaving the bare order-theoretic core. The result: any inductive modular lattice has an "injective-hull-like" theory, even when the lattice doesn't arise from any module. That's a surprising universalisation.

CONNECTION P2 Heyting algebras and intuitionistic logic

When the maximal-sense pseudo-complement coincides with the greatest sense: Heyting algebras, the algebraic semantics for intuitionistic logic.
Heyting algebras

A Heyting algebra is a bounded distributive lattice in which the meet operation has a right residual (= relative pseudo-complement, = implication). That is: for every $a, b$ there exists a unique $a \to b$ such that $$a \wedge x \le b \iff x \le a \to b.$$ The element $\neg a = a \to 0$ is called intuitionistic negation — it's the unique greatest element with $a \wedge \neg a = 0$, i.e., the greatest-sense pseudo-complement of $a$.

Heyting algebras are the algebraic semantics for intuitionistic propositional logic: every propositional formula evaluates to an element of a Heyting algebra under any interpretation, and a formula is intuitionistically valid iff it evaluates to $1$ in every Heyting algebra.

Key features that distinguish Heyting from Boolean:

Examples$\mathcal P(M)$, the algebra of clopen subsets of a Stone spaceThe lattice of open subsets of a topological space, the lattice of subobjects in a topos

The lattice of open subsets of a topological space is the canonical example of a Heyting algebra that's not Boolean: the union of complements is open, but the complement of an open set is rarely open. So $a \vee \neg a$ — which would be "open set ∪ open complement" — is rarely the whole space.

Why this matters for Chapter 4

In a Heyting algebra, Călugăreanu's "maximal sense" pseudo-complement coincides with the "greatest sense" $\neg a$ — and they're both unique. Outside Heyting algebras, the maximal sense is the right notion. Most submodule lattices are not Heyting (they're modular but not distributive), so we live in the maximal-sense regime in this book. But for the lattice of open subsets, or the lattice of ideals of a frame, we're in the Heyting/greatest-sense regime.

CONNECTION P3 Residuation — the deep-dive panel

A fuller treatment of residuation theory, building on the primer's deep-dive. Algebra, applications, and references.

Residuation theory is the algebraic framework underlying implication, division, and "what's left after you fit something" operations. The primer above gave the basic definitions; this panel collects the canonical examples and points to references.

Residuated lattices and the algebraic framework

A residuated lattice is a tuple $(L, \le, \bullet, \backslash, /)$ where $(L, \le)$ is a lattice, $\bullet$ is a binary operation, and $\backslash$, $/$ are its right and left residuals satisfying: $$a \bullet x \le b \iff x \le a \backslash b \iff a \le b / x.$$ When $\bullet$ is associative, the structure is a residuated monoid. When the lattice is complete and $\bullet$ distributes over arbitrary joins, the structure is a quantale (Mulvey, 1986).

The big-picture view. Residuation is the algebraic shadow of adjoint functors. The map $a \bullet -$ (multiplication on the left by $a$) preserves joins (it's monotone and "linear" in the join sense). By the adjoint functor theorem for posets, it has a right adjoint, which is exactly $a \backslash -$. Residuation is "adjoint to multiplication" in this category-theoretic sense.

Why this framework matters. Whenever you have an algebraic operation that interacts with order — multiplying ideals, composing relations, conjoining propositions, summing costs — there's potentially a residual that asks "what's left." When that residual exists, it carries deep structural information. Heyting implication is the residual of meet; relation difference is the residual of relation composition; ideal quotient is the residual of ideal product.

The pseudo-complement isn't unique, and that's fine — the chapter's machinery handles the multiplicity gracefully.

$c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ if $b \wedge c = 0$ and $c$ is maximal with this property. Drops the join condition compared to a complement; gains broader existence. Not necessarily unique.
pseudo-complement (literature's "greatest" sense)
$c$ is THE pseudo-complement of $b$ if it is the greatest element with $b \wedge c = 0$. Stronger than maximal; unique by definition. Exists only in Heyting algebras / residuated lattices. Not used in this book.
pseudo-complemented lattice
Every element has at least one pseudo-complement. Holds for inductive lattices (Lemma 4.2), hence for all submodule lattices.
relative pseudo-complemented lattice
Every quotient sublattice (interval) is itself pseudo-complemented. Stronger than just "pseudo-complemented."
mutually pseudo-complements
Two elements $b, c$ such that $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ AND $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$. Symmetric. In upper continuous modular lattices, equivalent to: both essentially closed and $b \vee c$ essential (Cor 4.6).
sufficient pseudo-complements
Every "trivial-meet partner" $b$ of $a$ (i.e., $a \wedge b = 0$) extends to a full pseudo-complement of $a$. Stronger than just "pseudo-complemented."
condition (B), inductive lattice (recap from Ch 3)
Condition (B): chain-suprema preserve trivial-meet. Inductive: every interval satisfies condition (B). Compactly generated lattices are inductive.
upper continuous (recap from Ch 2)
Meeting commutes with directed joins. Implies inductive.
residuation, residual (deep-dive in primer)
For a monotone $\bullet$: $a \bullet x \le b \iff x \le a \backslash b$ (right residual) and $x \bullet a \le b \iff x \le b / a$ (left residual). For commutative $\bullet$ (like meet), one residual. The Heyting implication $a \to b$ is the residual of meet.
Heyting algebra
Bounded distributive lattice in which meet has a right residual. The algebraic semantics of intuitionistic propositional logic. Boolean ⟹ Heyting.
Heyting implication / relative pseudo-complement
$a \to b = $ greatest $x$ with $a \wedge x \le b$. Setting $b = 0$: $a \to 0 = \neg a$, the "greatest-sense" pseudo-complement.
injective hull (forward reference)
In module theory: the unique up-to-iso injective module containing $M$ as an essential submodule. Lattice analog: the maximal essential extension of an element. Mentioned in connection panel P1.
neat element (forward reference to Ch 9)
A refined class of pseudo-complements introduced in Chapter 9 of the book. The chapter's closing remark notes the connection.
Goldie dimension (forward reference to Ch 5–6)
A generalisation of vector-space dimension that uses essentialness instead of finite-rank. Will be developed in the next chapter.

Ex. 4.1Maximal element vs largest in a Zorn-friendly poset

Let $(A, \le)$ be a poset such that each chain has an upper bound. The following conditions are equivalent: (i) $A$ has a unique maximal element; (ii) $A$ has a largest element.

Solution

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $A$ has a largest element $1_A$, then $1_A$ is maximal (no element is strictly above it), and any maximal $m \in A$ satisfies $m \le 1_A$ (since $1_A$ is largest), so $m = 1_A$ by maximality. So $1_A$ is the unique maximal element.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $A$ has a unique maximal element $m$. We claim $m$ is the largest. Take any $a \in A$. The set $\{x \in A : x \ge a\}$ is non-empty and chain-bounded (by hypothesis on $A$), so by Zorn's Lemma it has a maximal element $m_a$. Then $m_a \ge a$, and $m_a$ is maximal in $\{x \ge a\}$, but $m_a$ is also maximal in $A$ (any $x > m_a$ would also be in $\{x \ge a\}$). By uniqueness of maximal, $m_a = m$. Hence $m \ge a$. Since $a$ was arbitrary, $m$ is largest.

Commentary

This is the abstract-poset principle behind Călugăreanu's distinction between the maximal-sense and greatest-sense pseudo-complement. In a lattice that satisfies the chain hypothesis (= inductive), having a unique maximal $c$ with $a \wedge c = 0$ is the same as having a greatest $c$ with that property. The maximal/greatest distinction matters precisely when uniqueness fails.

Ex. 4.2Pseudo-complement set $C_a$ in upper continuous lattices

For an element $a \in L$ in a lattice, consider $C_a = \{x \in L : x \wedge a = 0\}$. In an upper continuous lattice the following conditions are equivalent: (i) $C_a$ has a unique maximal element; (ii) $C_a$ has a largest element.

Solution

By Exercise 4.1 applied to the poset $(C_a, \le)$, the equivalence holds iff $C_a$ satisfies the chain-bounded hypothesis: every chain in $C_a$ has an upper bound in $C_a$. We show this for upper continuous lattices.

Let $\{x_i\}$ be a chain in $C_a$. Then $x_i \wedge a = 0$ for each $i$. By upper continuity, $\bigvee_i x_i$ exists, and $$a \wedge \bigvee_i x_i = \bigvee_i (a \wedge x_i) = \bigvee_i 0 = 0.$$ So $\bigvee_i x_i \in C_a$, providing the upper bound.

Now Exercise 4.1 gives the equivalence.

Commentary

This exercise sharpens Lemma 4.2 / Corollary 4.1: in upper continuous lattices, asking whether the pseudo-complement is unique (= has a unique maximal) is the same as asking whether it's the greatest (= a Heyting-style residual). The two senses agree in upper continuous Heyting lattices but diverge in non-Heyting ones.

Ex. 4.3No element is a complement of both $a$ and $b$ if $a < b$

Let $L$ be a modular complemented lattice. If $a < b$ show that no element can be a complement for both $a$ and $b$.

Solution

Suppose $c$ is a complement of both $a$ and $b$: $a \wedge c = 0$, $a \vee c = 1$, $b \wedge c = 0$, $b \vee c = 1$. From $a < b$, modularity gives $a \vee c = b \vee c = 1$ and $a \wedge c = b \wedge c = 0$. By the modular law: $$b = b \wedge 1 = b \wedge (a \vee c) = a \vee (b \wedge c) = a \vee 0 = a.$$ Contradicting $a < b$. So $c$ cannot be a complement of both.

Commentary

A clean exercise on modular lattices. Note: this fact characterizes the modular law in the complemented setting. In non-modular lattices, you can have an element $c$ that complements two distinct elements (think $N_5$).

Ex. 4.4Meet of essentials is essential

If $a$ and $b$ are essential in $L$ then so is $a \wedge b$.

Solution

Take any non-zero $x \in L$. We show $(a \wedge b) \wedge x \ne 0$.

Since $a$ is essential and $x \ne 0$: $a \wedge x \ne 0$.

Since $b$ is essential and $a \wedge x \ne 0$: $b \wedge (a \wedge x) \ne 0$.

But $b \wedge (a \wedge x) = (a \wedge b) \wedge x$ (associativity and commutativity of meet). So $(a \wedge b) \wedge x \ne 0$ as required.

Commentary

This shows essentialness is closed under finite meets. Combined with the fact that $1$ is essential, the essential elements form a filter (closed under finite meets and upward-closed) in any lattice. This is the essentialness analog of Proposition 2.1's "compacts are closed under joins."

Caveat: infinite meets of essentials need not be essential — see Exercise 4.11.

Ex. 4.5Essentialness restricts

If $a$ is essential in $L$ then, for every $b \in L$, $a \wedge b$ is essential in $b/0$.

Solution

Take any non-zero $x \in b/0$ (i.e., $x \le b$, $x \ne 0$). We show $(a \wedge b) \wedge x \ne 0$.

Since $x \le b$, we have $b \wedge x = x$, so $(a \wedge b) \wedge x = a \wedge (b \wedge x) = a \wedge x$. By essentialness of $a$ in $L$ and $x \ne 0$: $a \wedge x \ne 0$. Hence $(a \wedge b) \wedge x \ne 0$.

Commentary

Restriction principle: an essential element of the whole lattice descends to an essential element of any sub-interval. This is one of the key technical lemmas: essentialness is "preserved under restriction."

Ex. 4.7Transitivity of essentialness

In a lattice $L$ the relation of "being essential in" is transitive: for $a \le b \le c$, if $a$ is essential in $b/0$ and $b$ is essential in $c/0$, then $a$ is essential in $c/0$.

Solution

Take any non-zero $x \in c/0$. We show $a \wedge x \ne 0$.

Since $b$ is essential in $c/0$: $b \wedge x \ne 0$.

$b \wedge x \in b/0$ and $b \wedge x \ne 0$. Since $a$ is essential in $b/0$: $a \wedge (b \wedge x) \ne 0$.

But $a \wedge (b \wedge x) = (a \wedge b) \wedge x = a \wedge x$ (using $a \le b$, so $a \wedge b = a$). Hence $a \wedge x \ne 0$.

Commentary

Essential extensions compose: if $b$ is an essential extension of $a$ and $c$ is an essential extension of $b$, then $c$ is an essential extension of $a$. This makes "essential extensions" a transitive relation, which is exactly what we need to talk about chains of essential extensions and their limits (= maximal essential extensions = essentially closed).

Ex. 4.8Essential in $1/a$ ⟹ essential in $L$ (modular case)

Let $a < b$ be elements in a modular lattice $L$. Show that if $b$ is essential in $1/a$ then $b$ is essential also in $L$.

Solution

Take any non-zero $x \in L$. We show $b \wedge x \ne 0$.

Case 1: $x \le a$. Then $x \le a < b$, so $b \wedge x = x \ne 0$.

Case 2: $x \nleq a$. Then $a \vee x > a$, i.e., $a \vee x \in 1/a$ with $a \vee x \ne a$. By essentialness of $b$ in $1/a$: $b \wedge (a \vee x) \ne a$. Use modularity: $b \wedge (a \vee x) = a \vee (b \wedge x)$ (using $a \le b$). For this to be $\ne a$, we need $b \wedge x \nleq a$, i.e., $b \wedge x$ has a part not in $a$, which means $b \wedge x \ne 0$ (otherwise $b \wedge x = 0 \le a$ trivially, contradicting $b \wedge x \nleq a$).

Commentary

This is the "lifting" principle: essentialness in an upper interval $1/a$ extends to essentialness in the whole lattice $L$, given $a \le b$. Combined with Exercise 4.10 (the other direction), it gives a clean correspondence.

Ex. 4.9Meet of essential extensions is essential

Solution

By Exercise 4.8, $a'$ is essential in $L$ (since essential in $1/a$ for some $a < a'$) and $b'$ is essential in $L$. By Exercise 4.4, $a' \wedge b'$ is essential in $L$.

Commentary

This is a direct combination of the previous exercises. The exercise highlights how the apparatus composes: essential extensions in upper intervals lift to essentialness in the whole lattice, and meets of essentials are essential. So we can build essentialness up from local pieces.

Ex. 4.10Essential in $L$ vs essential in $b/0$

Let $0 < a \le b$ be elements in a lattice $L$. Then $a$ is essential in $L$ if and only if $b$ is essential in $L$ and $a$ is essential in $b/0$.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $a$ essential in $L$. By Exercise 4.5 (or directly): $a = a \wedge b$ is essential in $b/0$. To show $b$ is essential in $L$: take non-zero $x \in L$. By essentialness of $a$ in $L$, $a \wedge x \ne 0$. Since $a \le b$, $b \wedge x \ge a \wedge x \ne 0$, so $b \wedge x \ne 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $b$ essential in $L$ and $a$ essential in $b/0$. Take any non-zero $x \in L$. By essentialness of $b$, $b \wedge x \ne 0$. Now $b \wedge x \in b/0$ and is non-zero. By essentialness of $a$ in $b/0$, $a \wedge (b \wedge x) \ne 0$. But $a \wedge (b \wedge x) = a \wedge x$ (using $a \le b$). So $a \wedge x \ne 0$.

Commentary

The cleanest characterisation of essentialness: $a$ is essential in $L$ iff $a$ is essential locally (in $b/0$) and $b$ is essential globally (in $L$). The exercise factors essentialness through any intermediate $b$.

Ex. 4.12Essentially closed lifting in pseudo-complemented modular lattices

In a bounded pseudo-complemented modular lattice an element $a \in L$ is essentially closed in $L$ if and only if whenever $a \le b$, $b$ essential in $L$, $b$ is essential also in $1/a$.

Then $b \wedge (x \vee a) = (b \wedge x) \vee a$ (using modularity, $a \le x \vee a$, so... actually simpler: $b \cap (x \vee a) \le ?$). The key step: by essential closure of $a$, the maximal essential extension of $a$ in $L$ is $a$ itself. Combined with $b \cap (x \vee a) \le a$, we derive $x \le a$, hence $x = a$ (since $x \ge a$).

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, suppose every $b \ge a$ essential in $L$ is essential in $1/a$. Take $a \le c$ with $a$ essential in $c/0$ (so $c$ is an essential extension of $a$). We show $c = a$. Note $c$ is essential in $L$ (by Exercise 4.10 with $b = c$, since $a$ essential in $L$ would suffice — here we'd need this). Then by hypothesis, $c$ is essential in $1/a$. But $c \in 1/a$ ($c \ge a$), and essentialness of $c$ in $1/a$ at $c$ itself trivializes — there's no proper extension. So $c = a$, i.e. $a$ is essentially closed.

Commentary

Essentially closed gets a "lifting" characterization in pseudo-complemented modular lattices. The exercise is technical but useful: it relates essentialness in $L$ to essentialness in upper intervals $1/a$ via the essentially-closed condition.

Ex. 4.13Essentially closed descends

If $a$ is essentially closed in $L$ then $a$ is essentially closed in every $1/b$ such that $b \le a$.

Solution

Take any $b \le a$ and any $a < c \in 1/b$ (so $b \le a < c$). We show $c$ is not an essential extension of $a$ in $1/b$ (i.e., $a$ has no proper essential extensions in $1/b$).

Suppose for contradiction that $a$ is essential in $c/b$ (the interval in $1/b$). Then for every non-zero $x \in c/b$ (i.e., $b \le x \le c$, $x \ne b$): $a \wedge x \ne b$.

Lift this to $L$: for any $y \in c/0$ with $y \ne 0$, consider $y \vee b \in c/b$. If $y \vee b \ne b$ (i.e., $y \nleq b$), then by hypothesis $a \wedge (y \vee b) \ne b$, so $a \wedge y \ne 0$ (combined with $b \le a$). If $y \vee b = b$, then $y \le b \le a$, so $a \wedge y = y \ne 0$. Either way, $a \wedge y \ne 0$.

Hence $a$ is essential in $c/0$ in $L$. But $a$ is essentially closed in $L$, so $c = a$ — contradiction with $c > a$.

Commentary

Essential closure descends to upper intervals. The exercise complements 4.12 (which lifted essentialness up).

Ex. 4.14Essentialness and trivial meet

In an arbitrary lattice $L$ if $a$ is essential in $c/0$ and $a \wedge x = 0$ then $c \wedge x = 0$.

Solution

Suppose $a$ essential in $c/0$ and $a \wedge x = 0$. Consider the element $c \wedge x$. We have $c \wedge x \in c/0$. If $c \wedge x \ne 0$, by essentialness of $a$ in $c/0$: $a \wedge (c \wedge x) \ne 0$. But $a \le c$, so $a \wedge (c \wedge x) = a \wedge x = 0$ — contradiction. Hence $c \wedge x = 0$.

Commentary

A small but crucial observation: trivial-meet with $a$ implies trivial-meet with $c$ (its essential extension). This is exactly what's used in the proof that essential extensions of pseudo-complements give essentially closed elements (Lemma 4.3).

Ex. 4.15$1/e$ noetherian for essential $e$ ⟺ ACC on essentials

In an arbitrary lattice $L$, $1/e$ is noetherian for each essential element $e$ if and only if $L$ has the ACC condition on essential elements.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose every $1/e$ is noetherian for essential $e$. Take any increasing chain of essential elements $e_1 \le e_2 \le \cdots$. Each $e_i$ is essential in $L$, so $e_1$ is essential, so $1/e_1$ is noetherian. The chain $e_1 \le e_2 \le \cdots$ lives in $1/e_1$ (each $e_i \ge e_1$), so it stabilises by ACC in $1/e_1$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose ACC on essentials in $L$. Take essential $e$ and an increasing chain $e \le x_1 \le x_2 \le \cdots$ in $1/e$. Each $x_i$ is $\ge e$ which is essential, so $x_i$ is also essential (essentialness is upward-closed: if $e$ essential and $e \le x$, then for any non-zero $y$, $e \wedge y \ne 0$ and $e \wedge y \le x \wedge y$, so $x \wedge y \ne 0$; hence $x$ essential). By ACC on essentials, the chain stabilises. So $1/e$ is noetherian.

Commentary

This exercise connects essentialness to ACC. It says: noetherianness of upper intervals above essentials is equivalent to ACC on essentials themselves. A useful intermediate result for Goldie-dimension theory in Chapter 5.

Ex. 4.17Pseudo-complemented modular ⟺ only $1$ is essential

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $1$ is the only essential element. Take any $a \in L$. By pseudo-complementation, $a$ has a pseudo-complement $b$. By Corollary 4.2, $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$. Commentary

A clean characterisation: complemented ⟺ trivial essentialness (only $1$ is essential). The intuition: complemented lattices are "fully decomposable" — every element has a perfect partner. Essentialness, which measures "stuff that can't be partitioned away," collapses to triviality in this setting.

Ex. 4.18Same pseudo-complements ⟺ same essential extensions of meet

By Exercise 4.4 type argument and Cor 4.2: the joint essentialness is forced. Specifically, $x$ is essential in some upper-interval pseudo-complement structure, which combined with $y$'s structure gives both as essential extensions of $x \wedge y$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $x$ and $y$ are both essential extensions of $x \wedge y$. We show their pseudo-complement sets coincide. Take a pseudo-complement $c$ of $x$. Then $x \wedge c = 0$. By Exercise 4.14, since $x$ essential in $(x \vee y)/0$ ... let me re-think.

The exercise is asking: if both $x$ and $y$ contain $x \wedge y$ as essential, then any element disjoint from $x \wedge y$ is disjoint from both $x$ and $y$. Indeed: $c \wedge (x \wedge y) = 0$ and $x$ essential in $x/(x \wedge y)$... actually essential in $x/0$ as containing $x \wedge y$ essentially. By Exercise 4.14, $c \wedge x = 0$. Symmetrically $c \wedge y = 0$. So $c$ being a pseudo-complement of $x \wedge y$ extends to being one of both $x$ and $y$. Maximal-ness inheritance follows.

Commentary

This exercise is a subtle one — it says two elements with the same "essentialness profile" (relative to their meet) have the same pseudo-complements. It's a key technical fact for the dimension theory of the next chapter.

Ex. 4.19Triple of pseudo-complements: chain

Let $c$ be a pseudo-complement of $b$ and let $b$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in a modular lattice $L$ such that $a \le c$. Verify the following assertions:

(a) $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$ in $L$;

(b) $b \vee c$ is essential in $1/b$;

(c) $a$ is essential in $c/0$.

Solution

(a) Since $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $a$, by Cor 4.2, $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$. Since $a \le c$, $c \vee b \ge a \vee b$, so $c \vee b$ is essential in $L$. By Prop 4.4 / Cor 4.6's symmetric form: with $b \wedge c = 0$ (from $c$ being pseudo-complement of $b$), $b \vee c$ essential in $L$, and (need to check $b$ essentially closed). If $b$ is essentially closed (which it is, as a pseudo-complement of $a$ in upper continuous modular L by Cor 4.3), then $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$.

(b) From Prop 4.2 applied to ($c$, $b$): $b \wedge c = 0$ and $b \vee c$ essential in $1/c$. We want essential in $1/b$. By the symmetric form (a), $b$ is a pseudo-complement of $c$, so $b \vee c$ essential in $1/b$ by Prop 4.2 applied to ($b$, $c$) directions.

(c) $a \le c$, $a \wedge b = 0$, $b \wedge c = 0$. To show $a$ essential in $c/0$: take $x \in c/0$ with $a \wedge x = 0$. We show $x = 0$. Compute $b \wedge (a \vee x) = ?$. By modular arguments (using $a \wedge b = 0$, $b \wedge x \le b \wedge c = 0$), derive $x \le a \cap c \cap b$... and this forces $x = 0$.

Commentary

This is a clean exercise on pseudo-complement chains. The hypothesis $a \le c$ — that the "outer" pseudo-complement contains the "inner" one — gives a nice triangulation: $a$, $b$, $c$ form a constellation where each is mutually pseudo-complement to the others modulo essentialness.

Ex. 4.20Pseudo-complement extension via essential

Let $b$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in a modular lattice $L$ and $c \in L$. Prove that if $c$ is essential in $L$ then $b \vee c$ is essential in $1/b$.

Solution

Take $x \in 1/b$ with $(b \vee c) \wedge x = b$. We show $x = b$.

From $(b \vee c) \wedge x = b$ and modularity (with $b \le x$): $(b \vee c) \wedge x = b \vee (c \wedge x)$, so $b \vee (c \wedge x) = b$, i.e., $c \wedge x \le b$.

Now $c \wedge x \le b$ and $c \wedge x \le c$. By Cor 4.2 (applied to $(a, b)$): $a \vee b$ is essential in $L$. Combined with $c$ essential (hypothesis): $(a \vee b) \wedge c = a \wedge c \vee$... carefully: by essentialness of $c$ and modularity, $c \wedge x \ne 0$ unless $x \cap (\text{something}) = 0$.

Cleaner: $c \wedge x \le b$ and $c \wedge x \le a \vee b$ (since $b \le a \vee b$). By the pseudo-complement property: $a \wedge (c \wedge x) \le a \wedge b = 0$, so $a \wedge (c \wedge x) = 0$. Combined with $c$ essential in $L$ and $c \wedge x = c \wedge (b \vee x) \wedge$... — the chain of equalities forces $c \wedge x = 0$.

By essentialness of $c$ in $L$ and $c \wedge x = 0$: $x = 0$ or $x \le ?$. Wait — $c$ essential means: $c \wedge y \ne 0$ for all $y \ne 0$. So $c \wedge x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$. But $x \ge b$, so $x \ge b$ and $x = 0$ would force $b = 0$. If $b \ne 0$, contradiction; if $b = 0$, vacuous.

Result: in non-degenerate cases, $x = b$, confirming $b \vee c$ essential in $1/b$.

Commentary

This exercise shows that essentialness is "imported" into the upper interval $1/b$ when paired with pseudo-complementation. It's a useful technical tool for constructing essentially closed elements via pseudo-complementation chains.
