Chapter Overview — from posets to lattices to closure systems

Chapter 1 ended with three poset invariants. Chapter 2 introduces the algebraic operations that turn posets into lattices, then reveals that "lattice" and "closure system" are essentially the same idea wearing two hats. The chapter closes with the Knaster-Tarski-Davis fixed-point theorem — a structural certificate that complete lattices ARE the right setting for fixed-point computations.

Chapter 1 dealt with ordered sets per se. Chapter 2 introduces the central objects of the book: semilattices, lattices, and complete lattices. Each adds more algebraic structure on top of the order. The chapter's arc has five movements:

  1. Semilattices. The simplest setting where binary glbs (or lubs) exist. Theorem 2.1 establishes the core duality: a semilattice is the same thing as a poset with all binary glbs. Theorem 2.2 is the Cayley-style certification — every semilattice embeds in its own order-ideal lattice.
  2. Lattices. Both glbs and lubs exist for pairs. Theorem 2.3 gives the order-theoretic / algebraic equivalence again. Caveat: lattices are NOT all sublattices of power sets — the distributive law fails for $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$. Theorem 2.4 (test for lattice: top + all meets) and Theorem 2.5 (complete meet semilattices are lattices) give practical lattice-construction tools.
  3. Closure systems, operators, rules — the trinity. Three different ways of looking at the same thing: closure systems (subsets closed under arbitrary intersection), closure operators (extensive, monotone, idempotent), closure rules ("if you have these, you must have that"). Theorem 2.6 establishes the three-way correspondence. Theorem 2.7 proves every complete lattice IS already a closure system on itself. The closing observation: "all important lattices arise this way."
  4. Join irreducibles + ACC/DCC representation. $J(\mathcal{L})$ — the join-irreducible elements. Lemma 2.8: in DCC lattices, every element is a finite join of join-irreducibles. Theorem 2.9: a lattice satisfying both ACC and DCC is isomorphic to the closure-rule lattice $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ on $J(\mathcal{L})$ — a clean structure theorem.
  5. Completions. What's the smallest complete lattice extending a given poset? Two answers: order-ideal completion $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ and MacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ (= "completion by cuts," a generalisation of Dedekind cuts). Theorem 2.10 connects join-dense completions to closure operators on $\mathcal{P}$. Theorem 2.12: the lattice $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ of all join-dense completions has $\mathcal{O}$ at the bottom and $\mathcal{M}$ at the top.

The chapter closes with Theorem 2.13 (Knaster-Tarski-Davis): a lattice is complete iff every order-preserving map has a fixed point. The forward direction (Knaster-Tarski 1955) is foundational for denotational semantics, abstract interpretation, and recursion theory. The converse (Davis 1955) is a beautiful piece of order theory that justifies the equivalence as structural rather than coincidental.

Density warning. This chapter packs more into 14 pages than most lattice books spend an entire chapter on. The companion expands generously, but readers should expect to go slow. The closure-trinity (s6) and the completions (s9–s10) are the conceptual hinges; everything else fans out from them.

New in Chapter 2 — full term-cards

NewSemilattice

An algebra $\mathcal{S} = (S, *)$ satisfying idempotence ($x * x = x$), commutativity ($x * y = y * x$), associativity ($x * (y * z) = (x * y) * z$). Equivalent (Theorem 2.1) to a poset where every pair has a g.l.b. (meet semilattice) — or l.u.b. (join semilattice). The simplest algebraic structure that has the lattice flavour.

NewMeet / join semilattice

If $* = $ glb, the semilattice is a meet semilattice (using $\wedge$ or $\cdot$). If $* = $ lub, it is a join semilattice (using $\vee$ or $+$). Same algebraic structure, dual order.

NewLattice

An algebra $\mathcal{L} = (L, \wedge, \vee)$ that is both a meet semilattice and a join semilattice, with the operations linked by the absorption laws: $x \wedge (x \vee y) = x$ and $x \vee (x \wedge y) = x$. Equivalent (Theorem 2.3) to a poset with all binary glbs and lubs.

NewDistributive law

$x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$. Holds in $\mathfrak{P}(X)$. Fails in $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$. This is why lattices in general do NOT embed in power-set lattices — see Birkhoff Ch 8 for the distributive case.

NewComplete lattice

An ordered set in which every subset $A$ has a glb $\bigwedge A$ and a lub $\bigvee A$. Theorem 2.5: complete meet semilattices are automatically complete lattices, with $\bigvee A = \bigwedge A^u$.

NewSublattice / subsemilattice

Subset closed under the operations. Caveat: the elements of a subsemilattice $\mathcal{T}$ form a semilattice in their own right, but if $\mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathcal{S}$ has the inherited order only (without inheriting the operations), the meets in $\mathcal{T}$ might be different — see footnote in Nation about $\{1,2\}, \{1,3\}, \emptyset$ in $\mathfrak{P}(\{1,2,3\})$.

NewInterval / quotient sublattice $[b, a] = a/b$

$\{x \in L : b \le x \le a\}$. Three notations are common: $[b, a]$ (modern), $a/b$ (Nation prefers $[b, a]$, but the alternative recurs in older literature). Special cases: $\downarrow a = a/0 = (a]$ (principal ideal), $\uparrow a = 1/a = [a)$ (principal filter).

New$A^u$ · $A^\ell$ — upper/lower bound sets

For $A \subseteq P$: $A^u = \{x \in P : x \ge a$ for all $a \in A\} = \bigcap_{a \in A} \uparrow a$ (set of all upper bounds). $A^\ell$ dual. Used pervasively in §E for completions: $\bigvee A = \bigwedge A^u$ when this exists.

NewClosure system

A collection $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $X$ closed under arbitrary intersection (including $\bigcap \emptyset = X \in \mathcal{C}$). The sets in $\mathcal{C}$ are closed. Examples: closed sets of a topology, subgroups of a group, subspaces of a vector space, convex subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$, order ideals of an ordered set.

NewClosure operator

A map $\Gamma : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to \mathfrak{P}(X)$ satisfying (1) $A \subseteq \Gamma(A)$ (extensive), (2) $A \subseteq B$ ⟹ $\Gamma(A) \subseteq \Gamma(B)$ (monotone), (3) $\Gamma(\Gamma(A)) = \Gamma(A)$ (idempotent). Examples: topological closure, span, generated subgroup, convex hull.

NewClosure rules

A collection $\Sigma$ of properties of the form "$x \in S$" or "$Y \subseteq S \Rightarrow z \in S$" with $x, z \in X$ and $Y \subseteq X$. A subset $D \subseteq X$ is $\Sigma$-closed if every rule is satisfied. The trinity (Theorem 2.6): closure systems ↔ closure operators ↔ closure rules — three faces of the same idea.

NewSubalgebra · Sg($B$)

A subset of an algebra $\mathcal{A} = \langle A, F, C \rangle$ closed under all operations $f \in F$ and containing all constants $c \in C$. The closure rules are exactly the operation-closure conditions. $\text{Sg}(B) = \bigcap\{\mathcal{S} : \mathcal{S} \supseteq B$ subalgebra$\}$ — the subalgebra generated by $B$.

NewJoin irreducible

$q \in \mathcal{L}$ is join irreducible if $q = \bigvee F$ for finite $F$ implies $q \in F$ — equivalently, $q$ is NOT a join of strictly smaller elements. $J(\mathcal{L}) = $ set of all join-irreducibles. By convention $0 \notin J(\mathcal{L})$, since $0 = \bigvee \emptyset$ — but $J_0(\mathcal{L}) = J(\mathcal{L}) \cup \{0\}$ adds it back when needed.

NewCompletely join irreducible

$q$ is completely join irreducible if $q = \bigvee X$ for arbitrary (possibly infinite) $X$ implies $q \in X$. Stronger than (finite) join irreducible. $J^*(\mathcal{L}) \subseteq J(\mathcal{L})$, with equality under ACC. A completely join-irreducible $q$ has a unique lower cover $q_*= \bigvee\{x : x < q\}$.

NewCompletion of an ordered set

A pair $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete lattice and $\phi$ is an order embedding $\mathcal{P} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{L}$. The completion preserves order; the question is which other structure it preserves.

NewJoin dense

$Q \subseteq \mathcal{L}$ is join dense if every $x \in L$ satisfies $x = \bigvee\{q \in Q : q \le x\}$. Every element is a join of $Q$-elements below it. A completion $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ is join dense if $\phi(P)$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$.

NewOrder ideal completion $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$

The order ideals of $\mathcal{P}$ form a complete lattice under inclusion. The map $\phi(p) = \downarrow p$ is an order embedding. $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is the smallest join-dense completion (Theorem 2.12) and is always distributive.

NewMacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$

(Also called normal completion or completion by cuts — generalising Dedekind cuts.) The closure operator $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell$ — "lower bounds of all upper bounds." $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P}) = $ lattice of $M$-closed sets. The largest join-dense completion (Theorem 2.12). Preserves all existing meets AND joins of $\mathcal{P}$.

New$\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ — lattice of completions

The complete lattice of all join-dense completions of $\mathcal{P}$, equivalently the closure operators $\Gamma$ on $\mathcal{P}$ with $\Gamma(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$. Theorem 2.12: $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ has least element $\mathcal{O}$ and greatest element $\mathcal{M}$. Studied by Nation & Pogel, Order 1996.

NewKnaster-Tarski-Davis

Theorem 2.13: a lattice is complete ⟺ every order-preserving map $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}$ has a fixed point. (⟹) is the classical Knaster-Tarski (1928, 1955); (⟸) is Anne Davis's 1955 converse.

Deep dive — extended explainers

Deep dive

The closure trinity — three faces of one idea

Theorem 2.6 establishes a three-way correspondence between closure systems, closure operators, and closure rules. Each is the "right" formalism in different contexts:

FaceWhat it isBest for
Closure system $\mathcal{C}$Subsets of $X$ closed under arbitrary intersectionStatic description — "what counts as closed"
Closure operator $\Gamma$Map $\mathfrak{P}(X) \to \mathfrak{P}(X)$ extensive, monotone, idempotentDynamic description — "how to close a set"
Closure rules $\Sigma$Rules of the form "$Y \subseteq S \Rightarrow z \in S$"Inductive description — "what stays closed"

The correspondences are perfectly symmetric:

  • System → operator: $\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A) = \bigcap\{D \in \mathcal{C} : A \subseteq D\}$ — the smallest closed set containing $A$.
  • Operator → system: $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma = \{D : \Gamma(D) = D\}$ — the fixed points.
  • System → rules: take all rules satisfied by every member of the system.
  • Rules → system: $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma = \{D : D$ satisfies all rules$\}$.

The closing observation in Nation's chapter: "It is only a slight exaggeration to say that all important lattices arise in this way." Topology, algebra, logic, geometry — all major examples of lattices come from a closure structure on some underlying set. The three faces let you pick the formalism that matches your problem.

Deep dive

The MacNeille completion as the "smallest" completion

What's the right way to embed a poset $\mathcal{P}$ in a complete lattice? Two natural answers:

  1. Order-ideal completion $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$. Every order ideal becomes an element. The biggest "natural" completion in the sense that it has many elements that aren't represented in $\mathcal{P}$. Always distributive (a sublattice of $\mathfrak{P}(P)$).
  2. MacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$. Take only the order ideals of the form $A^\ell$ for $A \subseteq P$ — equivalently, fixed points of the closure operator $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell$. Smaller and tighter.

The MacNeille completion is "smallest" in a precise sense (Theorem 2.12): it is the largest join-dense completion, but it has the property that it preserves all existing meets and joins of $\mathcal{P}$ — so any structure $\mathcal{P}$ already had transfers exactly. In contrast, $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ preserves meets but only weakly preserves joins.

The genealogy:

  • Dedekind 1872. Constructed the real numbers from the rationals via cuts — pairs $(A, B)$ with $A \cup B = \mathbb{Q}$, $A \cap B = \emptyset$, and every $a \in A$ less than every $b \in B$.
  • MacNeille 1937. Generalised to arbitrary posets in his Trans. AMS paper "Partially Ordered Sets" — defining the cut $A = B^\ell$, $B = A^u$ for a general poset. The resulting completion is named after both: Dedekind-MacNeille completion.

The MacNeille completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ recovers $\mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, +\infty\}$ exactly. For a finite poset, $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ is itself finite and has a clean description in terms of antichains. For more on this lineage, see Stenström's Rings of Quotients for the algebraic side, and Davey-Priestley's Introduction to Lattices and Order for the order-theoretic side.

Deep dive

The Knaster-Tarski-Davis fixed-point theorem

Theorem 2.13 has three independent contributions across three decades:

  • Knaster (1928) proved the special case for the power-set lattice $\mathfrak{P}(X)$: every monotone $f : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to \mathfrak{P}(X)$ has a fixed point.
  • Tarski (1955) generalised to arbitrary complete lattices in A lattice-theoretical fixpoint theorem and its applications, Pacific J. Math. 5: every monotone $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}$ on a complete lattice has fixed points; in fact the set of fixed points is itself a complete lattice.
  • Davis (1955) proved the converse in A characterization of complete lattices, Pacific J. Math. 5: if every monotone $f$ has a fixed point, then $\mathcal{L}$ is complete. The converse is harder than the forward direction — Davis used a clever Zorn-style argument explored in §E3.

The theorem's significance:

  • Denotational semantics. The meaning of recursive definitions is the least fixed point. Scott domains and Plotkin's CPO theory rely on Knaster-Tarski (or its CPO-theoretic counterpart Kleene's fixed-point theorem).
  • Abstract interpretation. Patrick Cousot's framework for sound program analysis uses Knaster-Tarski + Galois connections to compute approximate program semantics.
  • Game theory. Tarski's theorem proves existence of pure-strategy Nash equilibria in supermodular games.
  • Recursion theory. Defining functions by transfinite recursion uses Knaster-Tarski on complete lattices of partial functions.
  • Foundations. Davis's converse certifies that completeness IS the right structural property — you can't drop it without losing the fixed-point guarantee.

For Prologos, this is foundational. The BSP scheduler's "all propagators reach fixpoint after finitely many rounds" is a special case of Kleene-style iteration over a complete lattice (the cell-value lattice). Knaster-Tarski certifies that the fixpoint exists; Kleene gives the iteration that finds it. See P5 below.

Part A · Semilattices

Algebra ↔ Poset · Cayley-style for semilattices

A semilattice is the simplest object with a "lattice" operation. Theorem 2.1 establishes the algebra ↔ order duality; Theorem 2.2 is the Cayley-style certification.

A1 Semilattices · Theorem 2.1 (algebra ↔ poset)

A semilattice is an idempotent commutative semigroup. The order $x \le y$ defined by $x * y = x$ makes it a poset with all binary glbs.
Definition · semilattice

A semilattice is an algebra $\mathcal{S} = (S, *)$ satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in S$:

  1. $x * x = x$ (idempotent)
  2. $x * y = y * x$ (commutative)
  3. $x * (y * z) = (x * y) * z$ (associative)

In other words, a semilattice is an idempotent commutative semigroup. The symbol $*$ can be replaced by any binary operation symbol, and in fact we will most often use one of $\vee, \wedge, +, \cdot$, depending on the setting.

Examples

The most natural example of a semilattice is $(\mathfrak{P}(X), \cap)$, or more generally any collection of subsets of $X$ closed under intersection. For instance, the semilattice $\mathcal{PO}(X)$ of partial orders on $X$ is naturally contained in $(\mathfrak{P}(X^2), \cap)$.

Theorem 2.1 · algebra ↔ poset duality

In a semilattice $\mathcal{S}$, define $x \le y$ if and only if $x * y = x$. Then $(S, \le)$ is an ordered set in which every pair of elements has a greatest lower bound. Conversely, given an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ with that property, define $x * y = $ g.l.b. $(x, y)$. Then $(P, *)$ is a semilattice.

Proof · why

Let $(S, *)$ be a semilattice; define $\le$ as above. We check that $\le$ is a partial order:

  1. Reflexive. $x * x = x$ implies $x \le x$.
  2. Antisymmetric. If $x \le y$ and $y \le x$, then $x = x * y = y * x = y$.
  3. Transitive. If $x \le y \le z$, then $x * z = (x * y) * z = x * (y * z) = x * y = x$, so $x \le z$.

Now $x * y$ is a lower bound: $(x * y) * x = x * (x * y) = (x * x) * y = x * y$, so $x * y \le x$; similarly $x * y \le y$.

To see it is the greatest lower bound, suppose $z \le x$ and $z \le y$. Then $z * (x * y) = (z * x) * y = z * y = z$, so $z \le x * y$. Hence $x * y$ is the g.l.b. of $\{x, y\}$.

The converse is a direct application of definitions: glb is associative, commutative, idempotent. ∎

Meet vs join semilattice

A semilattice with the above ordering is called a meet semilattice; the convention is to use $\wedge$ or $\cdot$ for the operation. In Figure 2.1 (in the notes), shapes (a) and (b) are meet semilattices, while (c) fails (one of its elements has multiple incomparable upper bounds without unique glb).

Sometimes it is more natural to use the dual order, setting $x \ge y$ iff $x * y = x$. Then $\mathcal{S}$ is called a join semilattice, and the operation is denoted $\vee$ or $+$. Same algebraic structure, dual order.

Intuition · two definitions, same structure

Theorem 2.1 is the semilattice analogue of Theorem 1.1 of Călugăreanu Ch 1 (algebra ↔ order definition of lattices). Three observations:

  1. Idempotent commutative semigroups ARE meet semilattices. The classical "abstract algebra" definition (semigroup axioms) and the "concrete order" definition (every pair has glb) are the same.
  2. The order is canonical. Given $*$, the order $\le$ is uniquely determined by $x \le y \iff x * y = x$. Going back, the operation is also uniquely determined by $\le$.
  3. Generalises beyond power sets. The most natural example $(\mathfrak{P}(X), \cap)$ has every $\cap$ being a glb under $\subseteq$. But any structure that's an "intersection-closed family" works the same way: subgroups under intersection, subspaces under intersection, etc.

A2 Subsemilattices · homomorphisms · Theorem 2.2 (Cayley-style)

Substructures and morphisms in the semilattice category. Theorem 2.2: every meet semilattice embeds in $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S})$ via the principal-ideal map.
Subsemilattice · homomorphism · isomorphism

A subsemilattice of $\mathcal{S}$ is a subset $T \subseteq S$ closed under the operation $*$ of $\mathcal{S}$: $x, y \in T \Rightarrow x * y \in T$.

A homomorphism between two semilattices is a map $h : \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{T}$ with $h(x * y) = h(x) * h(y)$.

An isomorphism is a homomorphism that is one-to-one and onto. Note: because the operation is determined by the order and vice versa, two semilattices are isomorphic iff they are isomorphic as ordered sets.

Caveat · subsemilattice vs sub-poset

It is NOT enough that the elements of $T$ form a semilattice under the inherited ordering $\le$. For example, the sets $\{1, 2\}$, $\{1, 3\}$ and $\emptyset$ do NOT form a subsemilattice of $(\mathfrak{P}(\{1, 2, 3\}), \cap)$: in the parent, $\{1, 2\} \cap \{1, 3\} = \{1\}$, not $\emptyset$. The $T$-meet would be $\emptyset$ (the only element of $T$ below both), which differs from the parent meet. So $T$ inherits the order, but the operation in $T$ might disagree with the parent operation when restricted.

The right requirement is closure under the parent operation, not just under the parent order.

Theorem 2.2 · the Cayley-style theorem for semilattices

Let $\mathcal{S}$ be a meet semilattice. Define $\phi : S \to \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S})$ by

$$\phi(x) = \{y \in S : y \le x\} = \downarrow x.$$

Then $\mathcal{S}$ is isomorphic to $(\phi(\mathcal{S}), \cap)$.

Proof · why

We already know $\phi$ is an order embedding by Theorem 1.1 (Cayley for posets). Moreover,

$$\phi(x \wedge y) = \phi(x) \cap \phi(y)$$

because $x \wedge y$ is the greatest lower bound of $x$ and $y$, so $z \le x \wedge y$ iff $z \le x$ and $z \le y$ — i.e., $z \in \phi(x) \cap \phi(y)$. Hence $\phi$ is a semilattice homomorphism that is one-to-one. ∎

Intuition · the order-ideal lattice

The collection of all order ideals of a meet semilattice $\mathcal{S}$ forms a semilattice $\mathcal{O}(S)$ under set intersection. Each principal ideal $\downarrow x = \phi(x)$ is itself an order ideal, and the map $x \mapsto \phi(x)$ embeds $\mathcal{S}$ in $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S})$ as a subsemilattice.

This is the semilattice version of Theorem 1.1 from Ch 1: there, every poset embedded in $\mathfrak{P}(\mathcal{Q})$ via the same map. Here, with extra algebraic structure, the embedding is into the smaller order-ideal lattice $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S}) \subseteq \mathfrak{P}(S)$. The embedding preserves meets — the additional algebraic content of "semilattice" beyond "poset" is exactly the meet operation, and that's what gets added to the embedding.

Part B · Lattices

Theorem 2.3 (lattice as algebra) · Theorem 2.4 · Theorem 2.5 (complete lattices)

Adding the join to the meet. Lattices fail to embed in power-set lattices in general (the distributive law obstructs $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$). Tests for being a lattice; complete lattices and the meet-semilattice construction.

B1 Lattices · Theorem 2.3 · the $\mathcal{M}_3$/$\mathcal{N}_5$ caveat

A lattice is an algebra with both meet and join, linked by absorption. Theorem 2.3 lifts the algebra ↔ order duality. The distributive law holds in $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ but fails in general — so lattices have no clean power-set embedding theorem.
Definition · lattice

A lattice is an algebra $\mathcal{L} = (L, \wedge, \vee)$ satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in L$:

  1. $x \wedge x = x$ and $x \vee x = x$,
  2. $x \wedge y = y \wedge x$ and $x \vee y = y \vee x$,
  3. $x \wedge (y \wedge z) = (x \wedge y) \wedge z$ and $x \vee (y \vee z) = (x \vee y) \vee z$,
  4. $x \wedge (x \vee y) = x$ and $x \vee (x \wedge y) = x$ (absorption).

The first three pairs say $\mathcal{L}$ is both a meet semilattice and a join semilattice. The fourth pair (absorption) ensures the two operations induce the SAME order on $L$. Without absorption, $\wedge$ and $\vee$ could induce conflicting orders.

Example · $(\mathfrak{P}(X), \cap, \cup)$

The power set lattice with intersection and union is a lattice. It satisfies the additional distributive law:

$$(D) \quad A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C).$$

The distributive law does NOT hold in all lattices. In Figure 2.2 (in Nation's notes), $x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ FAILS for $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$.

Theorem 2.3 · the algebra ↔ order duality for lattices

In a lattice $\mathcal{L}$, define $x \le y$ if and only if $x \wedge y = x$. Then $(L, \le)$ is an ordered set in which every pair of elements has a greatest lower bound and a least upper bound. Conversely, given an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ with that property, define $x \wedge y = $ g.l.b. $(x, y)$ and $x \vee y = $ l.u.b. $(x, y)$. Then $(P, \wedge, \vee)$ is a lattice.

Proof · why · sketch

The crucial observation is that, in a lattice, $x \wedge y = x$ if and only if $x \vee y = y$ — by the absorption laws. So the order defined via $\wedge$ matches the order defined via $\vee$. The rest is a straightforward extension of Theorem 2.1 (which handles the meet side; the join side is dual).

Specifically: from the meet semilattice axioms (Thm 2.1) we know $\wedge$ gives a poset with binary glbs. The join semilattice axioms give a poset with binary lubs. Absorption ensures the orders match. ∎

Caveat · no clean power-set embedding

Theorems 1.1 and 2.2 gave Cayley-style embeddings for posets and semilattices into power-set-like structures. The analogue for lattices FAILS: not every lattice embeds in $(\mathfrak{P}(X), \cap, \cup)$ for some $X$.

The obstruction is the distributive law (D). Power-set lattices satisfy (D); $\mathcal{M}_3$ and $\mathcal{N}_5$ do not. So $\mathcal{M}_3$ cannot embed in any $\mathfrak{P}(X)$.

The right replacement: lattices DO embed in partition lattices (lattices of equivalence relations on a set) — this is Whitman's 1946 theorem, treated in Nation Ch 4. Distributive lattices DO embed in $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ — Birkhoff 1933, treated in Nation Ch 8. The general lattice case is more subtle.

B2 Sublattices · interval notation · $A^u$, $A^\ell$ · Theorem 2.4

Substructures, intervals, and a clean test for when a poset is a lattice.
Sublattice · 0 · 1 · interval

The correct definitions of sublattice, homomorphism, and isomorphism for lattices are left to the reader (subset closed under both operations; map preserving both; bijection in both directions).

If a lattice has a least element, it is denoted $0$; the greatest element, if it exists, is denoted $1$.

Of special importance are the interval (or quotient) sublattices, with various notations:

  • $[b, a] = a/b = \{x \in L : b \le x \le a\}$
  • $\downarrow a = a/0 = (a] = \{x \in L : x \le a\}$ — the principal ideal
  • $\uparrow a = 1/a = [a) = \{x \in L : a \le x\}$ — the principal filter

Nation prefers $[b, a]$, $\downarrow a$, $\uparrow a$. The notations $a/0$ and $1/a$ are historical and used regardless of whether $\mathcal{L}$ actually has a $0$ or $1$.

Upper / lower bound sets $A^u$, $A^\ell$

For a subset $A$ of an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$:

$$A^u = \{x \in P : x \ge a \text{ for all } a \in A\} = \bigcap_{a \in A} \uparrow a.$$

Dually:

$$A^\ell = \{x \in P : x \le a \text{ for all } a \in A\} = \bigcap_{a \in A} \downarrow a.$$

The sets $A^u$ and $A^\ell$ are the upper and lower bound sets of $A$. They will be central in §E for completions.

When does a lub exist?

For a subset $A$ of a poset, when does $\bigvee A$ exist? Two conditions:

  1. $A^u$ must be nonempty (at least one upper bound). Holds if $\mathcal{P}$ has a greatest element.
  2. $A^u$ must have a greatest lower bound $z = \bigwedge A^u$. (This $z$ would automatically lie in $A^u$, so it's the LUB of $A$.)

If both hold, $\bigvee A = \bigwedge A^u$. This is a partial computation of joins from meets.

Theorem 2.4 · finite test for lattice

Let $\mathcal{S}$ be a finite meet semilattice with greatest element $1$. Then $\mathcal{S}$ is a lattice with the join operation defined by

$$x \vee y = \bigwedge \{x, y\}^u = \bigwedge (\uparrow x \cap \uparrow y).$$

Intuition · efficient lattice test

Theorem 2.4 gives a practical algorithm: "if a finite ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ has a greatest element and every pair of elements has a meet, then $\mathcal{P}$ is a lattice."

The dual version is equally useful: a finite ordered set with a least element and all pair-joins is a lattice. This means: for finite posets, you only need to check ONE side (meets or joins) plus a top/bottom element. The other side is automatic.

This is why finite-lattice algorithms can focus on, say, computing all meets — the joins follow. Computer algebra systems exploit this: "first find all 1-generated subalgebras Sg($a$), then compute joins of sets of these" (Nation's footnote 6, referring to Ralph Freese's UACalc software at uacalc.org).

B3 Complete lattices · Theorem 2.5

Every subset has a glb and lub. Theorem 2.5 lifts Theorem 2.4 to the infinite-with-glb case.
Complete lattice

A complete lattice is an ordered set $\mathcal{L}$ in which every subset $A$ has a greatest lower bound $\bigwedge A$ and a least upper bound $\bigvee A$.

Note: every finite lattice is complete (induct on subset size). Every complete lattice is a lattice with $0$ and $1$ (set $0 = \bigvee \emptyset$ and $1 = \bigwedge \emptyset$). The converse fails: $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ is complete; the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ form a lattice that is NOT complete. $\textbf{Sub}(\mathcal{G})$ is a complete lattice for any group $\mathcal{G}$.

Complete meet semilattice

A complete meet semilattice is an ordered set $\mathcal{S}$ with a greatest element and the property that every nonempty subset $A \subseteq S$ has a glb $\bigwedge A$. By convention, $\bigwedge \emptyset = 1$ (the greatest element of $\mathcal{S}$).

Theorem 2.5

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete meet semilattice, then $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete lattice with the join operation defined by

$$\bigvee A = \bigwedge A^u = \bigwedge \left( \bigcap_{a \in A} \uparrow a \right).$$

Intuition · why this matters for closure systems

Theorem 2.5 is about the LIFT from finite (Theorem 2.4) to the complete case. The result says: if you have all meets (including infinite) and a top element, you automatically have all joins, and you have a complete lattice.

This is precisely the structure of a closure system: closed sets of a closure operator are closed under arbitrary intersection, with the whole space $X$ as the top element. By Theorem 2.5, the lattice of closed sets is automatically a complete lattice. This is the unification we'll see in §C: complete lattices and closure systems are the same thing in two different presentations.

Part C · The closure trinity

Closure systems · closure operators · closure rules · Theorems 2.6, 2.7

Three faces of the same idea, equivalently captured by a closure system, a closure operator, or a set of closure rules. Every complete lattice IS a closure system — the unifying observation.

C1 Three faces of closure · Theorem 2.6

Closure systems = static; closure operators = dynamic; closure rules = inductive. Theorem 2.6 establishes the three-way equivalence — pick the formalism that matches your problem.
Closure system

A closure system on a set $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $X$ that is closed under arbitrary intersections (including the empty intersection, so $\bigcap \emptyset = X \in \mathcal{C}$). The sets in $\mathcal{C}$ are called closed sets.

By Theorem 2.5, the closed sets of a closure system form a complete lattice (under inclusion). Examples come immediately to mind:

  1. Closed subsets of a topological space.
  2. Subgroups of a group.
  3. Subspaces of a vector space.
  4. Convex subsets of Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  5. Order ideals of an ordered set.
Closure operator

A closure operator on a set $X$ is a map $\Gamma : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to \mathfrak{P}(X)$ satisfying, for all $A, B \subseteq X$:

  1. $A \subseteq \Gamma(A)$ (extensive)
  2. $A \subseteq B$ implies $\Gamma(A) \subseteq \Gamma(B)$ (monotone)
  3. $\Gamma(\Gamma(A)) = \Gamma(A)$ (idempotent)

The closure operators corresponding to the closure systems above are:

  1. $X$ a topological space, $\Gamma(A) = $ the topological closure of $A$.
  2. $\mathcal{G}$ a group, $\text{Sg}(A) = $ the subgroup generated by $A$.
  3. $\mathcal{V}$ a vector space, $\text{Span}(A) = $ the linear span of $A$.
  4. $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $H(A) = $ the convex hull of $A$.
  5. $\mathcal{P}$ an ordered set, $\mathcal{O}(A) = $ the order ideal generated by $A$.

For a closure operator, a set $D$ is called closed if $\Gamma(D) = D$, or equivalently $D = \Gamma(A)$ for some $A$.

Closure rules

A set of closure rules on $X$ is a collection $\Sigma$ of properties of subsets of the form:

$$x \in S \quad \text{or} \quad Y \subseteq S \implies z \in S$$

with $x, z \in X$ and $Y \subseteq X$. (The first form is a degenerate case with $Y = \emptyset$.) A subset $D \subseteq X$ is $\Sigma$-closed if every rule of $\Sigma$ holds in $D$.

The closure rules corresponding to our previous examples:

  1. All rules $Y \subseteq S \implies z \in S$ where $z$ is an accumulation point of $Y$.
  2. The rule $1 \in S$, the rule $x \in S \implies x^{-1} \in S$, and rules $\{x, y\} \subseteq S \implies xy \in S$ for $x, y \in G$.
  3. $0 \in S$ and the rules $\{x, y\} \subseteq S \implies ax + by \in S$ for $a, b$ scalars.
  4. For all $\bar{x}, \bar{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $0 < t < 1$, the rules $\{\bar{x}, \bar{y}\} \subseteq S \implies t\bar{x} + (1-t)\bar{y} \in S$.
  5. For all pairs $x < y$ in $\mathcal{P}$, the rule $y \in S \implies x \in S$.
Theorem 2.6 · the three-way correspondence
  1. If $\mathcal{C}$ is a closure system on $X$, then the map $\Gamma_\mathcal{C} : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to \mathfrak{P}(X)$ defined by

$$\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A) = \bigcap \{D \in \mathcal{C} : A \subseteq D\}$$

is a closure operator. Moreover, $\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A) = A$ if and only if $A \in \mathcal{C}$.

  1. If $\Gamma$ is a closure operator on $X$, let $\Sigma_\Gamma$ be the set of all rules

$$c \in S \quad \text{where } c \in \Gamma(\emptyset),$$

$$Y \subseteq S \implies z \in S \quad \text{with } z \in \Gamma(Y).$$

Then a set $D \subseteq X$ satisfies all the rules of $\Sigma_\Gamma$ if and only if $\Gamma(D) = D$.

  1. If $\Sigma$ is a set of closure rules on $X$, let $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ be the collection of all subsets of $X$ that satisfy all the rules of $\Sigma$. Then $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ is a closure system.
Proof · why · sketch

(1) System → operator: $\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A) \in \mathcal{C}$ for all $A \subseteq X$ by closure under intersection. The operator axioms are direct: extensive (all $D \supseteq A$ contribute), monotone (more $A$ ⟹ smaller intersection requirement), idempotent ($\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(\Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A)) = \Gamma_\mathcal{C}(A)$).

(2) Operator → rules: a set $D$ satisfies all rules of $\Sigma_\Gamma$ iff $\Gamma(D) \subseteq D$ (each rule says "if you contain $Y$, you must contain $\Gamma(Y)$"). Combined with extensivity ($D \subseteq \Gamma(D)$), this gives $\Gamma(D) = D$.

(3) Rules → system: $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ is closed under arbitrary intersection — if each $D_i$ satisfies all rules, then so does $\bigcap D_i$ (because rules are universally quantified). ∎

Intuition · the three-way table

The collection of all closed sets of a closure operator forms a complete lattice. The lattice operations are:

$$\bigwedge_{i \in I} D_i = \bigcap_{i \in I} D_i, \qquad \bigvee_{i \in I} D_i = \Gamma\left(\bigcup_{i \in I} D_i\right).$$

The meet is set-theoretic intersection (closure rules are preserved). The join is the closure of the union.

Examples:

  • Topology: meet = intersection, join = closure of union.
  • Subgroup lattice: meet = intersection, join = subgroup generated by union.
  • Order ideals: meet = intersection, join = union (already an order ideal — exceptional).

The order-ideal lattice is exceptional because the union of order ideals is already an order ideal, so the closure operator is trivial (just the union). This is the "easy" closure system.

C2 Subalgebra example · Theorem 2.7 · Galois connections (forward reference)

Sg as a closure operator. Theorem 2.7: every complete lattice IS a closure system on itself, via $\Delta(A) = \{x : x \le \bigvee A\}$ — the slogan-like result that motivates the rest of the chapter.
Subalgebra · Sg generated by $B$

If $\mathcal{A} = \langle A, F, C \rangle$ is an algebra (universe + operations + constants), then $S \subseteq A$ is a subalgebra of $\mathcal{A}$ if (i) $c \in S$ for every constant $c \in C$, and (ii) for every basic operation $f \in F$ and $\{s_1, \dots, s_n\} \subseteq S$, we have $f(s_1, \dots, s_n) \in S$.

These are closure rules, so the intersection of any collection of subalgebras of $\mathcal{A}$ is again one. For a subset $B \subseteq A$, define

$$\text{Sg}(B) = \bigcap \{\mathcal{S} : \mathcal{S} \text{ is a subalgebra of } \mathcal{A} \text{ and } B \subseteq \mathcal{S}\}.$$

By Theorem 2.6, Sg is a closure operator, and $\text{Sg}(B)$ is the subalgebra generated by $B$. The corresponding lattice of closed sets is $\mathcal{C}_\text{Sg} = \textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$, the lattice of subalgebras of $\mathcal{A}$.

Theorem 2.7 · every complete lattice is a closure system

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete lattice, define a closure operator $\Delta$ on $L$ by

$$\Delta(A) = \{x \in L : x \le \bigvee A\}.$$

Then $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{C}_\Delta$.

The isomorphism $\varphi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{C}_\Delta$ is just given by $\varphi(x) = \downarrow x$.

Intuition · the closing slogan

Theorem 2.7 says: every complete lattice IS a closure system on itself. Concretely, take $X = L$ as the underlying set, define $\Delta(A) = \downarrow \bigvee A$. The closed sets of $\Delta$ are exactly the principal ideals $\downarrow x$, which form a lattice isomorphic to $\mathcal{L}$.

This is the unification that justifies "all important lattices arise from closure systems." Going from a complete lattice to a closure system is automatic. Going the other way (closure system → complete lattice = closed sets) is straightforward by Theorem 2.5. So:

complete lattices ↔ closure systems

is a bona fide equivalence. Every theorem about closure operators is a theorem about complete lattices, and vice versa.

Part D · Join irreducibles

Lemma 2.8 · Theorem 2.9 — chain-condition representation

Some elements are "indecomposable": they're not joins of strictly smaller elements. These join irreducibles generate the lattice (DCC). Under both ACC and DCC, they generate it via closure rules.

D1 Join irreducibles · Lemma 2.8 · Theorem 2.9

$J(\mathcal{L})$ is the structural skeleton of $\mathcal{L}$. Lemma 2.8: under DCC, every element is a finite join from $J(\mathcal{L})$. Theorem 2.9: under ACC + DCC, $\mathcal{L}$ is the closure-rule lattice on $J(\mathcal{L})$.
Join irreducible · $J(\mathcal{L})$

An element $q$ of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is called join irreducible if $q = \bigvee F$ for a finite set $F$ implies $q \in F$. Equivalently, $q$ is not the join of strictly smaller elements.

The set of all join-irreducible elements is denoted $J(\mathcal{L})$.

Note: by this definition, if $\mathcal{L}$ has a least element $0$, then $0 \notin J(\mathcal{L})$, because $0 = \bigvee \emptyset$. To include zero, write $J_0(\mathcal{L}) = J(\mathcal{L}) \cup \{0\}$.

Footnote (Nation): this convention is not universal. Some authors define $q$ join irreducible by "$q = r \vee s$ implies $q = r$ or $q = s$," which is equivalent for nonzero elements but includes 0 in the definition.

Lemma 2.8

If a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies the DCC, then every element of $\mathcal{L}$ is a join of finitely many join irreducible elements.

Proof · why

Suppose some element of $\mathcal{L}$ is not a join of join-irreducible elements. Let $x$ be a minimal such element (using DCC). Then $x$ is not itself join irreducible, nor is it zero. So $x = \bigvee F$ for some finite set $F$ of elements strictly below $x$. By the minimality of $x$, each $f \in F$ is the join of a finite set $G_f \subseteq J(\mathcal{L})$. Then $x = \bigvee_{f \in F} \bigvee G_f$, a finite join of join irreducibles — contradiction. ∎

Completely join irreducible · lower cover $q_*$

An element $q$ of a complete lattice is completely join irreducible if $q = \bigvee X$ for arbitrary $X \subseteq L$ (possibly infinite) implies $q \in X$.

If $q$ is completely join irreducible, then $q$ has a unique lower cover:

$$q_* = \bigvee \{x \in L : x < q\}.$$

Moreover, $x < q$ implies $x \le q_*$.

Let $J^*(\mathcal{L})$ denote the set of completely join irreducible elements. In general, $J^*(\mathcal{L}) \subseteq J(\mathcal{L})$, but for lattices satisfying the ACC, equality holds.

Theorem 2.9 · the ACC + DCC representation

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice satisfying the ACC and DCC. Let $\Sigma$ be the set of all closure rules on $J(\mathcal{L})$ of the form

$$F \subseteq S \implies q \in S$$

where $q$ is join irreducible, $F$ is a finite subset of $J(\mathcal{L})$, and $q \le \bigvee F$. (Include the degenerate cases $p \in S \implies q \in S$ for $q \le p$ in $J(\mathcal{L})$.) Then $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the lattice $\mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ of $\Sigma$-closed sets.

Proof · why

Define order-preserving maps $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{C}_\Sigma$ and $g : \mathcal{C}_\Sigma \to \mathcal{L}$ by

$$f(x) = \downarrow x \cap J(\mathcal{L}), \qquad g(S) = \bigvee S.$$

Then $gf(x) = x$ for all $x \in L$ by Lemma 2.8 (every $x$ is a join of finitely many join-irreducibles below it). On the other hand, $fg(S) = S$ for any $\Sigma$-closed set $S$, because by ACC we have $\bigvee S = \bigvee F$ for some finite $F \subseteq S$, which puts every join irreducible $q \le \bigvee F$ in $S$ by the closure rules. ∎

Intuition · structure theorem for ACC + DCC lattices

Theorem 2.9 says: every lattice with both chain conditions (= every chain finite, by Ch 1 Ex 6) is — up to isomorphism — the closure-rule lattice on its set of join irreducibles.

This gives a powerful structure theorem. To compute or describe such a lattice, you only need:

  1. The poset of join irreducibles $J(\mathcal{L})$ (with its inherited order from $\mathcal{L}$).
  2. The closure rules: for each pair $(F, q)$ with $q \le \bigvee F$, the rule $F \subseteq S \Rightarrow q \in S$.

For finite distributive lattices, this simplifies to Birkhoff's theorem (Nation Ch 8): the closure rules are trivial (only the order rules), so $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{L}))$ — every finite distributive lattice is the order-ideal lattice of its poset of join-irreducibles. The non-distributive case has nontrivial closure rules.

Algorithm: to find $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ for a finite algebra $\mathcal{A}$, first find all 1-generated subalgebras Sg($a$) for $a \in A$ (these include all join-irreducibles), then compute joins of sets of these via the closure rules.

Part E · Completions

Order-ideal · MacNeille · $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ · Knaster-Tarski-Davis (Theorems 2.10, 2.11, 2.12, 2.13)

What's the smallest complete lattice extending a given poset? Two natural answers: order-ideal completion and MacNeille completion. Both are special cases of "join-dense completion." The chapter closes with the Knaster-Tarski-Davis fixed-point theorem.

E1 Completions · order-ideal · MacNeille · Theorem 2.10

Embed $\mathcal{P}$ in a complete lattice. Order-ideal: every order ideal becomes an element. MacNeille: only "cuts" $A^u$ → $(A^u)^\ell$ become elements. The smaller, tighter completion.
Completion · join dense

An ordered set need not be complete. We say that a pair $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ is a completion of the ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ if $\mathcal{L}$ is a complete lattice and $\phi$ is an order embedding $\mathcal{P} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{L}$.

A subset $Q$ of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is join dense if for every $x \in L$,

$$x = \bigvee \{q \in Q : q \le x\}.$$

A completion $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ of $\mathcal{P}$ is join dense if $\phi(P)$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$ — every element of $\mathcal{L}$ is a join of $\mathcal{P}$-elements below it.

It is not hard to see that every completion of $\mathcal{P}$ contains a join-dense completion (the join-subsemilattice generated by $\phi(P)$). Hence we may reasonably restrict our attention to join-dense completions.

Example · order-ideal completion $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$

Order ideals are the closed sets of the closure operator on $P$ given by

$$O(A) = \bigcup_{a \in A} \downarrow a,$$

and the embedding $\phi$ is given by $\phi(p) = \downarrow p$. Note that the union of order ideals is again an order ideal, so $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ obeys the distributive law (D). Always distributive.

Example · MacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$

The MacNeille completion, a.k.a. normal completion or completion by cuts, generalises Dedekind cuts. Recall:

$$S^u = \{x \in P : x \ge s \text{ for all } s \in S\}, \quad T^\ell = \{y \in P : y \le t \text{ for all } t \in T\}.$$

The MacNeille completion is the lattice of closed sets of the closure operator on $P$ given by

$$M(A) = (A^u)^\ell.$$

I.e., $M(A)$ is the set of all lower bounds of all upper bounds of $A$. Note $M(A)$ is an order ideal of $\mathcal{P}$. Again the map $\phi(p) = \downarrow p$ embeds $\mathcal{P}$ into $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$.

Intuition · what each completion preserves

Every join-dense completion preserves all existing meets in $\mathcal{P}$: if $A \subseteq P$ has a glb $b$ in $\mathcal{P}$, then $\phi(b) = \bigwedge \phi(A)$ in $\mathcal{L}$.

The MacNeille completion has the additional property that it preserves all existing JOINs in $\mathcal{P}$: if $A$ has an lub $c$ in $\mathcal{P}$, then $\phi(c) = M(A) = \bigvee \phi(A)$ in $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$. This makes MacNeille the "tightest" completion in a structural sense — it adds NEW elements only where $\mathcal{P}$ was missing meets/joins.

The order-ideal completion, by contrast, adds many "redundant" elements: for any antichain $A$, $O(A)$ becomes a new element of $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ even if $\mathcal{P}$ already had a join for $A$. So $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is generally larger than $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$.

Theorem 2.10 · join-dense completions ↔ closure operators

Let $\mathcal{P}$ be an ordered set. If $\Phi$ is a closure operator on $P$ such that $\Phi(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$ for all $p \in P$, then $(\mathcal{C}_\Phi, \phi)$ is a join-dense completion of $\mathcal{P}$, where $\phi(p) = \downarrow p$.

Conversely, if $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ is a join-dense completion of $\mathcal{P}$, then the map $\Phi$ defined by

$$\Phi(A) = \{q \in P : \phi(q) \le \bigvee_{a \in A} \phi(a)\}$$

is a closure operator on $P$, $\Phi(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$ for all $p \in P$, and $\mathcal{C}_\Phi \cong \mathcal{L}$.

Proof · why · sketch

Forward: $(\mathcal{C}_\Phi, \phi)$ is a completion (Theorem 2.6). Join-dense because every closed set $C$ is the union of $\downarrow p = \Phi(\{p\})$ for $p \in C$:

$$C = \bigvee \{\Phi(\{p\}) : p \in C\} = \bigvee \{\downarrow p : p \in C\} = \bigvee \{\phi(p) : \phi(p) \le C\}.$$

Converse: $\Phi$ defined as above satisfies $A \subseteq \Phi(A)$, monotonicity, idempotence — direct verifications. Then $\mathcal{C}_\Phi \cong \mathcal{L}$ via the natural maps $f(A) = \bigvee_{a \in A} \phi(a)$ and $g(x) = \{p \in P : \phi(p) \le x\}$. ∎

E2 Lattice of completions $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ · Lemma 2.11 · Theorem 2.12

All join-dense completions form a complete lattice $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ — itself an interval in the lattice of all closure operators. The bottom is $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$; the top is $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$.
Lemma 2.11 · order on closure operators

Let $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$ be closure operators on a set $X$. The following are equivalent:

  1. $\Gamma(A) \subseteq \Delta(A)$ for all $A \subseteq X$.
  2. $\Delta(C) = C$ implies $\Gamma(C) = C$ for all $C \subseteq X$.
Proof · why

If (1) and $\Delta(C) = C$, then $C \subseteq \Gamma(C) \subseteq \Delta(C) = C$, whence $\Gamma(C) = C$.

If (2), then $\Gamma(A) \subseteq \Gamma(\Delta(A)) = \Delta(A)$ — the last equality because $\Delta(A)$ is $\Delta$-closed, so $\Gamma$-closed, so $\Gamma(\Delta(A)) = \Delta(A)$. ∎

$\text{Cl}(X)$ · $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$

Let $\text{Cl}(X)$ be the set of all closure operators on $X$, ordered by $\Gamma \le \Delta$ if the conditions of Lemma 2.11 hold. For any collection $\Gamma_i$ ($i \in I$) of closure operators, the operator $\bigwedge_{i \in I} \Gamma_i$ defined by

$$\left(\bigwedge_{i \in I} \Gamma_i\right)(A) = \bigcap_{i \in I} \Gamma_i(A)$$

is a closure operator. Hence $\text{Cl}(X)$ is a complete lattice.

Now let $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ be the collection of all closure operators on $\mathcal{P}$ such that $\Gamma(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$ for all $p \in P$. By Theorem 2.10, $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ is in bijection with the join-dense completions of $\mathcal{P}$. It is a complete sublattice — in fact, an interval — of $\text{Cl}(P)$.

Theorem 2.12 · structure of $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$

$\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ is a complete lattice with least element $O$ (the order-ideal closure operator) and greatest element $M$ (the MacNeille closure operator).

Proof · why

The condition $\Gamma(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$ implies $O(A) \subseteq \Gamma(A)$ for all $A \subseteq P$ (because $\Gamma(\{p\}) \subseteq \Gamma(A)$ for $p \in A$ by monotonicity, so the order ideal generated by $A$ is in $\Gamma(A)$). This makes $O$ the least element of $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$.

For any $\Gamma \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$: if $b \ge a$ for all $a \in A$, then $\downarrow b = \Gamma(\{b\}) \supseteq \Gamma(A)$. So $\Gamma(A) \subseteq \bigcap_{b \in A^u} \downarrow b = (A^u)^\ell = M(A)$. Hence $M$ is the greatest element. ∎

Intuition · what $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ tells us

Theorem 2.12 has structural significance:

  1. Both completions live in a unifying framework. The order-ideal and MacNeille completions are not separate objects — they are the bottom and top of a single complete lattice $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ of all join-dense completions.
  2. Many completions in between. Any closure operator $\Gamma$ on $P$ with $\Gamma(\{p\}) = \downarrow p$ gives a completion in $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$. There are many such operators "between" $O$ and $M$.
  3. Two natural extremes. $O$ is the "easiest" (just order-ideal closure); $M$ is the "tightest" (preserves all existing meets and joins). Choosing between them is a matter of which structural property you care about.

The structure of $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ was investigated by Nation & Pogel in their 1996 paper "The lattice of completions of an ordered set" (Order 14, 1–7). For finite $\mathcal{P}$, $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ has been computed for small cases and has interesting combinatorial structure.

E3 Knaster-Tarski-Davis (Theorem 2.13)

A lattice is complete iff every order-preserving self-map has a fixed point. Forward: Tarski 1955. Converse: Davis 1955. The chapter's punchline.
Theorem 2.13 (Knaster-Tarski-Davis)

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is complete if and only if every order preserving map $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}$ has a fixed point.

Proof of (⟹) (Tarski 1955)

Given a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ and an order preserving map $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}$, put

$$A = \{x \in L : f(x) \ge x\}.$$

Note $A$ is nonempty as $0 \in A$ (since $f(0) \ge 0$ trivially). Let $a = \bigvee A$. Since $a \ge x$ for all $x \in A$, $f(a) \ge \bigvee_{x \in A} f(x) \ge \bigvee_{x \in A} x = a$. Thus $a \in A$. But then $a \le f(a)$ implies $f(a) \le f^2(a)$, so $f(a) \in A$, whence $f(a) \le a$. Therefore $f(a) = a$. ∎

Proof of (⟸) (Davis 1955)

Conversely, let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice that is not a complete lattice. We construct an order-preserving $f$ with no fixed point.

Claim 1. Either $\mathcal{L}$ has no $1$, or there exists a chain $C \subseteq \mathcal{L}$ that satisfies the ACC and has no meet.

Suppose $\mathcal{L}$ has a $1$ and every chain $C$ in $\mathcal{L}$ satisfying the ACC has a meet. We show every subset $S \subseteq L$ has a join, making $\mathcal{L}$ complete (by the dual of Theorem 2.5). Consider $S^u$, the set of all upper bounds of $S$ (nonempty since $1 \in L$). Let $\mathcal{P}$ denote the collection of all chains $C \subseteq S^u$ satisfying the ACC, ordered by $C_1 \le C_2$ if $C_1$ is a filter (dual ideal) of $C_2$.

The order on $\mathcal{P}$ insures that if $C_i$ ($i \in I$) is a chain of chains in $\mathcal{P}$, then $\bigcup_{i \in I} C_i \in \mathcal{P}$. Hence by Zorn's Lemma, $\mathcal{P}$ contains a maximal element $C_m$. By hypothesis $\bigwedge C_m$ exists in $\mathcal{L}$, say $\bigwedge C_m = a$. In fact, $a = \bigvee S$ — direct verification using maximality of $C_m$.

If $\mathcal{L}$ has $1$ and Claim 1 fails, then $\mathcal{L}$ is complete. So in the failing case, take $C = \emptyset$ or use the chain hypothesis.

The dualizing argument: if $\mathcal{L}$ has no $1$, exists a chain $D \subseteq C^\ell$ (= $\emptyset^\ell = \mathcal{L}$ here, but use the hypothesis) satisfying DCC with no meet. (Detailed argument in Nation's notes pp. 24–25.)

Construction of fixed-point-free $f$. For each $x \in L$, put $C(x) = \{c \in C : x \not\le c\}$ and $D(x) = \{d \in D : x \not\ge d\}$. We have shown one of these two sets is nonempty for each $x$. If $C(x) \ne \emptyset$, let $f(x)$ be its largest element (using ACC); otherwise let $f(x)$ be the least element of $D(x)$ (using DCC). Now for any $x \in L$, either $x \not\le f(x)$ or $x \not\ge f(x)$, so $f$ has no fixed point.

Order preservation: if $x \le y$, then $C(y) \subseteq C(x)$ (smaller "set of chain elements not above") — direct verification. So $f(x) \ge f(y)$ when both come from $C$; the cross-cases are handled by the structure. ∎

Intuition · why this is the chapter's punchline

Theorem 2.13 is a structural characterization. The forward direction (Tarski 1955) is the famous fixed-point theorem; the converse (Davis 1955) certifies that completeness IS the right hypothesis.

The proof of the converse is delicate — Davis used a clever Zorn-style argument to build a chain witness for the failure of completeness, then constructed a fixed-point-free map from it. The proof rewards multiple readings; Nation's note that "Exercise 12 indicates why the argument is necessarily a bit involved" is appropriate.

Practical use: Theorem 2.13 is used wherever we compute by iteration. Denotational semantics: the meaning of `let rec f x = ...` is the least fixed point of an order-preserving map on a complete partial order. Abstract interpretation: program analyses are computed as fixed points. Verification: Hoare logic's recursion rule gives the meaning of a recursive procedure as a fixed point.

For Prologos: the BSP scheduler iterates each propagator until quiescence. This is a Kleene iteration — closely related to but distinct from Knaster-Tarski. The fixed-point existence is guaranteed by completeness of cell-value lattices.

P3 Connection: MacNeille completion and Dedekind cuts

From Dedekind's 1872 construction of $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ to MacNeille's 1937 generalisation to arbitrary posets.
Connection · the Dedekind-MacNeille genealogy

Dedekind 1872. Richard Dedekind, in his book Stetigkeit und irrationale Zahlen (Continuity and Irrational Numbers), constructed the real numbers from the rationals using cuts. A Dedekind cut is a pair $(A, B)$ of subsets of $\mathbb{Q}$ with:

  1. $A \cup B = \mathbb{Q}$, $A \cap B = \emptyset$, both nonempty.
  2. Every $a \in A$ is less than every $b \in B$.
  3. $A$ has no greatest element (the "open" cut convention).

The set of all cuts forms a complete totally ordered set, and the rationals embed via $q \mapsto (\{r : r < q\}, \{r : r \ge q\})$. This recovers the standard $\mathbb{R}$.

MacNeille 1937. Holbrook Mann MacNeille, in Partially ordered sets (Trans. AMS 42, pp. 416–460), generalised the construction. For an arbitrary partial order $\mathcal{P}$, define a cut to be a pair $(A, B)$ of subsets of $\mathcal{P}$ with:

  1. $A^u = B$ (= every upper bound of $A$ is in $B$).
  2. $B^\ell = A$ (= every lower bound of $B$ is in $A$).

The cuts form a complete lattice with $\mathcal{P}$ embedded via $p \mapsto (\downarrow p, \uparrow p)$. This is the MacNeille completion $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$, equivalent to the closure operator $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell$ on $\mathfrak{P}(P)$.

For $\mathcal{P} = \mathbb{Q}$, MacNeille's construction recovers Dedekind's $\mathbb{R}$ (modulo the addition of $\pm \infty$). For arbitrary posets, $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ gives the smallest complete lattice containing $\mathcal{P}$ that preserves all existing meets and joins.

Why MacNeille is the "right" completion. Among all join-dense completions (Theorem 2.12: $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$), MacNeille is the maximum. This is structurally appropriate: MacNeille adds elements only where $\mathcal{P}$ was missing meets/joins, so it preserves $\mathcal{P}$'s existing structure exactly. Order-ideal $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is the minimum and adds redundant elements (every order ideal becomes new, even when already a join).

Modern uses of MacNeille completion: in lattice theory (this chapter), in order theory (Davey-Priestley), in domain theory (an alternative to ideal completion), and in formal concept analysis (Nation Appendix 3, since MacNeille completion of a poset is exactly the concept lattice of its order relation as a binary relation).

P4 Connection: Knaster-Tarski + denotational semantics

From Knaster 1928 to Tarski 1955 to Scott domains to abstract interpretation. The fixed-point theorem is the foundation of modern programming language semantics.
Connection · the Knaster-Tarski lineage

The fixed-point theorem has had remarkable scope:

  • Bronisław Knaster 1928: Special case for $\mathfrak{P}(X)$.
  • Alfred Tarski 1955: General case for complete lattices. A lattice-theoretical fixpoint theorem and its applications, Pacific J. Math. 5, 285–309. The standard reference.
  • Anne C. Davis 1955: Converse — completeness is necessary. A characterization of complete lattices, Pacific J. Math. 5, 311–319. Same volume as Tarski!
  • Stephen Cole Kleene 1952: Iterative version for continuous maps on dcpos: $\mu f = \bigsqcup_n f^n(\bot)$. Underlies the operational reading of recursion.
  • Dana Scott 1970s: Domains and continuous lattices for denotational semantics. The meaning of a recursive definition is the least fixed point of a continuous operator on a complete lattice (or domain).
  • Patrick Cousot 1977: Abstract interpretation framework. Galois connections + Knaster-Tarski + Kleene iteration give a unified theory of static program analysis.
  • Game theory (Zermelo, Topkis 1979): Existence of Nash equilibria in supermodular games via Knaster-Tarski.

Why this matters for programming language semantics. Consider `factorial` defined recursively:

let rec factorial n = if n = 0 then 1 else n * factorial (n - 1)

The "meaning" of `factorial` is not directly given — it appears on both sides of the equation. Scott's idea: define the operator $F$ on partial functions $\mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ by $F(f)(n) = $ if $n = 0$ then $1$ else $n \cdot f(n-1)$. The set of partial functions, ordered by graph inclusion ($f \sqsubseteq g$ iff $f$ extends $g$ on more inputs), forms a complete lattice. $F$ is monotone. By Knaster-Tarski, $F$ has fixed points; the LEAST fixed point IS factorial.

This trick — defining recursive things as least fixed points of monotone operators on complete lattices — pervades modern denotational semantics. Knaster-Tarski is the structural certificate that the least fixed point exists.

Davis's converse certifies that completeness IS the right hypothesis. You can't drop it. Every well-defined semantic operator on a complete lattice has a fixed point; if completeness fails, some operator fails to have one.

Ex. 2.2Lattice with 0 and ACC ⟹ complete
Prove that a lattice that has a 0 and satisfies the ACC is complete.

Strategy. Use Theorem 2.5 dual: complete join semilattice + bottom ⟹ complete lattice. Equivalent to showing every nonempty subset has a join (then apply Theorem 2.5).

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice with 0 satisfying ACC. Take $A \subseteq L$. Consider $\mathcal{U} = \{\bigvee F : F \subseteq A, F$ finite$\}$.

Each $\bigvee F$ exists (lattice has finite joins). The set $\mathcal{U}$ has a maximum element $\bigvee F_0$ by ACC. We claim $\bigvee F_0 = \bigvee A$:

  • $\bigvee F_0$ is an upper bound of $A$: for any $a \in A$, $\bigvee F_0 \le \bigvee F_0 \vee a = \bigvee(F_0 \cup \{a\}) \in \mathcal{U}$. By maximality, $\bigvee F_0 = \bigvee F_0 \vee a$, so $a \le \bigvee F_0$.
  • $\bigvee F_0$ is the least upper bound: any upper bound of $A$ is an upper bound of $F_0 \subseteq A$, hence $\ge \bigvee F_0$.

So $\bigvee A = \bigvee F_0$ exists for every $A \subseteq L$. With 0 already in $\mathcal{L}$ (so $\bigwedge \emptyset = 1$ via dual, but we need $\bigwedge A$ for $A \ne \emptyset$... but the dual setup with ACC gives this: actually for the meet, take any $A \ne \emptyset$, $A^\ell$ is nonempty (contains 0); take $\bigvee A^\ell$ via above). Hence $\mathcal{L}$ is complete. ∎

Ex. 2.3$\mathbb{Z}_4$ subgroup lattice + closure
For the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_4$, give explicitly the subgroup lattice, the closure operator Sg, and the closure rules for subgroups.

Subgroup lattice of $\mathbb{Z}_4$. $\mathbb{Z}_4 = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. Subgroups: $\{0\}$, $\{0, 2\}$ (= $2\mathbb{Z}_4$), $\mathbb{Z}_4$. So 3 subgroups, forming a 3-chain: $\{0\} \subset \{0, 2\} \subset \mathbb{Z}_4$.

Closure operator Sg. Sg($\emptyset$) = $\{0\}$ (the trivial subgroup contains identity). Sg($\{0\}$) = $\{0\}$. Sg($\{2\}$) = $\{0, 2\}$ (cyclic generated by 2). Sg($\{1\}$) = Sg($\{3\}$) = $\mathbb{Z}_4$. Sg($\{0, 2\}$) = $\{0, 2\}$. Sg($\{0, 1\}$) = Sg($\{0, 1, 2\}$) = etc. = $\mathbb{Z}_4$. Sg of any set containing 1 or 3 = $\mathbb{Z}_4$.

Closure rules. The rules for subgroups are:

  1. $0 \in S$ (the identity must be in any subgroup).
  2. For all $x \in \mathbb{Z}_4$: $x \in S \implies -x \in S$ (closed under inverse).
  3. For all $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}_4$: $\{x, y\} \subseteq S \implies x + y \in S$ (closed under operation).

Specialised to $\mathbb{Z}_4$: rule 1 gives 0; rule 2 with $x = 2$ keeps 2 (since $-2 \equiv 2$); rule 3 with $\{1, 1\} \subseteq S$ gives 2, then $\{1, 2\} \subseteq S$ gives 3, then $\{2, 2\} \subseteq S$ gives 0 — so any $S$ containing 1 quickly grows to $\mathbb{Z}_4$. ∎

Ex. 2.4Convex hull as closure operator
Define a closure operator $F$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ by the rules $\{\bar{x}, \bar{y}\} \subseteq S \implies t\bar{x} + (1-t)\bar{y} \in S$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Describe $F(A)$ for an arbitrary subset $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$. What is the geometric interpretation of $F$?

Strategy. The rule says "any line through two elements of $S$ is in $S$." This is stronger than convex hull (which requires $0 \le t \le 1$).

$F(A)$ = the smallest set containing $A$ and closed under "any line through two elements." This is the affine hull of $A$ — the smallest affine subspace containing $A$.

Concretely: if $A$ has $k$ affinely independent points, $F(A)$ is the $(k-1)$-dimensional affine subspace they span. If $A$ is a single point, $F(A) = A$. If $A$ has two distinct points, $F(A)$ is the line through them. If $A$ has 3 non-collinear points, $F(A)$ is the plane they span. Etc.

Note: Nation's Theorem 2.6 example (4) gives the convex hull, with the restriction $0 < t < 1$. Without this restriction (any $t \in \mathbb{R}$), we get the affine hull instead. ∎

Ex. 2.5Three-way join density characterisation
Prove that the following are equivalent for a subset $Q$ of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$.
(1) $Q$ is join dense in $\mathcal{L}$, i.e., $x = \bigvee\{q \in Q : q \le x\}$ for every $x \in L$.
(2) Every element of $L$ is a join of elements in $Q$.
(3) If $y < x$ in $\mathcal{L}$, then there exists $q \in Q$ with $q \le x$ but $q \not\le y$.

Strategy. Cycle (1) ⟹ (2) ⟹ (3) ⟹ (1).

(1) ⟹ (2): trivial; $\{q \in Q : q \le x\}$ is a join from $Q$.

(2) ⟹ (3): suppose $y < x$. Then $x = \bigvee R$ for some $R \subseteq Q$. We have $\bigvee R = x \not\le y$, so some $q \in R$ has $q \not\le y$. Also $q \le \bigvee R = x$.

(3) ⟹ (1): let $b = \bigvee\{q \in Q : q \le x\}$. Clearly $b \le x$. Suppose $b < x$. Then by (3), there is $q \in Q$ with $q \le x$ but $q \not\le b$. But $q \in \{q' \in Q : q' \le x\}$, so $q \le b$ — contradiction. Hence $b = x$. ∎

Ex. 2.6Generalised completion via closure on join-dense $X$
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a complete lattice, and let $X$ be a join-dense subset of $L$. Define a closure operator $\Gamma$ on $X$ by $\Gamma(S) = (\downarrow \bigvee S) \cap X$. Prove that $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma \cong \mathcal{L}$. (This generalises Theorems 2.7 and 2.9, and anticipates Theorem 3.3.)

Strategy. The closed sets are exactly the principal-ideals-restricted-to-$X$, which are in bijection with elements of $L$.

Define maps $f : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ by $f(x) = \downarrow x \cap X$, and $g : \mathcal{C}_\Gamma \to \mathcal{L}$ by $g(C) = \bigvee C$.

$gf(x) = \bigvee(\downarrow x \cap X) = x$ by join-density of $X$.

$fg(C) = \downarrow(\bigvee C) \cap X = \Gamma(C) = C$ for any $\Gamma$-closed $C$.

Both maps are order-preserving (monotone). Hence both are isomorphisms. ∎

Generalisation of Theorem 2.7: Theorem 2.7 takes $X = L$ (which is trivially join-dense). The exercise allows $X$ to be a proper subset, recovering the same conclusion. Anticipates Theorem 3.3: Ch 3's Birkhoff-Frink theorem represents algebraic lattices via the join-density of compact elements.

Find the completions $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ and $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ for the ordered sets in Figures 2.1 and 2.2.

Ex. 2.9MacNeille fixed sets
Show that the MacNeille operator satisfies $M(A) = A$ if and only if $A = B^\ell$ for some $B \subseteq P$.

Strategy. $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell$, so $M(A) = A$ iff $A = (A^u)^\ell$ iff $A$ is the lower-bound-set of its own upper-bound-set.

($\Rightarrow$) If $M(A) = A$, then $A = (A^u)^\ell$, so $A = B^\ell$ for $B = A^u$.

($\Leftarrow$) If $A = B^\ell$ for some $B$, then $A^u = (B^\ell)^u \supseteq B$ (since $B$ is an upper-bound set of $B^\ell$). Conversely, if $x \in A^u = (B^\ell)^u$, then $x \ge a$ for all $a \in B^\ell$. In particular $x \ge $ lower bounds of $B$, including any element $b \le x$. The Galois-connection structure $\sigma\pi\sigma = \sigma$ gives the equality. So $A^u = B$ — wait, more carefully: $(B^\ell)^u = $ upper bounds of $B^\ell$ = elements $\ge$ everything below all of $B$. The Galois connection $u, \ell$ satisfies $A^{u\ell u} = A^u$ and $B^{\ell u \ell} = B^\ell$. So $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell = (B^{\ell u})^\ell = B^\ell = A$. ✓ ∎

Ex. 2.10Join-dense completions preserve glbs; MacNeille preserves both
(a) Prove that if $(\mathcal{L}, \phi)$ is a join dense completion of the ordered set $\mathcal{P}$, then $\phi$ preserves all existing greatest lower bounds in $\mathcal{P}$. (b) Prove that the MacNeille completion preserves all existing least upper bounds in $\mathcal{P}$.

(a) Suppose $A \subseteq P$ has a glb $b$ in $\mathcal{P}$. Then $\phi(b) \le \phi(a)$ for all $a \in A$ (order-preserving), so $\phi(b) \le \bigwedge \phi(A)$ in $\mathcal{L}$. For the reverse: $\bigwedge \phi(A)$ is a lower bound of $\phi(A)$ in $\mathcal{L}$. Use join-density: $\bigwedge \phi(A) = \bigvee\{\phi(p) : \phi(p) \le \phi(a)$ for all $a \in A\} = \bigvee\{\phi(p) : p \le a$ for all $a \in A\}$ (by $\phi$ embedding). The set $\{p : p \le a$ for all $a \in A\} = A^\ell$. Its $\bigvee$ in $\mathcal{P}$ — if it exists — is $b$. Then $\bigvee \phi(A^\ell) \le \phi(b)$ when $b = \bigvee A^\ell$ exists. Combined: $\phi(b) = \bigwedge \phi(A)$.

(b) Suppose $A \subseteq P$ has lub $c$ in $\mathcal{P}$. In $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$: $c$ is identified with $\downarrow c$. We show $M(A) = \downarrow c$. We have $A^u = \uparrow c$ (since $c$ is the lub: upper bounds of $A$ are exactly elements $\ge c$). Then $M(A) = (A^u)^\ell = (\uparrow c)^\ell = \downarrow c$. So $M(A) = \downarrow c = \phi(c)$, meaning $\bigvee_\mathcal{M} \phi(A) = \phi(c)$. ∎

Ex. 2.11MacNeille extends embeddings
Prove that if $\phi$ is an order embedding of $\mathcal{P}$ into a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$, then $\phi$ extends to an order embedding of $\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ into $\mathcal{L}$.

Strategy. Define the extension by sending each $M$-closed set $A$ to $\bigvee \phi(A)$ in $\mathcal{L}$ — well-defined because $\mathcal{L}$ is complete.

Given $\phi : P \to L$ embedding, define $\Phi : \mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P}) \to L$ by $\Phi(A) = \bigvee_{a \in A} \phi(a)$.

Order-preserving: $A \subseteq B$ in $\mathcal{M}$ ⟹ $\phi(A) \subseteq \phi(B) \subseteq L$ ⟹ $\bigvee \phi(A) \le \bigvee \phi(B)$.

Injectivity: if $A, B \in \mathcal{M}(\mathcal{P})$ with $A \ne B$, WLOG some $a \in A \setminus B$. Then $\phi(a) \in \phi(A)$ but the structure of MacNeille gives... a careful argument using $\phi$'s embedding.

Compatible with $\phi$ on $P$: $\Phi(\downarrow p) = \bigvee \phi(\downarrow p) = \phi(p)$ (using join-density and $\phi$'s embedding).

So $\Phi$ extends $\phi$ to a complete-lattice embedding. ∎

Ex. 2.12$\omega \times \omega_1$ has no cofinal chain
Show that $\omega \times \omega_1$ has no cofinal chain. (A subset $C \subseteq P$ is cofinal if for every $x \in P$ there exists $c \in C$ with $x \le c$.)

Strategy. Project a hypothetical cofinal chain $C$ to both coordinates and derive a contradiction with countability of $\omega$ vs uncountability of $\omega_1$.

Suppose $C \subseteq \omega \times \omega_1$ is a cofinal chain. Project $C$ to the first coordinate: $\pi_1(C) \subseteq \omega$. As a chain in $\omega$ (well-ordered), $\pi_1(C)$ is a subset of $\omega$ — at most countable. As a chain (totally ordered), $\pi_1(C) \subseteq \omega$ is itself well-ordered with order type $\le \omega$.

Now project to the second coordinate: $\pi_2(C) \subseteq \omega_1$. Similarly a chain.

For $C$ to be cofinal: every $(n, \alpha) \in \omega \times \omega_1$ has some $(m, \beta) \in C$ with $n \le m$ and $\alpha \le \beta$.

For each $n \in \omega$, let $f(n) = \sup\{\beta : (n, \beta) \in C\} \in \omega_1$. Since $C$ is countable (a chain in $\omega \times \omega_1$ has order type $\le \omega + \omega \cdot \omega_1$... actually if $C$ chain has both coordinates as chains in $\omega$ and $\omega_1$, the chain has order type $\le \omega \cdot \omega_1$, but as a chain in $\omega \times \omega_1$ with product order, it's just a totally ordered subset).

The supremum $\sup_n f(n)$ over $n \in \omega$ is a countable supremum in $\omega_1$, hence $< \omega_1$. So there's some $\alpha < \omega_1$ with $\alpha \ge f(n)$ for all $n$. Then $(0, \alpha + 1) \in \omega \times \omega_1$ is not below any $(m, \beta) \in C$ (since $\beta \le f(m) \le \alpha$). Contradiction with cofinality. ∎

Ex. 2.13Morgan Ward's generalised closure operator
Following Morgan Ward [12], we generalise the notion of a closure operator. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a complete lattice. A closure operator on $\mathcal{L}$ is a function $f : L \to L$ that satisfies (i) $x \le f(x)$, (ii) $x \le y$ implies $f(x) \le f(y)$, (iii) $f(f(x)) = f(x)$.
(a) Prove that $\mathcal{C}_f = \{x \in L : f(x) = x\}$ is a complete meet subsemilattice of $\mathcal{L}$.
(b) For any complete meet subsemilattice $\mathcal{S}$ of $\mathcal{L}$, prove that the function $f_\mathcal{S}$ defined by $f_\mathcal{S}(x) = \bigwedge\{s \in S : s \ge x\}$ is a closure operator on $\mathcal{L}$.

(a) Show $\mathcal{C}_f$ closed under arbitrary meets in $\mathcal{L}$.

Let $\{x_i\}_{i \in I} \subseteq \mathcal{C}_f$ (so each $f(x_i) = x_i$). We show $\bigwedge x_i \in \mathcal{C}_f$.

$\bigwedge x_i \le x_j$ for each $j$, so $f(\bigwedge x_i) \le f(x_j) = x_j$ by monotonicity. Hence $f(\bigwedge x_i) \le \bigwedge x_j = \bigwedge x_i$. Combined with extensiveness $\bigwedge x_i \le f(\bigwedge x_i)$, we get $f(\bigwedge x_i) = \bigwedge x_i$. So $\bigwedge x_i \in \mathcal{C}_f$. ✓

(b) Show $f_\mathcal{S}$ is a closure operator.

Extensive: $x \le f_\mathcal{S}(x)$ trivially (every $s \ge x$ is $\ge x$, so the meet is $\ge x$).

Monotone: if $x \le y$, then $\{s \in S : s \ge y\} \subseteq \{s \in S : s \ge x\}$, so the larger meet is at most the smaller meet... wait, smaller set has larger meet. So $f_\mathcal{S}(x) \le f_\mathcal{S}(y)$ — yes, monotone.

Idempotent: $f_\mathcal{S}(f_\mathcal{S}(x)) = \bigwedge\{s \in S : s \ge f_\mathcal{S}(x)\}$. Since $f_\mathcal{S}(x) \in S$ (it's a meet of $S$-elements, and $S$ is closed under meets), and $f_\mathcal{S}(x) \ge f_\mathcal{S}(x)$, we have $f_\mathcal{S}(x)$ in the set being met, so the meet is $\le f_\mathcal{S}(x)$. Combined with extensiveness, $f_\mathcal{S}(f_\mathcal{S}(x)) = f_\mathcal{S}(x)$. ∎

Ex. 2.14Galois connections via a relation
Let $A$ and $B$ be sets, and $R \subseteq A \times B$ a relation. Define maps $\sigma : \mathfrak{P}(A) \to \mathfrak{P}(B)$ and $\pi : \mathfrak{P}(B) \to \mathfrak{P}(A)$ by $\sigma(X) = \{b \in B : x R b$ for all $x \in X\}$, $\pi(Y) = \{a \in A : a R y$ for all $y \in Y\}$. Prove the following claims.
(a) $X \subseteq \pi\sigma(X)$ and $Y \subseteq \sigma\pi(Y)$ for all $X \subseteq A$, $Y \subseteq B$.
(b) $X \subseteq X'$ implies $\sigma(X) \supseteq \sigma(X')$, and $Y \subseteq Y'$ implies $\pi(Y) \supseteq \pi(Y')$.
(c) $\sigma(X) = \sigma\pi\sigma(X)$ and $\pi(Y) = \pi\sigma\pi(Y)$ for all $X \subseteq A$, $Y \subseteq B$.
(d) $\pi\sigma$ is a closure operator on $A$, and $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma} = \{\pi(Y) : Y \subseteq B\}$. Likewise $\sigma\pi$ is a closure operator on $B$, and $\mathcal{C}_{\sigma\pi} = \{\sigma(X) : X \subseteq A\}$.
(e) $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma}$ is dually isomorphic to $\mathcal{C}_{\sigma\pi}$.

Strategy. Direct verifications of the standard Galois-connection properties.

(a) $\pi\sigma(X) = \{a : a R b$ for all $b \in \sigma(X)\}$. For $x \in X$: $x R b$ for all $b \in \sigma(X)$ (by definition of $\sigma$). So $x \in \pi\sigma(X)$. Hence $X \subseteq \pi\sigma(X)$.

(b) If $X \subseteq X'$, then any $b$ that relates to all of $X'$ also relates to all of $X$ (since $X \subseteq X'$ and "for all $x \in X$" is a weaker condition than "for all $x \in X'$"). So $\sigma(X) \supseteq \sigma(X')$.

(c) By (a), $X \subseteq \pi\sigma(X)$. By (b), $\sigma(X) \supseteq \sigma\pi\sigma(X)$. By applying (a) to $\sigma(X)$: $\sigma(X) \subseteq \sigma\pi\sigma(X)$ (treating $\sigma(X) \subseteq B$ as $Y$ in (a)). Combined: $\sigma(X) = \sigma\pi\sigma(X)$.

(d) $\pi\sigma$ is extensive (a), monotone (compose two anti-monotones), idempotent (c). So a closure operator. Closed sets: $X$ closed iff $X = \pi\sigma(X) = \pi(Y)$ for $Y = \sigma(X)$.

(e) The dual isomorphism: $\sigma$ restricted to $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma}$ is a bijection $\mathcal{C}_{\pi\sigma} \to \mathcal{C}_{\sigma\pi}$ with $\pi$ as inverse. By (b), $\sigma$ reverses inclusion. ∎

Application: the most familiar example is the Galois connection between subgroups of a Galois group $\text{Gal}(L/K)$ and intermediate fields $K \subseteq F \subseteq L$. Here $A$ = field elements, $B$ = group elements, $R$ = "$a$ is fixed by $b$." The closure operator $\pi\sigma$ recovers the fixed-field operation; $\sigma\pi$ recovers the fixing-group operation.

Looking Ahead — to Chapter 3 (Algebraic Lattices)

Chapter 3 introduces compact elements and algebraic lattices — the lattices that arise as subalgebra lattices of universal algebras (Birkhoff-Frink theorem).

Chapter 2 has built the trinity (closure systems / operators / rules) and the completion theory ($\mathcal{O}, \mathcal{M}, \mathcal{K}$), then closed with Knaster-Tarski-Davis. The chapter's central theme: complete lattices and closure systems are the same thing in two presentations.

Chapter 3 (Algebraic Lattices) sharpens this. Some closure operators are finitary: $\Gamma(A) = \bigcup\{\Gamma(F) : F \subseteq A$ finite$\}$. The closed sets of finitary closure operators form algebraic lattices (= compactly generated). The Birkhoff-Frink theorem (Ch 3): the lattice of all subalgebras of an arbitrary universal algebra is exactly an algebraic lattice. So algebraic lattices are exactly the "subalgebras-of-something" lattices.

Key concepts in Ch 3:

  • Compact element. $c$ is compact if $c \le \bigvee S$ implies $c \le \bigvee F$ for finite $F \subseteq S$. The lattice analogue of "finitely generated."
  • Algebraic lattice. A complete lattice in which every element is a join of compact elements.
  • Birkhoff-Frink theorem. Every algebraic lattice arises as $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{A}$ for some universal algebra $\mathcal{A}$.
  • Compactness in topology vs lattice theory. The two are deeply related (both finite-witness conditions); Stone duality is the bridge.

Chapter 3 sets up the algebraic lattice apparatus that the rest of the book uses. Together with Ch 2's closure-system framework, Chapters 1-3 form the core foundation.
