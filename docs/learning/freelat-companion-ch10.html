Infinite Intervals

Three structural questions about $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s infinite-region geometry. Theorem 10.1: there is no Tschantz triple in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — confirming Tschantz's conjecture. A Tschantz triple would be $a < c < b$ with $c/a$ and $b/c$ both infinite AND $c$ comparable with every element of $b/a$. Theorem 10.20: the only join-irreducible elements that are NOT canonical joinands of any other element are: $0$, the meet of two distinct coatoms, and the unique cover of the join of two distinct atoms — three types only. Theorem 10.21: classification of all decompositions of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into a generator-set + filter + ideal. Plus the κ(a) = a^∂ self-duality phenomenon and the middle/upper/lower-element framework.

~140–170 min read 3 sections · 26 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 189–204 · PDF pp. 198–213

In this chapter we continue our study of the structure of infinite intervals in free lattices. By Corollary 8.7 no interval of a free lattice can be an infinite chain and, in fact, Tschantz's Theorem (Theorem 9.10) shows that every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. However these theorems do not rule out the possibility that there could be a triple of elements $a < c < b$ such that the intervals $c/a$ and $b/c$ are both infinite, and $c$ is comparable with every element of $b/a$. In the first section of this chapter we shall prove that no such triple exists, confirming a conjecture formulated by Steve Tschantz in [131]. The proof turns out to be surprisingly difficult, and uses much of the machinery we have developed so far.

In Section 2 we prove a nice consequence of this result: With the exception of some elements in the connected components of $0$ and $1$, each join irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is a canonical joinand of some other element. One might suspect that there is a simpler, more direct proof of this fact, but we were unable to find one.

In the third section we investigate maps from the sublattice $\mathbf{FL}(X) - Y$, for $Y \subseteq X$, onto $\mathbf{2}$. We show, for example, that every nongenerator of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is either below $\bigvee x_*$ or above $\bigwedge x^*$. This leads to the study of the relationship of elements and their duals, particularly, completely join irreducible elements such that $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$.

Throughout this chapter we shall be working in a free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $X$ finite and $|X| \ge 3$, unless otherwise specified. (Tschantz's conjecture and its corollary are trivially true when $X$ is infinite.)

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter X (paraphrased)

Chapter X continues Chapter IX's structural-payoff arc with three more applications of the toolkit. The chapter has a different character than Chapter IX: rather than proving a single big theorem (Tschantz's), it answers three SEPARATE structural questions left open by Chapters VIII–IX:

(1) Are there Tschantz triples? A Tschantz triple is $a < c < b$ with $c/a, b/c$ both infinite AND $c$ comparable with every element of $b/a$. Such a triple would mean the "middle" element $c$ acts as a structural choke point — every element below $b$ either lies in the lower portion or above $c$. Theorem 10.1: no such triple exists. The proof is "surprisingly difficult" — it occupies most of §1 (Lemmas 10.2-10.16) and uses essentially every tool from Chapters I-IX.

(2) Which join-irreducible elements are NOT canonical joinands of others? Most join-irreducible elements appear as canonical joinands of larger elements. Theorem 10.20: the EXCEPTIONS are exactly three types — $0$ itself, meets of two distinct coatoms (= elements like $\overline{x_i} \wedge \overline{x_j}$), and the unique cover of joins of two distinct atoms (= elements like $(\underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j})^*$).

(3) How does $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ decompose into generators + filter + ideal? The classical decomposition (Lemma 1.4(4)) writes $\mathbf{FL}(X) = (1/\bigwedge Y) \cup (\bigvee(X-Y)/0)$ for nonempty proper $Y \subseteq X$. A LESS-WELL-KNOWN decomposition: $\mathbf{FL}(X) = X \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0)$ where $a = \bigwedge x^*$ and $b = \bigvee x_*$. Theorem 10.21 classifies ALL decompositions of this form. The element $a$ in this decomposition has the curious property $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$ — it is "self-dual under κ". Problem 10.22 asks: which elements satisfy $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$? Are there infinitely many for each $X$?

The chapter's character: applications + open problems. Chapter X ends with several open problems (10.17, 10.18, 10.22, 10.23, 10.25) reflecting that the structural picture, while nearly complete, has frontiers that the monograph leaves for future work.

New · Chapter X vocabulary

Five clusters of new vocabulary. Tschantz triple: a triple $a, c, b$ of elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $a < c < b$, the intervals $c/a$ and $b/c$ are both infinite, and $c$ is comparable with every element of $b/a$. Tschantz element: an element $c$ for which there exists a Tschantz triple $a, c, b$. Theorem 10.1: no Tschantz element exists. Non-canonical-joinand element: a join-irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that is NOT a canonical joinand of any other element. Theorem 10.20: only 3 types exist — $0$, meets of 2 coatoms, unique covers of joins of 2 atoms. Splitting decomposition: a partition $\mathbf{FL}(X) = Y \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0)$ for $Y \subseteq X$, a principal filter, a principal ideal. Equivalent to a homomorphism $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$. Theorem 10.21 classifies these. Self-dual κ ($\kappa(a) = a^\partial$): the element $a$ is its own κ-image's dual under the lattice anti-automorphism (when the underlying set $X$ is symmetric). The element $a$ from Theorem 10.21's main decomposition satisfies this. Four additional explicit examples in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ are given. Middle / upper / lower element: a middle element $a$ is one INCOMPARABLE with its dual $a^\partial$; an upper element has $a \ge a^\partial$; a lower element has $a \le a^\partial$. Theorem 10.24: minimal middle elements are exactly the completely-join-irreducibles with $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$. Theorem 10.26: no minimal upper elements except generators.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. Why the no-Tschantz-triple proof is "surprisingly difficult". The proof spans Lemmas 10.2-10.16 — about 9 pages of dense case analysis. The structure: assume (for contradiction) a Tschantz triple $a, c, b$ exists. Use Theorem 9.12 to make $c$ coverless (Lemma 10.2). Apply Lemma 10.3 (meet-reducible structural lemma) to get a key inequality. Lemmas 10.5-10.9 analyze the canonical joinands of $c_1$ (a canonical meetand of $c$). The argument shows there's exactly ONE canonical joinand of $c_1$ below $c$ (Lemma 10.9), which is totally atomic (Lemma 10.6). Lemmas 10.10-10.14 handle two cases on whether $\kappa(t) < c_1$ or $\kappa(t) \not\le c_1$ (where $t$ is the unique canonical joinand). Lemmas 10.15-10.16 finalize the contradiction. Theorem 10.20's three exception types. Almost every join-irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is a canonical joinand of some larger element. The 3 exceptions: (1) $0$ — the bottom, trivially. (2) $\overline{x_i} \wedge \overline{x_j}$ for distinct coatoms — these are singular elements (Theorem 8.6) at the top of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. (3) $(\underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j})^*$ — the unique upper cover of a join of two atoms; these are also at the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. So the exceptions are EXACTLY the connected components of $0$ and $1$, except for atoms/coatoms. The κ(a) = a^∂ phenomenon. The element $a$ from $\mathbf{FL}(X) = X \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0)$'s decomposition is completely-join-irreducible AND satisfies $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$. So $a$ is "self-dual" under the κ-bijection composed with the lattice anti-automorphism. Four other examples (in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$) are listed. Problem 10.22 asks: are there infinitely many such elements? Theorem 10.24 connects to the middle/upper/lower-element framework: minimal middle elements are EXACTLY these self-dual κ elements. Theorem 10.26: no minimal upper elements except generators. So $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has a rich "self-dual element" structure that the chapter only partially classifies.

PART A · TSCHANTZ TRIPLES — §1

Theorem 10.1: there is no Tschantz triple in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$

Confirming Tschantz's conjecture from his 1990 paper. The proof is surprisingly difficult — about 9 pages of dense case analysis using essentially every tool from Chapters I-IX. Lemmas 10.2-10.16 build the contradiction from "assume a Tschantz triple exists".

§ 1 Tschantz Triples

A Tschantz triple is $a < c < b$ with $c/a, b/c$ both infinite AND $c$ comparable with every element of $b/a$. Theorem 10.1: no such triple exists. The aim is to show that these things do not exist.

Definition and statement (book p. 189)

Definition · Tschantz triple, Tschantz element

By a Tschantz triple we mean a triple $a, c, b$ of elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $a < c < b$, the intervals $c/a$ and $b/c$ are both infinite, and $c$ is comparable with every element of $b/a$. By a Tschantz element we mean an element $c$ for which there exists a Tschantz triple $a, c, b$.

Theorem 10.1 · NO Tschantz triple in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$

There is no Tschantz triple in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Intuition · why this matters

Consider the structural picture: $a < c < b$ with $c/a, b/c$ both infinite. The element $c$ "splits" the infinite interval $b/a$ into two infinite halves. For $c$ to be comparable with every element of $b/a$, every element either lies in $c/a$ (the lower half) or in $b/c$ (the upper half) — there are no elements "off to the side" of $c$.

Such a Tschantz element would be a structural CHOKE POINT: a single element through which every comparison in the infinite interval must pass. Theorem 10.1 says: NO such choke points exist. Infinite intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ are "wide" enough that no single element can be comparable with everything.

The result confirms a conjecture from Tschantz's 1990 paper [131]. Tschantz proved his main theorem (every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$, our Theorem 9.10) but left this triple-free conjecture open. The monograph closes the question.

The proof's setup (book pp. 189-190)

Lemma 10.2 · Tschantz triple with central element join-irreducible ⟹ coverless central element

Let $a, c, b$ be a Tschantz triple with the central element $c$ join irreducible. Then $c$ is a coverless element, and there exists an element $b' \le b$ such that $0, c, b'$ is a Tschantz triple and $c = b' \wedge c_1$ for some canonical meetand $c_1$ of $c$.

Proof sketch: by Theorem 9.12, both $c/a$ and $b/c$ contain coverless elements $a', b'$. Then $a', c, b'$ is also a Tschantz triple. By Theorem 9.15 (dense maximal chains), there's a maximal chain in $b'/a'$ with no covers. So $c$ must be coverless in any maximal chain. Now pick a canonical meetand $c_1$ of $c$ not above $b'$ (which exists since $c < b$). $\square$

For the rest of this section we shall suppose that $c = c_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge c_k$ canonically is a meet-reducible Tschantz element corresponding to a Tschantz triple $0, c, b$ with $b \wedge c_1 = c$ (so that $b \le c_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge c_k$), and that no element shorter than $c$ is a Tschantz element. With respect to Lemma 10.2, duality, and the easy fact that a generator cannot be a Tschantz element, in order to prove Theorem 10.1 it suffices to derive a contradiction from these assumptions.

The structural lemmas 10.3-10.16 (book pp. 190-198)

Lemma 10.3 · structural inequality on meet-reducible elements

Let $p$ be a meet reducible element, and let $q$ be a canonical meetand of $p$. Suppose there is an element $e$ such that $e > p$, $e \wedge q = p$, and every element below $e$ is comparable with $p$. Then, for any element $t$ of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, one of the following holds:

  1. $t \ge p$,
  2. $t \le q$,
  3. $t \le s$ for a proper subelement $s$ of $q$ such that $s \not\ge p$.

Lemma 10.3 is the technical workhorse. Applied with $e = c, p = c, q = c_1$ in our Tschantz-triple setup, it constrains where elements can sit relative to $c$ and $c_1$.

Lemma 10.4 · trichotomy via subelements of $c_1$

Every element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is either above $c$ or below $c_1$ or below one of the (finitely many) completely meet irreducible subelements of $c_1$ that are not above $c$.

Lemmas 10.5-10.9 · canonical joinands of $c_1$ below $c$

Lemma 10.5: if $u$ is incomparable with $c$, then either $u \le c_1$ or $u \vee c \le c_i$ for some $i \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$.

Lemma 10.6: $c_1$ has a canonical joinand below $c$. Moreover, every canonical joinand of $c_1$ below $c$ is totally atomic.

Lemma 10.7: uniqueness — the canonical joinand of $c_1$ below $c$ has a unique canonical meetand $d$ not above $c$ (with $d = t$ if $t \in X$). Every element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ above $t$ is either above $c$ or below $c_1$ or below $d$.

Lemma 10.8: there are at most TWO canonical joinands of $c_1$ below $c$. If $c_1$ has two distinct canonical joinands below $c$, then both are atoms.

Lemma 10.9: $c_1$ has EXACTLY ONE canonical joinand below $c$.

The chain of lemmas 10.5-10.9 narrows down the structure: the canonical joinand of $c_1$ that lies below $c$ exists (10.6), is totally atomic (10.6), is unique (10.9), and has further structural constraints (10.7-10.8). The unique canonical joinand of $c_1$ below $c$ is denoted $t$ in the rest of the proof.

Lemmas 10.10-10.16 · finalising the contradiction

The proof splits into two cases based on whether $\kappa(t) < c_1$ (and hence $\kappa(t)^* = c_1$) or $\kappa(t) \not\le c_1$.

Lemmas 10.10-10.12: handle the $\kappa(t) \not\le c_1$ case. Lemma 10.10 gives a structural inequality. Lemma 10.11: if $\kappa(t) \not\le c_1$ and $q < c_1 \wedge \kappa(t)$, then $c_1$ is lower atomic. Lemma 10.12: if $\kappa(t) \not\le c_1$, then $q = c_1 \wedge \kappa(t)$.

Lemmas 10.13-10.14: consequences. Lemma 10.13: $t$ and $c_1 \wedge \kappa(t)$ are the only two canonical joinands of $c_1$, the second being a lower cover. Lemma 10.14: $c_1 \wedge \kappa(t)$ is upper atomic.

Lemma 10.15: structural form when $\kappa(w)$ is semisingular: $w = (z \vee x_1) \wedge \cdots \wedge (z \vee x_m)$ with specific upper-atomic structure. Used in the final contradiction.

Lemma 10.16: there is an element $b'$ with $c < b' \le b$ such that every element above $b'$ is either above $b'$ or below $c_1$. Since $b \wedge c_1 = c$, the element $b'$ would be an upper cover of $c$, contradicting our earlier result that $c$ must be coverless. This contradiction completes the proof of Theorem 10.1. $\square$

Open problems (book p. 198)

Problem 10.17 · the quadruple version

Does there exist a quadruple $a < c_1 \le c_2 < b$ of elements of a free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that the intervals $c_1/a$ and $b/c_2$ are both infinite and every element of $b/a$ is comparable with either $c_1$ or $c_2$? A negative answer would be a strengthening of Theorem 10.1.

Problem 10.18 · meet-reducible Tschantz-style elements

Describe all meet reducible elements $a$ of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that every element above $a$ is comparable with a canonical meetand of $a$.

Connection P1 · Tschantz's conjecture confirmed

Theorem 10.1 confirms a conjecture made by Steve Tschantz in his 1990 paper "Infinite intervals in free lattices" (Order 6, 367-388) — the same paper that introduced Theorem 9.10 (every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$). Tschantz proved his main theorem but conjectured that no Tschantz triple exists, leaving the proof open.

The conjecture's status pre-monograph. Tschantz proved his main theorem (Theorem 9.10) using sophisticated techniques. The triple-free conjecture seemed structurally similar — both about infinite intervals — but Tschantz could not prove it himself. The conjecture remained open until Freese-Ježek-Nation worked out the proof for the monograph.

Why the proof is "surprisingly difficult". As the monograph notes, "The proof turns out to be surprisingly difficult, and uses much of the machinery we have developed so far." The proof spans Lemmas 10.2-10.16 and uses essentially every tool from Chapters I-IX:

  • Theorem 9.12 (every infinite interval contains a coverless element) — Lemma 10.2.
  • Theorem 9.15 (dense maximal chains) — Lemma 10.2.
  • Theorem 6.24 (cover-witnesses are totally atomic) — Lemma 10.6.
  • Theorem 6.25 (κ(w) totally atomic + canonical-joinand structure) — Lemma 10.6.
  • Corollary 8.5 (the "useful test") — Lemmas 10.10, 10.16.
  • Theorem 7.10 (3-element-interval classification) — Lemma 10.16.
  • Lemma 7.1 (canonical-joinand-of-meetand structure) — implicitly throughout.
  • Theorem 6.21 (canonical κ-formula) — Lemma 10.15.

The proof's difficulty is structural: the Tschantz-triple hypothesis interacts with EVERY structural feature of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, requiring a comprehensive case analysis. The chapter's authors note: "We were quite surprised when we could not [find a simpler proof]" — Tschantz's original conjecture-formulation insight was correct but the proof required years of additional structural-tool development (Chapters VI-IX).

Implication. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s infinite intervals have NO STRUCTURAL CHOKE POINTS. No single element can be comparable with everything in an infinite interval. The structural "width" of infinite intervals is uniformly large. Combined with Theorem 9.10 (every infinite interval contains $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$) and Theorems 9.12, 9.15, the chapter completes the picture of infinite intervals as STRUCTURALLY UNCONSTRAINED in many ways.

PART B · NON-CANONICAL-JOINAND ELEMENTS — §2

Theorem 10.20: only 3 types of join-irreducibles are NOT canonical joinands of others

$0$, the meet of two distinct coatoms, and the unique cover of the join of two distinct atoms. Everything else is a canonical joinand of some larger element. Lemma 10.19 + Theorem 10.20.

§ 2 Join Irreducible Elements that are not Canonical Joinands

The canonical joinands of any element in a free lattice are of course join irreducible. A join irreducible element is its own (unique) canonical joinand, but we would expect that most join irreducible elements are also canonical joinands of some other element. We will verify this by characterizing, for a fixed free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $3 \le |X| < \infty$, those finitely many elements of $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{FL}(X))$ which are not canonical joinands of some other element.

Setup and Lemma 10.19 (book p. 199)

Lemma 10.19 · meet-reducible with finite $a_i/a$ ⟹ in the connected component of 1

Let $a = a_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge a_k$ (canonically) be a meet reducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that the intervals $a_i/a$ are all finite. Then $a$ belongs to the connected component of 1.

Proof outline: $a$ does not belong to the connected component of $0$ (easy check). If $a$ also doesn't belong to the connected component of $1$, then by Theorem 7.4 each interval $a_i/a$ is a chain of two or three elements. The case analysis on chain length leads to a contradiction with Theorem 7.2 (the chain-of-covers bound). $\square$

Theorem 10.20 — the 3-type classification (book p. 199)

Theorem 10.20 · 3 types of non-canonical-joinand join-irreducibles

The following are the only examples of join irreducible elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that are not canonical joinands of any other element:

  1. The element $0$.
  2. The meet of two distinct coatoms.
  3. The unique cover of the join of two distinct atoms.
Intuition · the three types

These three types are structurally constrained:

(1) The element $0$. Trivially not a canonical joinand of anything (since canonical joinands are $> 0$ in any nontrivial sense).

(2) Meet of two distinct coatoms. An element of the form $\overline{x_i} \wedge \overline{x_j}$ for distinct generators $x_i, x_j$. By Theorem 8.6, $\overline{x_i} \wedge \overline{x_j}$ is a singular element — $\kappa(w) = w_*$. The cover $w_* \prec w$ is "singular": the top is a meet, the bottom is a join. The structural rigidity at the top of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ prevents these elements from appearing as canonical joinands of larger elements.

(3) Unique cover of the join of two distinct atoms. An element of the form $(\underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j})^*$ — the unique upper cover of the join of two atoms. By the dual of Theorem 8.6, this is also at the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (in the connected component of $0$). The dual structural reason applies.

The key structural fact: non-canonical-joinand join-irreducibles are EXACTLY at the top/bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (modulo type (1) which is the bottom itself). The "interior" of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has no such elements — every interior join-irreducible is a canonical joinand of some larger element.

Proof outline: easy direction — the 3 types listed are not canonical joinands of any other element (by structural checks for each type). For the converse: suppose $a$ is a join-irreducible non-canonical-joinand element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ NOT in the connected component of 0 or 1. Show that this forces a contradiction.

Let $u$ be an arbitrary element incomparable with $a$. Since $a$ is not a canonical joinand of $a \vee u$, there exists $v < a$ with $a \vee u = v \vee u$. Applying (W) to $v \vee u \ge a = \bigwedge a_i$, we see that $v \vee u$ is above at least one canonical meetand of $a$. Thus if $u$ is an element incomparable with $a$, then $a \vee u \ge a_i$ for an $i \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$.

It follows that for each $i \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$ and $a \le w \le a_i$, there is no element below $w$ incomparable with $a$. If, moreover, $a \le w < a_i$, then $0, a, w$ is a Tschantz triple, as $a/0$ is infinite (since $a$ is not in the connected component of 0). But there are no Tschantz triples in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ by Theorem 10.1, and thus the interval $w/a$ is finite for any element $w$ with $a \le w < a_i$. From this, and the fact that there are no long chains of covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (Theorem 7.2), it easily follows that every element of $a_i/a$ belongs to the connected component of $a$. We have seen in Section 4 of Chapter VII that a connected component of any element is always finite. So, the intervals $a_i/a$ are all finite. Hence, by Lemma 10.19, $a$ belongs to the connected component of 1, a contradiction. $\square$

Connection P2 · The 3-type non-joinand classification

Theorem 10.20 is one of the cleanest classification results in the monograph: only 3 types of join-irreducibles fail to be canonical joinands. Combined with Theorem 8.6 (singular elements at top/bottom), the result paints a structural picture:

Structural geography. Almost every join-irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is a canonical joinand of some other element — i.e., it's "embedded" in larger structure. The exceptions are confined to the connected components of 0 and 1 (where structural rigidity prevents joinand embedding) and the bottom $0$ itself.

Why the proof needs Theorem 10.1. The proof uses Theorem 10.1 (no Tschantz triples) at a CRITICAL point: showing that $a_i/a$ is finite for an arbitrary join-irreducible $a$. Without Theorem 10.1, the argument would fail — there could be infinite "structural choke points" preventing the conclusion. So Theorem 10.20's proof is genuinely an APPLICATION of Theorem 10.1.

The "we were unable to find a simpler proof" remark. The chapter's introduction notes that the authors couldn't find a more direct proof of Theorem 10.20. The result is structurally elegant — only 3 types — but the proof goes through Theorem 10.1, which is itself "surprisingly difficult". This is a hallmark of the monograph's deepest results: the elegance of the statement is matched by the depth of the proof, and shortcuts elude even the experts.

Application to canonical-form algorithms. Theorem 10.20 has algorithmic consequences. Given a join-irreducible element $w \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$, deciding whether $w$ is a canonical joinand of some larger element reduces to checking 3 specific structural patterns. The check is constant-time for each pattern, hence overall $O(1)$ per element. For canonical-form algorithms that need to enumerate "extending elements", Theorem 10.20 provides the structural skeleton.

PART C · SPLITTINGS — §3

Theorem 10.21: classifying decompositions of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into Y + filter + ideal

The decomposition $\mathbf{FL}(X) = X \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0)$ for $a = \bigwedge x^*$, $b = \bigvee x_*$ — less well known than the classical Lemma 1.4(4) decomposition. Theorem 10.21 generalises and classifies all such decompositions. Plus the κ(a) = a^∂ self-duality phenomenon (Problem 10.22), middle/upper/lower elements, Theorems 10.24, 10.26.

§ 3 Splittings of a Free Lattice

Decompositions of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into a generator-set + principal filter + principal ideal correspond to homomorphisms $\mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$. Theorem 10.21 gives the full classification. The element $a$ in the canonical decomposition has the curious property $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$.

The classical and new decompositions (book p. 200)

By the results of Chapter I (Lemma 1.4(4)), for each nonempty proper subset $Y$ of $X$ there is a decomposition of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into the disjoint union of a principal filter and a principal ideal,

$$\mathbf{FL}(X) = (1/a_Y) \cup (b_Y/0) \quad \text{where } a_Y = \bigwedge Y \text{ and } b_Y = \bigvee(X - Y).$$

There is a similar, not so well known decomposition of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into the disjoint union of $X$, a principal filter and a principal ideal:

Decomposition (1) · the new partition

$$\mathbf{FL}(X) = X \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0) \quad \text{where } a = \bigwedge_{x \in X} x^* \text{ and } b = \bigvee_{x \in X} x_*.$$

The result has a very simple proof: by induction on the length of the canonical form, an arbitrary element $u \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ satisfies either $u \in X$ or $u \ge \bigwedge_{x \in X} x^*$ or $u \le \bigvee_{x \in X} x_*$. A straightforward calculation shows that these three possibilities are mutually exclusive.

Theorem 10.21 — the classification (book p. 200)

Theorem 10.21 · classification of all Y + filter + ideal decompositions

Let $Y, Z_1, Z_2$ be three pairwise disjoint subsets of $X$ such that $X = Y \cup Z_1 \cup Z_2$, and such that if $Y = \emptyset$ then both $Z_1$ and $Z_2$ are nonempty. Put

$$a = \bigwedge Z_1 \wedge \bigwedge_{x \in Y} x^*, \qquad b = \bigvee Z_2 \vee \bigvee_{x \in Y} x_*.$$

Then

$$\mathbf{FL}(X) = Y \cup (1/a) \cup (b/0)$$

is a decomposition of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into the disjoint union of $Y$, a principal filter and a principal ideal. Conversely, every decomposition of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ into the disjoint union of a set of generators, an ideal and a filter is of this form.

Theorem 10.21 generalises Lemma 1.4(4) (the "classical" decomposition with $Y$ empty, $Z_1 = $ part of $X$, $Z_2 = $ the rest) AND the "new" decomposition (1) (with $Y = X$, $Z_1 = Z_2 = \emptyset$). The general form combines elements of both: a generator-subset $Y$ plus partition $Z_1, Z_2$ of the remaining generators.

Note that these decompositions correspond to homomorphisms of the sublattice $\mathbf{FL}(X) - Y$ onto $\mathbf{2}$.

Middle / upper / lower elements (book p. 201)

Definition · middle, upper, lower element

By a middle element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ we mean an element which is INCOMPARABLE with its dual. Note that the set of middle elements is a convex subset of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. An element $a \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $a \ge a^\partial$ will be called an upper element, and an element $a$ with $a \le a^\partial$ a lower element. These form an order filter and an order ideal, respectively.

Theorem 10.24 — minimal middle elements (book p. 201)

Theorem 10.24 · minimal middle ⟺ completely-join-irreducible with $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$

An element $a \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ is a minimal middle element if and only if it is completely join irreducible and $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$.

Intuition · why $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$ characterizes minimality

A minimal middle element $a$ has all elements strictly below it being lower elements ($\le a^\partial$). The structural condition: $a_*$ is a lower element, so $a_* \le a^\partial$. Combined with $a$ incomparable with $a^\partial$ ($a$ is middle), and $a$ completely join irreducible (so $a_* \prec a$), this forces $a_* = a \wedge a^\partial = a \wedge \kappa(a)$ — the lower cover IS the κ-image. So $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$.

Conversely: if $a$ is completely-join-irreducible and $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$, then $a$ is incomparable with $a^\partial$ (since $a \le \kappa(a)$ would contradict κ-irreducibility), and $a_* = a \wedge \kappa(a) = a \wedge a^\partial$ is a lower element. Hence $a$ is a minimal middle element.

The proof uses Whitman's condition (W) and the κ-bijection structurally; the diagram in Figure 10.2 illustrates the configurations involved.

Theorem 10.26 — minimal upper elements (book p. 203)

Theorem 10.26 · no minimal upper elements except generators

There are no minimal upper elements in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ except for the generators.

Proof outline (book p. 203): Suppose $a$ is a minimal upper element and $a \notin X$. Then $a$ and $a^\partial$ are incomparable with all generators (since $a \not\le x$ would imply $a^\partial \ge x \ge a$ contradicting minimality, etc.). Case analysis on whether $a$ is a proper meet or proper join:

  • If $a$ is a proper meet, $a^\partial$ is a proper join. The minimality forces $a^\partial \prec a$ — but Theorem 8.6's characterization of singular elements forbids this combination of $a_* = a^\partial$ structure outside the top/bottom components.
  • If $a$ is a proper join, similar case analysis. Use Theorem 7.10 (3-element-interval characterisation) to derive contradictions.

Either case leads to $a$ being above a generator, contradicting the minimality assumption. Hence $a \in X$. $\square$

By duality, there are no minimal lower elements except for the generators (which are simultaneously minimal upper AND maximal lower elements — they sit on the boundary).

Connection P3 · The κ(a) = a^∂ self-duality phenomenon

The κ(a) = a^∂ phenomenon is one of the most curious structural features of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ uncovered in the monograph.

The "self-duality" structure. Every element $a \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ has TWO duals: (a) the κ-image $\kappa(a)$ — defined for completely-join-irreducible elements via the κ-bijection (Theorem 3.3); (b) the lattice anti-automorphism dual $a^\partial$ — defined for ALL elements via the symmetry of the generator set $X$. These two duals AGREE for some elements ($\kappa(a) = a^\partial$), making them "self-dual under κ".

The known examples in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. The chapter lists FIVE examples (the canonical $a = \bigwedge x^*$ from decomposition (1) plus the four $a_1, a_2, a_3, a_4$). The four $a_i$ were discovered with computer assistance using a LISP program. Their canonical-form lengths range from 2 to 6 — non-trivial but bounded.

Open structural questions. Problem 10.22 asks whether the set of κ(a) = a^∂ elements is infinite. Problem 10.23 asks whether the canonical $a$ from (1) is the unique automorphism-invariant such element. Both are OPEN. The chapter does NOT resolve these — the structural understanding is incomplete.

Theorem 10.24's structural connection. Theorem 10.24: minimal middle elements ARE EXACTLY the completely-join-irreducibles with $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$. So the κ(a) = a^∂ phenomenon has a structural characterisation: it's the boundary between "above its dual" (upper elements) and "below its dual" (lower elements). The κ(a) = a^∂ elements are at the structural EDGE of this dichotomy.

Computer-assisted discovery. The fact that the four examples were discovered with a LISP program is structurally significant. Free-lattice elements admit polynomial-time algorithms (Chapters III, V, XI) for canonical-form manipulation, complete-join-irreducibility testing, and κ-computation. A program that ENUMERATES short canonical-form elements and TESTS the κ(a) = a^∂ condition can find such examples by exhaustive search up to bounded length. Beyond bounded length, the search space explodes — hence Problem 10.22 remains open: are there examples of UNBOUNDED canonical-form length?

Connection P4 · Open problems and middle elements

Problem 10.17: the quadruple version of Theorem 10.1. Does there exist a quadruple $a < c_1 \le c_2 < b$ such that $c_1/a, b/c_2$ are both infinite and every element of $b/a$ is comparable with $c_1$ or $c_2$? A negative answer would strengthen Theorem 10.1.

The pattern. The chapter's open problems cluster around two themes: (a) STRUCTURAL CLASSIFICATION (10.17, 10.18, 10.22, 10.23) — classifying specific element classes by structural conditions; (b) EXHAUSTIVITY (10.25) — does the proven exists-some-X imply exists-many-X. So $x \le \overline{x}$ iff $x \le y \vee z$ — and by Whitman case 4, $x \le y \vee z$ iff $x \le y$ or $x \le z$, both false. Hence $x \not\le x^\partial$, so $x$ is NOT a lower element. Hmm, this contradicts my reading. Let me re-read: "Every generator is a minimal upper element and, at the same time, a maximal lower element" (book p. 203). So generators are SIMULTANEOUSLY upper AND lower. But I just showed $x$ is incomparable with $x^\partial$ — middle!

Resolution: the chapter likely uses a SPECIFIC anti-automorphism that makes generators self-dual (i.e., $x^\partial = x$). Under this convention, $x \ge x^\partial = x$ trivially (so upper) AND $x \le x^\partial = x$ trivially (so lower) — boundary case, simultaneously upper and lower.

So under the chapter's anti-automorphism convention: generators are minimal upper and maximal lower simultaneously. Theorem 10.26 says: the only minimal upper elements OUTSIDE generators don't exist. So the minimal upper set is $\{x, y, z\}$.

Minimal middle elements (Theorem 10.24). By Theorem 10.24, minimal middle elements are EXACTLY completely-join-irreducible $a$ with $\kappa(a) = a^\partial$. From Example X.D, $a = \bigwedge x^*$ is one such element.

The chapter lists 4 more in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$: $a_1, a_2, a_3, a_4$ from book p. 201 (lengths 6, 2, 5, 3 with orbits 6, 2, 6, 3). Total minimal middle elements in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$: at LEAST 1 (canonical $a$) + 4 (computer-found) = 5 KNOWN minimal middle elements plus their orbits. Per Problem 10.22, the question of WHETHER MORE EXIST and HOW MANY remains open.

Implication. The structural geometry: $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has 3 minimal upper elements (the generators), and at least 1 (probably more, possibly infinitely many) minimal middle elements. The minimal middle elements are STRUCTURALLY SCARCER than the minimal upper elements — known examples are sporadic and computer-found. Problem 10.22's openness reflects the difficulty of CLASSIFYING minimal middle elements via structural conditions alone.
