Distributive Lattices

The most tractable lattice variety: a single subdirectly irreducible $\mathbf{2}$, the unique-up-to-isomorphism set-representation, finite Birkhoff duality with posets, free distributive lattices and Dedekind's problem, and Stone's representation of Boolean algebras.

~85–95 min read 11 sections + 5 connections 12 worked exercises PDF pp. 89–98

Every dog must have his day.

— Chapter epigraph (proverbial; chosen by Nation)

Chapter 7 mapped the variety lattice $\Lambda$ from above: the equational scaffolding, the lattice of all lattice varieties, Jónsson's Lemma and McKenzie's finite-basis theorem. Chapter 8 zooms in on a single named member of $\Lambda$ — the variety $\mathbf{D}$ of distributive lattices — and gives it the deep structural treatment it deserves.

The chapter has four conceptual movements. First: every variety generated by a finite lattice is locally finite (Theorem 8.1, with explicit cardinality bound) — and Lemma 8.2 establishes that distributivity admits multiple equivalent equational forms, including a particularly elegant self-dual one. Lemma 8.3 reduces homomorphisms onto $\mathbf{2}$ to ideal/filter pairs, the building block for the entire representation theory. Second: the Birkhoff Prime Ideal Theorem (Theorem 8.4) — separation of any incomparable pair $a \not\le b$ by a prime filter — yields a cascade: $\mathbf{2}$ is the only subdirectly irreducible distributive lattice; $\mathbf{D} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{2})$; $\mathbf{D}$ is locally finite; every distributive lattice embeds in some power set $\mathfrak{P}(S)$ (Theorem 8.5). Third: for finite distributive lattices, Birkhoff's representation theorem (Theorem 8.6, 1933) gives an exact equivalence with finite posets via $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$. Free distributive lattices $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)$ become the order ideals of the antichain-poset on subsets of $X$ — but counting them is Dedekind's problem, an old combinatorial puzzle whose 9th value was finally computed in 2023 by independent groups (Jäkel; Van Hirtum, De Causmaecker, Goemaere, Kenter, Riebler, Lass, Plessl). Fourth: Boolean algebras, free Boolean algebras $\text{FBA}(X) = \mathfrak{P}(\mathfrak{P}(X))$ for finite $X$, the unique countable atomless Boolean algebra, and Stone's representation theorem (1936) — every Boolean algebra is the clopen subsets of a compact totally disconnected Hausdorff space. The chapter closes with C. S. Peirce's 1880 work on uniquely complemented lattices, Huntington's 1904 conjecture, and Dilworth's 1945 surprise: every lattice embeds in a uniquely complemented lattice — the conjecture is dramatically false.

New · Chapter 8 vocabulary

Four clusters of new vocabulary. Local finiteness: a variety $\mathbf{V}$ is locally finite if every finitely-generated lattice in $\mathbf{V}$ is finite — equivalently $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(n)$ finite for every $n$. Theorem 8.1 gives an explicit cardinality bound. Prime ideals + filters: a proper ideal $I$ is prime if $x \wedge y \in I \Rightarrow x \in I$ or $y \in I$ (dually for prime filter). The complement of a prime ideal is a prime filter, and vice versa. Birkhoff's Prime Ideal Theorem (Theorem 8.4) says distributive lattices have many prime filters — enough to separate any $a \not\le b$. Set representation: Theorem 8.5 embeds every distributive lattice in $\mathfrak{P}(S)$. Theorem 8.6 (Birkhoff 1933 finite duality): a finite distributive lattice $\mathcal{D}$ is exactly the lattice of order ideals $\mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$ of its poset of join-irreducibles — finite distributive lattices and finite posets are equivalent categorical structures. Boolean algebras: complemented distributive lattice. Free Boolean $\text{FBA}(X)$ has $2^{2^{|X|}}$ elements for finite $X$; $\text{FBA}(\aleph_0)$ is the unique countable atomless Boolean algebra. Stone duality (Theorem 8.8, 1936): every Boolean algebra is isomorphic to the Boolean algebra of clopen subsets of a Stone space (compact, Hausdorff, totally disconnected). Tarski-Papert (Theorem 8.7): a complete lattice embeds completely in some $\mathcal{P}(X)$ iff its completely-join-prime elements separate. Uniquely complemented: every element has exactly one complement. Theorem 8.9 (Birkhoff-Ward 1939): complete + atomic + uniquely complemented ⟹ Boolean.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. Birkhoff's representation duality — the equivalence between finite distributive lattices and finite posets is one of the cleanest and oldest dualities in mathematics (Birkhoff 1933). It generalises to Stone duality for Boolean algebras (1936), Priestley duality for arbitrary distributive lattices (1970, with ordered Stone spaces), and Esakia duality for Heyting algebras (1974, with intuitionistic Kripke frames). Standard reference: Davey-Priestley's Introduction to Lattices and Order (CUP, 1990; 2nd ed. 2002). Dedekind's problem. Counting $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)|$ is one of combinatorics' famous unsolved problems. Sequence: 2, 3, 6, 20, 168, 7581, 7,828,354, 2,414,682,040,998, 56,130,437,228,687,557,907,788, … The 9th Dedekind number was computed in April 2023 by Christian Jäkel (TU Dresden, 5311 GPU-hours) and independently by Van Hirtum, De Causmaecker, Goemaere, Kenter, Riebler, Lass & Plessl. The 10th remains beyond computational reach. Kleitman's asymptotic (1969): $\log_2 |\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)| \sim \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$. Huntington's conjecture and Dilworth's surprise. Peirce 1880, then Huntington 1904, conjectured: every uniquely complemented lattice is distributive (= Boolean). Birkhoff-Ward 1939 proved it under "complete + atomic." The conjecture survived four decades. Dilworth 1945 obliterated it: every lattice embeds in a uniquely complemented one. So uniquely complemented lattices are AS WILD as arbitrary lattices. Subsequent work (Adams, Grätzer, Salii) maps the boundaries of conditions that DO force distributivity.

PART A · LOCAL FINITENESS & THE FORMS OF DISTRIBUTIVITY

Setting up: when a finite lattice generates a tractable variety

Local finiteness gives finite presentations. Three equivalent forms of the distributive law (one self-dual). The reduction of homomorphisms onto $\mathbf{2}$ to ideal/filter partitions.

§ 8.1 Local finiteness and Theorem 8.1

A variety is locally finite when its finitely-generated lattices are all finite — and HSP of any finite lattice is locally finite, with explicit bound.
Definition · locally finite variety

A variety $\mathbf{V}$ is locally finite if every finitely-generated lattice in $\mathbf{V}$ is finite. Equivalently, $\mathbf{V}$ is locally finite iff the relatively-free lattice $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(n)$ is finite for every integer $n > 0$.

Theorem 8.1 · cardinality bound for HSP of finite

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a finite lattice and $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$, then

$$|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(n)| \le |L|^{|L|^n}.$$

Hence $\text{HSP}(\mathcal{L})$ is locally finite.

Proof

If $\mathbf{K}$ is any collection of lattices and $\mathbf{V} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{K})$, then $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X) \cong \text{FL}(X)/\theta$ where $\theta = \bigcap \ker f$ over all $f : \text{FL}(X) \to \mathcal{L}$ with $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbf{K}$. (This is the technical statement that $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)$ satisfies exactly the equations of $\mathbf{K}$.)

When $\mathbf{K} = \{\mathcal{L}\}$ (single finite lattice) and $|X| = n$: there are $|L|^n$ distinct mappings $X \to L$, hence $|L|^n$ distinct homomorphisms $f_i : \text{FL}(X) \to \mathcal{L}$. (Distinct homomorphisms can have equal kernels, but that's fine.) The range of each $f_i$ is a sublattice of $\mathcal{L}$, hence has at most $|L|$ elements. So $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)$ embeds as a subdirect product into the direct product $\prod_{1 \le i \le |L|^n} \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}^{|L|^n}$. Cardinality at most $|L|^{|L|^n}$. $\square$

Intuition · why the bound matters

$|L|^{|L|^n}$ is a tower function in $n$ — astronomically large quickly. But finite. That's enough for Theorem 8.1: the variety generated by a finite lattice has finite presentations of every arity. The bound is rarely tight (often very loose), but its existence is structural. A constructive view: $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{V}(X)$ is the sublattice of $\mathcal{L}^{|L|^n}$ generated by the vectors $\overline x = (f_1(x), \dots, f_{|L|^n}(x))$ for $x \in X$.

Note: Not every locally finite lattice variety is generated by a finite lattice. The converse to Theorem 8.1 is FALSE.

It is clear that there is a unique minimum nontrivial lattice variety — the one generated by $\mathbf{2}$, the 2-element lattice. We will show this is precisely the variety of all distributive lattices, $\mathbf{D} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{2})$.

§ 8.2 Lemma 8.2: equivalent forms of distributivity

Three equivalent equations for distributivity, including a self-dual symmetric form rarely seen in textbooks.
Lemma 8.2 · three equivalent forms

The following lattice equations are equivalent:

  1. $x \wedge (y \vee z) \approx (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$,
  2. $x \vee (y \wedge z) \approx (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z)$,
  3. $(x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) \approx (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$.

Thus each of these equations determines the variety $\mathbf{D}$ of all distributive lattices.

Proof

(1) ⟹ (2): If (1) holds in $\mathcal{L}$, then for any $x, y, z \in L$:

$$(x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) = [(x \vee y) \wedge x] \vee [(x \vee y) \wedge z] = x \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z) = x \vee (y \wedge z),$$

using absorption in the last step. Dually, (2) ⟹ (1).

(1) ⟹ (3): Apply (1) to the LHS of (3). LHS = $(x \vee y) \wedge ((x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z))$. The right factor expands by (1) [the dual]: $(x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) = z \vee (x \wedge y)$. Continuing: LHS = $(x \vee y) \wedge (z \vee (x \wedge y)) = ((x \vee y) \wedge z) \vee (x \wedge y)$, which equals the RHS of (3) after dualisation.

(3) ⟹ (1): Substitute $y \mapsto x \wedge y$ in (3):

$$x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z) \approx (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \quad\text{(modular form)}.$$

This is the modular law in slightly different form, so $\mathcal{L}$ must be modular. Then for arbitrary $x, y, z$, meet $x$ with both sides of (3) and use modularity:

$$x \wedge (y \vee z) = x \wedge [(x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)] = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (x \wedge y \wedge z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z),$$

since $x \wedge y \wedge z \le (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$. Thus (3) ⟹ (1).

Note that we have shown (3) ⟺ (1). Since (3) is self-dual, it follows that (3) ⟺ (2). The first argument, that (1) ⟺ (2), is redundant. $\square$

Intuition · the symmetric form

(3) is the median identity: $(x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$. Both sides equal the median $m(x, y, z)$ — the middle of three elements in any total order. In a distributive lattice, $m$ is well-defined and self-dual: meet of pairwise joins = join of pairwise meets. The Pixley term from Ch 5's congruence-distributivity (Theorem 5.8) is exactly $(x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$ — the right side of (3). Distributive lattices are exactly those where the Pixley term is self-dual.

In the first Corollary of the next chapter, we'll see that a lattice is distributive if and only if it contains neither $\mathcal{N}_5$ nor $\mathcal{M}_3$ as a sublattice — the celebrated forbidden-substructure characterisation. But before that, the chapter develops the representation theory.

§ 8.3 Lemma 8.3: homomorphisms onto $\mathbf{2}$

Surjective homomorphisms $\mathcal{L} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{2}$ correspond exactly to disjoint partitions of $L$ into a proper ideal and a proper filter.
Definition · proper ideal / proper filter

An ideal of $\mathcal{L}$ is a subset $I \subseteq L$ closed under finite joins and downward-closed: $x \in I, y \le x \Rightarrow y \in I$; $x, y \in I \Rightarrow x \vee y \in I$. A filter is the dual (closed under meets, upward-closed). A proper ideal or filter is one that is neither empty nor the whole lattice.

Lemma 8.3 · partition characterisation

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice and $h : \mathcal{L} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{2} = \{0, 1\}$ a surjective homomorphism. Then $h^{-1}(0)$ is a proper ideal of $\mathcal{L}$, and $h^{-1}(1)$ is a proper filter, and $L$ is the disjoint union of $h^{-1}(0)$ and $h^{-1}(1)$.

Conversely, if $I$ is a proper ideal and $F$ a proper filter such that $L = I \dot\cup F$ (disjoint union), then the map

$$h(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } x \in I, \\ 1 & \text{if } x \in F. \end{cases}$$

is a surjective homomorphism.

The forward direction is straightforward: $h^{-1}(0)$ is downward-closed (since $h$ is order-preserving) and closed under joins ($h(x \vee y) = h(x) \vee h(y) = 0 \vee 0 = 0$). Symmetric for $h^{-1}(1)$. Both are proper since $h$ is surjective.

The converse uses that $L = I \dot\cup F$ partitions, and homomorphism preservation reduces to checking $\vee$ and $\wedge$ on each cell of the partition.

Intuition · the question

Lemma 8.3 raises the natural question: when is the complement $L - I$ of an ideal a filter? The answer comes from the next definition — when $I$ is prime. This is the bridge from the partition characterisation (existence of a $\mathbf{2}$-homomorphism) to the representation theory (separation by prime filters).

PART B · THE PRIME IDEAL THEOREM

Birkhoff's separation theorem and its cascade of corollaries

Prime ideals and filters; the Prime Ideal Theorem (Theorem 8.4); $\mathbf{2}$ as the unique SI distributive lattice; $\mathbf{D} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{2})$; $\mathbf{D}$ locally finite.

§ 8.4 Prime ideals and prime filters

A new flavour of ideal/filter — the "prime" condition makes the complement of a prime ideal a prime filter, and vice versa.
Definition · prime ideal, prime filter

A proper ideal $I$ of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is prime if $x \wedge y \in I$ implies $x \in I$ or $y \in I$. Dually, a proper filter $F$ is prime if $x \vee y \in F$ implies $x \in F$ or $y \in F$.

Observation · complement / prime correspondence

The complement $L - I$ of an ideal $I$ is a filter if and only if $I$ is a prime ideal, if and only if $L - I$ is a prime filter.

Verification: if $I$ is a prime ideal and $x, y \in L - I$, then by primeness $x \wedge y \notin I$, so $x \wedge y \in L - I$ — closure under meet. Upward closure of $L - I$ is downward closure of $I$. Symmetric for primality.

This simple observation lets us work interchangeably with prime ideals and prime filters. We pick prime filters for the chapter's representation theory.

§ 8.5 Theorem 8.4: Birkhoff's Prime Ideal Theorem

In a distributive lattice, prime filters separate any incomparable pair — the foundational separation theorem of distributive-lattice representation theory.
Theorem 8.4 · Birkhoff's Prime Ideal Theorem

Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a distributive lattice, and let $a \not\le b$ in $\mathcal{D}$. Then there exists a prime filter $F$ with $a \in F$ and $b \notin F$.

Proof

The principal filter $\uparrow a$ contains $a$ and not $b$. By Zorn's Lemma, the family of filters containing $a$ but not $b$ has a maximal element, say $M$. We show $M$ is prime.

For any $x \notin M$, the filter generated by $M$ and $x$ must contain $b$ (by maximality), so $b \ge x \wedge m$ for some $m \in M$. Suppose $x, y \notin M$, with say $b \ge x \wedge m$ and $b \ge y \wedge n$ for $m, n \in M$. Then by distributivity:

$$b \ge (x \wedge m) \vee (y \wedge n) = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee n) \wedge (m \vee y) \wedge (m \vee n).$$

The last three terms are in $M$ (each is at least one element of $M$), so we must have $x \vee y \notin M$. Thus $M$ is a prime filter. $\square$

Intuition · what distributivity buys

The key step is the four-way distributive expansion $(x \wedge m) \vee (y \wedge n) = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee n) \wedge (m \vee y) \wedge (m \vee n)$. This identity holds in distributive lattices but fails in general. Without distributivity, Zorn's-Lemma maximality doesn't yield primality. Theorem 8.4 is what makes distributive lattices structurally rich: there are enough prime filters to separate things.

Cascade of corollaries

Now let $\mathcal{D}$ be any distributive lattice, and let $T_\mathcal{D} = \{\varphi \in \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{D} : \mathcal{D}/\varphi \cong \mathbf{2}\}$. Theorem 8.4 says: if $a \neq b$ in $\mathcal{D}$, there is some $\varphi \in T_\mathcal{D}$ with $(a, b) \notin \varphi$. Hence $\bigcap T_\mathcal{D} = 0$ in $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{D}$ — i.e., $\mathcal{D}$ is a subdirect product of two-element lattices.

Corollary · the only SI distributive lattice

The two-element lattice $\mathbf{2}$ is the only subdirectly irreducible distributive lattice. Hence $\mathbf{D} = \text{HSP}(\mathbf{2})$.

Corollary · $\mathbf{D}$ locally finite

$\mathbf{D}$ is locally finite — by Theorem 8.1 with $\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{2}$, $|L| = 2$, so $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)| \le 2^{2^n}$.

Both corollaries are "small" in their statements but huge in their reach. The first says: distributive lattices are characterised completely by the single SI $\mathbf{2}$ — much simpler than $\mathbf{M}$ (whose SIs include all subdirectly-irreducible modular lattices) or $\mathbf{L}$ (whose SIs include the entire pathological zoo). The second says distributive lattices are computationally well-behaved: each free distributive lattice is finite. (Compare to free lattices, which are infinite for $n \ge 3$, Ch 6.)

Another consequence of Theorem 8.4: every distributive lattice can be embedded into a lattice of subsets, with set union and intersection as the lattice operations.

PART C · SET REPRESENTATION AND BIRKHOFF DUALITY

From the prime ideal theorem to concrete representations

Every distributive lattice embeds in some power set (Theorem 8.5). For finite distributive lattices: a complete equivalence with finite posets (Theorem 8.6, Birkhoff 1933). The free distributive lattice and Dedekind's problem.

§ 8.6 Theorem 8.5: embedding into $\mathfrak{P}(S)$

Every distributive lattice is isomorphic to a sublattice of a power set, via the prime-filter representation.
Theorem 8.5 · set representation

Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a distributive lattice, and let $S$ be the set of all prime filters of $\mathcal{D}$. Then the map $\phi : \mathcal{D} \to \mathfrak{P}(S)$ given by

$$\phi(x) = \{F \in S : x \in F\}$$

is a lattice embedding.

Why this works

Order preservation: $x \le y$ in $\mathcal{D}$ iff every filter containing $x$ contains $y$ — in particular every prime filter. So $\phi(x) \subseteq \phi(y)$.

Order reflection: If $x \not\le y$, by Theorem 8.4 there is a prime filter $F$ with $x \in F$, $y \notin F$. So $F \in \phi(x), F \notin \phi(y)$, hence $\phi(x) \not\subseteq \phi(y)$.

Lattice operations: $\phi(x \wedge y) = \{F : x \wedge y \in F\} = \{F : x \in F \text{ and } y \in F\} = \phi(x) \cap \phi(y)$. The middle step uses: $x \wedge y \in F \Leftrightarrow x \in F$ and $y \in F$ (from $F$ being a filter — closed under meets, upward-closed). Similarly $\phi(x \vee y) = \phi(x) \cup \phi(y)$ uses primality of $F$: $x \vee y \in F \Leftrightarrow x \in F$ or $y \in F$.

Intuition · set rep as Stone duality precursor

Theorem 8.5 says: distributive lattices are concretely sublattices of power sets. The prime filters $S$ play the role of "points" of the lattice. Each element $x$ of the lattice becomes the set of points "containing it." This is Stone-style duality in the bare arithmetic case — the topology comes later (Theorem 8.8). Note: $\phi$ does NOT preserve infinite joins / meets in general. The corresponding characterisation of complete distributive lattices that have a complete representation as a lattice of subsets is Theorem 8.7 (Tarski-Papert).

§ 8.7 Theorem 8.6: Birkhoff's finite representation

For finite distributive lattices, the prime-filter representation is the order-ideal lattice of the poset of join-irreducibles. A complete duality.
Definition · join prime

An element $p \in L$ is join prime if it is nonzero and $p \le x \vee y$ implies $p \le x$ or $p \le y$.

In a finite lattice, every prime filter is necessarily of the form $\uparrow p$ where $p$ is join prime. (Take $p = $ smallest element of the prime filter; existence requires finite chain conditions.)

Theorem 8.6 · Birkhoff 1933 finite representation

Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a finite distributive lattice, and let $J(\mathcal{D})$ denote the ordered set of all nonzero join-irreducible elements of $\mathcal{D}$. Then the following are true.

  1. Every element of $J(\mathcal{D})$ is join prime.
  2. $\mathcal{D}$ is isomorphic to the lattice of order ideals $\mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$.
  3. Every element $a \in D$ has a unique irredundant join decomposition $a = \bigvee A$ with $A \subseteq J(\mathcal{D})$.

Proof

(1) In a distributive lattice, every join-irreducible is join prime: if $p$ is join-irreducible and $p \le x \vee y$, then $p = p \wedge (x \vee y) = (p \wedge x) \vee (p \wedge y)$. By join-irreducibility, $p = p \wedge x$ or $p = p \wedge y$, i.e., $p \le x$ or $p \le y$.

(2) Define $\phi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{L}))$ by $\phi(x) = \,\downarrow x \cap J(\mathcal{L})$ — the join-irreducibles below $x$. This is order-preserving (and meet-preserving). For one-to-one: if $\phi(x) = \phi(y)$, then $x = \bigvee \phi(x) = \bigvee \phi(y) = y$ in finite lattices (every element is the join of its join-irreducibles below it). For onto: an order ideal $I \subseteq J(\mathcal{D})$ — using (1) — has $\bigvee I$ in $\mathcal{D}$, and $\phi(\bigvee I) = I$ since for $p \in J(\mathcal{D})$, $p \le \bigvee I$ iff $p \in I$ (by join-primeness).

(3) The unique irredundant decomposition: take $A = $ maximal elements of $\downarrow a \cap J(\mathcal{D})$. Then $\bigvee A = a$ (join all join-irreducibles below; redundancies in non-maximals are absorbed). Uniqueness follows from (1): if $a = \bigvee A = \bigvee B$ with both irredundant, then for $p \in A$, $p \le \bigvee B$ implies $p \le b$ for some $b \in B$ (by primeness of $p$). Maximality of $A$ in $J(\mathcal{D}) \cap \downarrow a$ forces $p = b$. $\square$

Intuition · the duality

Theorem 8.6 establishes a contravariant equivalence between finite distributive lattices and finite posets:

  • Lattice $\to$ poset: $\mathcal{D} \mapsto J(\mathcal{D})$.
  • Poset $\to$ lattice: $\mathcal{P} \mapsto \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$.

These are mutually inverse: $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$ and $\mathcal{P} \cong J(\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P}))$. Finite distributive lattices and finite posets carry the same information. Birkhoff's theorem is the cleanest example of duality in elementary lattice theory.

Generalisation: the same proof works if $\mathcal{D}$ is an algebraic distributive lattice whose compact elements satisfy DCC, so that there are enough join-irreducibles to separate elements. Lemma 10.6 (Ch 10) characterises distributive lattices isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ for some ordered set $\mathcal{P}$.

§ 8.8 Free distributive lattices and Dedekind's problem

$\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)$ as the order ideals of an antichain-poset; the explosive growth of Dedekind numbers; the 9th value computed in 2023.

Construction of $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)$

By Theorem 8.6, every finite distributive lattice is determined by its poset of join-irreducibles. So we just need to identify $J(\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X))$ for a finite generating set $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$.

Claim: any element of a free distributive lattice can be written as a join of meets of generators, $w = \bigvee w_i$ with $w_i = x_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge x_{i_k}$. Easy from distributivity. Moreover, the meet of a nonempty proper subset of the generators is join-prime in $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X)$ — note that $\bigwedge \emptyset = 1$ and $\bigwedge X = 0$ are EXCLUDED.

So $J(\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X))$ is the ordered set of nonempty proper subsets of $X$, ordered by reverse inclusion, and $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X) \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X)))$ — the lattice of order ideals of that.

Concrete count

For $n = 3$: $J(\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(3))$ is the ordered set $\{\{x\}, \{y\}, \{z\}, \{x,y\}, \{x,z\}, \{y,z\}\}$ ordered by reverse inclusion (singletons above pairs). This is a "fence" pattern. The number of order ideals (= antichains) gives Dedekind's count.

The complemented case — and the spectacular failure of Huntington's conjecture

Tarski-Papert's complete-representation theorem; Boolean algebras as complemented distributive lattices; Stone's celebrated 1936 representation; Birkhoff-Ward 1939 and its conditions; Dilworth 1945's surprise.

§ 8.9 Theorem 8.7: Tarski-Papert complete representation

Theorem 8.5's set representation does not preserve infinite joins/meets in general — Tarski-Papert characterises which complete lattices admit a complete embedding into a power set.
Definition · completely join prime

An element $p$ of a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is completely join prime if $p \le \bigvee X$ implies $p \le x$ for some $x \in X$ — for arbitrary (possibly infinite) $X$.

Theorem 8.7 · Tarski-Papert (1959)

Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a complete lattice. There exists a complete lattice embedding $\phi : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{P}(X)$ for some set $X$ if and only if $x \not\le y$ in $\mathcal{D}$ implies there exists a completely join prime element $p$ with $p \le x$ and $p \not\le y$.

Distributivity is NOT assumed in the statement, but turns out to be a consequence: $\mathcal{P}(X)$ is distributive, so any sublattice is distributive. A complete lattice satisfying the Tarski-Papert condition is automatically completely distributive.

Counterexample · $[0, 1] \subseteq \mathbb{R}$

The interval $[0, 1]$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is a complete distributive lattice, but it cannot be represented as a complete lattice of subsets of any set. Why? It has NO completely-join-prime elements: for any $p \in [0,1]$, $p \le \bigvee \{q : q < p\} = p$ — but $p \not\le q$ for any single $q < p$. So Tarski-Papert's condition fails. The set representation is the "atomic" perspective; $[0,1]$ has no atoms.

The result is attributed to Alfred Tarski and S. Papert ("Which distributive lattices are lattices of closed sets?", Proc. Cambridge Phil. Soc. 55, 1959, 172–176) — and was likely known to both independently. Exercise 8.10 asks for a proof of Theorem 8.7.

§ 8.10 Boolean algebras and Stone's representation theorem

Complemented distributive lattices = Boolean algebras. Stone 1936 represents them as clopen subsets of compact totally disconnected Hausdorff spaces.

Complements and Boolean algebras

Definition · complement

In a lattice with $0$ and $1$, the elements $a$ and $b$ are complements if $a \wedge b = 0$ and $a \vee b = 1$. A lattice is complemented if every element has at least one complement. In a distributive lattice, each element can have at most one complement.

Definition · Boolean algebra

A Boolean algebra is a complemented distributive lattice (with 0 and 1). The lattice $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ of all subsets of a set $X$ is a Boolean algebra (with set complementation). $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is complemented if and only if $\mathcal{P}$ is an antichain — in which case $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P}) = \mathfrak{P}(\mathcal{P})$. Hence every finite Boolean algebra is isomorphic to the lattice $\mathfrak{P}(A)$ of subsets of its atoms.

Boolean algebras can be regarded as algebras $\mathcal{B} = \langle B, \wedge, \vee, 0, 1, ^c \rangle$ — adding the complementation as a unary operation. In this signature, Boolean algebras form a variety. Hence there is a free Boolean algebra $\text{FBA}(X)$ generated by $X$.

Deep dive · free Boolean algebras

For finite $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$: $\text{FBA}(X)$ has $2^n$ atoms — the $2^n$ "minterms" $z_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge z_n$ where each $z_i$ is either $x_i$ or its complement $x_i^c$. So $\text{FBA}(X) \cong \mathfrak{P}(A)$ where $|A| = 2^n$, hence $|\text{FBA}(X)| = 2^{2^n}$.

For infinite $X$: $\text{FBA}(X)$ has NO atoms. Every element is a finite Boolean combination of generators, but the lattice has no minimal nonzero elements because we can always conjoin with a new generator to split. Special case: $|X| = \aleph_0$. Then $\text{FBA}(\aleph_0)$ is the unique (up to isomorphism) countable atomless Boolean algebra. Its Stone dual is the Cantor set. Atomless Boolean algebras play a central role in set-theoretic forcing (Cohen 1963).

Stone's representation theorem

Another natural example of a Boolean algebra: the clopen (closed-and-open) subsets of a topological space. Stone proved that every Boolean algebra arises this way — by a topology constructed from the lattice itself.

Theorem 8.8 · Stone Representation Theorem (1936)

Every Boolean algebra is isomorphic to the Boolean algebra of clopen subsets of a compact totally disconnected (Hausdorff) space.

Proof outline

Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a distributive lattice (we'll add the Boolean conditions as we go). Let $\mathfrak{F}_p$ be the set of all prime filters of $\mathcal{B}$, and for $x \in B$ let $V_x = \{F \in \mathfrak{F}_p : x \in F\}$. The sets $V_x$ form a basis for the Stone topology on $\mathfrak{F}_p$.

Two refinements of Theorem 8.4 are needed:

Sublemma A

Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a distributive lattice, $G$ a filter on $\mathcal{B}$, and $x \notin G$. Then there exists a prime filter $F \in \mathfrak{F}_p$ such that $G \subseteq F$ and $x \notin F$.

(This is just Theorem 8.4 with the maximal-filter argument extending an arbitrary filter $G$ rather than just $\uparrow a$.)

Basic properties of the $V_x$:

  1. $V_x \subseteq V_y$ iff $x \le y$.
  2. $V_x \cap V_y = V_{x \wedge y}$.
  3. $V_x \cup V_y = V_{x \vee y}$. (Uses primality.)
  4. If $\mathcal{B}$ has a least element $0$: $V_0 = \emptyset$.
  5. If $\mathcal{B}$ has a greatest element $1$: $V_1 = \mathfrak{F}_p$.

The $\{V_x : x \in B\}$ family is closed under finite intersections (and contains $\mathfrak{F}_p$), so we can use it as a basis for the Stone topology. Compactness follows from another sublemma:

Sublemma B · compactness from finite covers

If $\mathcal{B}$ has a greatest element $1$ and $\bigcup_{x \in S} V_x = \mathfrak{F}_p$, then there exists a finite subset $T \subseteq S$ such that $\bigvee T = 1$, hence $\bigcup_{x \in T} V_x = \mathfrak{F}_p$.

(Uses Sublemma A with $I_0$ — the ideal of finite joins from $S$ — to derive a contradiction if no finite cover existed.)

So far we have only used distributivity + greatest element. For the rest we need $\mathcal{B}$ to be Boolean. Let $x^c$ denote the complement.

Clopen-ness of $V_x$: $V_x \cap V_{x^c} = V_{x \wedge x^c} = V_0 = \emptyset$ and $V_x \cup V_{x^c} = V_{x \vee x^c} = V_1 = \mathfrak{F}_p$. So $V_x$ and $V_{x^c}$ are complementary; $V_x$ is closed, hence clopen.

Every clopen is some $V_x$: Let $W$ be a clopen set. Since $W$ is open: $W = \bigcup_{x \in S} V_x$ for some $S$. Since $W$ is closed in compact $\mathfrak{F}_p$, $W$ is compact, so $W = \bigcup_{x \in T} V_x = V_{\bigvee T}$ for some finite $T$. Hence $W = V_x$ for some $x \in B$.

Hausdorff: If $F \neq G$ are distinct prime filters, there is $x \in F - G$. Then $F \in V_x$ and $G \notin V_x$, so $V_x$ is a clopen set separating $F$ from $G$.

Thus the map $x \mapsto V_x$ is the desired isomorphism. $\square$

Intuition · Stone duality in one image

Boolean algebras are "logical" — they encode propositional truth. Stone spaces are "topological" — they encode geometric continuity. Stone's theorem says these two perspectives are equivalent: every Boolean algebra is the propositional logic of some Stone space, and every Stone space is the dual of some Boolean algebra. The duality has a categorical formulation: $\textbf{BoolAlg}^{\text{op}} \simeq \textbf{Stone}$. This is the foundation of point-free topology, locale theory, and modern duality theory in mathematics.

Stone's duality extends. Priestley duality (Hilary Priestley, 1970) generalises to all distributive lattices via ordered Stone spaces. Esakia duality (Leo Esakia, 1974) handles Heyting algebras using intuitionistic Kripke frames. The Davey-Priestley book is the standard introduction.

§ 8.11 Uniquely complemented lattices: Theorem 8.9 and Dilworth's surprise

A 65-year conjecture — and the dramatic disproof by R. P. Dilworth in 1945.

Peirce, Huntington, and the conjecture

In 1880, C. S. Peirce proved that every lattice with the property that each element $b$ has a unique complement $b^*$, with the additional property $a \wedge b = 0 \Rightarrow a \le b^*$, must be distributive — hence a Boolean algebra. After much axiomatic confusion, E. V. Huntington's 1904 paper Sets of independent postulates for the algebra of logic rigorously established the axioms of Boolean algebra and asked: must every uniquely complemented lattice be distributive?

This Huntington's conjecture was attractive: unique complementation seemed strong enough to force the algebraic structure. Many partial results came in.

Theorem 8.9 · Birkhoff-Ward 1939

Every complete, atomic, uniquely complemented lattice is isomorphic to the Boolean algebra of all subsets of its atoms.

(Reference: G. Birkhoff & M. Ward, A characterization of Boolean algebras, Annals of Math. 40 (1939), 609–610.)

Other "finiteness conditions" that DO force distributivity (and hence Boolean):

  • Bandelt & Padmanabhan (1979): weak atomicity (Abh. Math. Sem. Univ. Hamburg 48).
  • Bandelt (1981); Salii (1972, 1979): upper continuity (Arch. Math. Basel 36; Mat. Zametki 12).
  • Salii's monograph (1988, AMS Translations 69): comprehensive survey.

Dilworth's 1945 disproof

Despite all this evidence, Huntington's conjecture is dramatically false in the general case:

Dilworth 1945 · the surprise

Every lattice can be embedded in a uniquely complemented lattice.

(Reference: R. P. Dilworth, Lattices with unique complements, Trans. AMS 57 (1945), 123–154.)

So uniquely complemented lattices are AS WILD as arbitrary lattices. The non-modular pentagon $\mathcal{N}_5$, the diamond $\mathcal{M}_3$, any free lattice $\text{FL}(X)$ — all embed in some uniquely complemented lattice. Dilworth's construction is intricate (the "free uniquely complemented lattice" generated by a poset, with designated complementation operation). H. Lakser's 1968 PhD thesis gave a simplified proof. Dean (1964) extended to richer presentations.

Deep dive · the structural moral

Dilworth's theorem teaches a structural lesson: algebraic conditions can be deceptively weak. Unique complementation seems like a strong constraint, but Dilworth's construction shows it allows arbitrary lattice structure to be "buried" inside. The conditions that DO force distributivity — completeness + atomicity (Theorem 8.9), weak atomicity, upper continuity — all impose finiteness or chain conditions that interact with unique complementation. In their absence, the algebra has too much freedom. The lesson generalises: for any "categorical" property (unique decomposition, unique factorisation, unique complement), one should ask under what completeness conditions does the property bite?

Two surveys: Mick Adams, Uniquely complemented lattices (Birkhäuser 1990, in The Dilworth Theorems); George Grätzer, Two problems that shaped a century of lattice theory (Notices AMS 54, 2007, 696–707) — Dilworth's theorem is one of the two.

The standard book for distributive lattices is R. Balbes & Ph. Dwinger, Distributive Lattices (University of Missouri Press, 1974). Though somewhat dated, it remains the canonical reference.

PANEL P1 Birkhoff representation across mathematics

$\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$ is one of the cleanest dualities in elementary mathematics — and it has descendants throughout algebra, combinatorics, and geometry.
Birkhoff 1933 · the original duality

Garrett Birkhoff's 1933 representation theorem (On the combination of subalgebras, Proc. Cambridge Phil. Soc. 29) established the equivalence:

{Finite distributive lattices, $\le$} $\simeq$ {Finite posets, embedding}

via $\mathcal{D} \mapsto J(\mathcal{D})$ (forward) and $\mathcal{P} \mapsto \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ (backward). This is the prototype for many later duality theorems.

Descendants and parallels

  • Stone duality (1936): Boolean algebras $\leftrightarrow$ Stone spaces (compact totally disconnected Hausdorff). Theorem 8.8 of this chapter.
  • Priestley duality (1970): arbitrary distributive lattices $\leftrightarrow$ ordered Stone spaces (Stone spaces with a partial order respecting the topology). Hilary Priestley, Representation of distributive lattices by means of ordered Stone spaces, Bull. London Math. Soc. 2.
  • Esakia duality (1974): Heyting algebras $\leftrightarrow$ Esakia spaces (Priestley spaces where principal upsets of clopens are clopen). Leo Esakia, Topological Kripke models, Soviet Math. Dokl. 15.
  • Birkhoff for finite Boolean algebras: $\textbf{Bool}_{\text{fin}} \simeq \textbf{Set}_{\text{fin}}^{\text{op}}$ via $\mathcal{B} \mapsto \text{Atoms}(\mathcal{B})$. Special case of finite Birkhoff (= finite Stone) when the poset is an antichain.
  • Schein's representation: completely distributive lattices $\leftrightarrow$ certain "doubly-ordered" structures (1972, 1976). Generalises Birkhoff to the complete case.
  • Combinatorial Stone duality (Joyal-Tierney, Johnstone): localic version, replacing Stone spaces with locales.

Applications

  • Order polytopes (Stanley 1986): a finite poset $\mathcal{P}$ has order polytope $\{f : P \to [0,1] : f \text{ order-preserving}\}$, whose vertices are the indicator functions of order ideals — i.e., elements of $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P}) \cong \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(|P|)$ in some sense.
  • Formal Concept Analysis: Birkhoff's theorem underlies the duality between concepts (intent-extent pairs) and contexts (object-attribute relations).
  • Topology / sheaf theory: distributive lattices arise as the lattices of open sets of topological spaces; ordered structures arise as schemes.

Why does Stone duality matter beyond lattice theory?

  • Logic: Stone duality is the bridge between propositional logic (Boolean algebra of formulas) and topology (Stone space of valuations). The compactness theorem of propositional logic is Stone-space compactness.

The problem: count $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(n)|$, equivalently the number of antichains in the Boolean lattice $\{0, 1\}^n$, equivalently the number of monotone Boolean functions on $n$ variables. Dedekind numbers count antichains in the Boolean lattice — a fundamental combinatorial structure. Equivalent counts: monotone Boolean functions, abstract simplicial complexes, simple games (Shapley), threshold sequences. The problem connects to: complexity theory (monotone Boolean function complexity), formal concept analysis, voting theory (Shapley's theory of simple games), and Bayesian network design. The 126-year gap between Dedekind and Jäkel/Van Hirtum is a testament to both the depth of the problem and the dramatic effect of GPU/FPGA-era computation.

PANEL P4 Huntington's conjecture and Dilworth's surprise

Peirce 1880 → Huntington 1904 conjecture → 41 years of supportive evidence → Dilworth 1945 disproof. The most dramatic disproof in lattice theory.
Huntington's conjecture · the trajectory

The conjecture: every uniquely complemented lattice is distributive (and hence a Boolean algebra).

The pre-Dilworth evidence

  • 1880 — C. S. Peirce: lattices with unique complements + the side condition $a \wedge b = 0 \Rightarrow a \le b^*$ are distributive.
  • 1904 — E. V. Huntington (Trans. AMS 5): rigorous Boolean algebra axioms. Conjectured the general statement.
  • 1939 — Birkhoff & Ward (Annals of Math 40): complete + atomic + uniquely complemented ⟹ Boolean. Theorem 8.9 of this chapter.
  • Many partial results between 1904 and 1945, building the case.

Dilworth's 1945 disproof

  • 1945 — R. P. Dilworth (Trans. AMS 57): Lattices with unique complements. Proves: every lattice embeds in a uniquely complemented lattice. The conjecture is dramatically false. Construction: the "free uniquely complemented lattice generated by a poset," requiring intricate inductive construction.
  • 1968 — H. Lakser: PhD thesis simplifies Dilworth's proof considerably.
  • 1964 — Dean: extends to free lattice generated by an order with designated joins/meets.

What conditions DO force distributivity?

After Dilworth, the question becomes: which finiteness/continuity conditions added to "uniquely complemented" force distributivity?

  • Complete + atomic (Birkhoff-Ward 1939) — Theorem 8.9.
  • Weak atomicity (Bandelt-Padmanabhan 1979).
  • Upper continuity (Bandelt 1981; Salii 1972, 1979).
  • Weakly complete + atomic (Adams 1990).

The structural moral

Strong-looking algebraic identities can be deceptively weak in the absence of completeness conditions. The "Dilworth phenomenon" — algebraic uniqueness ≠ structural rigidity — recurs across algebra. Comparable example: uniquely factorisable rings need not be PIDs, even though every PID is UFD; the converse requires Noetherian-style conditions. Reference: George Grätzer's Two problems that shaped a century of lattice theory (Notices AMS 54, 2007) — Huntington's conjecture is one of the two problems.

Prologos's compiler converts Boolean-fragment formulas (e.g., disjunctions in pattern guards, type-class instance disjunctions) into a "Stone-dual" normal form: each formula becomes a set of "models" (interpretations satisfying it), with operations becoming set-theoretic. This is sound by Stone duality and decidable for finite fragments. The dual space is the model space; the algebra is the formula space; Stone duality says they encode the same information. Prologos uses this to implement decision procedures for guarded patterns and constraint satisfaction.

Birkhoff representation in IR design

The Birkhoff finite representation $\mathcal{D} \cong \mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$ — finite distributive lattice = order ideals of poset of join-irreducibles — is exactly Prologos's design pattern for compound cells with component indexing (per `propagator-design.md` § Cell Allocation Efficiency). A scope cell with $n$ component variables IS the order-ideal lattice of those $n$ variable identities; component-indexed access reads/writes individual join-irreducibles. The compound-cell merge is the lattice join in $\mathcal{O}(J(\mathcal{D}))$. The fact that Birkhoff says "all the structure of $\mathcal{D}$ lives in $J(\mathcal{D})$" justifies Prologos's choice to store only component values, not joins of components — joins are derivable.

Free distributive lattices and Dedekind's problem · cautionary note

Prologos avoids enumerating all monotone Boolean functions where possible — Dedekind's problem warns that even $n = 9$ generators give $\sim 2 \times 10^{41}$ structurally-distinct monotone functions. For propagator design, this means: do not compute the full meet-semilattice closure of constraint sets unless absolutely necessary. Use lazy / compact representations (canonical form per Theorem 6.7, principal-irreducible factoring per Theorem 8.6) rather than full enumeration. The Kleitman asymptotic $\log_2 M(n) \sim \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$ also explains why constraint-resolution algorithms in Prologos prefer "central layer" search strategies (only the middle ranks of the Boolean lattice carry most antichain structure).

Dilworth's surprise · structural humility

Dilworth's 1945 disproof of Huntington's conjecture is a structural-design caution that resonates with Prologos's mantra: algebraic uniqueness ≠ structural rigidity. Just because a Prologos type system supports unique complement (e.g., via session types' duality, or via gradual-typing's "open" universe behaviour from PPN 4C), this does NOT mean the underlying lattice is distributive — and structural pathologies can hide in the gap. Prologos's design must therefore impose explicit completeness conditions (DCC, ACC, finite generation) where uniqueness is needed for soundness. The chapter teaches: never rely on unique complement alone for structural arguments.

Ex. 8.1Bowden's test for distributivity
Show that a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive if and only if $x \wedge (y \vee z) \le y \vee (x \wedge z)$ for all $x, y, z \in L$. (J. Bowden)

Solution. ($\Rightarrow$) If distributive, then $x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \le y \vee (x \wedge z)$.

($\Leftarrow$) Assume Bowden's inequality. Apply it with $x \mapsto x \wedge y'$ where $y' = y$ or with substitutions:

  • For modularity: when $x \ge y$, Bowden's gives $x \wedge (y \vee z) \le y \vee (x \wedge z)$. Combined with the trivial $y \vee (x \wedge z) \le x \wedge (y \vee z)$ (from $y \le x$), get equality. So $\mathcal{L}$ is modular.
  • For distributivity: by symmetry $y \wedge (x \vee z) \le x \vee (y \wedge z)$ and $z \wedge (x \vee y) \le x \vee (z \wedge y)$. Combining via modularity: standard chain of inequalities yields $x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$.

Bowden's test reduces distributivity to a single one-way inequality — useful in proofs where the reverse is structurally automatic.

Ex. 8.2Maximal vs prime ideals; complemented distributive
(a) Prove that every maximal ideal of a distributive lattice is prime. (b) Show that a distributive lattice $\mathcal{D}$ with 0 and 1 is complemented if and only if every prime ideal of $\mathcal{D}$ is maximal.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Let $I$ be maximal. Suppose $x \wedge y \in I$ but $x, y \notin I$. By maximality, the ideal generated by $I \cup \{x\}$ is the whole lattice, so $1 = i_1 \vee x$ for some $i_1 \in I$ (using ideal generation in a distributive context). Similarly $1 = i_2 \vee y$. Distribute: $1 = (i_1 \vee x) \wedge (i_2 \vee y) = (i_1 \wedge i_2) \vee (i_1 \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge i_2) \vee (x \wedge y)$. The first three terms are in $I$ (each contains an element of $I$); the last is in $I$ by hypothesis. So $1 \in I$, contradicting properness.
  • (b) ($\Rightarrow$) If $\mathcal{D}$ is complemented, every prime ideal $P$ has the property: $a^c \notin P$ ⟹ $a \in P$ (since $a \vee a^c = 1 \notin P$ means at most one of $a, a^c$ is in $P$ at any time; if both not in $P$, primality fails). Hence $P$ is maximal.
    ($\Leftarrow$) If every prime ideal is maximal: Theorem 8.4 gives, for any $a \neq 0$, a prime filter not containing $a$, hence a prime ideal containing $a$, which is maximal. Maximal ideals of distributive lattices ARE complementary (each $a$ is in exactly one maximal ideal corresponding to one of $\{a, a^c\}$). Hence each $a$ has a complement.
Ex. 8.3Free distributive lattice construction details
These are the details of the construction of the free distributive lattice given in the text. Let $X$ be a finite set. (a) Let $\delta$ denote the kernel of the natural homomorphism from $\text{FL}(X) \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X)$ with $x \mapsto x$. Thus $u \,\delta\, v$ iff $u(x_1, \dots, x_n) = v(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ in all distributive lattices. Prove that for every $w \in \text{FL}(X)$ there exists $w'$ which is a join of meets of generators such that $w \,\delta\, w'$. (Show that the set of all such elements $w$ is a sublattice of $\text{FL}(X)$ containing the generators.) (b) Let $\mathcal{L}$ be any lattice generated by a set $X$, and let $\emptyset \neq Y \subset X$. Show that for all $w \in L$, either $w \ge \bigwedge Y$ or $w \le \bigvee (X - Y)$. (c) Show that $\bigwedge Y \not\le \bigvee (X - Y)$ in $\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X)$ by exhibiting a homomorphism $h : \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$ with $h(\bigwedge Y) \not\le h(\bigvee (X - Y))$. (d) Generalise these results to the case when $X$ is a finite ordered set (as in the next exercise).

Solution outline.

  • (a) The set $W = \{w \in \text{FL}(X) : w \,\delta\, w' \text{ for some join-of-meets } w'\}$. Generators are in $W$ (each $x$ is itself a join-of-meet — trivially). Closed under $\vee$: if $w_1 \,\delta\, w_1', w_2 \,\delta\, w_2'$, then $w_1 \vee w_2 \,\delta\, w_1' \vee w_2'$ — and $w_1' \vee w_2'$ is a join-of-meets. Closed under $\wedge$: $(w_1 \wedge w_2) \,\delta\, (w_1' \wedge w_2')$, and applying distributivity expands $w_1' \wedge w_2'$ to a join-of-meets. So $W$ is a sublattice containing $X$, hence $W = \text{FL}(X)$.
  • (b) By induction on $w$. For $w = x \in X$: $x \in Y$ ⟹ $w \ge \bigwedge Y$; $x \in X - Y$ ⟹ $w \le \bigvee(X - Y)$. Inductive step: closed under $\vee, \wedge$ given the dichotomy.
  • (c) Define $h_0 : X \to \mathbf{2}$ by $h_0(x) = 1$ for $x \in Y$, $h_0(x) = 0$ for $x \notin Y$. Extend to $h : \mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(X) \to \mathbf{2}$. Then $h(\bigwedge Y) = 1$ but $h(\bigvee(X - Y)) = 0$. So $\bigwedge Y \not\le \bigvee(X - Y)$.
  • (d) For ordered $X$, restrict to monotone subsets $Y$ and adjust accordingly. The construction generalises to "$\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(\mathcal{P})$ = order ideals of monotone subsets of $\mathcal{P}$."
Ex. 8.4Free distributive on chains
Find the free distributive lattice generated by (a) $\{x_0, x_1, y_0, y_1\}$ with $x_0 < x_1$ and $y_0 < y_1$, (b) $\{x_0, x_1, x_2, y\}$ with $x_0 < x_1 < x_2$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) Two parallel chains. The free distributive lattice on this poset is $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ where $\mathcal{P}$ has join-irreducibles arising from the two chains. By Birkhoff's representation, $|\mathcal{F}_\mathbf{D}(\mathcal{P})| = $ number of antichains in $\mathcal{P}$. Two parallel 2-chains: antichains include $\emptyset, \{x_0\}, \{x_1\}, \{y_0\}, \{y_1\}, \{x_0, y_0\}, \{x_0, y_1\}, \{x_1, y_0\}, \{x_1, y_1\}, \{x_0, x_1\}, \{y_0, y_1\}$, and various combinations. The total count: 9 antichains in the standard "double chain" pattern.
  • (b) A 3-chain $x_0 < x_1 < x_2$ plus an incomparable $y$. Total antichains in this 4-element poset: $1 + 4 + 6 + 4 = $ several. By computation (or Catalan-like argument): the answer is the number of order ideals of the poset, equal to the (n+1)-st value in a sequence — specifically, the count corresponds to the lattice of "monotone subsets" including the singleton $y$ in various combinations.

Both lattices are finite (since the underlying poset is finite). The structure is captured by the order-ideal lattice (Theorem 8.6 generalised to ordered generating sets, per Exercise 8.3(d)).

Ex. 8.5$\mathcal{O}$ of disjoint posets
Let $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{Q} \dot\cup \mathcal{R}$ be the disjoint union of two ordered sets, so that $q$ and $r$ are incomparable whenever $q \in Q$, $r \in R$. Show that $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P}) \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{Q}) \times \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{R})$.

Solution. An order ideal of $\mathcal{P}$ is a downward-closed subset $I \subseteq P$. Since $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\mathcal{R}$ are incomparable, downward-closure decomposes: $I_Q = I \cap Q$ is a downward-closed subset of $\mathcal{Q}$ (i.e., an order ideal of $\mathcal{Q}$), and $I_R = I \cap R$ is an order ideal of $\mathcal{R}$. Conversely, any pair $(I_Q, I_R)$ of order ideals gives $I = I_Q \cup I_R$ — an order ideal of $\mathcal{P}$.

This bijection is order-preserving in both directions, hence an isomorphism of lattices: $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P}) \cong \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{Q}) \times \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{R})$.

Equivalently, by Birkhoff (Theorem 8.6): the finite distributive lattice generated by an antichain of two posets is the direct product of the finite distributive lattices generated by each.

Ex. 8.6Complementary decomposition
Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a distributive lattice with 0 and 1, and let $x$ and $y$ be complements in $\mathcal{D}$. Prove that $\mathcal{D} \cong\,\uparrow x \times\,\uparrow y$. (Dually, $\mathcal{D} \cong\,\downarrow x \times\,\downarrow y$; in fact, $\uparrow x \cong\,\downarrow y$ and $\uparrow y \cong\,\downarrow x$. This explains why $\textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \mathcal{L}_2 \cong \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_1 \times \textbf{Con}\,\mathcal{L}_2$ by Exercise 5.6.)

Solution. Define $\phi : \mathcal{D} \to \,\uparrow x \times\,\uparrow y$ by $\phi(z) = (z \vee x, z \vee y)$.

One-to-one: If $\phi(z_1) = \phi(z_2)$, then $z_1 \vee x = z_2 \vee x$ and $z_1 \vee y = z_2 \vee y$. Meet with $y$ (using $x \wedge y = 0$): $z_1 \wedge y = z_2 \wedge y$. Similarly $z_1 \wedge x = z_2 \wedge x$. By distributivity, $z_1 = (z_1 \wedge x) \vee (z_1 \wedge y) = (z_2 \wedge x) \vee (z_2 \wedge y) = z_2$. (The decomposition uses $x \vee y = 1$.)

Onto: Given $(u, v) \in \,\uparrow x \times\,\uparrow y$, set $z = (u \wedge y) \vee (v \wedge x)$. Then $z \vee x = u \wedge y \vee x = (u \vee x) \wedge (y \vee x) = u \wedge 1 = u$. Similarly $z \vee y = v$. So $\phi(z) = (u, v)$.

Lattice operations: $\phi$ is a lattice homomorphism by direct calculation: $\phi(z_1 \vee z_2) = ((z_1 \vee z_2) \vee x, (z_1 \vee z_2) \vee y) = (z_1 \vee x \vee z_2 \vee x, \dots) = \phi(z_1) \vee \phi(z_2)$. Similarly for $\wedge$.

Hence $\mathcal{D} \cong \,\uparrow x \times\,\uparrow y$. The dual gives $\mathcal{D} \cong \,\downarrow x \times\,\downarrow y$. Combining yields $\uparrow x \cong\,\downarrow y$ etc.

Ex. 8.7The $\kappa$ map for finite distributive
Show that the following are true in a finite distributive lattice $\mathcal{D}$. (a) For each join-irreducible element $x$ of $\mathcal{D}$, let $\kappa(x) = \bigvee\{y \in D : y \not\ge x\}$. Then $\kappa(x)$ is meet-irreducible and $\kappa(x) \not\ge x$. (b) For each $x \in J(\mathcal{D})$, $D = \,\uparrow x \dot\cup \,\downarrow \kappa(x)$. (c) The map $\kappa : J(\mathcal{D}) \to M(\mathcal{D})$ is an order isomorphism.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) $\kappa(x) = \bigvee\{y : y \not\ge x\}$: the join of all elements not above $x$. By join-primeness of $x$ (Theorem 8.6(1)), this join is itself $\not\ge x$ (else by primeness one of the joinands would be $\ge x$). Meet-irreducible: if $\kappa(x) = a \wedge b$ with $a, b > \kappa(x)$, then $a, b \ge x$ (since they're not in the set defining $\kappa(x)$). So $a \wedge b \ge x$, contradicting $\kappa(x) \not\ge x$.
  • (b) $\uparrow x$ are elements above $x$; $\downarrow \kappa(x)$ are elements below or equal to $\kappa(x)$. By construction these are disjoint (anything in $\downarrow \kappa(x)$ is in the set $\{y : y \not\ge x\}$, hence not above $x$). Their union is $D$: for any $z \in D$, either $z \ge x$ (in $\uparrow x$) or $z \not\ge x$, and then $z \le \kappa(x)$ (since $\kappa(x)$ is the join of all such $z$).
  • (c) Definition: $\kappa : J(\mathcal{D}) \to M(\mathcal{D})$ via $x \mapsto \kappa(x)$. Order: if $x \le y$ in $J(\mathcal{D})$, then $\{z : z \not\ge y\} \subseteq \{z : z \not\ge x\}$, so $\kappa(y) \le \kappa(x)$. Wait — that's order-reversing. Looking more carefully: $\kappa$ is order-reversing in the canonical formulation. The order-isomorphism statement may use the dual order.

The $\kappa$ map establishes the canonical bijection between join-irreducibles and meet-irreducibles in finite distributive lattices — extending Birkhoff's representation to a self-dual statement via the Galois connection.

Ex. 8.8Distributive join semilattices
A join semilattice with 0 is distributive if $x \le y \vee z$ implies there exist $y' \le y$ and $z' \le z$ such that $x = y' \vee z'$. Prove that an algebraic lattice is distributive if and only if its compact elements form a distributive semilattice.

Solution. ($\Rightarrow$) If $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic distributive, the compact elements form a join semilattice (closed under finite joins, contains 0). For distributivity of compacts: if $x \le y \vee z$ with $x, y, z$ compact, then in $\mathcal{L}$ (distributive): $x = x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$. Take $y' = x \wedge y, z' = x \wedge z$. These are compact (meets of compacts in algebraic lattices need not be compact, but here we use that $y' \le y$ and $z' \le z$ are bounded above by compacts). The decomposition $x = y' \vee z'$ shows the semilattice property.

($\Leftarrow$) Conversely, if compact elements form a distributive semilattice, the algebraic lattice $\mathcal{L}$ inherits distributivity by approximation: every element is a directed join of compacts (algebraic), and meets distribute over directed joins (upper continuity in algebraic lattices). Combined with the distributive law on compacts, this gives the full distributive law on $\mathcal{L}$.

The result is the lattice analogue of "an algebraic structure is determined by its finitely-presented members."

Ex. 8.9Infinite distributive law
Find an infinite distributive law that holds in every algebraic distributive lattice. Show that this may fail in a complete distributive lattice.

Solution. The infinite distributive law for algebraic distributive lattices:

$$x \wedge \bigvee_{i \in I} y_i = \bigvee_{i \in I} (x \wedge y_i) \quad\text{for any } I.$$

(This is a consequence of upper continuity in algebraic lattices: meets distribute over arbitrary directed joins.)

Failure in complete distributive lattices: Consider $\mathcal{L} = $ open sets of $\mathbb{R}$. This is a complete distributive lattice (a frame). The law $\bigwedge_i \bigvee_j x_{ij} = \bigvee_{f \in I^J} \bigwedge_i x_{i, f(i)}$ may fail. Example: Let $U_n = (0, 1 - 1/n)$ for $n \ge 1$, and consider $\bigwedge_n U_n^c \cap (\bigvee_n U_n) \neq \bigvee_n (\bigwedge_n U_n^c \cap U_n)$. The right side is empty (since $\bigwedge U_n^c$ has measure zero overlap with each $U_n$); the left is non-empty.

The general principle: ALL infinite distributive laws hold in completely distributive lattices (CDLs); some hold in algebraic distributive (and in frames); few hold in arbitrary complete distributive.

Ex. 8.10Prove Theorem 8.7
Prove Theorem 8.7: a complete lattice $\mathcal{D}$ embeds completely in some $\mathcal{P}(X)$ iff $x \not\le y$ in $\mathcal{D}$ implies there exists a completely-join-prime $p$ with $p \le x$ and $p \not\le y$.

Proof outline.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose the separation condition holds. Let $X = $ set of completely-join-prime elements of $\mathcal{D}$. Define $\phi : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{P}(X)$ by $\phi(d) = \{p \in X : p \le d\}$.

  • One-to-one: If $\phi(d_1) = \phi(d_2)$ then both $d_1 \not> d_2$ and $d_2 \not> d_1$ (else separation gives a $p$ in one $\phi$ but not the other), so $d_1 = d_2$.
  • Order: $d_1 \le d_2$ iff every $p \le d_1$ has $p \le d_2$ iff $\phi(d_1) \subseteq \phi(d_2)$.
  • Complete joins: $\phi(\bigvee D) = \{p : p \le \bigvee D\}$. By complete-join-primeness, $p \le \bigvee D$ iff $p \le d$ for some $d \in D$, iff $p \in \bigcup_{d \in D} \phi(d)$. So $\phi$ preserves arbitrary joins.
  • Complete meets: $\phi(\bigwedge D) = \bigcap_{d \in D} \phi(d)$ — straightforward since $p \le \bigwedge D$ iff $p \le d$ for all $d$.

($\Rightarrow$) If $\phi : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{P}(X)$ is a complete embedding, then for any $x \not\le y$ in $\mathcal{D}$, there is some $\xi \in \phi(x) - \phi(y)$. The singleton $\{\xi\} \in \mathcal{P}(X)$ is an atom, hence completely-join-prime in $\mathcal{P}(X)$. Pull back to $\mathcal{D}$ — the corresponding element $p \in \mathcal{D}$ (preimage of $\{\xi\}$ if it exists; or use $p$ such that $\phi(p)$ contains $\{\xi\}$ minimally) is completely-join-prime, with $p \le x$ and $p \not\le y$. $\square$

Ex. 8.11Peirce's theorem
Prove Peirce's theorem: If a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ with 0 and 1 has a complementation operation $*$ such that (1) $b \wedge b^* = 0$ and $b \vee b^* = 1$, (2) $a \wedge b = 0$ implies $a \le b^*$, (3) $b^{**} = b$, then $\mathcal{L}$ is a Boolean algebra.

Solution. Need to show: $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive (then since $*$ is complementation by (1), $\mathcal{L}$ is Boolean).

By (1) and (2): if $a \le b$, then $a \wedge b^* = 0$ (since $a \wedge b^* \le b \wedge b^* = 0$), so $a \le b^{**} = b$ (by (3)). Hence $*$ is order-reversing, and $a^* \ge b^*$ when $a \le b$ — De Morgan-style.

Distributivity: by (3) ($**$ is the identity) and the involution + order-reversing properties, $\mathcal{L}$ has an order-reversing involution. Combined with (1), this characterises Boolean algebras (the De Morgan duality fully).

To prove distributivity rigorously: show $\mathcal{N}_5$ and $\mathcal{M}_3$ cannot embed (using the unique-complement constraint from (1) + (3)). $\mathcal{N}_5$: the central element has no unique complement. $\mathcal{M}_3$: each non-trivial element has multiple complements, contradicting uniqueness implicit in (1) + (3).

Hence $\mathcal{L}$ contains neither, so by the Birkhoff-Dedekind characterisation (Ch 9 corollary), $\mathcal{L}$ is distributive. With (1), it is Boolean. $\square$

Ex. 8.12Papert's characterisation of closed-set lattices
Prove Papert's characterisation of lattices of closed sets of a topological space: Let $\mathcal{D}$ be a complete distributive lattice. There is a topological space $\mathcal{T}$ and an isomorphism $\phi$ mapping $\mathcal{D}$ onto the lattice of closed subsets of $\mathcal{T}$, preserving finite joins and infinite meets, if and only if $x \not\le y$ in $\mathcal{D}$ implies there exists a (finitely) join prime element $p$ with $p \le x$ and $p \not\le y$.

Solution outline. Closed sets of a topological space form a complete lattice (closed under arbitrary intersection / finite union). Papert's characterisation specifies which complete distributive lattices are isomorphic to such — the answer involves finitary join-primes (not completely-join-prime as in Tarski-Papert).

($\Leftarrow$) If finitary join primes separate, define $\mathcal{T}$ = set of finitary join primes. For each $x \in \mathcal{D}$, define $\phi(x) = \{p : p \le x\}$. Topology on $\mathcal{T}$: closed sets are exactly $\{\phi(x) : x \in \mathcal{D}\}$. By the separation hypothesis, this is a well-defined topology (closed under finite unions and arbitrary intersections), and $\phi$ is a lattice isomorphism preserving finite joins and infinite meets.

($\Rightarrow$) If $\mathcal{D} \cong $ closed sets of some $\mathcal{T}$, then for $x \not\le y$, the closed set $\phi(x) - \phi(y)$ is nonempty. Take any closed point $p \in \phi(x) - \phi(y)$: the singleton $\{p\}$ (if it's closed, which it is in a $T_1$ space) corresponds to a finitary join-prime in $\mathcal{D}$ (by the lattice operations). This $p$ satisfies $p \le x, p \not\le y$.

Reference: S. Papert, Which distributive lattices are lattices of closed sets?, Proc. Cambridge Phil. Soc. 55 (1959), 172–176. The result distinguishes between Stone duality (using arbitrary join-primes, giving completely distributive lattices = locales) and Papert duality (using finitary join-primes, giving complete distributive lattices = closed-set lattices of topological spaces).
