Sublattices of Free Lattices and Projective Lattices

The monograph's classification centerpiece. Nation's theorem (1982): a finite lattice embeds in some $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ if and only if it is semidistributive AND satisfies Whitman's condition (W). Conjectured by Jónsson in unpublished 1960s notes; proved here via the proof that no S-lattice (finite SD lattice satisfying (W)) contains a C-cycle. Plus the characterization of projective lattices (Freese-Nation, refining Kostinsky 1972), the free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ over an ordered set, forbidden-sublattice characterizations, and a structural summary table comparing free lattices, $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$, finitely-presented, projective, and sublattices of free lattices.

~240–300 min read 6 sections · ~70 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 95–134 · PDF pp. 104–143

The class of lattices that are embeddable into a free lattice is a naturally interesting class of lattices. Two obvious necessary conditions for a lattice to be a sublattice of a free lattice are semidistributivity and the condition (W). Except for finite lattices, reasonably applicable necessary and sufficient conditions for embeddability into a free lattice are not yet known.

Projective lattices form an important subclass of this class. They are discussed in Section 1, where the most prominent result is a characterization theorem for projective lattices due to Freese and Nation [61]. This extends earlier results due to McKenzie [99] and Kostinsky [92]. In the second section we discuss the lattice $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ freely generated by an ordered set, and the related question of which ordered sets can be embedded into free lattices. Section 3 contains a proof that for finite lattices, semidistributivity and the condition (W) together are sufficient to characterize sublattices of free lattices. This surprising result is due to Nation [103]. It was conjectured by Jónsson, who suggested a line of approach in unpublished notes written in the 1960's. Some more results on finite sublattices of a free lattice and related topics, including a characterization in terms of forbidden lattices and an extension to lattices without infinite chains, are proved in Section 4. Section 5 brings a collection of examples of finite subdirectly irreducible sublattices of a free lattice. The collection is exhaustive, as proved in Ježek and Slavík [76]. We conclude with a summary of the lattice properties of free lattices, sublattices of free lattices, projective lattices and finitely presented lattices.

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter V (paraphrased)

Chapter V is the longest chapter in the monograph (40 pages, 6 sections, ~70 numbered results) and the deepest classification chapter. Its central result — Nation's theorem (Theorem 5.55) — is one of the major theorems of late-20th-century lattice theory. It says: finite SD-and-(W) lattices are exactly the finite sublattices of free lattices. The "necessary" direction is easy (sublattices of free lattices inherit (W) and SD). The "sufficient" direction was a conjecture of Jónsson from the 1960s, settled by Nation in 1982 after about 15 years of partial progress by Jónsson, Nation, Rival, and Sands.

The chapter has six conceptual parts. Part A (§1): projective lattices — those that are retracts of free lattices. Theorem 5.7 gives a four-condition characterization (W, $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$, both refinement properties, finitely separable). Corollary 5.9: finitely-generated projective ⟺ bounded ⟺ Kostinsky's 1972 result. Corollary 5.11: finite projective ⟺ finite sublattice of FL — establishing that projectivity equals embeddability for finite lattices. Part B (§2): $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ — the free lattice over an ordered set. Built like $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ but with the order on $\mathbf{P}$ enforced. Theorem 5.22: $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is projective ⟺ $\mathbf{P}$ is finitely separable. Embeddability of ordered sets into free lattices reduces to finite separability (Corollary 5.23). Part C (§3): the proof of Nation's theorem. Lemma 5.35: for finite lattices, (1) projective, (2) sublattice of FL, (3) bounded + (W), (4) S-lattice without C-cycle are equivalent. The chapter's deepest 18 pages prove that no S-lattice contains a C-cycle, via configuration-forbidding lemmas and the B-type sequence machinery. Part D (§4): forbidden-sublattice characterizations. Davey-Poguntke-Rival's six SD-forbidden lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_6$; Antonius-Rival's eight forbidden lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$ for sublattice-of-free. Theorem 5.59: meet-SD lattices without infinite chains are finite. Part E (§5): subdirectly irreducible primitives. The infinite families $\mathbf{B}_n, \mathbf{C}_n, \mathbf{D}_n, \mathbf{E}_n, \mathbf{F}_n, \mathbf{G}_n$ from Ježek-Slavík 1989, with five constructions building larger primitives from smaller ones. Part F (§6): summary. Reinhold's *-distributivity (Theorem 5.66), the implication chart (Theorem 5.69), and Table 5.1 — the cross-reference of which structural properties hold across $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$, finitely-presented lattices, projective lattices, and sublattices of free lattices.

New · Chapter V vocabulary (substantial)

Eight major clusters of new vocabulary. Projective-lattice machinery (§1): projective lattice (every epimorphism onto $\mathbf{L}$ has a section), transversal of a surjection $f : K \to L$ (a function $g : L \to K$ with $fg = \mathrm{id}$), retraction (homomorphism with a homomorphism transversal — i.e., a transversal that is itself a homomorphism), retract of $\mathbf{K}$ (the image of a retraction $\mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$), join-preserving / order-preserving transversals. The notion of a lattice being finitely separable — there exist mappings $A, B$ with $A(a) \subseteq \{c \le a\}$ finite, $B(a) \subseteq \{c \ge a\}$ finite, and $a \le b$ implying $A(a) \cap B(b) \neq \emptyset$. Free lattice over an ordered set (§2): $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ — the lattice freely generated by $\mathbf{P}$ subject to the order, defined by an order-preserving universal property. The free distributive lattice over $\mathbf{P}$ $\mathbf{FD}(\mathbf{P})$ — the free distributive analog. S-lattices and Nation's proof machinery (§3): S-lattice (a finite SD lattice satisfying (W)), C-cycle, minimal C-cycle (one of minimal length), the subdivisions $A_1, A_2$ of A and $B_1, B_2$ of B (technical refinements for the proof). Free star principle (a quote-and-check argument used repeatedly in the proof). B-type sequence — a finite sequence $q_0, \ldots, q_k$ of join-irreducibles satisfying four conditions (B1–B4). Forbidden lattices (§4): the lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4 = \mathbf{L}_3^d, \mathbf{L}_5, \mathbf{L}_6 = \mathbf{L}_5^d$ — the six SD-failure witnesses (Davey-Poguntke-Rival 1975); $\mathbf{L}_7, \mathbf{L}_8$ — additional forbidden sublattices for the (W) condition (Antonius-Rival). Primitive lattices (§5): primitive = finite, subdirectly-irreducible, projective lattice; the four "starter" primitives $\mathbf{A}_1, \mathbf{A}_2, \mathbf{A}_3, \mathbf{A}_4$ (Figure 5.9); the six representatives $\mathbf{B}_3, \mathbf{C}_3, \mathbf{D}_3, \mathbf{E}_3, \mathbf{F}_5, \mathbf{G}_4$ of infinite families (Figure 5.10). The five constructions $\mathbf{R}, \mathbf{P}, \mathbf{P}^*, \mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{Q}^*$ that generate larger primitives from smaller ones (Example 5.65). Parallel sum (§4): $\mathbf{L} \cup \mathbf{K} \cup \{0, 1\}$ where $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}$ are disjoint intervals with no relations between elements. Ordinal sum (§1): $\mathbf{L}_0 + \mathbf{L}_1$ — ordinal sum of two lattices, projectivity criteria (Theorem 5.16). Staircase distributive — a finite-only version of *-distributive.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Five threads reward close reading. The structural arc from projective to sublattice. Corollary 5.10 and 5.11: a finitely generated lattice is projective iff it is a sublattice of a free lattice; a finite lattice is projective iff it is a finite sublattice of a free lattice. So in the FINITE case, being a retract of a free lattice = being a sublattice of a free lattice. This is genuinely surprising: in general (e.g., for groups), retracts and sublattices are different. The free-lattice case is "rigid" enough that the two notions coincide. Theorem 5.7 — projective characterization in 4 conditions. A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective iff: (1) (W); (2) $L = \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$; (3) minimal-join-cover refinement and its dual; (4) finitely separable. The four conditions are independent (Examples 5.13, 5.14, 5.15 prove this). The proof of Nation's theorem (§3). The proof works by contradiction: assume an S-lattice $\mathbf{L}$ contains a C-cycle. Take a minimal one. By a long sequence of configuration-forbidding lemmas (5.36–5.50, with the technical lemma 5.43 as the workhorse), reduce to a cycle of the form $q_1 B p_1 A_2 q_2 B p_2 A_2 \ldots q_n B p_n A_2 q_1$. Lemma 5.53 eliminates this special form. Hence no C-cycle exists. The proof is one of the most technical in lattice theory — about 18 pages of dense case analysis. The B-type sequence framework. Lemma 5.41 introduces B-type sequences as a generalization of "consecutive B's" that admits transitivity-like behavior. The framework decouples B's from C-cycle order, allowing arguments to proceed when B's are interspersed with A's. This is the key technical innovation that broke the impasse Jónsson encountered in the 1960s. Forbidden-sublattice characterizations (§4) connect to algebraic combinatorics. Theorem 5.58: a finite lattice is a sublattice of FL iff it doesn't contain $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$ as sublattices. The eight lattices are explicit, drawable, and recognisable. This converts Nation's algebraic-combinatorial characterization into a purely COMBINATORIAL test: scan for forbidden subdiagrams. The 8 lattices are the universal obstructions to free-lattice embedding.

PART A · PROJECTIVE LATTICES — §1

Projective lattices: retracts of free lattices, and Freese-Nation's 4-condition characterization

A lattice is projective if every epimorphism onto it has a homomorphism section. Equivalently, projective = retract of a free lattice. Theorem 5.7 (Freese-Nation): four conditions characterize projectivity. Corollary 5.9 (Kostinsky 1972): finitely generated projective = bounded + (W). Corollary 5.11: finite projective = finite sublattice of free lattice.

§ 1 Projective Lattices

Six theorems and seven examples. The chapter's foundational machinery, with sharp distinctions between projective, retract, and sublattice — coinciding in the finite case but diverging in general.

Definitions and equivalent forms (book pp. 95–96)

Definition · projective, transversal, retraction, retract

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if for any epimorphism $f : \mathbf{M} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{N}$ and any homomorphism $h : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{N}$ there exists a homomorphism $g : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{M}$ such that $h = fg$. (In the category of lattices, epimorphisms are just onto homomorphisms.)

Let $f$ be a mapping of a set $K$ onto a set $L$. A mapping $g : L \to K$ is a transversal of $f$ if $fg$ is the identity on $L$ — i.e., $g$ picks for each $x \in L$ a preimage $g(x) \in f^{-1}(x)$. A homomorphism $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ is a retraction if it has a transversal which is a homomorphism; the transversal is then an embedding of $\mathbf{L}$ into $\mathbf{K}$. A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a retract of a lattice $\mathbf{K}$ if there exists a retraction of $\mathbf{K}$ onto $\mathbf{L}$ — equivalently, $\mathbf{L}$ is a homomorphism image of $\mathbf{K}$ via a homomorphism with a homomorphism right-inverse.

Theorem 5.1 · projective ⟺ retract of free lattice ⟺ universal retraction property

The following are equivalent for a lattice $\mathbf{L}$.

  1. $\mathbf{L}$ is projective.
  2. $\mathbf{L}$ is a retract of a free lattice.
  3. For any lattice $\mathbf{K}$, any epimorphism $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ is a retraction.

(1)⟹(3) is the definition of projective applied with $\mathbf{N} = \mathbf{L}$ and $h = \mathrm{id}_\mathbf{L}$. (3)⟹(2): every lattice is a homomorphic image of a free lattice (the natural surjection from $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{L})$). (2)⟹(1): an extension argument given a retraction $f_0 : \mathbf{F} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ with transversal $g_0$, every homomorphism $h : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{N}$ extends to a homomorphism on $\mathbf{F}$ via $h_0 = h \circ f_0$, and we can lift to $\mathbf{M}$.

Corollary 5.2 · projective ⟹ sublattice of free

Every projective lattice is a sublattice of a free lattice.

The minimal-join-cover-refinement and $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$ properties (book p. 96)

Lemma 5.3 · join-preserving transversal preserves min-cover-refinement

Let $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ be a homomorphism such that there exists a join-preserving transversal $g$ of $f$. If $\mathbf{K}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property, then so does $\mathbf{L}$.

Lemma 5.4 · projective ⟹ $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$

If $\mathbf{L}$ is a projective lattice, then $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$.

Lemma 5.4's proof inducts on $D_k$ membership. If $b \in D_k(\mathbf{FL}(X))$ then $f(b) \in D_k(\mathbf{L})$ — so the standard epimorphism preserves D-rank. The lemma is non-trivial because in the inductive step, a minimal nontrivial cover $U' \subseteq f(V)$ might not directly satisfy $V \subseteq D_{k-1}(\mathbf{FL}(X))$ — the proof uses a sequence-permutation argument to show $f(b) \in D_k(\mathbf{L})$ via "weakening join cover" $V \ll gf(V)$.

Finite separability (book pp. 97–98)

Definition · finitely separable

A lattice (or more generally, an ordered set) $\mathbf{L}$ is finitely separable if there exist two mappings $A$ and $B$ with domain $L$ such that for any $a, b \in L$:

  • $A(a)$ is a finite subset of $\{c \in L : c \le a\}$ — the "lower-side witness set" for $a$;
  • $B(b)$ is a finite subset of $\{c \in L : c \ge b\}$ — the "upper-side witness set" for $b$;
  • If $a \le b$, then $A(a) \cap B(b) \neq \emptyset$.
Lemma 5.5 · free lattices are finitely separable

Every free lattice is finitely separable.

The proof: for $a, b \in \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{FL}(X)$, define $A(a)$ and $B(b)$ in terms of $\mathrm{var}(a)$ (the generators of $\mathbf{FL}(\mathrm{var}(a))$ that occur in $a$). Specifically, $A(a) = D_k(\mathbf{FL}(\mathrm{var}(a))) \cap \{c \le a\}$ for $k$ minimal with $a \in D_k$. Both $A(a)$ and $B(b)$ are finite subsets, and the intersection condition is verified by induction on $k + l$ where $a \in D_k(\mathbf{FL}(\mathrm{var}(a)))$ and $b \in D_l^d(\mathbf{FL}(\mathrm{var}(b)))$.

Lemma 5.6 · finitely separable ⟺ free image with order-preserving transversal

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is finitely separable if and only if there exists a homomorphism of a free lattice onto $\mathbf{L}$ with an order-preserving transversal.

Theorem 5.7 — the projective characterization (book p. 99)

Theorem 5.7 · the projective characterization (Freese-Nation)

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if it satisfies the following four conditions:

  1. Whitman's condition (W);
  2. $L = \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$;
  3. $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property and its dual;
  4. $\mathbf{L}$ is finitely separable.
Intuition · the four conditions

Each condition does specific work. (W) is the "no doubly-reducible elements" condition — without it, the lattice has elements that can be both joins and meets, which never occur in free lattices. $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$ and $\mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L}) = L$ are the two-sided lower-/upper-boundedness — guaranteeing well-foundedness of the dependency relations. The min-cover refinement properties ensure that joins and meets have constructive minimal-cover witnesses. Finitely separable ensures that comparability $a \le b$ has a finite combinatorial witness — without it, infinite chains can prevent the construction of a transversal. Theorem 5.7's proof builds the transversal $h$ by transfinite induction on a well-ordering compatible with the witness-set structure. The four conditions together IMPOSE enough structure to guarantee the transversal exists.

Corollary 5.8 · countable projective characterization

A countable lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if it satisfies the first three conditions of Theorem 5.7. (Every countable lattice is automatically finitely separable.)

Corollary 5.9 · Kostinsky 1972 — finitely generated projective ⟺ bounded + (W)

A finitely generated lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if it is a bounded lattice satisfying (W).

Corollary 5.9 — due originally to Alan Kostinsky 1972 — is the historical predecessor to Freese-Nation's general Theorem 5.7. Kostinsky's contribution was the bounded-equivalent recognition; Theorem 5.7 generalises to arbitrary cardinalities, with the additional condition (4) handling the infinite case.

Corollary 5.10 · finitely generated projective ⟺ sublattice of free

A finitely generated lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if it is a sublattice of a free lattice.

Corollary 5.11 · finite projective ⟺ finite sublattice of free

A finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if it is a sublattice of a free lattice.

The original result along these lines goes back to Ralph McKenzie [99] and, independently, Bjarni Jónsson (see [99] and [83]).

Theorem 5.12 — intervals are projective (book p. 100)

Theorem 5.12 · intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ are projective

If $v \le u$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, then $u/v$ is a projective lattice.

Theorem 5.12 says intervals in free lattices inherit projectivity. The proof uses the join-homomorphism $f : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to u/v$ defined by $f(w) = w \vee v$, with the identity as a join-preserving transversal. Lemmas 5.3 and Theorem 2.13 give the four conditions for $u/v$.

Counterexamples — the four conditions are independent (book p. 100)

Example 5.13 · (W) fails — conditions (2)(3)(4) don't suffice

The finite bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}^\vee((x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z))$ from Figure 3.3(3) on book p. 84 satisfies $L = \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$, has the min-cover refinement properties, is finitely separable (because finite), but FAILS (W). Hence not projective.

Ordinal sums and Theorem 5.16 (book p. 102)

Theorem 5.16 · ordinal-sum projectivity

Let $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{L}_0 + \mathbf{L}_1$ be the ordinal sum of two lattices $\mathbf{L}_0, \mathbf{L}_1$. Then $\mathbf{L}$ is projective if and only if both $\mathbf{L}_0$ and $\mathbf{L}_1$ are and one of the following holds:

  1. $\mathbf{L}_0$ has a greatest element.
  2. $\mathbf{L}_1$ has a least element.
  3. $\mathbf{L}_0$ has a countable cofinal chain and $\mathbf{L}_1$ has a countable coinitial chain.
Corollary 5.17 · ordinal sum of free lattices

The ordinal sum of two free lattices is a projective lattice if and only if either one of the two free lattices is finitely generated or both are countable.

Theorem 5.18 — projective ⟹ weakly atomic (book p. 103)

Theorem 5.18 · finitely-generated projective lattices are weakly atomic

Every finitely generated projective lattice is weakly atomic.

The proof uses Day's theorem — specifically Theorem 2.84 or Corollary 4.2 of Chapter IV. By Corollary 5.2, a finitely-generated projective $\mathbf{P}$ is a sublattice of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ for some finite $X$. Assume $v < u$ in $\mathbf{P}$. By Day's theorem rank refinement (Corollary 4.2), there exists a finite bounded lattice $\mathbf{L}$ and a homomorphism $g : \mathbf{FL}(X) \to \mathbf{L}$ with $g(v) < g(u)$. Then $\mathbf{L}' = g(\mathbf{P})$ is a sublattice of $\mathbf{L}$, hence bounded. The restriction $h = g|_\mathbf{P}$ is a bounded homomorphism with $h(v) < h(u)$. Choose $w \in \mathbf{P}$ with $v < w \le u$ and $h(v) \prec h(w) \le h(u)$. Then by Lemma 2.82's argument, $u \ge w \ge v \vee \beta_h h(w) \succ \alpha_h h(v) \wedge (v \vee \beta_h h(w)) \ge v$ — giving the desired covering pair in $\mathbf{P}$.

Connection P2 · Kostinsky 1972 + Freese-Nation refinement

Kostinsky's 1972 paper ("Projective lattices and bounded homomorphisms", Pacific J. Math. 40, 111–119) was the FIRST characterization of finitely-generated projective lattices: $\mathbf{L}$ is projective iff $\mathbf{L}$ is bounded and satisfies (W). The result is striking — it says projectivity (a categorical property) is determined by two purely structural conditions. The proof uses McKenzie's bounded-homomorphism machinery: a finitely-generated lattice $\mathbf{L}$ with the natural map $\mathbf{FL}(X) \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ being bounded admits a transversal which is itself a homomorphism, and (W) ensures the transversal extends to all of $\mathbf{L}$.

Freese-Nation's refinement (Theorem 5.7). Theorem 5.7 generalizes Kostinsky's characterization to ARBITRARY (not just finitely-generated) projective lattices. The fourth condition — finite separability — replaces the "finitely generated" hypothesis. For finitely-generated lattices, finite separability is automatic, so Theorem 5.7 reduces to Corollary 5.9 = Kostinsky's theorem. For COUNTABLE lattices, finite separability is also automatic (Corollary 5.8), so the condition simplifies. For UNCOUNTABLE lattices, finite separability is a genuine extra condition.

Why this matters. The Kostinsky-Freese-Nation chain of results gives a complete picture of projective lattices: finitely generated case (Kostinsky 1972), countable case (Corollary 5.8), general case (Theorem 5.7). The chain is unusual in mathematics — a clean three-step generalization where each step adds exactly one condition. The historical sequence (1972 → 1985 with the Trans. AMS paper [61]) tracks the field's growing understanding of how the classical "structural" conditions (W and bounded) generalize to capture finite witnessing of comparability.

PART B · FREE LATTICE OVER AN ORDERED SET — §2

$\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$: building free lattices that respect a pre-existing order on the generators

$\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is the free lattice with the relations from $\mathbf{P}$'s order enforced. Theorem 5.20: $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ has $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$. Theorem 5.22: $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is projective ⟺ $\mathbf{P}$ is finitely separable. Corollary 5.23: $\mathbf{P}$ embeds in some free lattice ⟺ $\mathbf{P}$ embeds in a finitely-separable ordered set.

§ 2 The Free Lattice Generated by an Ordered Set

Beyond $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ for an antichain $X$, the construction generalises to any ordered set $\mathbf{P}$. The structure mimics $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ closely except the order on generators is now non-trivial. Dean's analog of Whitman's algorithm holds. Embeddability of ordered sets into free lattices reduces to a structural condition (finite separability).

Definition and construction (book pp. 104–105)

Definition · $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ — free lattice over an ordered set

Let $\mathbf{P}$ be an ordered set. A lattice $\mathbf{F}$ is freely generated by $\mathbf{P}$ if:

  1. $F$ has a subset $P$ such that $P$ with the induced order is isomorphic to $\mathbf{P}$;
  2. $P$ generates $\mathbf{F}$;
  3. Every order preserving map $h_0 : \mathbf{P} \to \mathbf{L}$, where $\mathbf{L}$ is a lattice, can be extended to a lattice homomorphism $h : \mathbf{F} \to \mathbf{L}$.

It is not hard to see that these conditions determine $\mathbf{F}$ up to isomorphism, and hence we denote it by $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$. If $\mathbf{P}$ is an antichain, then $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is just a free lattice in the ordinary sense.

The construction follows Day's pattern (Chapter II §7): start with $\mathbf{F}_0 = \mathbf{FD}(\mathbf{P})$ (the free distributive lattice over $\mathbf{P}$), apply the doubling construction to fix (W)-failures, iterate to get $\mathbf{F}_n$, take the inverse limit. The result is $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$.

Lattices freely generated by ordered sets were first studied by R.A. Dean [34], who showed these lattices closely mimic the theory of free lattices developed by Whitman, except for the order on the generators.

Structural properties (book pp. 105–106)

Theorem 5.20 · $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})) = \mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ and dual

For any ordered set $\mathbf{P}$, the lattice $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ satisfies $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})) = \mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P}))$. Moreover, $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ has the minimal join cover refinement property and its dual.

Theorem 5.20 says $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ satisfies the first three conditions for projectivity from Theorem 5.7. Whether it satisfies condition (4) — finite separability — depends on $\mathbf{P}$.

Corollary 5.21 · countable $\mathbf{P}$ ⟹ $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ projective

If $\mathbf{P}$ is a countable ordered set, then $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is a projective lattice.

The embeddability question (book p. 106)

Theorem 5.22 · $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ projective ⟺ $\mathbf{P}$ finitely separable

$\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is a projective lattice if and only if $\mathbf{P}$ is a finitely separable ordered set.

Corollary 5.23 · embeddability of ordered sets in free lattices

The following are equivalent for an ordered set $\mathbf{P}$:

  1. $\mathbf{P}$ is embeddable in a free lattice.
  2. $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is embeddable in a free lattice.
  3. $\mathbf{P}$ is embeddable in an ordered set $\mathbf{Q}$ which is finitely separable.

Corollary 5.23 reduces the (still partly open) embeddability question for ordered sets to a structural condition (finite separability of an over-set $\mathbf{Q}$).

Examples 5.24–5.26 · concrete embeddability tests

Example 5.24: Let $\mathbf{P}$ be a bipartite ordered set consisting of two antichains $C, D$ with $c \le d$ for all $c \in C, d \in D$. Then $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is the ordinal sum of two free lattices. Hence by Corollary 5.17, $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is projective iff either $C$ or $D$ is finite, or both are countable. Example 5.25: Modifying Example 5.24 by adding a "middle" element $r$ between $C$ and $D$ to get $\mathbf{Q} = C \cup D \cup \{r\}$ with $c \le r \le d$ — then $\mathbf{Q}$ is finitely separable for any cardinalities of $C, D$. Hence by Corollary 5.23, every $\mathbf{P}$ from Example 5.24 (regardless of cardinalities) embeds in some $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{Y})$. Example 5.26: Let $X$ be uncountable, $\mathbf{R}$ be the bipartite ordered set isomorphic to all atoms and coatoms of $\mathbf{2}^X$. By [61], $\mathbf{R}$ cannot be embedded into a finitely separable ordered set, hence not into a free lattice.

Theorem 5.27 · Nation-Schmerl — order dimension of $\mathbf{F}_\mathcal{V}(X)$

If $\mathcal{V}$ is a nontrivial lattice variety and $X$ is an infinite set, then the order dimension of the relatively free lattice $\mathbf{F}_\mathcal{V}(X)$ is the smallest cardinal $\lambda$ such that $|X| \le 2^\lambda$.

Problem 5.28 · open problem on embeddability of ordered sets

Characterize those ordered sets which can be embedded into a free lattice.

Corollary 5.23, while a significant reduction of the problem, hardly counts as a characterization. We only know two general necessary conditions: (1) $\mathbf{P}$ is a countable union of antichains; (2) if $\mathbf{P}$ is infinite, $\dim \mathbf{P} \le \lambda$ where $\lambda$ is the smallest cardinal with $|\mathbf{P}| \le 2^\lambda$. Some further restrictions are in Nation [105].

Lower bounded homomorphic images of $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ (book pp. 107–108)

Theorem 5.31 · lower-bounded image of $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ characterization

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a lower bounded homomorphic image of some $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ if and only if $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property and $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$.

Theorem 5.32 · retract of $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ characterization

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a retract of $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ for some ordered set $\mathbf{P}$ if and only if it satisfies (1) Whitman's condition (W); (2) $L = \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$; (3) $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property and its dual.

Theorem 5.33 · lower-bounded image of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ characterization

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a lower bounded image of a free lattice if and only if it satisfies: (1) $L = \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$; (2) $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property; (3) there exists a generating set $Y$ for $\mathbf{L}$ such that $\{y \in Y : y \ge a\}$ is finite for every $a \in L$.

Corollary 5.34 · $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ as lower-bounded image

$\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ is a lower bounded image of a free lattice if and only if $\{q \in \mathbf{P} : q \ge p\}$ is finite for every $p \in \mathbf{P}$.

§ 3 Finite Sublattices of Free Lattices · Nation's Theorem

"The proof is fairly long and complicated, and we have not found any significant improvements." — Freese-Ježek-Nation, opening of §3. This section is one of the most technical proofs in lattice theory. We companion the structure carefully, focusing on conceptual content and key innovations.

The reduction to S-lattices (book pp. 108–109)

Definition · S-lattice

An S-lattice is a finite semidistributive lattice satisfying Whitman's condition (W). The aim of §3 is to prove that every S-lattice is projective, hence a sublattice of a free lattice.

Lemma 5.35 · 4-fold equivalence for finite lattices

The following are equivalent for a finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$:

  1. $\mathbf{L}$ is projective.
  2. $\mathbf{L}$ is a sublattice of a free lattice.
  3. $\mathbf{L}$ is bounded and satisfies (W).
  4. $\mathbf{L}$ is an S-lattice containing no C-cycle.
Intuition · the strategy via Lemma 5.35

By Corollaries 5.9 and 5.10, conditions (1), (2), (3) are equivalent for finitely-generated lattices. (3)⟹(W) and (3)⟹SD by Theorem 2.20 and its dual. The HARD direction is (4)⟹(3) — i.e., showing an S-lattice without C-cycle is bounded. By Lemma 2.62 of Chapter II, a finite SD lattice is bounded iff it contains no C-cycle. So if we can prove that NO S-lattice contains a C-cycle, condition (4) collapses into "$\mathbf{L}$ is an S-lattice", and we can drop the no-C-cycle condition. This is the strategy: prove that an S-lattice with a C-cycle leads to a contradiction. The contradiction takes 18 pages of technical work.

Setup: the relations $A, B, C$ subdivided (book p. 110)

Recall from Chapter II §5 that the C-relation on completely-join-irreducibles in a finite SD lattice is the union of two relations $A$ and $B$:

  • $a A b$ if $b < a$ and $a \le b \vee \kappa(b)$ (equivalently $b < a \le \kappa(b)^*$).
  • $a B b$ if $a \neq b$, $a \le a_* \vee b$, and $a \not\le a_* \vee b_*$ (equivalently $b \not\le \kappa(a)$ and $b_* \le \kappa(a)$).

For the proof of §3, the relations are FURTHER subdivided into $A_1, A_2$ and $B_1, B_2$:

Definition · A1, A2, B1, B2 (the proof's technical refinement)

Define:

  • $a A_1 b$ if $a A b$ and $a \wedge \kappa(b) > b_*$.
  • $a A_2 b$ if $a A b$ and $a \wedge \kappa(b) = b_*$.
  • $a B_1 b$ if $a B b$ and $b_* \not\le a$.
  • $a B_2 b$ if $a B b$ and $b_* < a$.

Figure 5.4 of the monograph diagrams these four cases pictorially.

Free-star principle and easy lemmas (book pp. 110–112)

Definition · Free star principle

The "free star" principle is a quote-and-check argument used many times in the proof: if $p, q$ are two distinct members of a minimal C-cycle and $q$ is not the successor of $p$ in the cycle, then $p B q$ does not hold; therefore $q \not\le \kappa(p)$ implies $q_* \not\le \kappa(p)$.

This structural fact arises because if $p B q$ held in a minimal C-cycle, $p$ and $q$ could be connected by a shorter cycle, contradicting minimality. The free star principle is invoked dozens of times in the proof.

Lemmas 5.36–5.37 · consecutive A's or B's in minimal C-cycle

Lemma 5.36: If $p_0 A_2 p_1 A p_2$ in a minimal C-cycle, then $p_2 = p_{1*}$ and $p_1 A p_2$.

Lemma 5.37: If $q_0 B q_1 B q_2$ in a minimal C-cycle, then $q_1 B_1 q_2$.

Forbidden configurations in (W)-lattices (book pp. 112–113)

Lemmas 5.38, 5.39, 5.40 · configurations forbidden by (W)

These three configuration-forbidding lemmas are the key technical tools.

Lemma 5.38: A finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ satisfying (W) cannot contain elements $a, a_0, a_1, b, b_0$ with seven specific conditions (1)–(7) (book p. 112) — including $b$ join reducible, $a \le a_1 \prec a_0$, $b \le b_0$, $b \not\le b_0$, etc. The proof iterates a Whitman-condition-derivation argument on $a_2 = a_{1*}, b_1 = b_0 \wedge (a_1 \vee b)$ and produces an infinite descending chain.

Lemma 5.39: A finite (W)-lattice cannot contain $a, b, c$ with five conditions: $b \wedge (c \vee (a \wedge b)) \not\le a$, $a \wedge (c \vee (a \wedge b)) \not\le b$, $a \wedge b \not\le c$, $a \not\le b \vee c$, $b \not\le a \vee c$.

Lemma 5.40: A finite (W)-lattice cannot contain $a, a_0, b, b_0$ with eight conditions. (Motivation: rules out infinite chains in $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ — Figure 5.3 on page 105.)

B-type sequences (book p. 113)

Definition · B-type sequence

A finite sequence $q_0, \ldots, q_k$ of join-irreducible elements of an S-lattice is a B-type sequence if the following four conditions are satisfied:

  • (B1) $q_{i-1*} \not\le q_i$ for $1 \le i \le k$.
  • (B2) $q_i \le \kappa(q_j)$ for $0 \le i < j \le k$.
  • (B3) $q_{i*} \le \kappa(q_{i-1})$ for $1 \le i \le k$.
  • (B4) $q_{j*} \not\le \kappa(q_i)$ for $0 \le i < i + 2 \le j \le k$.

Note that (B2) implies $q_i \neq q_j$ for $i \neq j$.

Lemma 5.41 · subsequence of consecutive B's is B-type

If $q_0, \ldots, q_k$ is a subsequence of a minimal C-cycle such that $q_i B q_{i+1}$ for $0 \le i < k$, then $q_0, \ldots, q_k$ is a B-type sequence.

Intuition · why B-type sequences are the right framework

B-type sequences GENERALIZE consecutive-B subsequences in two ways. First, they don't require $q_{i-1} B q_i$ — only the four properties (B1–B4) — so a B-type sequence can have non-consecutive B's separated by A's. Second, the conditions (B1–B4) admit a natural transitivity-like structure: combining sequences yields a sequence. This decouples B's from the cycle order. The crucial technical innovation that broke through the impasse in Jónsson's 1960s notes: the property "$p$ acts like a B-element in an extended cycle" can be captured WITHOUT actually placing $p$ adjacent to its B-partner. This is what allows the proof's case analysis to consolidate disparate cycle structures.

Eliminating the residual cycles (book pp. 123–126)

Lemma 5.52 · the exceptional case $r_0 A_2 r_1 B_1 r_2 A_2 r_3 B_1 r_4 A_2 r_5$

Let $r_0, \ldots, r_5$ be a subsequence of a minimal C-cycle with $r_0 A_2 r_1 B_1 r_2 A_2 r_3 B_1 r_4 A_2 r_5$. Then (1) $r_0 \le r_2 \vee r_4$; (2) $r_4 \not\le r_0 \vee r_2$.

Lemma 5.53 · alternating $B_1 A_2 B_1 A_2 \ldots$ cycle is impossible

An S-lattice cannot contain a minimal C-cycle of the form $q_1 B_1 p_1 A_2 q_2 B_1 p_2 A_2 \ldots q_n B_1 p_n A_2 q_1$.

Lemma 5.54 · S-lattice contains NO C-cycle

An S-lattice contains no C-cycle.

The proof of Lemma 5.54 combines all preceding lemmas: a minimal C-cycle, after careful analysis ruling out $A_1$'s, $A$'s adjacent to other $A$'s, $B_2$'s, and consecutive $B_1$'s except via $A_2$ separators, must take the form excluded by Lemma 5.53. Hence no minimal C-cycle exists, so no C-cycle exists. $\square$

Theorem 5.55 — Nation's theorem (book p. 126)

Theorem 5.55 · Nation 1982 — Jónsson's conjecture

A finite lattice is a sublattice of a free lattice if and only if it is semidistributive and satisfies (W).

Combining Lemmas 5.35 and 5.54: an S-lattice (= finite SD + (W)) contains no C-cycle, hence is bounded by Lemma 2.62, hence is projective by Corollary 5.10, hence is a sublattice of a free lattice. Conversely, sublattices of free lattices inherit (W) and SD trivially. $\square$

Connection P1 · Nation 1982 + the resolution of Jónsson's conjecture

Theorem 5.55 settled a conjecture from unpublished notes of Bjarni Jónsson from the 1960s. Jónsson's notes outlined a program for the proof but couldn't complete it; the program was published as Jónsson-Nation 1977 (Algebra universalis 7) with partial results. Rival-Sands 1976 proved the planar case [115]. Ježek-Slavík 1980 proved Jónsson's conjecture for SUBDIRECTLY IRREDUCIBLE S-lattices using different methods [76] (the same paper that classified the primitive lattices in §5). Nation 1982 ("Finite sublattices of a free lattice", Trans. AMS 269, 311–337) completed Jónsson's program in full generality, proving that EVERY S-lattice is a sublattice of a free lattice. The 1982 paper introduced the B-type sequence machinery (Lemma 5.41 here) as the key technical innovation. Computer-assisted origins. The motivation for many of the technical lemmas came from Nation's 1970s computer programs that attempted to construct S-lattices generated by C-cycles. Invariably, the programs would start producing infinite chains. The argument that these chains become infinite was formalized into the configuration-forbidding lemmas (5.38–5.40). This was, as the monograph notes, "one of the first computer assisted proofs in lattice theory". Companion implication. The FORBIDDEN-LATTICE forms of Theorem 5.55 (Theorem 5.58 in §4) translate Nation's algebraic-combinatorial characterization into a SCANNING test: a finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a sublattice of FL iff $\mathbf{L}$ contains no copies of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. Eight specific finite lattices are the "universal obstructions". Nation 1982 + Antonius-Rival together give a complete characterization in this combinatorial form.

Connection P3 · the B-type sequence machinery

The B-type sequence framework (Definition before Lemma 5.41) is the technical innovation that made Nation's 1982 proof possible. Why was it needed?

The problem with consecutive-B reasoning. Jónsson's 1960s approach reasoned about C-cycles by analyzing their A/B alternation pattern. Consecutive B's $q_i B q_{i+1} B q_{i+2}$ have a transitivity-like behavior: there are constraints on how they fit together. But the CRUCIAL configurations involve B's that are NOT consecutive in the cycle — separated by A's. The "consecutive B" reasoning couldn't directly apply.

The B-type sequence solution. A B-type sequence captures the structural CONTENT of consecutive B's WITHOUT requiring them to be cycle-adjacent. Specifically: properties (B1), (B2), (B3), (B4) abstract what's needed. Then Lemma 5.41: any subsequence of a minimal C-cycle satisfying $q_i B q_{i+1}$ is a B-type sequence. This is the bridge: cycle adjacency ⟹ B-type, but B-type doesn't require cycle adjacency.

How it works in the proof. Many of the lemmas 5.42–5.51 use B-type sequences as the WORKING STRUCTURE rather than cycle subsequences directly. The proofs can pass to non-adjacent subsequences, apply structural manipulations, and re-enter the cycle context through Lemma 5.41's bidirectional bridge. The framework decouples local manipulation from global cycle structure.

Generalization potential. Configurations which make a lattice satisfying (W) infinite are now much better understood thanks to the wonderful results of V. Slavík on (W)-lattices of finite lattices [123]. The B-type sequence framework was specific to this proof but informed later work on (W)-lattices and on the structure of finitely generated free lattices.

Theorem 5.56 — Davey-Poguntke-Rival (book pp. 126–127)

Theorem 5.56 · 6 forbidden sublattices for semidistributivity

A finite lattice is semidistributive if and only if it contains no sublattice isomorphic to one of the six lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4, \mathbf{L}_5$ or $\mathbf{L}_6$ of Figure 5.8.

Figure 5.8 shows eight lattices labeled $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. The first six characterize SD failure (this theorem); the last two ($\mathbf{L}_7, \mathbf{L}_8$) extend to (W) failure (Theorem 5.58). Specifically, $\mathbf{L}_1, \mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_3 = \mathbf{L}_4^d, \mathbf{L}_5 = \mathbf{L}_6^d$ are the failing lattices; $\mathbf{L}_4 = \mathbf{L}_3^d, \mathbf{L}_6 = \mathbf{L}_5^d$ are duals included for SD$_\wedge$.

The proof is by careful case analysis: assume SD$_\vee$ fails with witnesses $a, b, c$ such that $a \vee b = a \vee c > a \vee (b \wedge c)$. Two claims (Claim 1, Claim 2) about the structure of $b, c$ relative to $s = a \wedge b$ — leading to either a sublattice $\mathbf{L}_5$ or $\mathbf{L}_3$ (depending on whether $a \wedge b > 0$, $a \wedge c > 0$). Symmetric arguments give $\mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_1$ as alternatives. Hence a finite SD$_\vee$ failure embeds one of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_5$.

Theorem 5.57 · variety form of Theorem 5.56

A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is semidistributive if and only if the variety $\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{L})$ contains none of the lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4, \mathbf{L}_5$ or $\mathbf{L}_6$ of Figure 5.8.

Theorem 5.58 — 8 forbidden sublattices for sublattice-of-FL (book p. 128)

Theorem 5.58 · Antonius-Rival — 8 forbidden sublattices

A finite lattice is a sublattice of a free lattice if and only if it contains no sublattice isomorphic to one of the eight lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$ of Figure 5.8.

By Nation's theorem (5.55), finite-sublattice-of-FL ⟺ SD + (W). By Theorem 5.56, SD ⟺ no $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_6$ sublattices. The two additional lattices $\mathbf{L}_7, \mathbf{L}_8$ characterize (W) failure on top of SD: a finite SD lattice fails (W) iff it contains $\mathbf{L}_7$ or $\mathbf{L}_8$ as a sublattice.

Theorem 5.59 — finiteness of SD lattices without infinite chains (book p. 128)

Theorem 5.59 · meet-SD lattice without infinite chains is finite

A meet semidistributive lattice without infinite chains is finite; if it is of length $n$, then it has at most $2^n$ elements.

The proof inducts on length $n$. Take an atom $c$ of $\mathbf{L}$. By SD$_\wedge$, the maximal element $d$ with $c \not\le d$ — namely $d = \kappa_\mathbf{L}(c)$ — is unique. The lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is the disjoint union of $1/c$ and $d/0$. Both intervals have length $\le n - 1$, so by induction each has $\le 2^{n-1}$ elements, giving $\le 2 \cdot 2^{n-1} = 2^n$ total.

Example 5.60 · sharpness of the $2^n$ bound

For positive integer $n$, let $f_n$ be the largest cardinality of a length-$n$ lattice embeddable in a free lattice. By Theorem 5.59, $f_n \le 2^n$. The parallel sum construction gives the lower bound: $f_{n+2} \ge 2 f_n + 2$. With $f_1 = 2, f_2 = 4$, induction gives $f_n \ge \sqrt{2^n}$. Hence

$$\sqrt{2^n} \le f_n \le 2^n$$

for all $n$. The exact growth rate is open.

The parallel sum: given lattices $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}$, the parallel sum is the lattice $L \cup K \cup \{0, 1\}$ where $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}$ are disjoint intervals and there are no relations between elements of $L$ and elements of $K$ beyond the $0, 1$ endpoints. The parallel sum of two finite sublattices of a free lattice is again a finite sublattice of a free lattice.

Connection P4 · forbidden-sublattice characterizations

Theorem 5.58 is one of the most useful forms of Nation's theorem: a finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a sublattice of FL iff $\mathbf{L}$ contains no copies of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. The 8 lattices are EXPLICIT, DRAWABLE, and RECOGNISABLE — converting Nation's algebraic characterization (SD + (W)) into a purely combinatorial test (no forbidden subdiagrams).

The 8 lattices. $\mathbf{L}_1$ — a 6-element pentagon-like lattice. $\mathbf{L}_2$ — a 6-element diamond-like lattice. $\mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4 = \mathbf{L}_3^d$ — 7-element configurations (dual pair). $\mathbf{L}_5, \mathbf{L}_6 = \mathbf{L}_5^d$ — additional 7-element configurations (dual pair). $\mathbf{L}_7$ — a (W)-failure witness with 8 elements. $\mathbf{L}_8$ — a related 8-element (W)-failure witness. Figure 5.8 of the monograph diagrams all eight.

How to use the test. Given a finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ (specified by its diagram or operation tables), enumerate all sub-orderings of $\mathbf{L}$ and check whether any is isomorphic to one of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. If none is, $\mathbf{L}$ is a sublattice of FL. Polynomial-time in $|\mathbf{L}|$.

Comparison to Davey-Poguntke-Rival. The characterization for SD alone (Theorem 5.56) uses only 6 forbidden lattices. The additional 2 lattices ($\mathbf{L}_7, \mathbf{L}_8$) for (W) reflect that (W) is a STRICTLY stronger condition than SD. Equivalently: SD-and-(W) is the intersection of two combinatorial conditions; each contributes its forbidden witnesses.

Open question for arbitrary varieties. Theorem 5.57 says SD failure is detected by $\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{L})$ containing some $\mathbf{L}_i$. The analogous statement for sublattice-of-FL is open: does $\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{L})$ containing none of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$ characterize $\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{L})$'s lattices being sublattices of FL? The answer is unknown for non-finite cases.

Theorem 5.61 — primitive ⟺ projective + SI ⟺ Hom-class is a variety (book p. 129)

Definition · primitive lattice

A primitive lattice is a finite, subdirectly irreducible, projective lattice. By Corollary 5.11, equivalently: a finite, subdirectly irreducible, sublattice of a free lattice. By Nation's theorem (5.55), equivalently: a finite SI S-lattice (SD + (W)).

Theorem 5.61 · 3-fold equivalence for primitive lattices

The following three conditions are equivalent for a lattice $\mathbf{L}$:

  1. $\mathbf{L}$ is a finite, subdirectly irreducible sublattice of a free lattice.
  2. $\mathbf{L}$ is projective and subdirectly irreducible.
  3. The class of lattices containing no sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{L}$ is a variety.

Reinhold's *-distributivity (book pp. 132–133)

Definition · the $a * \langle b_1, \ldots, b_n \rangle$ operation, *-distributive, staircase distributive

For an element $a$ and an $n$-tuple $\langle b_1, \ldots, b_n\rangle$ ($n \ge 1$) of a lattice $\mathbf{L}$, define an element $a * \langle b_1, \ldots, b_n\rangle$ by induction on $n$:

$$a * \langle b_1\rangle = a \wedge b_1,$$ $$a * \langle b_1, \ldots, b_n\rangle = a \wedge (b_n \vee (a * \langle b_1, \ldots, b_{n-1}\rangle)) \quad \text{for } n \ge 2.$$

Expanded: $a * \langle b_1, \ldots, b_n\rangle = a \wedge (b_n \vee (a \wedge (b_{n-1} \vee \ldots (a \wedge (b_2 \vee (a \wedge b_1)))\ldots)))$.

$\mathbf{L}$ is *-distributive if for any $a \in L$ and any subset $B$ of $L$ such that $\bigvee B$ exists, $\bigvee(a * B)$ also exists and

$$\bigvee(a * B) = a \wedge \bigvee B$$

(where $a * B = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \{a * \mathbf{s} : \mathbf{s} \in B^n\}$). $\mathbf{L}$ is staircase distributive if the same holds for all FINITE $B$.

Theorem 5.66 · $\mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L}) = L$ + dual min-cover refinement ⟹ *-distributive

If a lattice $\mathbf{L}$ satisfies $\mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L}) = L$ and the dual of the minimal join cover refinement property, then $\mathbf{L}$ is *-distributive.

Corollary 5.67 · $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is *-distributive (and dually)

Every free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is both *-distributive and dually *-distributive.

A *-distributive lattice is upper continuous. Indeed, let $\{c_i : i \in I\}$ be a chain with $c = \bigvee c_i$, and $a$ an element. Then $a \wedge c$ is clearly an upper bound for $\{a \wedge c_i\}$, and if $d$ is any other upper bound, then $a * \{c_i : i \in I\}$ joins to $a \wedge c$.

Also, a staircase distributive lattice is meet semidistributive: if $a \wedge b_1 = a \wedge b_2 = c$, then $a \wedge (b_1 \vee b_2) = \bigvee(a * \{b_1, b_2\})$ where $a * \{b_1, b_2\}$ consists of the element $c$ only, so that $a \wedge (b_1 \vee b_2) = c$.

It follows from Corollary 5.67 that each sublattice of a free lattice is staircase and dually staircase distributive. However, the combination of this property with Whitman's condition (W) is NOT sufficient to characterize sublattices of free lattices.

Problem 5.68 · open characterization of countable-sublattice-of-free

Which lattices (and in particular which countable lattices) are sublattices of a free lattice?

A characterization is known for distributive sublattices of free lattices, due to Galvin and Jónsson [65]. A distributive lattice can be embedded into a free lattice if and only if it is isomorphic to the union of a countable chain of sublattices, each of which is either a one-element lattice, an eight-element Boolean algebra, or a direct product of a two-element chain and a countable chain.

Theorem 5.69 — implication chart (book p. 133)

Theorem 5.69 · 5 implications for arbitrary lattices

The following implications hold for an arbitrary lattice $\mathbf{L}$:

  1. If $\mathbf{L}$ satisfies (W) and is generated by a set of join prime elements, then $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$ and $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property.
  2. If $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$, then $\mathbf{L}$ satisfies SD$_\vee$.
  3. If $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property, then it is lower continuous.
  4. If $\mathbf{L}$ is finitely separable, then it can be order-embedded into a free lattice, and hence it inherits the countable chain condition, and has order dimension at most $\gamma$ whenever $\gamma$ is an infinite cardinal with $|\mathbf{L}| \le 2^\gamma$.
  5. If $\mathbf{L}$ has the minimal join cover refinement property and $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$, then it is dually *-distributive.

Prologos · projective lattices, Nation's theorem, forbidden patterns

Chapter V is the structural-classification chapter that produces a categorical answer to "what kind of lattice is this?" The Prologos analogy is rich.

(1) Projective lattices as retract-stable cells. A projective lattice $\mathbf{L}$ has the property that for any epimorphism $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$, there is a homomorphism transversal $g : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{K}$. In Prologos terms: a projective cell is one whose value can be "lifted" into any larger network without semantic loss. The standard epimorphism $\mathbf{FL}(X) \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ is a projection; the transversal — when it exists — is the "retract section" that recovers the original from the projection. Theorem 5.1's three-fold equivalence (projective ⟺ retract of free ⟺ universal retraction property) is the categorical content of "this cell can be embedded back-and-forth between any covering network without information loss".

(2) Theorem 5.7's four conditions as a CALM-safety checklist. The conditions (1) (W), (2) $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$, (3) min-cover refinement and dual, (4) finite separability map to four CALM-safety properties of a propagator cell. (1) "no doubly-reducible elements" — the cell value uniquely decomposes into joins-of-meets or meets-of-joins. (2) "well-founded D-rank" — every cell value has bounded recursion depth in the network. (3) "minimal cover refinement" — any join cover has a refined minimal version (constructive minimal-cover witnesses). (4) "finite separability" — comparability of cell values has a finite combinatorial certificate. Together, these are the conditions for a cell to support a stable retract section, i.e., to be projective.

(3) Nation's theorem as a structural-completeness check. Theorem 5.55: a finite lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is a sublattice of FL iff $\mathbf{L}$ is SD + (W). In Prologos terms: this is a structural-completeness check on a finite-domain cell. Given a cell value (= a finite lattice element), checking whether it can be embedded into the network's "free generator" structure reduces to checking SD and (W) — both of which are local, syntactically-checkable conditions on canonical-form representatives. The polynomial-time check uses Theorem 5.58's forbidden-sublattice version.

(4) The 8 forbidden lattices $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$ as anti-pattern detectors. Theorem 5.58 says a finite lattice is a sublattice of FL iff it contains no copies of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. In Prologos's adversarial-VAG framework: these 8 lattices are universal anti-patterns. A propagator network can be checked for "free-lattice compatibility" by scanning for sub-configurations isomorphic to $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_8$. Any match flags an architectural violation — the network has structure that cannot occur in a free-lattice-of-cells substrate. The check is polynomial in network size.

(5) The B-type sequence machinery as a stratified analysis tool. Nation's proof uses B-type sequences (Lemma 5.41) to decouple local from global cycle structure. In Prologos terms: this is exactly stratified propagator analysis — a tool for reasoning about cycle properties without requiring all cycle elements to be "in scope" simultaneously. The four conditions (B1–B4) are local invariants on a sub-sequence; the global conclusion (no minimal C-cycle) follows from local-to-global compositionality. The Prologos parallel: stratified VAG analysis on a propagator network proves global properties from local sub-network conditions.

(6) Table 5.1 as the structural property cross-reference. The table's rows are 13 structural properties; the columns are 5 lattice classes. In Prologos terms: this is the structural-property matrix that benchmarks a candidate lattice's algebraic features. When designing a propagator-network architecture, the table gives a quick lookup: "which target class do I need this network to land in?" If the answer is "FL(X)", every property must hold; if "sublattice of FL", a smaller subset suffices. The matrix is the architectural checklist for choosing a lattice realization.

(7) Reinhold's *-distributivity as upper-continuity strengthening. Theorem 5.66 + Corollary 5.67: every free lattice is *-distributive. In Prologos: *-distributivity is a strengthening of upper continuity that allows "staircase" join distributions across an arbitrary subset $B$ — even infinite ones with existing joins. A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ such that for every epimorphism $f : \mathbf{M} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{N}$ and every homomorphism $h : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{N}$, there is $g : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{M}$ with $h = fg$. $\mathbf{L}$ is a retract of $\mathbf{K}$ if there exist homomorphisms $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ and $g : \mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{K}$ with $fg = \mathrm{id}_\mathbf{L}$. The map $g$ is a homomorphism transversal; $f$ is a retraction.
Retraction
A surjection $f : \mathbf{K} \twoheadrightarrow \mathbf{L}$ with a homomorphism transversal — a homomorphism right-inverse.
S-lattice
A finite semidistributive lattice satisfying Whitman's condition (W). The proof of Nation's theorem (5.55) shows: every S-lattice is bounded (= no C-cycle = projective = sublattice of free).

Like *-distributive but only for FINITE subsets $B$. A finite *-distributive lattice is staircase distributive. Sublattices of free lattices are staircase distributive (and dually).
*-distributive
A lattice $\mathbf{L}$ is *-distributive if $\bigvee(a * B) = a \wedge \bigvee B$ for any subset $B$ with existing $\bigvee B$, where $a * B = \bigcup_n \{a * \mathbf{s} : \mathbf{s} \in B^n\}$. Transversal
A function $g : L \to K$ for a surjection $f : K \to L$ such that $fg = \mathrm{id}_L$. (No homomorphism property required for a generic transversal.) An order-preserving transversal preserves order; a join-preserving transversal preserves joins; a homomorphism transversal preserves all lattice operations.

Example V.A$\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ — structure verification
Verify that $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ — the free lattice over the disjoint union of two 2-chains — is infinite, and identify its key structural features.

Setup. $\mathbf{2 + 2}$ has four elements arranged as two disjoint 2-chains: $\{a_0, a_1\}$ with $a_0 \le a_1$, and $\{b_0, b_1\}$ with $b_0 \le b_1$, with no relations between the $a$-chain and $b$-chain.

$\mathbf{FD}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ vs $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$. $\mathbf{FD}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ — the free distributive lattice — is a finite lattice (with $\le$ a few dozen elements; its diagram is in Figure 5.3). $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ is INFINITE — Sorkin's classical result.

Why infinite. The (W)-failure correction process via Day's doubling never terminates. Specifically, in $\mathbf{FD}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$, there exist (W)-failures — meets of joins not refinable per Whitman's algorithm. Doubling these creates new elements; the new elements participate in further (W)-failures; doubling those creates yet more elements. The inverse limit is the infinite $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$.

Concrete example of an infinite chain. The element $u = a_0 \wedge (b_0 \vee a_1)$ is below $a_0$. The element $v = b_0 \wedge (a_0 \vee b_1)$ is below $b_0$. Joining: $u \vee v$ is below $a_0 \vee b_0$ but not equal. Iterating constructs an infinite ascending chain of distinct elements.

Sublattice content. H.L. Rolf showed: $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{n_1 + n_2})$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{1 + 1 + 1}) = \mathbf{FL}(3)$ exactly when $n_1 n_2 \ge 5$. Hence $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ does NOT contain $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ as a sublattice ($n_1 n_2 = 4 < 5$). The structural threshold is exact.

Implication. Free lattices over ordered sets generalize but DEGRADE relative to free lattices over antichains. $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{2 + 2})$ is "smaller" than $\mathbf{FL}(\{a_0, a_1, b_0, b_1\}) = \mathbf{FL}(4)$ — the order on generators imposes nontrivial structural constraints. Yet Theorem 5.20 says $\mathbf{FL}(\mathbf{P})$ retains the structural-property checks of free lattices ($\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L$ etc.). Best of both worlds when $\mathbf{P}$ is well-behaved.

Identify and describe the six forbidden sublattices for semidistributivity (Theorem 5.56, Figure 5.8).

$\mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4 = \mathbf{L}_3^d$: a dual pair of 7-element configurations, witnessing more delicate SD failures.

$\mathbf{L}_5, \mathbf{L}_6 = \mathbf{L}_5^d$: another dual pair of 7-element configurations.

The proof of Theorem 5.56's necessity. If $\mathbf{L}$ contains $\mathbf{L}_1$ as a sublattice, then in $\mathbf{L}_1$ we have $a \vee b = a \vee c = 1$ but $a \vee (b \wedge c) < 1$. Hence SD$_\vee$ fails in $\mathbf{L}_1$, hence in $\mathbf{L}$. Symmetric arguments rule out the other lattices.

Sufficiency direction. Suppose $\mathbf{L}$ is finite and fails SD$_\vee$. Take $a, b, c$ a minimal SD$_\vee$ failure: $a \vee b = a \vee c > a \vee (b \wedge c)$. The Claim 1 / Claim 2 case analysis (book pp. 126–127) shows $a, b, c$ together with related elements generate a sublattice isomorphic to one of $\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_5$. SD$_\wedge$ failure dually gives one of $\mathbf{L}_1, \mathbf{L}_2, \mathbf{L}_3, \mathbf{L}_4, \mathbf{L}_6$.

Example V.CThe pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ — primitive or not?
Apply Theorem 5.61 to verify $\mathbf{N}_5$ (the pentagon, Example V.E of Chapter II) is primitive.

Setup. $\mathbf{N}_5$ has 5 elements: $\{0, c, a, b, 1\}$ with $0 < c < a < 1$ and $0 < b < 1$, $a \vee b = 1$, $a \wedge b = c \wedge b = 0$.

Check semidistributivity. SD$_\vee$: $a \vee b = a \vee 1 = 1$? No, $b \neq 1$. Test: is $\mathbf{N}_5$ join-semidistributive? Take $a, b, c$. $a \vee b = 1$, $a \vee c = a$, so $a \vee b \neq a \vee c$. Test fails to apply — SD$_\vee$ vacuously holds for triples where the antecedent fails. Check all triples: it's known that $\mathbf{N}_5$ FAILS SD$_\vee$? Wait — the standard result is that $\mathbf{N}_5$ is non-modular but DOES satisfy semidistributivity. Let me verify: $\mathbf{N}_5$ satisfies neither the modular law nor the distributive law, but it does satisfy both SD$_\vee$ and SD$_\wedge$. Modulo correctness, $\mathbf{N}_5$ is SD.

Actually checking carefully: $\mathbf{N}_5$ has the relation $b \vee c = b \vee a$ (both equal $1$), and $b \wedge c = 0 = b \wedge a$. SD$_\vee$ would require $b \vee (a \wedge c) = b \vee a$. But $a \wedge c = c$, so $b \vee c = 1 = b \vee a$. ✓. So that triple works for SD$_\vee$. Other triples by symmetry. Hence $\mathbf{N}_5$ satisfies SD$_\vee$. Dually it satisfies SD$_\wedge$. So $\mathbf{N}_5$ is semidistributive.

Check (W). $\mathbf{N}_5$ has two doubly irreducible elements: $0$ and $1$. The element $a$ is meet-reducible: $a = (a \vee b) \wedge a = 1 \wedge a$. Also join-reducible: $a = c \vee a$? No that's trivial. $a$ is join-irreducible because the only $\le$-chain ending at $a$ goes through $c$, and $a \neq c \vee 0$ in a useful way. Actually $a$ has unique lower cover $c$ — completely join irreducible. Symmetrically $b$ has lower cover $0$ (an atom), $c$ has lower cover $0$, all join-irreducible. (W) requires no doubly-reducible elements: an element that is BOTH a non-trivial join AND a non-trivial meet violates (W). In $\mathbf{N}_5$: $0$ is not a non-trivial join (it's the bottom). $1$ is not a non-trivial meet (it's the top). $a = ?\vee?$ from below — no element below $a$ except $c$ and $0$, so $a$ is join-irreducible. By Corollary 1.9, $a$ is either join or meet irreducible — let's check meet: $a = ?\wedge?$ — only $1 \wedge a = a$, trivial. So $a$ is meet-irreducible. Symmetric for $b$ and $c$. So no element of $\mathbf{N}_5$ is doubly-reducible — $\mathbf{N}_5$ satisfies (W).

Check subdirectly irreducible. $\mathbf{N}_5$ has the monolith congruence $\theta = \{(c, 0)\}$ — collapse $c$ and $0$. This is the unique smallest non-zero congruence. Any other congruence contains $\theta$. Hence $\mathbf{N}_5$ is SI.

Conclusion. $\mathbf{N}_5$ is finite, SD, satisfies (W), and is SI. By Lemma 5.35 (4)⟺(1) and definition of primitive, $\mathbf{N}_5$ is a primitive lattice. ✓

Which family? Looking at Figure 5.9: $\mathbf{N}_5$ is NOT one of the four "starter" primitives ($\mathbf{A}_1$ is the 2-chain, $\mathbf{A}_2, \mathbf{A}_3, \mathbf{A}_4$ have 5, 7, 9 elements with specific structures). Looking at $\mathbf{A}_2$ in Figure 5.9: it's described as a 5-element lattice. Could $\mathbf{A}_2 = \mathbf{N}_5$? Possibly — both are 5-element non-modular lattices. The figure would clarify. Likely, $\mathbf{A}_2$ is the pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ in the monograph's notation.

Example V.DVerifying Example 5.13's lattice fails (W)
Take the lattice $\mathbf{L}^\vee((x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z))$ from Figure 3.3(3), book p. 84. Verify it satisfies conditions (2)(3)(4) of Theorem 5.7 but FAILS (W), hence is not projective.

Setup. The lattice $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ where $w = (x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$. By Chapter III §6 (Example 3.38, table row 3), $\kappa(w)$ is undefined — i.e., $w$ has no lower cover in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. By Theorem 3.26, $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ is NOT meet semidistributive — it is a 5-element non-SD lattice.

Check finite separability. $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ is finite, hence finitely separable trivially.

Check $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}) = L = \mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L})$. $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ has 5 elements; its D-rank is finite. By inspection, $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)) = \mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$. By the dual reasoning, $\mathbf{D}^d(\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)) = \mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$.

Check minimal join cover refinement and dual. Trivially, since the lattice is finite.

Check (W). The lattice is NOT semidistributive (failure of SD$_\wedge$). By Corollary 1.9, every (W)-lattice is join- or meet-irreducible at every element. The non-SD failure typically presents as a "doubly-reducible element" — the element $w$ itself, in some embedding. Specifically, $w$ is a meet ($w = (x \vee (y \wedge z)) \wedge (y \vee z)$) AND a non-trivial join in $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ (since $w$ has more than one canonical joinand structurally). Hence $w$ is doubly-reducible, hence (W) fails.

Conclusion. $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ satisfies (2)(3)(4) of Theorem 5.7 but fails (1). Hence not projective. ✓ (The four conditions are independent.)

Example V.EParallel sum construction — $f_4 \ge \sqrt{2^4} = 4$ and tighter bounds
Use the parallel sum construction to derive a lower bound on $f_n$, the largest length-$n$ lattice embeddable in a free lattice.

Recursive bound from parallel sum. Given two finite sublattices of a free lattice $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}$ with $|\mathbf{L}| = a, |\mathbf{K}| = b$, both of length $n$. The parallel sum $\mathbf{L} \parallel \mathbf{K}$ has $|L| + |K| + 2 = a + b + 2$ elements (adding fresh top and bottom) and length $n + 2$. So $f_{n+2} \ge a + b + 2 \ge 2 f_n + 2$.

Base cases. $f_1 = 2$ (the 2-chain). $f_2 = 4$ (a 2x2 boolean / a 4-element diamond?). Check: a length-2 lattice has up to 4 elements (top, bottom, two intermediates). The 4-element boolean is a sublattice of $\mathbf{FL}(2)$ as $\{0_2, x \vee y, x \wedge y, 1_2\}$? Actually $\mathbf{FL}(2) = \{x, y, x \vee y, x \wedge y\}$ has 4 elements. So $f_2 \ge 4$. By Theorem 5.59, $f_2 \le 4$. Hence $f_2 = 4$.

Iteration. $f_3 \ge 2 \cdot 2 + 2 = 6$ (from $f_1$). $f_4 \ge 2 \cdot 4 + 2 = 10$ (from $f_2$). $f_5 \ge 2 \cdot 6 + 2 = 14$ (from $f_3$). $f_6 \ge 2 \cdot 10 + 2 = 22$ (from $f_4$). Etc.

Closed form. The recurrence $f_{n+2} \ge 2 f_n + 2$ with $f_1 = 2, f_2 = 4$ gives:

  • $f_n \ge 2^{(n-1)/2} \cdot ?$ — let's derive carefully. Solving $f_{n+2} = 2 f_n + 2$ with constants: characteristic equation $r^2 = 2$ gives $r = \sqrt{2}$. So $f_n \approx C \cdot \sqrt{2}^n$ for large $n$.
  • The exact bound via induction: $f_n \ge \sqrt{2^n}$, i.e., $f_n^2 \ge 2^n$.

Comparison to upper bound. By Theorem 5.59, $f_n \le 2^n$. So we have $\sqrt{2^n} \le f_n \le 2^n$, with both bounds growing exponentially but at different rates ($\sqrt{2}^n$ vs $2^n$). The gap is $\sqrt{2}^n$, which grows arbitrarily large.

Open question. The exact growth rate of $f_n$ is open. It's between $\sqrt{2}^n$ and $2^n$; finding the exact rate is a problem in lattice combinatorics.
