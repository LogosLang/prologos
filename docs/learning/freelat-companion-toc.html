This companion volume parallels the monograph chapter-by-chapter, providing prose reformulations of the dense theorem-proof structure, marginal historical context, computed examples that illustrate abstract results concretely, and connection panels showing where each result connects to broader themes — including, in the final panel of each chapter, the design of the Prologos functional-logic language whose architecture is grounded in lattice theory.

The monograph is research-level and dense. The companion is not a substitute for the source — read both side-by-side, with the companion offering plain-English prose reformulations and the source providing rigorous proofs.

For background lattice theory at the undergraduate level, see also our companion to Nation's textbook Notes on Lattice Theory: Nation Companion ToC. Chapter 6 of Nation's textbook is the entry point; this monograph develops the same material at research depth.

Authors

Ralph Freese (University of Hawai‘i): co-author of the monograph; collaborator with Nation on free lattices, finitely-presented lattices, and lattice algorithms since 1973. Author of the 1980 Trans. AMS paper proving the word problem for free modular lattices on 5+ generators is undecidable.

J. B. Nation (University of Hawai‘i): co-author. Settled Jónsson's 1961 conjecture in 1982 (Trans. AMS 269) — Nation's Theorem characterising finite sublattices of free lattices. Author of Notes on Lattice Theory (the textbook our other companion treats).

A brief history of free lattices, and a guide to the book

From Dedekind 1900 (free modular lattice on 3 generators) → Skolem 1920 (decidable word problem, forgotten) → Birkhoff 1935 (FL(3) infinite) → Whitman 1941–42 (canonical form, six conditions) → Dean, Jónsson, Galvin, Kiefer (1950s–60s) → Day 1970 (doubling) → McKenzie 1972 (bounded homomorphisms) → Nation 1982 (sublattice characterization) → Freese-Nation 1985 (covers).

Day's Theorem Revisited

A constructive sharpening of Day's 1979 weak-atomicity theorem with explicit D-rank control. Theorem 4.1: for $u \not\le v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, there exists a completely-join-irreducible $q$ with $q \le u$, $v \le \kappa(q)$, and $\rho(q) \le \rho^d(u) + \rho(v) - 1$. Theorem 4.3: the bound is sharp via the explicit $s_m, t_n$ sequences.

Three named results in 4 pages, with structural weight far beyond the size. Theorem 4.1: the refined Day's theorem — for any $u, v \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $u \not\le v$, a completely-join-irreducible $q$ exists with $q \le u$, $v \le \kappa(q)$, and $\rho(q) \le m + n - 1$ where $m = \rho^d(u), n = \rho(v)$. The covering pair $\kappa(q) \wedge (q \vee v) \prec q \vee v$ lies in the interval $u/v$ — making weak atomicity explicitly witnessed by a canonical-form term constructible in polynomial time. Proof by 3-case induction on canonical-form complexity (generator, join, meet-of-joins-meets), with Case 3 using Day's doubling at the level of $\mathbf{L}^\vee$. Corollary 4.2: the bounded-lattice form. Every nontrivial $u \not\le v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is witnessed by an epimorphism onto a finite bounded splitting lattice $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{L}^\vee(q)$ with $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{L}) \subseteq D_{m+n-1}(\mathbf{L})$. Recovers Theorem 2.84 with explicit complexity. Theorem 4.3: sharpness. Two recursively-defined sequences $s_m, t_n$ with $\rho^d(s_m) = m$, $\rho(t_n) = n$, where $s_1$ is Dean's coverless element $x \wedge (y \vee z)$. For these, every completely-join-irreducible $q$ with $q \le s_m, t_n \le \kappa(q)$ satisfies $\rho(q) \ge m + n - 1$ exactly. Proof inducts on $m + n$ with three cases (parity of $m, n$). Establishes that Theorem 4.1's bound is best possible and is achieved by an explicit family of canonical-form terms.

Totally Atomic Elements

The first chapter of Part II — fine structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Totally atomic = both lower atomic AND upper atomic. The chapter proves $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains only finitely many totally atomic elements (Corollary 6.11) — explicit count via Stirling numbers: 17 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, 94 in $\mathbf{FL}(4)$, 667 in $\mathbf{FL}(5)$, 125,499,820 in $\mathbf{FL}(10)$. Plus the canonical-form formula for $\kappa(w)$ when $w$ is totally atomic, and the structural role of totally atomic elements as universal cover-witnesses.

Three sections, 33 numbered results. (§1) Characterization. Theorem 6.1: 4-fold equivalence — $w$ is totally atomic iff every subelement is totally atomic iff $\mathbf{L}^\vee(w)$ AND $\mathbf{L}^\wedge(w)$ are both SD iff $w$ is $0$, $1$, or has the strict canonical-form alternation pattern. The σ/μ endomorphisms: $\sigma_x(w) = x \vee w$, $\mu_x(w) = x \wedge w$. The set $G$: smallest containing $X$ closed under $\sigma_x, \mu_x$ for fresh $x$. Singular and Semisingular Elements

Specialised classes of completely-join-irreducible elements. Singular: $\kappa(w) = w_*$ (κ-image is the lower cover); the cover $w_* \prec w$ is then a "singular cover". Semisingular: $\kappa(w) \le w_i$ for some canonical meetand. Theorem 8.1: $w$ semisingular ⟺ $w$ singular OR $w$ is middle of a 3-element interval. Theorem 8.6 (Freese-Ježek-Nation-Slavík 1986): singular elements live ONLY at the very top and bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — retroactively justifies Theorem 7.2's footnote.

Two sections + bridge to Chapter IX, 7 named results. (§1) Semisingular Elements. Notational conventions: $w_{i1}$ for the unique canonical joinand of $w_i$ not below $w_*$. Theorem 8.1: 2-fold characterization (singular OR 3-element-interval-middle). Lemma 8.2 (structural inequality on κ): $w_{i1*} \le \kappa(w) \prec \kappa(w)^* \le \kappa(w_{i1})$. Lemma 8.3: $v \vee w_{11} = w_1$, $\{w_{1j} : j > 1\} \ll \{v_1, v_2, \ldots\}$. The 4-case proof: case i (works), cases ii and iii (don't occur — forbidden by Theorem 6.31), case iv (reduces to case i via inductive substitution). Corollary 8.4: structural location of semisingular ($w$ middle OR $w$ top of 3-element interval). Corollary 8.5 (the "useful test"): for completely-join-irreducible $w$ with $\kappa(w) \neq w_*$, if $u \wedge \kappa(w) = w_*$, then $u \in \{w_*, w\}$ or $u \succ w$; moreover, if $u \succ w$ then $w$ is semisingular. (§2) Singular Elements. Theorem 8.6: every singular element has $\kappa(w) = w_*$ being either the meet of two coatoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 1) or the join of two atoms (so $w$ in the connected component of 0). Proof uses Theorem 7.2's chain-bound + Theorem 6.31 + Lemma 7.1's "wide span" condition. (§3) Bridge. Corollary 8.7: no interval of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is isomorphic to an infinite chain. Proof uses Day's theorem + Theorem 7.2 + Theorem 8.6 + Corollary 8.5. The first foreshadowing of Chapter IX's Tschantz theorem — every infinite interval contains a copy of $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$.

Tschantz's Theorem and Maximal Chains

The structural payoff of Part II. Theorem 9.10 (Tschantz 1990): every infinite interval $u/v$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathbf{FL}(\omega)$. Generalises Whitman's $\mathbf{FL}(\omega) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{FL}(3)$ from a specific embedding to ALL infinite intervals. Theorem 9.12: every infinite interval contains a coverless element. Theorem 9.15: if $c/d$ is neither atomic nor dually atomic, there's a maximal chain in $c/d$ with NO covers — surprising in light of Day's Theorem.
