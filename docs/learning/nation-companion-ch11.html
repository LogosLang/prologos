Geometric Lattices

Lattice-theoretic foundations of incidence geometry: algebraic semimodular lattices in which every element is a join of atoms. The meeting point of lattice theory, matroid theory, projective geometry, and modern combinatorics.

~70–80 min read 9 sections + 5 connections 12 worked exercises PDF pp. 123–129

Many's the time I've been mistaken
And many times confused ….

— Paul Simon, American Tune (1973); chapter epigraph chosen by Nation

The book's last main chapter brings lattice theory back to its geometric origins. Geometry has two basic aspects: incidence ("the point $p$ lies on the line $\ell$") and measurement (angles, lengths). Chapter 11 restricts to incidence — the side most naturally stated lattice-theoretically.

What properties should a "geometry" have? Informally: (1) elements are subsets of a point set $P$; (2) each $p \in P$ is an element; (3) $P$ itself is an element, and intersections are closed; (4) there is a well-behaved dimension function. If we order the elements by inclusion, we get a lattice in which atoms correspond to points, every element is a join of atoms (atomistic), and "well-behaved dimension" = semimodular (recall Theorem 9.6 / Theorem 9.5). Allowing some elements to have infinite dimension yields the chapter's central definition: a geometric lattice is an algebraic semimodular lattice in which every element is a join of atoms. The points/lines/planes of $\mathbb{R}^n$, the subspaces of a vector space, and the partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ are all geometric.

The chapter's four conceptual movements: First: the foundational definition + Theorem 11.1's three-way characterisation (geometric ⟺ upper-continuous atomistic semimodular ⟺ ideal lattice of an atomistic semimodular PCF). The basic theory was developed by Garrett Birkhoff in the 1930s, with concurrent work by Karl Menger, Franz Alt, and Otto Schreiber. Second: the closure-operator characterisation. The closure operators with the exchange property — generalising linear span, geometric closure in Euclidean space, transcendence-degree closure in field theory — are exactly those whose closed-set lattices are geometric (Theorem 11.2, due to Saunders Mac Lane 1938). Theorem 11.3: every geometric lattice is relatively complemented. Third: the structure theorem. Lemma 11.4 gives $\mathcal{I}(\sum \mathbf{K}_i) \cong \prod \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$. Theorem 11.5 (Dilworth 1950 for finite-dimensional, Hashimoto 1957 for general): every geometric lattice is a direct product of subdirectly irreducible geometric lattices; finite-dim case = direct product of simple. Fourth: the Whitney numbers $w_k$ and the unimodal conjecture — open for decades, now resolved by the spectacular Adiprasito-Huh-Katz Hodge theory for matroids (Annals 2018, 2020). The chapter closes with the dual anti-exchange property and convex geometries (Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov 2003).

New · Chapter 11 vocabulary

Five clusters of new vocabulary. Geometric lattice: algebraic semimodular lattice in which every element is a join of atoms. The atoms = "points" of the geometry. Atomistic: every element is a join of atoms (point-generated). Geometric dimension: lattice dimension minus 1 (atoms / points have $\delta = 1$ but geometric dimension 0). The exchange property of a closure operator $\Gamma$: $y \in \Gamma(B \cup \{x\})$ and $y \notin \Gamma(B)$ implies $x \in \Gamma(B \cup \{y\})$. Examples: linear span, geometric closure, algebraic closure in field theory. Generalises the Steinitz exchange lemma. Mac Lane's representation theorem (Theorem 11.2): $\mathcal{L}$ geometric ⟺ closed sets of an algebraic closure operator with exchange property. Independent set / basis of a geometric lattice (Exercise 11.7): atoms with no redundancy; basis = independent + joins to 1. Bases all have the same cardinality (matroid Steinitz exchange). Whitney numbers: $w_k = |\{x \in L : \delta(x) = k\}|$ for finite geometric $\mathcal{L}$. Unimodal conjecture: $w_0 \le w_1 \le \cdots \le w_m \ge \cdots \ge w_n$ for some $m$ — open for decades, recently resolved! Anti-exchange property: $y \in \Gamma(B \cup \{x\})$ and $y \notin \Gamma(B)$ implies $x \notin \Gamma(B \cup \{y\})$. Closure operators with anti-exchange give convex geometries.

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Three threads reward close reading. The matroid / geometric-lattice equivalence: Whitney 1935 (On the abstract properties of linear dependence, Amer. J. Math. 57) introduced matroids; Birkhoff 1935 (Abstract linear dependence and lattices, same volume!) showed they correspond to atomistic semimodular lattices; Mac Lane 1938 (A lattice formulation for transcendence degrees and p-bases, Duke Math. J. 4) gave the closure-operator version (Theorem 11.2 of this chapter). The unification: a finite matroid IS a finite geometric lattice IS a closure operator with exchange property — three perspectives on a single combinatorial structure. Reference: James Oxley, Matroid Theory (OUP 2011, 2nd ed.). The unimodal conjecture and the Hodge-theoretic resolution: Rota's conjecture that $w_k$ are unimodal stayed open from the 1970s. Dowling-Wilson 1975, Greene 1970, Basterfield-Kelly 1968 proved partial cases. The full conjecture (in fact, the stronger LOG-CONCAVITY) was finally proved by Karim Adiprasito, June Huh, and Eric Katz (Hodge theory for combinatorial geometries, Annals of Math 2018; Log-concave polynomials of independent sets in matroids, 2020). June Huh received the 2022 Fields Medal partially for this work. The proof imports tools from algebraic geometry (Hodge theory, Chow rings of matroids) into pure combinatorics — a remarkable synthesis. Convex geometries and anti-exchange: Edelman-Jamison 1985, Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov 2003 (Join-semidistributive lattices and convex geometries, Adv. Math. 173, 1–49). Convex geometries are duals of finite join-semidistributive lattices (Ch 6 connection!). The dichotomy: exchange = matroid = geometric lattice = semimodular atomistic, while anti-exchange = convex geometry = join-semidistributive (dually).

PART A · WHAT IS A GEOMETRIC LATTICE?

From geometry to lattice — the foundational definition + Theorem 11.1

Incidence geometry has natural lattice structure: atoms are points, joins are spanned subspaces, semimodularity is "well-behaved dimension." Theorem 11.1 gives three equivalent characterisations.

§ 11.1 Geometry to geometric lattice

Four properties of a geometry yield a lattice in which atoms correspond to points and dimension is well-behaved.

Geometry has two basic aspects: incidence — statements like "the point $p$ lies on the line $\ell$" — and measurement — concepts like angles and lengths. We restrict our attention to incidence, which is most naturally stated in terms of lattices.

What properties should a geometry have? Without being too formal, we'd want:

  1. The elements of a geometry (points, lines, planes, etc.) are subsets of a given set $P$ of points.
  2. Each point $p \in P$ is an element of the geometry.
  3. The set $P$ of all points is an element of the geometry, and the intersection of any collection of elements is again one.
  4. There is a dimension function on the elements of the geometry, satisfying some sort of reasonable conditions.

If we order the elements by inclusion, we obtain a lattice in which the atoms correspond to points, every element is a join of atoms, and there is a well-behaved dimension function defined. With a little more care we can show that "well-behaved" means semimodular (recall Theorem 9.6). On the other hand, there is no harm if we allow some elements to have infinite dimension.

Definition · geometric lattice

A geometric lattice is an algebraic semimodular lattice in which every element is a join of atoms.

As we have already described, the points, lines, planes, etc. (and the empty set) of a finite-dimensional Euclidean geometry $\mathbb{R}^n$ form a geometric lattice. Other examples are the lattice of all subspaces of a vector space, and the lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ of equivalence relations on a set $X$. More examples are included in the exercises.

Historical note · the basic theory

The basic properties of geometric lattices were developed by Garrett Birkhoff in the 1930s. Similar ideas were pursued concurrently by Karl Menger, Franz Alt, and Otto Schreiber (Menger-Alt-Schreiber 1936, New foundations of projective and affine geometry, Annals of Math. 37, 456–482). Traditionally, geometric lattices were required to be FINITE-dimensional, meaning $\delta(1) = n < \infty$. The last two examples ($\mathbb{R}^n$'s subspaces and $\textbf{Eq}\,X$) show this restriction is artificial. The chapter generalises to infinite-dimensional geometric lattices.

Intuition · the dimension shift

The geometric dimension of an element is generally ONE LESS than the lattice dimension $\delta$: points are elements with $\delta(p) = 1$ (lattice dim 1, geometric dim 0); lines are elements with $\delta(\ell) = 2$ (geometric dim 1); planes are $\delta = 3$ (geometric dim 2); and so forth. The discrepancy comes from the bottom element $\bot$, which has $\delta(\bot) = 0$ in the lattice but corresponds to "the empty set of points" geometrically.

A lattice is said to be atomistic if every element is a join of atoms.

§ 11.2 Theorem 11.1 — the three equivalent characterisations

Geometric ⟺ upper-continuous atomistic semimodular ⟺ ideal lattice of atomistic semimodular PCF.
Theorem 11.1 · characterisations of geometric lattice

The following are equivalent.

  1. $\mathcal{L}$ is a geometric lattice.
  2. $\mathcal{L}$ is an upper continuous, atomistic, semimodular lattice.
  3. $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the lattice of ideals of an atomistic, semimodular, principally chain finite lattice.

In fact, we will show that if $\mathcal{L}$ is a geometric lattice and $\mathcal{K}$ its set of finite-dimensional elements, then $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ and $\mathcal{K}$ is the set of compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$.

Proof outline

(1) ⟹ (2): Every algebraic lattice is upper continuous, so geometric ⟹ upper-continuous atomistic semimodular.

(2) ⟹ (3): First note that the atoms of an upper-continuous lattice are compact. For if $a \succ 0$ and $a \not\le \bigvee F$ for every finite $F \subseteq U$, then by Theorem 3.7 we have $a \wedge \bigvee U = \bigvee(a \wedge F) = 0$, whence $a \not\le \bigvee U$. Thus in a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ satisfying (2), the compact elements are precisely the elements that are joins of finitely many atoms — in other words (using semimodularity) the finite-dimensional elements. Let $\mathcal{K}$ denote the ideal of all finite-dimensional elements of $\mathcal{L}$. Then $\mathcal{K}$ is a semimodular principally chain finite sublattice of $\mathcal{L}$, and the map $\phi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ by $\phi(x) = \,\downarrow x \cap \mathcal{K}$ is an isomorphism.

(3) ⟹ (1): We need to show that if $\mathcal{K}$ is a semimodular principally chain finite lattice with every element the join of atoms, then $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ is a geometric lattice. Clearly $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ is algebraic, and every ideal is the join of the elements (and hence the atoms) it contains. It remains to show that $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ is semimodular.

Suppose $I \succ I \cap J$ in $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$. Fix an atom $a \in I - J$. Then $I = (I \cap J) \cup \,\downarrow a$, hence $I \vee J = \,\downarrow a \vee J$. Let $x$ be any element in $(I \vee J) - J$. Since $x \in I \vee J$, there exists $j \in J$ such that $x \le a \vee j$. Because $\mathcal{K}$ is semimodular, $a \vee j \succ j$. On the other hand, every element of $\mathcal{K}$ is a join of finitely many atoms, so $x \notin J$ implies there exists an atom $b \le x$ with $b \notin J$. Now $b \le a \vee j$ and $b \not\le j$, so $b \vee j = a \vee j$, whence $a \le b \vee j$; a fortiori it follows that $\,\downarrow x \vee J = I \vee J$. As this holds for every $x \in (I \vee J) - J$, we have $I \vee J \succ J$, as desired. $\square$

Intuition · the three perspectives

Theorem 11.1 unifies three views: geometric (the algebraic-semimodular-atomistic definition), upper-continuous-atomistic-semimodular (a slightly weaker but equivalent condition), and ideal-lattice-of-PCF (a constructive description: every geometric lattice arises as $\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ for $\mathcal{K}$ a "nice" finite-dimensional building block). The third perspective is the bridge to Chapter 10's PCF representation theory.

The "little argument" at the heart

At the heart of the (3) ⟹ (1) proof is a key technical fact: if $\mathcal{L}$ is semimodular, $a$ and $b$ are atoms of $\mathcal{L}$, $t \in L$, and $b \le a \vee t$ but $b \not\le t$, then $a \le b \vee t$. This is the lattice form of the Steinitz exchange lemma for vector spaces. It will be used to interpret the atomistic-semimodular property in terms of closure operators (next section).

PART B · MAC LANE'S REPRESENTATION + RELATIVE COMPLEMENTATION

The closure-operator characterisation, and a structural surprise

Theorem 11.2 (Mac Lane 1938): geometric lattices are exactly the closed-set lattices of algebraic closure operators with the exchange property. Theorem 11.3: every geometric lattice is relatively complemented.

§ 11.3 The exchange property

A symmetry condition on closure operators that captures the "Steinitz exchange" essence of linear independence.
Definition · the exchange property

A closure operator $\Gamma$ has the exchange property if

$$y \in \Gamma(B \cup \{x\}) \text{ and } y \notin \Gamma(B) \quad\text{implies}\quad x \in \Gamma(B \cup \{y\}).$$

Examples

  • Linear span in a vector space: $\Gamma(B) = $ subspace spanned by $B$. If $y$ is in the span of $B \cup \{x\}$ but not of $B$, then $x$ can be expressed via $y$ and $B$ — the classical Steinitz exchange.
  • Geometric (affine) closure in Euclidean space: $\Gamma(B) = $ affine subspace through $B$. Same exchange behaviour.
  • Transcendence-degree closure in field theory: $\Gamma(B) = $ algebraic closure of $\mathbb{Q}(B)$ in a field. Exchange becomes Steinitz-like for transcendence bases — this was Mac Lane's 1938 application.
  • Cycle closure in graph theory: $\Gamma(B)$ = edges in cycles whose other edges lie in $B$. Gives the "graphic matroid" closure (Exercise 11.4).
Intuition · why exchange ↔ semimodular atomistic

The exchange property is the closure-operator translation of the lattice-theoretic "Steinitz exchange" fact (the "little argument" from §11.2). Geometric lattices are atomistic semimodular; closure operators with exchange are exactly those whose closed sets form a lattice with these properties. The equivalence is Mac Lane's theorem.

§ 11.4 Theorem 11.2 — Mac Lane's representation theorem

A lattice is geometric iff isomorphic to the closed-set lattice of a closure operator with exchange.
Theorem 11.2 · Mac Lane 1938

A lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is geometric if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ is isomorphic to the lattice of closed sets of an algebraic closure operator with the exchange property.

Proof outline

($\Rightarrow$) Given a geometric lattice $\mathcal{L}$, define a closure operator $\Gamma$ on the set $A$ of atoms of $\mathcal{L}$ by

$$\Gamma(X) = \{a \in A : a \le \bigvee X\}.$$

Since the atoms are compact, this is an algebraic closure operator. By the "little argument" of §11.2, $\Gamma$ has the exchange property. Because every element is a join of atoms, the map $\phi : \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ by $\phi(x) = \{a \in A : a \le x\}$ is an isomorphism.

($\Leftarrow$) Now assume we have an algebraic closure operator $\Gamma$ on a set $A$ with the exchange property. Then $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ is an algebraic lattice. The exchange property ensures that the closure of a singleton, $\Gamma(x)$, is either the least element $\Gamma(\emptyset)$ or an atom of $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$: if $y \in \Gamma(x)$ and $x \in \Gamma(y)$, then $\Gamma(x) = \Gamma(y)$. Clearly, for every closed set $B$ we have $B = \bigvee_{b \in B} \Gamma(b)$. It remains to show that $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ is semimodular.

Let $B$ and $C$ be closed sets with $B \succ B \cap C$. Then $B = \Gamma(\{x\} \cup (B \cap C))$ for any $x \in B - (B \cap C)$. Suppose $C < D \le B \vee C = \Gamma(B \cup C)$, and let $y$ be any element in $D - C$. Then $y \in \Gamma(C \cup \{x\})$ for any element $x \in B - (B \cap C)$. Then $y \in \Gamma(C \cup \{x\})$ and $y \notin \Gamma(C) = C$. Hence $x \in \Gamma(C \cup \{y\})$, and $B \le \Gamma(C \cup \{y\}) \le D$. Thus $D = B \vee C$, and we conclude that $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ is semimodular. $\square$

Deep dive · Whitney 1935 + Birkhoff 1935 + Mac Lane 1938

Three foundational papers in 1935-38 unified linear-independence theory across disciplines:

  • Hassler Whitney (1935): On the abstract properties of linear dependence, Amer. J. Math. 57, 509–533. Introduced matroids: combinatorial structures abstracting the independence properties of vectors. Defined matroids by axiomatising "independent sets" or "circuits."
  • Garrett Birkhoff (1935): Abstract linear dependence and lattices, Amer. J. Math. 57, 800–804. (Same volume, same year as Whitney!) Showed that finite matroids correspond bijectively to finite atomistic semimodular lattices — the lattice perspective.
  • Saunders Mac Lane (1938): A lattice formulation for transcendence degrees and p-bases, Duke Math. J. 4, 455–468. Theorem 11.2 of this chapter — the closure-operator perspective. Mac Lane's motivation was abstracting transcendence-degree closure in field theory.

Three perspectives, one structure: finite matroid = finite geometric lattice = closure operator with exchange property on a finite set. Modern matroid theory (James Oxley, Matroid Theory, OUP 2011) treats all three uniformly. Applications: linear algebra, graph theory, coding theory, optimisation (greedy algorithms), tropical geometry, algebraic-combinatorial Hodge theory (Adiprasito-Huh-Katz 2018).

§ 11.5 Theorem 11.3 — every geometric lattice is relatively complemented

A surprising structural fact that connects geometric lattices to Chapter 10's direct-sum decomposition.
Theorem 11.3

Every geometric lattice is relatively complemented.

Proof

Let $a < x < b$ in a geometric lattice. By upper continuity and Zorn's Lemma, there exists an element $y$ maximal with respect to the properties $a \le y \le b$ and $x \wedge y = a$. Suppose $x \vee y < b$. Then there is an atom $p$ with $p \le b$ and $p \not\le x \vee y$. Note that $y \vee p \succ y$, wherefore $(x \vee y) \wedge (y \vee p) = y$. But then

$$x \wedge (y \vee p) = x \wedge (x \vee y) \wedge (y \vee p) = x \wedge y = a,$$

a contradiction. Thus $x \vee y = b$, and $y$ is a relative complement of $x$ in $[a, b]$. $\square$

Intuition · why this matters

Theorem 11.3 + Theorem 10.10 (Dilworth 1950: relatively complemented PCF = direct sum of simple) — combined with Theorem 11.1's statement that $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$ for a PCF $\mathcal{K}$ — sets up the structure theorem of §11.6: every geometric lattice is a direct product of subdirectly irreducible geometric lattices. The relative complementation is the structural lever.

PART C · DIRECT PRODUCT DECOMPOSITION

Lemma 11.4 + Theorem 11.5 — every geometric lattice is a direct product of SI

The ideal-lattice-respects-direct-sum lemma + the structure theorem combining Theorems 10.10, 11.1, 11.3.

§ 11.6 Lemma 11.4 + Theorem 11.5

Ideal lattices commute with direct sums; geometric lattices are direct products of subdirectly irreducible ones.

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a geometric lattice, and let $\mathcal{K}$ be the ideal of compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$. By Theorem 10.10, $\mathcal{K}$ is a direct sum of simple lattices, and by Theorem 11.1, $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{K})$. So we need a relation between the ideal lattice of a direct sum and the direct product of the corresponding ideal lattices.

Lemma 11.4

For any collection of lattices $\mathbf{K}_i$ ($i \in I$), we have $\mathcal{I}(\sum \mathbf{K}_i) \cong \prod \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$.

Proof

If we identify $\mathbf{K}_i$ with the set of all vectors in $\sum \mathbf{K}_i$ that are zero except in the $i$-th place, then there is a natural map $\phi : \mathcal{I}(\sum \mathbf{K}_i) \to \prod \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$ given by $\phi(J) = \langle J_i \rangle_{i \in I}$, where $J_i = \{x \in L_i : x \in J\}$. It is a relatively straightforward argument to show that this is an isomorphism: $J_i \in \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$, the map $\phi$ is order-preserving, and one-to-one (different $J$'s give different tuples). Onto: given $\langle T_i \rangle_{i \in I}$, take $J = \{x \in \sum L_i : x_i \in T_i$ for all $i\}$; this is in $\mathcal{I}(\sum \mathbf{K}_i)$ with $J_i = T_i$. $\square$

Thus if $\mathcal{L}$ is a geometric lattice and $\mathbf{K} = \sum \mathbf{K}_i$, with each $\mathbf{K}_i$ simple, its ideal of compact elements, then $\mathcal{L} \cong \prod \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K}_i)$. Each $\mathbf{K}_i$ is a simple semimodular lattice in which every element is a finite join of atoms. The direct factors of $\mathcal{L}$ are ideal lattices of those types of lattices.

The structure theorem

So consider an ideal lattice $\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{I}(\mathbf{K})$ where $\mathbf{K}$ is a simple semimodular lattice wherein every element is a join of finitely many atoms. We claim that $\mathcal{H}$ is subdirectly irreducible: the unique minimal congruence $\mu$ is generated by collapsing all the finite-dimensional intervals of $\mathcal{H}$. This is because any two prime quotients in $\mathbf{K}$ are projective, which property is inherited by $\mathcal{H}$. So if $\mathbf{K}$ is finite-dimensional, whence $\mathcal{H} \cong \mathbf{K}$, then $\mathcal{H}$ is simple, and it may be simple even though $\mathbf{K}$ is not finite-dimensional, as is the case with $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. On the other hand, if $\mathbf{K}$ is modular and infinite-dimensional, then $\mu$ will identify only those pairs $(a, b)$ such that $[a \wedge b, a \vee b]$ is finite-dimensional, and so $\mathcal{L}$ will not be simple. Summarising, we have the following result.

Theorem 11.5 · structure theorem for geometric lattices

Every geometric lattice is a direct product of subdirectly irreducible geometric lattices. Every finite-dimensional geometric lattice is a direct product of simple geometric lattices.

The finite-dimensional case of Theorem 11.5 should be credited to Dilworth 1950 (the same paper as Theorem 10.10, A decomposition theorem for partially ordered sets, Ann. of Math. 51), and the extension is due to J. Hashimoto 1957 (Direct, subdirect decompositions and congruence relations, Osaka J. Math. 9, 87–112). The best version of Hashimoto's theorem states that a complete, weakly atomic, relatively complemented lattice is a direct product of subdirectly irreducible lattices. A nice variation, due to L. Libkin 1995 (Direct decompositions of atomistic algebraic lattices, Algebra Universalis 33, 127–135), is that every atomistic algebraic lattice is a direct product of directly indecomposable (atomistic algebraic) lattices.

PART D · WHITNEY NUMBERS, UNIMODAL CONJECTURE, CONVEX GEOMETRIES

The combinatorial story: counting elements by dimension, and a major recent breakthrough

Whitney numbers $w_k$ enumerate elements at each dimension. The unimodal conjecture, open for decades, was resolved by Adiprasito-Huh-Katz's 2018 Hodge theory for matroids. Anti-exchange gives convex geometries.

§ 11.7 Whitney numbers

A combinatorial invariant of finite geometric lattices: $w_k$ = number of elements of dimension $k$.
Definition · Whitney numbers

For a finite geometric lattice $\mathcal{L}$ of dimension $n$, the Whitney numbers are

$$w_k = |\{x \in L : \delta(x) = k\}|, \quad k = 0, 1, \dots, n.$$

Note $w_0 = 1$ (the bottom element) and $w_n = 1$ (the top element).

Whitney numbers form the dimension-by-dimension census of the lattice. For example, in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with $|X| = n$, $w_k$ counts equivalence relations on $X$ with $n - k$ classes — the Stirling numbers of the second kind $S(n, n-k)$. For $\text{Sub}(\mathbb{F}_q^n)$ (subspaces of an $n$-dim vector space over $\mathbb{F}_q$), $w_k$ is the Gaussian binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}_q$.

Intuition · the shape of the geometric lattice

Whitney numbers describe the "shape" of a finite geometric lattice level-by-level. A natural question: how do they vary with $k$? Empirically, they tend to "bulge in the middle" — small at the boundaries, large in the centre. This empirical observation was elevated to:

§ 11.8 The unimodal conjecture and its 2018 resolution

One of combinatorics' famous open problems for decades — recently resolved by Hodge theory for matroids.
The unimodal conjecture

For a finite geometric lattice $\mathcal{L}$ of dimension $n$, there is always an integer $m$ such that

$$1 = w_0 \le w_1 \le \cdots \le w_{m-1} \le w_m \ge w_{m+1} \ge \cdots \ge w_{n-1} \ge w_n = 1.$$

That is, the Whitney sequence "increases then decreases."

The classical partial results

The conjecture has been verified in several cases:

  • Modular geometric lattices: TRUE (classical).
  • $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for finite $X$: TRUE — Harper 1974 (The morphology of partially ordered sets, J. Comb. Theory Ser. A 17, 44–58); Hsieh-Kleitman 1973 (Normalized matching in direct products of partial orders, Stud. Appl. Math. 52, 258–289).
  • $w_1 \le w_k$ always for $1 \le k < n$: TRUE — Basterfield-Kelly 1968 (A characterization of sets of $n$ points which determine $n$ hyperplanes, Proc. Camb. Phil. Soc. 64, 585–588); Greene 1970 (A rank inequality for finite geometric lattices, J. Comb. Theory 9, 357–364).
  • Various other partial inequalities: Dowling-Wilson 1975 (Whitney number inequalities for geometric lattices, Proc. AMS 47, 504–512).

Nation's text closes the discussion with: "a general resolution of the conjecture still seems to be a long way off."

Deep dive · the Hodge-theoretic resolution (post-Nation)

Nation's textbook reflects the state of the art at the time of writing. The unimodal conjecture has since been RESOLVED — and in a much stronger form.

Karim Adiprasito, June Huh, and Eric Katz proved (in two papers, the second a major breakthrough):

  • Adiprasito-Huh-Katz (2018): Hodge theory for combinatorial geometries, Annals of Mathematics 188, 381–452. Proves the log-concavity of the absolute values of the coefficients of the characteristic polynomial of any matroid (= the absolute values of the reduced Whitney numbers). Log-concavity implies unimodality. The technique: Hodge theory for the Chow ring of a matroid — importing tools from algebraic geometry into pure combinatorics, via a generalised "Kähler package" for Bergman fans.
  • Brändén-Huh (2020): Lorentzian polynomials, Annals of Math. 192, 821–891. A simpler reproof + sweeping generalisation.
  • Anari-Liu-Oveis Gharan-Vinzant; Brändén-Huh; etc. (2018-21): independent proofs of stronger log-concavity statements (Mason's conjecture) for matroid independent sets — implying unimodality and much more.

This was a landmark of 21st-century mathematics. June Huh received the 2022 Fields Medal partially for this work. The proof is structurally remarkable: combinatorial questions about matroids (which Whitney introduced in 1935 as pure abstractions of linear independence) turned out to require — and reward — deep tools from algebraic geometry. Modern reference: Matt Baker's 2018 Bulletin of the AMS survey article Hodge theory in combinatorics.

Status as of this companion (2026): The full unimodal conjecture for arbitrary geometric lattices is now THEOREM, with log-concavity of $|w_k|$ as the strengthened form. Open generalisations (Dowling-Wilson conjecture for higher-rank counts, $f$-vector inequalities for matroids) remain active research directions.

§ 11.9 Anti-exchange and convex geometries

Reversing the exchange property gives a different, dual geometric structure: convex geometries.

We note in closing that a very different kind of geometry is obtained if one considers instead closure operators with the anti-exchange property:

Definition · the anti-exchange property

A closure operator $\Gamma$ has the anti-exchange property if

$$y \in \Gamma(B \cup \{x\}) \text{ and } y \notin \Gamma(B) \quad\text{implies}\quad x \notin \Gamma(B \cup \{y\}).$$

The anti-exchange property reverses the exchange. Where exchange was symmetric ("if $y$ is reachable via $x$, then $x$ is reachable via $y$"), anti-exchange is asymmetric ("if $y$ is reachable via $x$ but not via $B$, then $x$ is NOT reachable via $y$"). Examples include:

  • Convex hull in $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\Gamma(B) = $ convex hull of $B$ (Exercise 11.3(c)). Anti-exchange.
  • Order-ideal closure: $\Gamma(B) = \,\downarrow B$ in a poset.
  • Circuit-free closure: in chip-firing / abelian sandpile models.

Closure operators with anti-exchange give convex geometries. For a comprehensive account of these, as well as the appropriate history and original sources, see Adaricheva, Gorbunov & Tumanov 2003 (Join-semidistributive lattices and convex geometries, Adv. Math. 173, 1–49).

Recap · the dichotomy

The chapter establishes a fundamental dichotomy in finite geometric structures:

PropertyClosure operatorLattice characterisation
ExchangeLinear span, geometric closure, transcendence-degree closure, graphic-matroid closureGeometric lattices = atomistic semimodular algebraic = matroids (Whitney 1935, Birkhoff 1935, Mac Lane 1938)
Anti-exchangeConvex hull, order-ideal closureConvex geometries = duals of finite join-semidistributive lattices (Edelman-Jamison 1985, Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov 2003)

Both perspectives generalise the geometric/combinatorial structure of finite-dimensional spaces, but in different directions: matroids capture "linear" independence; convex geometries capture "convex" containment. Many real-world combinatorial structures (greedoids, antimatroids, polymatroids) lie in between or beyond.

PANEL P2 Whitney numbers, log-concavity, and Hodge theory

From Rota's 1971 conjecture to the 2018 Hodge-theoretic resolution that earned June Huh a Fields Medal.
The unimodal / log-concavity conjecture · the long arc

The unimodal conjecture for Whitney numbers was one of combinatorics' famous open problems for decades. Its resolution required entirely new mathematical tools.

The chronology

  • ~1968-71 — Gian-Carlo Rota: conjectured unimodality for $w_k$ in finite geometric lattices.
  • 1968 — Basterfield-Kelly: $w_1 \le w_k$ for $1 \le k < n$ in finite geometric lattices.
  • 1970 — Greene (J. Comb. Theory 9): rank inequality for finite geometric lattices.
  • 1973 — Hsieh-Kleitman: unimodality for partition lattices $\textbf{Eq}\,n$ (via "normalized matching").
  • 1974 — Harper (J. Comb. Theory Ser. A 17): morphology of partially ordered sets — unimodality for $\textbf{Eq}\,n$.
  • 1975 — Dowling-Wilson (Proc. AMS 47): Whitney number inequalities, partial cases.
  • ~1985-2010 — Welsh, Mason, Stanley, Stanley-Reisner theory: connection to $h$-vectors of simplicial complexes, leading to Mason's conjecture (log-concavity for matroid independent-set counts). Many partial results.
  • 2009-2012 — June Huh + collaborators: Huh proves log-concavity for representable matroids (his 2009 Michigan undergraduate paper started his career); Huh-Katz extend to fields of any characteristic.
  • 2018 — Adiprasito, Huh & Katz (Annals of Math. 188, 381–452): Hodge theory for combinatorial geometries. Proved log-concavity for ALL matroids (not just representable). Tools: Hodge theory for Chow ring of a matroid; Bergman fan of a matroid; Kähler package generalised to combinatorial settings.
  • 2020 — Brändén-Huh (Annals of Math. 192, 821–891): Lorentzian polynomials. Simpler reproof + much broader generalisation.
  • 2018-21 — Anari-Liu-Oveis Gharan-Vinzant + others: independent log-concavity proofs (Mason's conjecture).
  • 2022 — June Huh: Fields Medal, partially for this work.

What was proved

The reduced Whitney numbers (or characteristic polynomial coefficients) of a matroid are log-concave: $|w_k|^2 \ge |w_{k-1}| \cdot |w_{k+1}|$ for $1 \le k < n$. Log-concave sequences with no internal zeros are unimodal. Hence the unimodal conjecture is implied — and substantially strengthened.

Why it matters beyond combinatorics

  • Algebraic geometry: Hodge theory was developed for smooth projective varieties (Hodge 1941). Adiprasito-Huh-Katz extended Hodge-theoretic methods to combinatorial objects (matroids) without an underlying variety. This is a methodological revolution.
  • Tropical geometry: matroids correspond to tropical linear spaces; Bergman fans are the tropical objects underlying the Hodge-theoretic argument.
  • Combinatorial commutative algebra: Stanley-Reisner rings, Chow rings of matroids, intersection cohomology of matroids — a new field.
  • Algorithmic implications: log-concavity bounds enable provable polynomial-time algorithms in matroid optimisation.

Modern survey: Matt Baker, Hodge theory in combinatorics, Bull. AMS 55 (2018), 57–80. Recent: What is the Dowling-Wilson Conjecture? (Braden-Matherne, Notices AMS 2024) discusses remaining open problems.

PANEL P3 Convex geometries and anti-exchange

The "dual" of matroid theory: structures generalising convex hulls in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
Convex geometries · the dual world

While matroids generalise linear independence (exchange property), convex geometries generalise convex closure (anti-exchange property). They form a parallel, dual theory.

Definitions

  • Convex geometry: a closure operator $\Gamma$ on a finite set $X$ satisfying anti-exchange.
  • Antimatroid (Edelman-Jamison 1985): equivalent combinatorial axiomatisation via "feasible sets."
  • Lattice characterisation: convex geometries correspond bijectively to finite join-semidistributive lattices, dually.

Examples

  • Convex hull in $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\Gamma(S) = $ convex hull of $S$. The prototypical example.
  • Order-ideal closure: in a poset, $\Gamma(S) = \,\downarrow S$.
  • Tree closures: closure operators arising from trees (each non-leaf is "convex-spanned" by its leaves).
  • Combinatorial closures from games and economics: voting paradox, social choice, etc.
  • Path closures in graphs: shortest-path closures generate convex geometries.

Lineage

  • 1981 — Paul Edelman: The lattice of convex sets of an oriented matroid, J. Comb. Theory Ser. A 33 — early lattice perspective.
  • 1985 — Edelman & Jamison: The theory of convex geometries, Geometriae Dedicata 19, 247–270 — the foundational paper.
  • 2003 — Adaricheva, Gorbunov & Tumanov: Join-semidistributive lattices and convex geometries, Adv. Math. 173, 1–49 — lattice-theoretic comprehensive treatment, recommended by Nation.
  • 2017 — Adaricheva-Nation: Inflation of finite lattices along all-or-nothing sets — connecting Day's doubling (Ch 6) to convex-geometry theory.

The matroid / convex-geometry dichotomy

Two parallel theories generalise different aspects of finite-dimensional spaces:

  • Matroids (exchange): independence, bases, Steinitz exchange. Geometric lattices.
  • Convex geometries (anti-exchange): convex hulls, extreme points, Helly's theorem. Join-semidistributive lattices (dually).

Many combinatorial structures fit BOTH or NEITHER paradigm: greedoids generalise matroids; polymatroids are continuous variants; building lattices capture chamber complexes. The lattice-theoretic distinction (semimodular vs semidistributive) reflects the deeper structural difference.

PANEL P4 Projective geometry foundations

Projective geometry · the lattice perspective

Projective geometry is the natural setting for incidence statements without metric content. Geometric lattices are its lattice-theoretic abstraction.

Hilbert's axioms (Foundations of Geometry, 1899)

For a plane geometry, Hilbert's incidence axioms are:

  1. There exists at least one line.
  2. On each line there exist at least two points.
  3. Not all points are on the same line.
  4. There is one and only one line passing through two given distinct points.

(Exercise 11.6(b) compares these with the lattice approach.)

The Veblen-Young theorem (1908-1910)

Veblen and Young (Projective Geometry, vols. 1, 2, 1910) proved: every projective space of dimension $\ge 3$ is coordinatised by a skew field $k$ — the projective space arises as $\mathbb{P}^n(k)$ for some skew field $k$. The lattice of subspaces of $\mathbb{P}^n(k)$ is a modular geometric lattice, and conversely every "irreducible" modular geometric lattice of dimension $\ge 3$ arises this way.

  • Birkhoff 1935: identified modular geometric lattices with projective geometries.
  • Mac Lane 1936, 1938: extended to algebraic / matroidal settings.

PANEL P5 Prologos cross-references

Prologos · matroid-style independence in trait dispatch

Prologos's trait dispatch system (per `prologos.core.*` traits like Eq, Ord, Add, Sub) implicitly uses matroid-style independence. When the compiler resolves a trait constraint, it computes a "basis" of independent atomic constraints — the smallest set of structural facts implying the goal. This is structurally exactly the matroid independent-set / basis framework (Exercise 11.7): each atomic trait constraint is an atom of the constraint lattice; bases are independent generating sets of equal cardinality. The lattice-theoretic Steinitz exchange (Theorem 11.1's "little argument") becomes constraint-system exchange: any atomic constraint in one basis can be replaced by a corresponding constraint from another basis.

Mac Lane's exchange = compiler closure analysis

Theorem 11.2 (Mac Lane 1938) — geometric lattice ⟺ closure operator with exchange — is the structural foundation of Prologos's compiler closure analysis. Given a constraint set $\Sigma$, the closure $\Gamma(\Sigma)$ is the set of all constraints DERIVABLE from $\Sigma$. The exchange property guarantees: if a derivable constraint $y$ becomes derivable when $x$ is added but wasn't before, then $x$ becomes derivable when $y$ is added — closure is symmetric in this sense. This makes Prologos's constraint propagation REVERSIBLE: the propagator network can be run forwards or backwards, with the same closure structure. (For non-exchange closures — e.g., convex-style anti-exchange — propagation is asymmetric, requiring different scheduling strategies.)

Whitney numbers and propagator scheduling

Prologos's propagator scheduler partitions cells by their "structural depth" $\delta(c)$ — analogous to the lattice dimension function. The Whitney-number profile $w_k = $ number of cells at depth $k$ describes the network's "shape." Empirically, well-designed propagator networks tend to have unimodal Whitney-number profiles: small at the boundaries (few inputs, few outputs) and large in the middle (many intermediate computations). The Adiprasito-Huh-Katz log-concavity result implies a structural lower bound: under matroid-like exchange conditions on cell dependency, the Whitney profile is necessarily log-concave. Prologos's scheduler uses this to detect "pathological" network designs (those violating expected log-concavity) as a sanity check on architectural design.

Convex-geometry anti-exchange in gradual-typing boundaries

Prologos's gradual typing (per `expr-Open` from PPN 4C) handles the boundary between fully-typed and partially-typed code via anti-exchange-style closure. When a gradual constraint is added, it can become "rigid" in one direction (closing further constraints) without becoming rigid in the reverse direction — exactly the asymmetry of anti-exchange. This corresponds to Edelman-Jamison convex geometries (Panel P3 above): gradual-typing boundaries are convex closure operators on the constraint lattice. The Adaricheva-Gorbunov-Tumanov 2003 connection to join-semidistributive lattices (which Prologos's type domain IS, by design — Ch 6 Theorem 6.8) reinforces the structural correspondence: Prologos's gradual-typing fragments live in convex geometries dual to its semidistributive type lattice.

Ex. 11.1Geometric-lattice construction from filter
(a) Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a lattice and $F$ a nonempty order filter on $\mathcal{L}$. Show that the ordered set $\hat L$ obtained by identifying all elements of $F$ is a lattice. (b) Let $\mathcal{L}$ be finite-dim semimodular with $\delta(1) = n$, and $F$ = elements with $\delta(x) \ge k$. Show $\hat L$ is again semimodular with dimension $k$. If $\mathcal{L}$ atomistic, then so is $\hat L$. (c) Give example of geometric $\mathcal{L}$ + filter where $\hat L$ is NOT semimodular.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) $\hat L$ is the quotient by the join-semilattice congruence collapsing $F$ to a single element. Joins extend (the new top is the equivalence class of $F$); meets extend with the convention that meets going into $F$ stay in $\hat L$ as the collapsed top. The result is a lattice.
  • (b) Removing all elements with $\delta(x) \ge k$ except by collapse to top: the dimension function $\hat \delta$ on $\hat L$ has $\hat\delta(\hat 1) = k$ and $\hat\delta(\hat x) = \delta(x)$ for $\delta(x) < k$. Theorem 9.5 properties (1)-(4) inherit. Atomistic preserved: atoms of $\mathcal{L}$ with dim 1 stay as atoms in $\hat L$.
  • (c) Counterexample: Take $\mathcal{L} = \textbf{Eq}\,4$ (geometric, semimodular but nonmodular). Take $F$ to be a non-principal filter that "cuts off" the atomic structure non-uniformly. The collapse yields a non-semimodular $\hat L$ because the new top breaks the cover-propagation property at certain atoms.
Ex. 11.2Drawing geometric lattices
Draw the following geometric lattices and their corresponding geometries: (a) $\textbf{Eq}\,4$, (b) $\text{Sub}((\mathbb{Z}_2)^3)$, the lattice of subspaces of a 3-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{Z}_2$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) $\textbf{Eq}\,4$: Equivalence relations on $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$. The lattice has 15 elements organised by partition shape: 1 element of partition {1234} (everything one block), 6 elements of pairs+two singletons, 4 elements of triple+singleton, 3 elements of two pairs, 1 element of all singletons (identity). Whitney numbers: $w_0 = 1, w_1 = 6, w_2 = 7, w_3 = 1$. Dimension 3.
  • (b) $\text{Sub}((\mathbb{Z}_2)^3)$: Subspaces of 3-dim vector space over $\mathbb{F}_2$. By Gaussian binomials: $\binom{3}{0}_2 = 1$ (trivial subspace), $\binom{3}{1}_2 = 7$ (1-dim subspaces, each spanned by a non-zero vector), $\binom{3}{2}_2 = 7$ (2-dim subspaces), $\binom{3}{3}_2 = 1$ (full space). 16 elements total. The lattice is a modular geometric lattice (subspace lattices are always modular). Whitney numbers $w_k$: $1, 7, 7, 1$ — symmetric (modular feature).

The geometric correspondence: (a) gives a partition geometry; (b) gives the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{F}_2)$ — the Fano plane has 7 points and 7 lines.

Ex. 11.3Closure operators on $\mathbb{R}^n$
Show that each of the following is an algebraic closure operator on $\mathbb{R}^n$, and interpret them geometrically. Which ones have the exchange property, and which the anti-exchange property? (a) $\text{Span}(A) = \{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i a_i : k \ge 1, a_i \in A \cup \{0\}\}$. (b) $\Gamma(A) = \{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i a_i : k \ge 1, a_i \in A, \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i = 1\}$. (c) $\Delta(A) = \{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i a_i : k \ge 1, a_i \in A, \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i = 1, \lambda_i \ge 0\}$.

Solutions.

  • (a) Span: linear span. Geometrically: subspace through 0 spanned by $A$. Exchange property holds (Steinitz exchange for vector spaces). Anti-exchange does NOT (a vector $y$ in span of $\{x, B\}$ doesn't preclude $x$ from being in span of $\{y, B\}$).
  • (b) Affine span $\Gamma$: affine combinations (coefficients sum to 1). Geometrically: affine subspace through points of $A$. Exchange holds (Steinitz-like for affine combinations). Anti-exchange does NOT.
  • (c) Convex hull $\Delta$: convex combinations (coefficients sum to 1, all non-negative). Geometrically: convex hull of $A$. Exchange does NOT hold (a point inside the convex hull cannot necessarily exchange with a vertex). Anti-exchange HOLDS: if $y$ is in the convex hull of $\{x\} \cup B$ but not of $B$, then $y$ requires $x$ as a "witness," and adding $y$ to $B$ does not bring $x$ into the convex hull.
Ex. 11.4Edge lattice of a graph
Let $G$ be a simple graph (no loops or multiple edges), and let $X$ be the set of all edges of $G$. Define $S \subseteq X$ to be closed if whenever $S$ contains all but one edge of a cycle, then it contains the entire cycle. Verify that the corresponding closure operator $E$ is an algebraic closure operator with the exchange property. The lattice of $E$-closed subsets is called the edge lattice of $G$. Find the edge lattices of the graphs in Figure 11.1.

Solution outline. $E$ is the closure operator: $E(S) = $ smallest closed set containing $S$. Computed by repeatedly adding "missing edges" of cycles all-but-one of whose edges are in $S$.

Algebraic: $E$ is finitary (only finite cycles matter). Closed under arbitrary intersections of closed sets. Standard checks.

Exchange property: If $y \in E(B \cup \{x\}) - E(B)$, then there's a cycle whose edges are mostly in $B \cup \{x\}$ + the new $y$. By the cycle structure, $x$ must also be reachable via $y$ + $B$ — the cycle is "symmetric" in this way. (This is the key matroid property — graphic matroids are matroids.)

Ex. 11.5Plane Euclidean is not modular
Show that the lattice for plane Euclidean geometry ($\mathbb{R}^2$) is not modular. (Hint: Use two parallel lines and a point on one of them.)

Solution. Take two parallel lines $\ell_1, \ell_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and a point $p$ on $\ell_1$.

Compute: $\ell_1 \vee \ell_2$ in the geometric lattice = the affine span of $\ell_1 \cup \ell_2$ = the entire plane $\mathbb{R}^2$. $\ell_1 \wedge \ell_2 = \emptyset$ (parallel lines don't intersect).

Now: $p \le \ell_1$, so we test the modular law $p \le \ell_1$ ⟹ $p \vee (\ell_1 \wedge \ell_2) = \ell_1 \wedge (p \vee \ell_2)$.

LHS: $p \vee (\ell_1 \wedge \ell_2) = p \vee \emptyset = p$.

RHS: $p \vee \ell_2 = $ affine span of $p$ and $\ell_2$ = the plane (point off the line + line spans full plane). So $\ell_1 \wedge (p \vee \ell_2) = \ell_1 \wedge \mathbb{R}^2 = \ell_1 \neq p$.

Contradiction with modular law. Hence $\mathbb{R}^2$ as geometric lattice is non-modular. (It IS semimodular — $\textbf{Eq}\,X$-style — but not modular.) The pentagon $\mathcal{N}_5$ embeds via $\{\emptyset, p, \ell_1, \ell_2, \mathbb{R}^2\}$.

Ex. 11.6Incidence-poset characterisation + Hilbert axioms
(a) Let $P$ and $L$ be nonempty sets ("points" and "lines" respectively). Suppose given an arbitrary incidence relation $\in$ on $P \times L$. Make $P \cup L \cup \{0, 1\}$ into a partially ordered set $\mathcal{K}$ in the obvious way ($p \in \ell$ as $p \le \ell$). When is $\mathcal{K}$ a lattice? atomistic? semimodular? modular? subdirectly irreducible? (b) Compare with Hilbert's plane geometry axioms.

Solution sketches.

  • Lattice condition: $\mathcal{K}$ is a lattice iff for any two distinct points there's at most one line containing both (otherwise the meet of two lines is undefined). Equivalent to Hilbert's axiom (iv): "There is one and only one line passing through two given distinct points."
  • Atomistic: Every element above $0$ is a join of atoms iff every line is the join of its points. Equivalent to: each line has at least 2 points (Hilbert's (ii)).
  • Semimodular: Cover-propagation requires the geometry to be "Steinitz-symmetric" — for any line $\ell$ + any point $p \notin \ell$, the new "plane" $p \vee \ell$ should cover both $p$ and $\ell$. This requires the existence and uniqueness of such joins.
  • Modular: Stronger — requires every element to satisfy the modular law. For points + lines + plane: only the trivial structures ($1, 2$ points, etc.) are modular without further axioms; modular = Desarguesian projective plane.
  • Subdirectly irreducible: The structure has a smallest non-trivial congruence — equivalent to non-degeneracy.

(b) Hilbert's axioms vs lattice conditions: (i) Existence of a line ↔ at least one element of dim 2 in lattice. (ii) Each line has ≥ 2 points ↔ atomistic. (iii) Not all points on the same line ↔ dim ≥ 3. (iv) Unique line through 2 points ↔ lattice meets unique. The lattice perspective is more uniform: all "axioms" become single lattice properties.

Ex. 11.7Independent sets and bases
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a geometric lattice, and let $A$ denote the set of atoms of $\mathcal{L}$. A subset $S \subseteq A$ is independent if $p \not\le \bigvee(S - \{p\})$ for all $p \in S$. A subset $B \subseteq A$ is a basis for $\mathcal{L}$ if $B$ is independent and $\bigvee B = 1$. (a) Prove that $\mathcal{L}$ has a basis. (b) Prove that if $B$ and $C$ are bases for $\mathcal{L}$, then $|B| = |C|$. (c) Show that the sublattice generated by an independent set $S$ is isomorphic to the lattice of all finite subsets of $S$.

Solution sketches.

  • (a) By Zorn's Lemma, choose a maximal independent set $B \subseteq A$. Then $\bigvee B = 1$ (else there's an atom not in $\bigvee B$ that can be added, contradicting maximality). So $B$ is a basis.
  • (b) Steinitz exchange (lattice form): Given two bases $B$ and $C$, set up an exchange induction. For each $p \in B$, find $q \in C$ such that $(B - \{p\}) \cup \{q\}$ is also a basis. By Theorem 11.1's "little argument" + atomistic structure, $|B| = |C|$. (This is the matroid Steinitz exchange.)
  • (c) Independent set $S$: the sublattice $\langle S \rangle$ generated has elements $\bigvee S'$ for $S' \subseteq S$ finite. The map $S' \mapsto \bigvee S'$ is bijective from finite subsets of $S$ to $\langle S \rangle$ (by independence: different $S'$ give different joins). $\bigvee S' \vee \bigvee S'' = \bigvee(S' \cup S'')$ matches set union; $\bigvee S' \wedge \bigvee S'' = \bigvee(S' \cap S'')$ matches set intersection (since $S$ is independent, no "interaction" between distinct subsets). Hence $\langle S \rangle \cong \mathfrak{P}_\text{fin}(S)$.
Ex. 11.8Atomic + relatively complemented ⟹ join of atoms
A lattice is atomic if for every $x > 0$ there exists $a \in L$ with $x \ge a > 0$. Prove that every element of a complete, relatively complemented, atomic lattice is a join of atoms.

Solution. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be complete, relatively complemented, atomic. Let $x > 0$ in $\mathcal{L}$, and let $A_x = \{a : 0 < a \le x, a $ is atom$\}$ (the atoms below $x$). Define $y = \bigvee A_x$.

Clearly $y \le x$. We show $y = x$.

Suppose $y < x$. By relative complementation in $[0, x]$, there exists $z$ with $z \wedge y = 0$ and $z \vee y = x$. Note $z \neq 0$ (else $y = x$, contradiction).

By atomicity, there exists an atom $a \le z, a > 0$. Since $a \le z$ and $z \wedge y = 0$, $a \wedge y = 0$. So $a$ is an atom not in $A_x$. But $a \le z \le x$, so $a \le x$, contradiction with $a$ being below $x$ but not in $A_x$.

Hence $y = x$, and $x = \bigvee A_x$ is a join of atoms.

Ex. 11.9Counterexample to Theorem 11.1's semimodularity hypothesis
Let $I$ be an infinite set, and let $X = \{p_i : i \in I\} \dot\cup \{q_i : i \in I\}$. Define a subset $S$ of $X$ to be closed if $S = X$ or, for all $i$, at most one of $p_i, q_i$ is in $S$. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be the lattice of all closed subsets of $X$. (a) Prove that $\mathcal{L}$ is a relatively complemented algebraic lattice with every element the join of atoms. (b) Show that the compact elements of $\mathcal{L}$ do not form an ideal. (This example shows that the semimodularity hypothesis of Theorem 11.1 cannot be omitted.)

Solution outline.

  • (a) Algebraic, atomistic, relatively complemented: Closed sets are downward-closed under "$S \cup \{p_i, q_i\} = S \cup \{p_i\}$ if $p_i \in S$" (preserve at most one). The lattice operations are set union (modulo closure) and intersection. Atoms are singletons $\{p_i\}$ and $\{q_i\}$. Every closed set $S$ proper is a join of its singleton elements. Relative complementation: given $a < x < b$, find $y$ in $[a, b]$ with $x \wedge y = a$ and $x \vee y = b$ — uses the "alternative coordinate" choice ($p_i$ vs $q_i$).
  • (b) Compact elements don't form an ideal: A closed set is compact iff it's finite. The set $X$ is in $\mathcal{L}$ as the maximum element, but it's NOT compact (no finite subset generates it via closure — the closure of a finite set is the finite set itself, not $X$). However, any non-trivial join of compact elements (finite closed sets) is again finite. The ideal of finite closed sets is missing the structural elements $X$, breaking the "ideal lattice" property required by Theorem 11.1(3).

Hence $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic, relatively complemented, atomistic — but NOT semimodular. The example shows: semimodularity is essential to Theorem 11.1's characterisation; the other three conditions (algebraic, atomistic, relatively complemented) are not enough.

Ex. 11.10$\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is relatively complemented and simple (Ore 1942)
Prove that $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is relatively complemented and simple (Ore [13]).

Solution outline. Reference: O. Ore, Theory of equivalence relations, Duke Math. J. 9 (1942), 573–627.

Relatively complemented: Given $a < x < b$ in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ — equivalence relations with $a \subseteq x \subseteq b$. Need $y$ with $x \wedge y = a$ and $x \vee y = b$. Construct $y$ by "splitting" some equivalence classes of $b$ in a way that re-merges with $x$ to give $a$. Concretely: for each $b$-class $C$, partition $C$ into "$x$-blocks" — sub-classes that are also $x$-classes. Define $y$ to identify all elements within each $x$-block but separate distinct $x$-blocks within the same $b$-class. Then $x \wedge y = a$ (intersect to get $b$-blocks intersected with $x$-blocks) and $x \vee y = b$ (union closes back up).

Simple: Need $\textbf{Con}\,(\textbf{Eq}\,X) \cong \mathbf{2}$. By Theorem 11.5 + the "little argument" + relative complementation, $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ has trivial congruence structure for $|X| \ge 2$. Specifically: any non-trivial congruence on $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ would identify two distinct equivalence relations, but the structure is "rigid" enough that any such identification cascades to identify all of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. (Detailed argument uses Theorem 10.9: relatively complemented PCF ⟹ Boolean Con; and $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ being SI in geometric lattices.)

Hence $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is simple.

Ex. 11.11Modular geometric SI iff diamond condition
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a modular geometric lattice. Prove that $\mathcal{L}$ is subdirectly irreducible (in the finite-dimensional case, simple) if and only if the following condition holds: for any two distinct atoms $a, b$ of $\mathcal{L}$, there exists a third atom $c$ such that $a \vee b = a \vee c = b \vee c$, i.e., the three atoms generate a diamond. Give an example to show that this condition is not necessary in the nonmodular (but still semimodular) case.

Solution outline.

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\mathcal{L}$ is modular geometric SI. Take two distinct atoms $a, b$. Their join $a \vee b$ has dimension 2 (line through two points). In a modular geometric lattice with atom set $A$, the line $a \vee b$ contains other atoms — by SI + modular structure, there's an atom $c \le a \vee b$ with $c \neq a, b$. Then $a \vee c = a \vee b$ (both are $\le$ the line, both span the same dim 2 element when joined with $a$). Similarly $b \vee c = a \vee b$. So $a, b, c$ generate a diamond $\mathcal{M}_3$.

($\Leftarrow$) The diamond condition is structurally strong enough that any congruence trying to identify two atoms cascades to identify everything via the diamonds — making the lattice SI.

Counterexample in semimodular non-modular case: $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for $|X| \ge 4$ is semimodular but not modular. It IS SI (Exercise 11.10) — but the diamond condition fails for some pairs of atoms. So the diamond condition is sufficient but not necessary for SI in semimodular non-modular geometric lattices.

Ex. 11.12Modular μ congruence by finite interval length
On a modular lattice $\mathcal{M}$, define a relation $a \,\mu\, b$ iff $[a \wedge b, a \vee b]$ has finite length. Show that $\mu$ is a congruence relation.

Solution.

Reflexivity: $[a \wedge a, a \vee a] = [a, a] = \{a\}$, length 0. So $a \,\mu\, a$.

Symmetry: $[a \wedge b, a \vee b] = [b \wedge a, b \vee a]$ — same interval. So $a \,\mu\, b \Leftrightarrow b \,\mu\, a$.

Transitivity: Suppose $a \,\mu\, b$ and $b \,\mu\, c$. Need $[a \wedge c, a \vee c]$ has finite length. Use modularity: $[a \wedge c, a \vee c] \subseteq [a \wedge b \wedge c, a \vee b \vee c]$. The bigger interval has length $\le$ length($[a \wedge b, a \vee b]$) + length($[b \wedge c, b \vee c]$) (modular addition of lengths via Dedekind transposition Theorem 9.3). Both addends finite, so the sum is finite.

Compatible with $\vee$: Suppose $a \,\mu\, b$ and $c \,\mu\, d$. Need $(a \vee c) \,\mu\, (b \vee d)$. Show $[(a \vee c) \wedge (b \vee d), (a \vee c) \vee (b \vee d)]$ has finite length. The interval is bounded by $[(a \wedge b) \vee (c \wedge d), a \vee b \vee c \vee d]$, whose length is bounded by length($[a \wedge b, a \vee b]$) + length($[c \wedge d, c \vee d]$) via modular addition.

Compatible with $\wedge$: By duality (or direct argument).

Hence $\mu$ is a congruence on the modular lattice $\mathcal{M}$. The congruence $\mu$ identifies elements that are "infinitesimally close" — those whose joining interval is finite-length.

LOOKING AHEAD The Appendices

With Chapter 11, the main text concludes. The book continues with three appendices: cardinals/ordinals/universal algebra, the axiom of choice, and formal concept analysis.

Chapter 11 is the last main chapter of Nation's text. Geometric lattices — the lattice abstraction of incidence geometry, equivalent to matroids and to closure operators with exchange — close the structural arc that began with posets in Chapter 1.

The three appendices that follow extend the lattice-theoretic toolkit:

Appendix 1 — Cardinals, Ordinals, and Universal Algebra

Background on set-theoretic foundations needed throughout the text: cardinal arithmetic, ordinals and transfinite induction, universal algebra basics (algebras, terms, varieties, free algebras). Many proofs in Chapters 5-11 use Zorn's Lemma (axiom of choice equivalent), transfinite induction, or universal-algebra constructions (Birkhoff's HSP); the appendix provides the foundations.

Appendix 2 — The Axiom of Choice

A survey of the axiom of choice (AC) and its lattice-theoretic equivalents. Birkhoff: every lattice has a complete maximal chain ⟺ AC. Other equivalents: Zorn's Lemma, Hausdorff maximal principle, Tukey's lemma. Discussion of lattice-theoretic statements that depend on AC (Wehrung 2007's CLP counterexample requires AC) vs those that don't.

Appendix 3 — Formal Concept Analysis

An alternate approach to lattice representation, due to Rudolf Wille (1982): Restructuring lattice theory: an approach based on hierarchies of concepts (Ordered Sets, ed. Rival, 445–470). Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) represents finite lattices via "formal contexts" (object-attribute relations). The lattice of formal concepts (intent-extent pairs) is a complete lattice. FCA has applications in data mining, knowledge representation, and ontology engineering. Modern reference: Ganter-Wille, Formal Concept Analysis: Mathematical Foundations (Springer 1999).

Carrying forward · the toolkit

The appendices use everything from the main text: posets, lattices, complete lattices, congruences, free algebras, varieties, modular and distributive structure. They round out the lattice-theoretic education by providing foundational tools (sets/ordinals, AC) and an alternate representation perspective (FCA) that complements the main text's structural-algebraic emphasis.
