MODULE 0 Why Lattices for Prologos

Before the algebra, the shape. Lattices turn up in four distinct roles here — and Prologos bets that they are the same thing.

This curriculum exists because the Prologos Project rests on a single structural conjecture: every computable function is a fixpoint on a lattice, and the Hasse diagram of that lattice is the optimal parallel decomposition of the computation. That sentence compresses four traditions:

  1. Order theory. Lattices as partially ordered sets with meet and join — the oldest and most elementary layer.
  2. Abstract interpretation & dataflow. Lattices as abstract domains; programs as monotone functions; program meaning as a least fixpoint.
  3. Annotation algebras. Lattices enriched to quantales — the natural algebra for weighted, resource-tracking, or probabilistic propagator networks.
  4. Module theory. The submodule lattice $\mathrm{L}(M)$ of an $R$-module as a modular lattice, the bridge between linear-algebra intuition and abstract structure.
Intuition

Think of a computation and a module as two faces of the same pattern. In a module, submodules sit in a lattice and climb from $0$ to $M$ via sums and intersections. In a propagator network, cell values sit in a lattice and climb from $\bot$ to a fixpoint via joins. Prologos reads both faces at once: "the Hasse diagram of any such lattice is the parallel structure of whatever it describes."

What this curriculum does

It builds the vocabulary and fluency to hold all four traditions simultaneously, in the order Prologos actually needs them:

  • Part I — Fundamentals. Posets, lattices, special classes, morphisms. The base layer.
  • Part II — Computation. Semilattices, CALM, fixpoints (Tarski & Gauss–Seidel), Galois connections, Heyting and Boolean lattices, hypercubes. The working lattice theory of propagator networks.
  • Part III — Quantales. Semirings' limits, quantales as their completion, residuation, bilattices, QTT multiplicities. The annotation layer.
  • Part IV — Modules. Full classical R-module theory through the submodule lattice $\mathrm{L}(M)$: modular law, chain conditions, Jordan–Hölder, Krull–Schmidt. Then Prologos's own sense of "module of lattices."
  • Part V — Prologos Applications. The Hyperlattice Conjecture in operational form, the SRE Lattice Lens as a design methodology, and a guided tour of the Prologos codebase cell by cell.

"All-at-once, all in parallel, structurally emergent information flow ON-NETWORK."

It is the operational form of the Hyperlattice Conjecture. We earn the right to say it in Module 16; until then, every theorem we learn is a deposit against that account.

Lattice Fundamentals

The base layer: posets, meets & joins, special classes, and the maps between lattices.

MODULE 1 Posets — The Order Beneath Everything

A lattice is a poset with two extra operations. Get the poset right and the lattice writes itself.
Prereq: comfort with set notation Goal: read and draw Hasse diagrams fluently
Definition 1.1 — Partially ordered set

A poset $(P, \leq)$ is a set $P$ with a binary relation $\leq$ that is reflexive ($a \leq a$), antisymmetric ($a \leq b$ and $b \leq a$ imply $a = b$), and transitive ($a \leq b \leq c$ implies $a \leq c$).

Examples 1.2

(a) $(\mathbb{N}, \mid)$ under divisibility. (b) $(\mathcal{P}(X), \subseteq)$, the power set of $X$. (c) $(\mathrm{Sub}(G), \subseteq)$, subgroups of a group $G$. (d) $(\mathrm{L}(M), \subseteq)$, submodules of an $R$-module $M$. (e) The worldview lattice $Q_n = (\{0,1\}^n, \leq)$, where bitstrings order component-wise — the lattice the BSP scheduler forks over (Module 8).

Hasse diagrams

A Hasse diagram draws a poset by its covering relation: $a \lessdot b$ means $a < b$ with nothing strictly between. Edges go upward; transitive edges are omitted.

Bounds, sup, inf

Definition 1.3 — Least upper / greatest lower bound

For $S \subseteq P$: $u$ is an upper bound if $s \leq u$ for all $s \in S$. The supremum $\sup S$ (a.k.a. join, $\bigvee S$) is the least upper bound, if it exists. Dually, the infimum $\inf S$ (a.k.a. meet, $\bigwedge S$) is the greatest lower bound.

Posets where every finite non-empty subset has both a meet and a join are called lattices.

Self-Check 1.A
  1. Draw the Hasse diagram of $(\{1,2,3,4,6,12\}, \mid)$ — the divisors of 12.
  2. In $(\mathcal{P}(\{a,b,c\}), \subseteq)$, compute $\{a,b\} \wedge \{b,c\}$ and $\{a\} \vee \{c\}$.
  3. Is every finite poset a lattice? Give a counterexample.
Reveal discussion

(1) The diagram is the three-dimensional "divisor cube" on 12: $1$ at bottom; $2,3$ at the next level; $4,6$ above them; $12$ at top. (2) $\{b\}$ and $\{a,c\}$. (3) No — the 3-element antichain $\{a,b,c\}$ with no relations has no pairwise joins or meets at all. Another: the "V" poset with top elements $x,y$ and bottom $z$: $x \vee y$ doesn't exist (there's no top).

Prologos Hook — Module 1

Every propagator cell in Prologos carries a value from some poset (usually a lattice). The smallest running examples: mult-lattice (racket/prologos/mult-lattice.rkt) — the 5-element flat lattice $\{\bot, m_0, m_1, m_\omega, \top\}$ over QTT multiplicities; term-lattice (racket/prologos/term-lattice.rkt) — a 4-element lattice ($\bot$, variable, constructor, $\top$) whose structure carries structural unification (PUnify). Both are flat: $\bot \leq x \leq \top$ with incomparable middle. That flatness is the reason their merges are cheap and their contradiction checks are a single equality test.

MODULE 2 Lattices — Meet & Join

A lattice has two lives: an order and an algebra. They agree, and that's the whole trick.
Definition 2.1 — Lattice (two ways)

Order-theoretic: a poset $(L, \leq)$ in which every pair $\{a,b\}$ has a meet $a \wedge b$ and a join $a \vee b$.

Algebraic: a set $L$ with two binary operations $\wedge, \vee$ satisfying, for all $a,b,c \in L$:

Commutativity: $a \wedge b = b \wedge a$; $a \vee b = b \vee a$.
Associativity: $(a \wedge b) \wedge c = a \wedge (b \wedge c)$; same for $\vee$.
Idempotence: $a \wedge a = a$; $a \vee a = a$.
Absorption: $a \wedge (a \vee b) = a$; $a \vee (a \wedge b) = a$.

The two definitions coincide via $a \leq b \iff a \wedge b = a \iff a \vee b = b$.

Why absorption is the whole game

Absorption pins the two operations to a common order. Remove it and you have two unrelated semilattices. Keep it and $\leq$ is recoverable from $\wedge$ (or from $\vee$) alone. The equivalence of "poset-with-bounds" and "two-op algebra" is what lets us slide between drawing pictures and computing identities.

Bounded and complete lattices

A lattice is bounded if it has a greatest element $\top$ and a least element $\bot$. It is complete if every subset has a meet and a join. Submodule lattices are complete; so is every cell value lattice in Prologos (by design — this is what makes CALM-safe merges well-defined).

Theorem 2.2 — Submodule lattices are complete

Let $M$ be an $R$-module. The set $\mathrm{L}(M)$ of submodules of $M$, ordered by inclusion, is a complete lattice with $\bigwedge_i N_i = \bigcap_i N_i$ and $\bigvee_i N_i = \sum_i N_i$. Intersections of submodules are submodules; the sum $\sum N_i$ is the smallest submodule containing each $N_i$.

MODULE 3 Special Classes — Distributive, Modular, Complemented

Modularity is the law submodule lattices obey. Distributivity is the stronger law they almost never do.
Definition 3.1 — Distributive & modular

A lattice is distributive if for all $a,b,c$: $$a \wedge (b \vee c) = (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge c).$$ It is modular if the weaker implication holds: $$a \leq c \implies a \vee (b \wedge c) = (a \vee b) \wedge c.$$ Every distributive lattice is modular; the converse fails.

Theorem 3.2 — Forbidden sublattices (Dedekind–Birkhoff)

A lattice $L$ is modular $\iff$ it contains no sublattice isomorphic to $N_5$ (the pentagon).
A lattice $L$ is distributive $\iff$ it contains no sublattice isomorphic to $N_5$ or $M_3$ (the diamond).

Theorem 3.3 — The submodule lattice is modular

For any $R$-module $M$, the lattice $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is modular. This is the lattice-level content of the modular law and the isomorphism theorems (Module 13).

$\mathrm{L}(M)$ is distributive precisely when $M$ decomposes as a direct sum of pairwise non-isomorphic uniserial pieces — e.g. $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$ for distinct primes. Most interesting modules live in the modular-but-not-distributive zone, which is why modularity is the headline axiom.

Definition 3.4 — Complemented lattice

A bounded lattice $L$ is complemented if for every $a \in L$ there exists $a' \in L$ with $a \wedge a' = \bot$ and $a \vee a' = \top$. In a modular, complemented lattice, complements are not unique — a fact that foreshadows the multiplicity of direct-sum decompositions (Module 15).

Prologos Hook — Module 3

Where Prologos lattices sit on this map: the worldview lattice $Q_n$ (Module 8) is Boolean — distributive and complemented. The session-type and term lattices (Module 2 hook) are distributive but unbounded-complemented (flat meets/joins). $\mathrm{L}(M)$ and the submodule-of-lattice structure used in the compiler's self-hosted representation (Module 15) are modular but not distributive. That distinction is why the BSP scheduler can prune subcubes in $O(1)$ for nogoods (distributivity) but must track independent per-branch assumptions with bitmask tags for the elaborator (modular-only).

MODULE 4 Morphisms, Congruences, Galois Connections (Preview)

The lattice category is smaller than you'd think, and richer.
Definition 4.1 — Lattice homomorphism

A map $f : L \to L'$ is a lattice homomorphism if $f(a \wedge b) = f(a) \wedge f(b)$ and $f(a \vee b) = f(a) \vee f(b)$. An order-preserving map need not be a lattice homomorphism.

Kernels in this setting are congruences: equivalence relations $\theta$ compatible with both operations. The quotient $L/\theta$ is again a lattice, and the three isomorphism theorems run parallel to the module case — modularity of $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is exactly what makes the parallels run.

Definition 4.2 — Galois connection (preview)

A pair of monotone maps $f : P \to Q$, $g : Q \to P$ forms a Galois connection if $f(p) \leq q \iff p \leq g(q)$. These connections manufacture lattices out of raw relations and are the abstract backbone of every bridge between lattices — between abstract and concrete domains (Module 7), between cells in a Prologos network (Module 17), between module-theoretic dualities (annihilators, polarities).

Self-Check 4.A

  2. Describe the Galois connection $(\mathrm{L}(M), \mathrm{L}(M^*))$ given by annihilators, for a finite-dimensional vector space $M$.
Prologos Hook — Module 4

Every "bridge" between Prologos cells is, at bottom, a Galois connection — and that is not a metaphor. The SRE Lattice Lens (Module 17) requires each bridge between cells to be a Galois connection: if you can't produce the pair $(f, g)$ with $f(x) \leq y \iff x \leq g(y)$, the bridge is not well-typed. Example: the projection from decision cells to the worldview cache IS a left adjoint (joins of committed bits) and the cache IS its right adjoint (the set of worldviews compatible with a commitment). We return to this in Module 7 with infrastructure to prove it.

MODULE 5 Semilattices, Monotonicity, and the CALM Theorem

If your merge is a join-semilattice operation, you get coordination-free distributed execution for free.
Definition 5.1 — Semilattice

A join-semilattice is a set with an associative, commutative, idempotent binary operation $\vee$. Equivalently, a poset in which every finite non-empty subset has a join. Meet-semilattices are dual. A lattice is both.

Definition 5.2 — Monotone function

$f : L \to L'$ is monotone (order-preserving) if $a \leq b \implies f(a) \leq f(b)$. It is $\vee$-preserving if $f(a \vee b) = f(a) \vee f(b)$ (which is strictly stronger).

In propagator-network terms, a cell's value evolves monotonically up a join-semilattice: new information can only add to what is already known. Contradictions are detected by reaching $\top$.

Theorem 5.3 — The CALM theorem (Ameloot–Neven–Van den Bussche; Hellerstein)

A distributed computation has a consistent, coordination-free execution plan if and only if it is expressible as a monotone function over a semilattice. More precisely: a query is CALM-safe (eventually consistent regardless of message order) iff it is monotone in its input relations.

Consequence: monotone merges do not need coordination. Non-monotone operations (negation, retraction, aggregation) require stratification — barriers that serialise non-monotone steps between monotone rounds.

Why this matters operationally

If every cell on your propagator network holds a semilattice value and every propagator is monotone, the BSP scheduler can fire propagators in any order, across any partition, and the fixpoint is the same. That is the engineering payoff of insisting every cell's value carry a monotone merge — it buys unconditional parallelism. When a non-monotone step is forced (negation-as-failure, retraction), it gets quarantined at a stratum boundary (Module 6, and Module 18's stratification tour).

Compounding semilattices — the product construction

Definition 5.4 — Product lattice

Given lattices $L_1, \ldots, L_n$, the product $L = L_1 \times \cdots \times L_n$ is a lattice under component-wise meet and join: $(a_1, \ldots, a_n) \wedge (b_1, \ldots, b_n) = (a_1 \wedge b_1, \ldots, a_n \wedge b_n)$. The Hasse diagram of a product is the Cartesian product of graphs — for $n$ two-point chains, this is the hypercube $Q_n$.

Self-Check 5.A
  1. Show that composition of monotone functions is monotone, but composition of $\vee$-preserving functions need not be $\vee$-preserving.
  2. Give an example of a monotone function that is not $\vee$-preserving.
  3. What is the Hasse diagram of $\{0,1\} \times \{0,1\} \times \{0,1\}$?
Reveal discussion

(1) Composition of monotone is monotone by transitivity. For $\vee$-preservation, consider $f(x) = $ constant: monotone, but doesn't preserve joins unless the codomain is the trivial lattice. (2) $f : \mathcal{P}(\{a,b\}) \to \mathcal{P}(\{a,b\})$ mapping $S \mapsto \{a\}$ if $S \neq \emptyset$, else $\emptyset$: monotone but $f(\{a\}) \vee f(\{b\}) = \{a\} \neq f(\{a,b\}) = \{a\}$ — this one does preserve! Better: $f(S) = $ "first element of $S$ in some ordering" — monotone but not join-preserving. (3) The 3-cube $Q_3$: 8 nodes, 12 edges, the adjacency structure of three independent bits.

Prologos Hook — Module 5

CALM is Prologos's architectural gravity. Every cell on the propagator network carries a monotone merge; every propagator is monotone on its inputs. The architectural consequence (stated in .claude/rules/on-network.md): "every make-parameter with a hasheq value is a cell that hasn't been born yet" — because a parameter is ambient, off-network state, but a cell with a hash-union merge is monotone and CALM-safe. The propagator-design rules (.claude/rules/propagator-design.md) mandate that any candidate for a for/fold over independent items be rewritten as a broadcast propagator: one install, one fire, one merge — exactly the parallel structure CALM licenses.

MODULE 6 Fixpoints — Tarski, Gauss–Seidel, Well-Founded

Programs are monotone functions; meanings are least fixpoints; non-monotone steps live at higher strata.
Theorem 6.1 — Knaster–Tarski

Let $L$ be a complete lattice and $f : L \to L$ monotone. Then the set of fixpoints of $f$ is itself a complete lattice, with least fixpoint $\operatorname{lfp}(f) = \bigwedge \{x : f(x) \leq x\}$ and greatest fixpoint $\operatorname{gfp}(f) = \bigvee \{x : x \leq f(x)\}$.

Theorem 6.2 — Kleene iteration (ω-continuous case)

If additionally $f$ is $\omega$-continuous (preserves sups of ascending chains), then $\operatorname{lfp}(f) = \bigvee_{n \geq 0} f^n(\bot)$. This is the computational engine: starting from $\bot$, iterate $f$; the iterates climb monotonically; their sup is the least fixpoint.

Kleene iteration as "BSP rounds"

Each application of $f$ is one BSP round: read all cells, compute new values, write the joins. The schedule terminates when $f^{n+1}(\bot) = f^n(\bot)$ — quiescence. The number of rounds is bounded by the diameter of the lattice.

Stratified fixpoints — when monotonicity breaks

Real systems are not globally monotone: negation, retraction, and aggregation can shrink information. The standard trick is stratification: split the computation into layers $S_0, S_1, S_2, \ldots$ where each $S_i$ is internally monotone, and $S_{i+1}$ sees $S_i$ only after $S_i$ has reached its fixpoint.

Definition 6.3 — Gauss–Seidel (stratified) fixpoint

Given monotone operators $f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_k$ whose dependencies stratify (each $f_i$ depends only on $f_0, \ldots, f_{i}$), the stratified fixpoint is computed by finding $\operatorname{lfp}(f_0)$, freezing it, then $\operatorname{lfp}(f_1)$ on top, and so on. The composite is well-founded when each stratum terminates.

Definition 6.4 — Well-founded semantics (three-valued fixpoint)

For programs with negation-as-failure, the well-founded semantics assigns each atom a truth value in $\{\mathtt{true}, \mathtt{false}, \mathtt{unknown}\}$ via a three-valued fixpoint on a bilattice (Module 10). The lower cell accumulates evidence FOR; the upper cell eliminates evidence AGAINST. The gap between them is the "unknown" region.

MODULE 7 Galois Connections & Abstract Interpretation

Every sound approximation between lattices is a Galois connection. Every bridge between Prologos cells must be one.
Definition 7.1 — Galois connection (proper)

A pair of monotone maps $\alpha : C \to A$ (abstraction) and $\gamma : A \to C$ (concretisation) between posets $C$ and $A$ forms a Galois connection if $$\alpha(c) \leq_A a \iff c \leq_C \gamma(a).$$ Equivalently (and usefully): $\alpha$ preserves all joins; $\gamma$ preserves all meets. We say $\alpha$ is a left adjoint to $\gamma$ (and $\gamma$ is a right adjoint to $\alpha$).

Theorem 7.2 — Composition and properties

(1) Galois connections compose: $(\alpha_1, \gamma_1) \circ (\alpha_2, \gamma_2) = (\alpha_2 \alpha_1, \gamma_1 \gamma_2)$ is again a Galois connection.
(2) $\gamma \circ \alpha$ is a closure operator on $C$ (extensive, monotone, idempotent).
(3) $\alpha \circ \gamma$ is a kernel operator on $A$ (contractive, monotone, idempotent).
(4) The left adjoint is uniquely determined by the right adjoint (and conversely).

Abstract interpretation in one slide

Given a concrete semantics on $C$ (e.g. "set of reachable program states") and an abstract domain $A$ (e.g. "intervals of integers"), a Galois connection $(\alpha, \gamma)$ makes the abstract analysis sound: any fixpoint computed on $A$ concretises to an overapproximation in $C$. Interval domain is the canonical example — in Prologos, the file racket/prologos/interval-domain.rkt IS this exact construction.

Example 7.3 — The interval lattice as an abstraction

Take $C = \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{Z})$ (sets of integers, ordered by $\subseteq$), $A = \{[l, u] : l \leq u\} \cup \{\bot\}$ (intervals, ordered by containment). Define $\alpha(S) = [\min S, \max S]$ (the tightest enclosing interval), $\gamma([l,u]) = \{n : l \leq n \leq u\}$. This is a Galois connection, and it's the abstract domain Prologos uses to narrow numeric constructor enumeration during FL narrowing.

MODULE 8 Heyting, Boolean, Hypercube — The $Q_n$ Lattice

The Boolean lattice $Q_n$ and its Hasse diagram — the hypercube — is the shape of worldview exploration.
Definition 8.1 — Heyting algebra

A Heyting algebra is a bounded distributive lattice $H$ with an implication operation $\to$ satisfying $a \wedge b \leq c \iff a \leq b \to c$. This is the lattice-theoretic shape of intuitionistic logic: $\to$ is the residuation of $\wedge$.

Definition 8.2 — Boolean lattice

A Boolean lattice is a complemented distributive lattice. Equivalently, a Heyting algebra with $\neg\neg a = a$. The canonical example is the power set $\mathcal{P}(X)$; the finitary canonical example is $Q_n = \{0,1\}^n$ under component-wise $\leq$.

Theorem 8.3 — Structural properties of $Q_n$

(1) $|Q_n| = 2^n$; rank (maximal chain length) is $n$; diameter is $n$.
(2) Every interval $[a, b]$ in $Q_n$ is itself a Boolean lattice $Q_{|b|-|a|}$ — the subcubes.
(3) Complement is the bitwise NOT; intervals of the form $[\bot, a]$ and $[a, \top]$ are subcubes.
(4) The Hasse diagram IS the hypercube graph $Q_n$ — edges correspond to single-bit flips (covering relation).
(5) Gray code traversal is a Hamiltonian path on the hypercube: a single-bit-flip sequence visiting every node exactly once.

$Q_n$ is the worldview space

Suppose a proof search commits to $n$ independent binary assumptions (each is either in or out of the worldview). The space of possible worldviews is exactly $Q_n$: $2^n$ nodes, each a bitmask. Two worldviews that differ in one bit are adjacent in $Q_n$ — they are one commit/retract step apart. A nogood (contradiction) rules out an entire subcube at once. The BSP scheduler's pruning is a subcube check: one bitmask AND. Gray-code traversal means adjacent rounds differ by one assumption flip, maximising structural sharing of propagator state. This is the Hyperlattice Conjecture in operational form.

Heyting vs Boolean — and which one Prologos uses

Heyting algebras are the semantic home of constructive/intuitionistic reasoning: "a proposition holds" is monotone information. Boolean algebras add classical complement: every element has a unique negation. The worldview lattice $Q_n$ is Boolean (bits can be 0 or 1 with classical negation). The information lattice of a Prologos cell value (e.g. session types, term lattice) is typically Heyting but not Boolean — there is no classical negation of "this session type is $\mathtt{send}(\mathrm{Int}) \to \mathtt{end}$." This asymmetry is why Prologos needs both.

Quantales & Annotation Algebras

Why semirings are not enough. Quantales complete the square: a complete lattice and an associative, join-distributive tensor. This is Prologos's annotation layer.

MODULE 9 Semirings → Quantales

Weighted logic programming uses semirings. Propagator networks need the completion: quantales.
Definition 9.1 — Semiring

A semiring $(K, \oplus, \otimes, 0, 1)$ is a set with two associative operations: $\oplus$ commutative with identity $0$, $\otimes$ with identity $1$, $\otimes$ distributing over $\oplus$, and $0$ annihilating under $\otimes$. Examples: $(\mathbb{N}, +, \times)$ (counting), $(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, +, \times)$ (probabilities), $(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \cup \{\infty\}, \min, +)$ (tropical — shortest paths), Boolean $(\{0,1\}, \vee, \wedge)$.

Semirings are the standard annotation algebra for weighted/probabilistic logic programming: ProbLog, aProbLog, provenance semirings, Dyna, Scallop. They aggregate across derivations ($\oplus$) and compose within a derivation ($\otimes$).

Theorem 9.2 — Four gaps between semirings and propagator networks

Propagator-network merges require, simultaneously, four properties that generic semirings lack:

  • Idempotent aggregation. Cells take joins; $x \oplus x = x$. This rules out probability semirings (where $p \oplus p = 2p \neq p$).
  • A contradiction element $\top$. Propagators must detect inconsistency, which semirings lack.
  • Completeness. All (infinite) joins must exist, for Tarski fixpoints on recursive programs. Generic semirings allow only finite $\oplus$.
  • An induced partial order. Monotonicity is stated against $\leq$; generic semirings have no canonical order.

Only idempotent complete semirings begin to satisfy all four — and idempotency sacrifices the probabilistic and provenance annotations that motivated semirings in the first place.

Definition 9.3 — Quantale

A quantale $(Q, \leq, \otimes)$ is a complete sup-lattice with an associative tensor $\otimes$ that distributes over arbitrary joins on both sides: $$a \otimes \bigvee_i b_i = \bigvee_i (a \otimes b_i), \qquad \left(\bigvee_i a_i\right) \otimes b = \bigvee_i (a_i \otimes b).$$ Unital if $\otimes$ has an identity $e$; commutative if $a \otimes b = b \otimes a$.

Theorem 9.4 — Automatic consequences of the quantale axioms

(1) $\bot = \bigvee \emptyset$ is a two-sided annihilator: $\bot \otimes a = a \otimes \bot = \bot$.
(2) Residuations exist: $a \backslash b := \bigvee \{x : a \otimes x \leq b\}$ and $b / a := \bigvee \{x : x \otimes a \leq b\}$. These are the internal homs (linear implications), and they exist automatically by the adjoint functor theorem.
(3) Every quantale is a complete Heyting algebra when $\otimes = \wedge$.
(4) The fixpoint theorem: for monotone $f : Q \to Q$, $\operatorname{lfp}(f)$ exists (Tarski) and is computable by transfinite iteration.

MODULE 10 Residuated Lattices, Bilattices, QTT Annotations

Residuation is implication. Bilattices pair two ordered views. QTT multiplicities sit at the intersection.
Definition 10.1 — Residuated lattice

A residuated lattice is a lattice $L$ with an associative operation $\otimes$ and two residuations $\backslash, /$ satisfying $$x \otimes y \leq z \iff y \leq x \backslash z \iff x \leq z / y.$$ A commutative residuated lattice is the algebraic structure of substructural logic (linear, affine, relevant). Quantales are complete residuated lattices.

In propagator terms: $x \otimes y$ is "composing cell states $x$ and $y$ sequentially"; $x \backslash z$ is "the weakest input that, when composed with $x$, yields at most $z$." Residuation is how you invert a monotone operation along the lattice order — an abstract division.

Bilattices — two orderings on one carrier

Definition 10.2 — Bilattice

A bilattice is a set $B$ with two complete lattice structures: the truth order $(B, \leq_t, \wedge_t, \vee_t)$ and the information order $(B, \leq_i, \wedge_i, \vee_i)$. The canonical example is $\mathrm{FOUR} = \{\mathtt{false}, \mathtt{unknown}, \mathtt{true}, \mathtt{contradict}\}$ — truth-order ascending $\mathtt{false} <_t \mathtt{unknown} =_t \mathtt{contradict} <_t \mathtt{true}$; information-order ascending $\mathtt{unknown} <_i \mathtt{false} =_i \mathtt{true} <_i \mathtt{contradict}$.

Well-founded semantics runs a three-valued fixpoint on this bilattice: the lower cell accumulates truth evidence upward from $\bot_i$; the upper cell reduces from $\top_i$; quiescence identifies exact values (where the bounds meet) and gaps ("unknown" atoms). This is the construction racket/prologos/bilattice.rkt implements.

QTT multiplicities as a quantale-adjacent lattice

Definition 10.3 — QTT multiplicities (Atkey)

Quantitative Type Theory tracks each variable's multiplicity: how many times it is used at runtime. Standard choices are $\{0, 1, \omega\}$ (erased, linear, unrestricted), organised as a commutative semiring with $\oplus$ (sum of usages) and $\otimes$ (scalar scale). Prologos lifts this to a lattice $\{\bot, 0, 1, \omega, \top\}$ — the flat lattice in mult-lattice.rkt, where $\bot$ is "unknown multiplicity" and $\top$ is "contradictory multiplicity."

The move from semiring to lattice is exactly the completion Module 9 described: the lattice view adds the partial order and contradiction element the semiring lacks, making the multiplicity a cell value with a monotone merge. The open research question is whether multiplicities, session types, and future probabilistic annotations all factor through a common non-commutative quantale acting on cells.

MODULE 11 Modules Over Rings — A Working Refresher

The minimum you need so that the lattice translation feels like review, not a new subject.
Definition 11.1 — Left $R$-module

Given a ring $R$ (associative, with $1$), a left $R$-module is an abelian group $(M, +)$ with a scalar multiplication $R \times M \to M$ satisfying $1 \cdot m = m$, $r(m + n) = rm + rn$, $(r+s)m = rm + sm$, and $(rs)m = r(sm)$.

Examples 11.2

$R = \mathbb{Z}$: modules are abelian groups. $R$ a field $k$: modules are $k$-vector spaces. $R$ itself: $R$ is an $R$-module (the regular representation), and its submodules are exactly the left ideals. For a polynomial ring $k[x]$: a $k[x]$-module is a $k$-vector space plus a chosen endomorphism $T$ (with $x \cdot v := T(v)$).

Submodules, quotients, homomorphisms

A submodule $N \subseteq M$ is an additive subgroup closed under scalar multiplication. The quotient $M/N$ inherits an $R$-module structure. An $R$-module homomorphism $f: M \to M'$ satisfies $f(rm + sn) = rf(m) + sf(n)$.

Theorem 11.3 — Isomorphism theorems (module version)

(i) $M/\ker f \cong \mathrm{im}(f)$.
(ii) For $N, P \leq M$: $(N + P)/P \cong N/(N \cap P)$.
(iii) For $P \leq N \leq M$: $(M/P)/(N/P) \cong M/N$.

Each of these is the module-flavoured trace of a lattice isomorphism inside $\mathrm{L}(M)$. The second one is the modular law in disguise — see Module 13.

MODULE 12 The Submodule Lattice $\mathrm{L}(M)$

From here on, every module-theoretic question is a lattice-theoretic question.
Definition 12.1 — The submodule lattice

Let $M$ be a left $R$-module. Its submodule lattice $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is the set of submodules of $M$ ordered by inclusion, with $N \wedge P = N \cap P$ and $N \vee P = N + P$. It is a complete, bounded lattice with $\bot = 0$ and $\top = M$.

Theorem 12.2 — Correspondence theorem

For any submodule $N \leq M$, the interval $[N, M] := \{P : N \leq P \leq M\}$ in $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is isomorphic (as a lattice) to $\mathrm{L}(M/N)$ via $P \mapsto P/N$.

This is the key "zoom" tool: to study the submodule lattice above $N$, study the module $M/N$ itself. To study the lattice below $N$, study $N$.

MODULE 13 The Modular Law — The Translation Key

One identity that makes everything about modules behave like linear algebra.
Theorem 13.1 — Dedekind's modular law for modules

For submodules $A, B, C$ of $M$ with $A \leq C$: $$A + (B \cap C) = (A + B) \cap C.$$ Proof. $(\subseteq)$: both $A \leq C$ and $B \cap C \leq C$, so $A + (B \cap C) \leq C$; and $A, B \cap C \leq A + B$, so $A + (B \cap C) \leq (A + B) \cap C$. $(\supseteq)$: if $x \in (A + B) \cap C$, write $x = a + b$ with $a \in A, b \in B$; then $b = x - a \in C$ (since $x, a \in C$) so $b \in B \cap C$, hence $x = a + b \in A + (B \cap C)$.

What the modular law means

It says: "you can distribute a sum over an intersection as long as one of the summands is already inside the intersection's ambient space." It is the algebraic fingerprint of the fact that submodules of a module behave like subspaces of a vector space — they slide past each other cleanly. The second isomorphism theorem is its direct corollary.

Where modularity actually shows up

  • Diamond isomorphism theorem. $(N + P)/N \cong P/(N \cap P)$ — immediate from the modular law plus the first iso theorem.
  • Dimension/length additivity. $\ell(N + P) + \ell(N \cap P) = \ell(N) + \ell(P)$ in the finite-length case. The analogue of $\dim(U + V) + \dim(U \cap V) = \dim U + \dim V$ for subspaces.
  • Jordan–Hölder. Uniqueness of composition factors — proved by a lattice-theoretic refinement argument (Schreier), which only works because $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is modular.

MODULE 14 Chain Conditions & Length

When the lattice has finite height, modules behave almost like linear spaces.
Definition 14.1 — ACC, DCC, length

A poset satisfies the ascending chain condition (ACC) if every ascending chain $a_1 \leq a_2 \leq \cdots$ eventually stabilises. Dually, DCC. A module $M$ is Noetherian / Artinian if $\mathrm{L}(M)$ has ACC / DCC respectively. The length $\ell(M)$ is the supremum of lengths of chains in $\mathrm{L}(M)$.

Theorem 14.2 — Jordan–Hölder

If $M$ has finite length, any two composition series of $M$ have the same length and the same multiset of simple factors (up to isomorphism). Proof rests on the modular law (Schreier refinement lemma).

MODULE 15 Decomposition & Modules of Lattices

Semisimplicity, Krull–Schmidt, and Prologos's own sense of "module" — a collection of lattices glued by Galois bridges.

Classical decomposition

Theorem 15.1 — Semisimple ⇔ complemented lattice

A module $M$ is semisimple (a direct sum of simple submodules) if and only if $\mathrm{L}(M)$ is a complemented modular lattice — equivalently, every submodule has a direct complement in $M$.

Theorem 15.2 — Krull–Schmidt (short form)

Every module of finite length decomposes as a direct sum of indecomposables, uniquely up to order and isomorphism of summands. The lattice-side witness is that two decompositions give two maximal "independent" families of atoms in $\mathrm{L}(M)$ that can be matched.

Where the classical lattice loses information

The submodule lattice sees which submodules exist and how they sit, but not how the ring acts. Two modules can share a lattice without sharing an isomorphism type (e.g. $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$ for distinct primes — both have the 2-element lattice). For everything up to that subtlety, the lattice suffices.

Prologos's sense of "module of lattices"

In the structural-thinking document, Prologos uses the word "module" in a second, distinct sense. A module (in Prologos-sense) is a collection of lattices together with Galois-connection bridges between them. Each relation in a tabled relational engine is a morphism (input lattice → output lattice). The collection of all relations is a product module. The fixpoint on the product is computed by independent per-component fixpoints (for the non-recursive case).

Definition 15.3 — Prologos-sense module

A Prologos-sense module is a triple $(\{L_i\}, \{f_{ij}\}, \{g_{ij}\})$ where each $L_i$ is a complete lattice (a cell value), and each $(f_{ij}, g_{ij})$ is a Galois connection between $L_i$ and $L_j$ (a bridge). The module's fixpoint is the simultaneous least fixpoint on the product lattice $\prod L_i$ under the combined monotone operator induced by the bridges.

Direct sum has two realisations — and the difference matters

A key lesson from Prologos's BSP-LE Track 2B (documented in .claude/rules/structural-thinking.md): a direct-sum decomposition $R = C_1 \oplus C_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus C_n$ is an algebraic fact, but its realisation on a propagator network is a design choice with two options:

Definition 15.4 — The two realisations

Realisation A — separate cells with bridge morphisms. Each $C_i$ is its own cell; bridges between them are Galois-connection morphisms. Worldview-filtered reads during speculation collapse tagged entries on the bridge — the bridge sees only the merged value at the current worldview, losing per-branch identity.

Realisation B — bitmask-tagged layers on a shared carrier cell. One cell holds all components; each component's identity is a bitmask tag in the value's tagged entries. Reconciliation is automatic through the cell's merge function. No bridges, no tag collapse.

Heuristic — which realisation to choose

When designing "bridges between X cells," always ask: is there a shared-carrier-with-tagging realisation? For value-level decomposition with worldview semantics, Realisation B is structurally simpler AND eliminates the tag-collapse bug class. Bridges are the right answer only when the $C_i$ carry genuinely different types or live at different strata.

MODULE 16 The Hyperlattice Conjecture & the Design Mantra

Every computable function is a fixpoint on lattices. The Hasse diagram of the lattice IS the optimal parallel decomposition of the computation.
Conjecture 16.1 — The Hyperlattice Conjecture

Two claims, taken together:

  1. Universality. Every computable function is expressible as a fixpoint computation on a (product of) complete lattice(s).
  2. Optimality. The Hasse diagram of the lattice provides the optimal parallel decomposition of that computation — independent sub-lattices run concurrently; diameter bounds BSP rounds; subcubes identify prunable subgraphs in $O(1)$.

Equivalently: the computation's parallel structure is not a design choice — it is a structural property of the lattice.

Why it's not a metaphor

The universality claim is classical: Knaster–Tarski (Module 6) shows that every monotone function on a complete lattice has a least fixpoint; Scott–Strachey denotational semantics builds programs' meanings this way; CALM (Module 5) tells us which computations can run without coordination. The optimality claim is the novel part: the hypercube $Q_n$ (Module 8) IS the ATMS worldview space, and its adjacency structure directly yields Gray code traversal, subcube pruning, and hypercube all-reduce. The parallel structure falls out of the lattice topology; no scheduler heuristics needed.

The Design Mantra — operational form of the conjecture

If the Hyperlattice Conjecture is true, then every line of Prologos code must obey five simultaneous demands. Compressed into one sentence:

The Design Mantra

"All-at-once, all in parallel, structurally emergent information flow ON-NETWORK."

Each word is a separate challenge:

  • All-at-once. Is this processing N independent items sequentially? If item $i$ doesn't depend on item $j$, they must not be sequenced. for/fold over independent items is step-think. Broadcast is the answer.
  • All in parallel. Is there imposed ordering? BSP fires everything in a round simultaneously. Ordering must emerge from dataflow depth, not from installation sequence or imperative dispatch.
  • Structurally emergent. Does the computation's shape fall out of the lattice topology? Or is imperative control flow deciding what happens when?
  • Information flow. Do values move through cells via propagators? Or through return values, parameters, for/fold accumulators?
  • ON-NETWORK. Is this a cell with a monotone merge? Or is it a parameter, a struct field, a hasheq threaded through a loop? Off-network state is debt against self-hosting.

MODULE 17 The SRE Lattice Lens — Six Questions for Every Cell

The working checklist Prologos designers run before any lattice goes on-network.

The SRE Lattice Lens is a diagnostic protocol. Any design that introduces or references a lattice must be analysed through six questions before it is accepted. The lens is codified in .claude/rules/structural-thinking.md.

The six questions

Q1 — Classification: VALUE or STRUCTURAL?

VALUE lattice: the cell holds a single evolving value (type, constraint, answer set). Information refines monotonically.
STRUCTURAL lattice: the cell holds a compound structure where components evolve independently (scope-cell, decisions-state, commitments-state). The lattice is a product of component lattices.
Why it matters: STRUCTURAL cells require component-path indexing; propagators watching them must declare #:component-paths, otherwise they fire on every component change and thrash the scheduler.

Q2 — Algebraic properties

What algebraic structure does the lattice have? Boolean? Distributive? Heyting? Quantale? Plain join-semilattice (CALM-safe, coordination-free)?
Most Prologos lattices are join-semilattices by design, to inherit CALM's coordination-freeness. Where the lattice is Boolean (worldview $Q_n$), subcube pruning and Gray-code traversal become available.

Q3 — Bridges to other lattices

How does this lattice compose with others? Every bridge between lattices must be a Galois connection. Left adjoint preserves joins; right adjoint preserves meets. If you can't produce the pair $(\alpha, \gamma)$ with $\alpha(c) \leq a \iff c \leq \gamma(a)$, the bridge is not well-typed.
Bridges define information flow paths between cells: decision cells → worldview cache (projection); table cell → consumer query (filter+project); scope cell → table entry (identity).

Q4 — Composition: full bridge diagram

Draw the full bridge diagram showing ALL lattices in the design and ALL bridges between them. Composition of bridges must be type-correct (Galois connections compose — Theorem 7.2).
This is where per-lattice analysis falls apart; only the full diagram can reveal that a "primary" cell is actually being recomputed from derived cells, or that a "small" bridge is actually a bottleneck for every round.

Q5 — Primary vs derived

Which lattice is PRIMARY (the authoritative source) and which is DERIVED (a projection / cache)?
Decision cells are PRIMARY → worldview cache is DERIVED. Table cells are PRIMARY → consumer results are DERIVED. Scope cells are PRIMARY → table entries are copies.
Why it matters: BSP-LE Track 2 discovered that the worldview lattice was treated as primary when decision cells were actually primary — this mistake is invisible without the lens.

Q6 — Hasse diagram: the optimality argument

What is the Hasse diagram of this lattice? It reveals: (a) adjacency (which elements are one step apart, determining optimal traversal order); (b) recursive decomposition (how the lattice factors into sub-lattices — the parallel decomposition); (c) diameter (maximum distance, bounding BSP rounds); (d) subcube structure (for Boolean lattices, nogoods identify subcubes prunable in $O(1)$).
The Hasse diagram IS the computation's parallel structure. This is the strongest claim of the lens — directly from the Hyperlattice Conjecture.
