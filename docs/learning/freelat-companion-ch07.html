Finite Intervals and Connected Components

A complete combinatorial picture of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s local structure. Theorem 7.2: chains of covers have at most 5 elements (in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$) or 4 elements (in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 4$); not at the very top or bottom, at most 3 elements. Theorem 7.5: only 6 finite-lattice forms appear as intervals. Theorem 7.10: every 3-element interval has a specific construction (Theorem 7.9). Theorem 7.32: the connected components of the cover relation are classified into exactly 4 types — 4-element Boolean, the connected-component-of-0 / -1, the labeled pentagon-cluster $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$, or the "chain bouquet" $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$.

~135–165 min read 4 sections · 33 named results 5 connection panels Book pp. 151–169 · PDF pp. 160–178

A natural question which had been around for some time is whether there exist large finite intervals, or at least long chains of covers, in finitely generated free lattices. It turns out that the longest possible chain of covers in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has five elements and, in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $|X| \ge 4$, only four elements. Moreover, in Section 1 we shall prove that for any $X$, each chain of covers not at the very bottom or the very top of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains at most three elements.

Using this we will be able to show that finite intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ cannot be large. In Section 2 we will see that the only finite intervals are those at the bottom or the top, which can be described easily, and chains with at most three elements. We also show that if an interval contains a maximal three element chain, then the interval either is just this chain or has a very specific form resembling a pentagon. There is an easy criterion to determine which case holds.

The three element intervals will be discussed in Section 3. Although there are infinitely many in each $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $X$ finite and $|X| \ge 3$, a detailed description of all such intervals can be given. This description is very useful as we shall see in the later chapters.

The last section characterizes the ordered sets that are connected components (with respect to the covering relation) of a free lattice.

Most of the results of the first three sections of this chapter were obtained by Freese and Nation in [62], and those of Section 4 by Freese in [51].

— Freese, Ježek & Nation, opening of Chapter VII (paraphrased)

Chapter VII is the structural-classification chapter for $\mathbf{FL}(X)$'s local Hasse-diagram fragments. Where Chapters III-IV-VI gave the toolkit (cover algorithm, rank refinement, totally-atomic finiteness), Chapter VII applies the toolkit to deliver four classification theorems:

(1) Cover-chain length is bounded. Theorem 7.2 says any chain of covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has length at most 5 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, or 4 in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 4$. Away from the top/bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$: at most 3 elements per chain. (2) Finite intervals come in 6 forms only. Theorem 7.5 lists exactly 6 finite lattices that can appear as intervals: the 8-element diamond at the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, the 7-element variant for the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, three 4-element intervals, the 3-element chain, and the 2-element chain. (3) Three-element intervals are infinite in number but have a specific construction. Theorem 7.9 gives a constructive recipe; Theorem 7.10 shows every 3-element interval arises this way. Corollary 7.14: $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals. (4) Connected components of the cover relation classify into 4 types. Theorem 7.32: every connected component (other than 4-element Boolean and the connected-component-of-0/1) is either a labeled pentagon-cluster $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ or a "chain bouquet" $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$.

The chapter's structural arc relies on Chapter VI's totally-atomic-element machinery. Theorem 6.27 (κ(w)* not completely meet irreducible) prevents long cover-chain extensions; Corollary 6.11 (finitely many totally atomic) provides the search-space finiteness needed to enumerate finite-interval forms; Theorem 6.24 (cover-witnesses are totally atomic) constrains where covers can occur. Without Chapter VI's toolkit, none of Chapter VII's classifications would be effective.

New · Chapter VII vocabulary

Five clusters of new vocabulary. Chains of covers: a chain $a_0 \prec a_1 \prec \cdots \prec a_n$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The "length" is $n$ (number of cover relations). Theorem 7.2 bounds length: ≤ 4 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (5 elements), ≤ 3 in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 4$ (4 elements), ≤ 2 anywhere except connected components of $0, 1$ (3 elements). Finite intervals (§2): intervals $w/u$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that are finite as lattices. The 6 forms (Figure 7.3): (1) the connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — the 11-element diamond from Example 3.44; (2) a 7-element variant occurring only in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$; (3) the 4-element diamond ($\mathbf{2}^2$); (4) a 4-element variant; (5) the 3-element chain; (6) the 2-element chain. Maximal 3-element chain in an interval (§2): a 3-element subinterval $u \prec v \prec w$ with no other elements between. Theorem 7.6 gives a structural test for when $w/u = \{u, v, w\}$. Three-element interval construction (§3): Theorem 7.9 — given a meet-irreducible totally atomic $q = q_1 \vee \cdots \vee q_m$ canonically, and a finite collection $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ of completely-join-irreducibles satisfying 3 conditions, the element $w = q \wedge (\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k)$ is the middle of a 3-element interval $\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k / w_*$. Labeled ordered set + interpretations (§4): a poset where each cover-pair has a label $\in \{0, 1\}$. Label 1 = covers in the free lattice; label 0 = noncovers in the free lattice (drawn as dotted lines). An interpretation of a labeled ordered set $P$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is an order isomorphism $f$ of $P$ onto a connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $f(a)$ is covered by $f(b)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ iff $a, b$ are a covering pair labeled 1 in $P$. $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ and $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ (§4): two infinite families of labeled ordered sets. $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ = $k$ copies of the labeled pentagon $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$, with all least elements identified and all middle elements identified. $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ = $m$ chains of length 2 plus $k$ chains of length 1, with their greatest elements identified, all labels 1. Theorem 7.32: connected components are exactly 4-element Boolean OR connected-component-of-0/1 OR $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ OR $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ (or duals).

Deep dive · the threads worth tracing

Four threads reward close reading. Theorem 7.2's structural mechanism. The proof is a case analysis on completely-join/meet-irreducibility of consecutive elements in a cover chain $t \prec u \prec v \prec w$. The key observation: if $v$ is completely meet irreducible, the unique canonical joinand $v_1$ of $v$ not below $u$ must be totally atomic (Theorem 6.24). Then $v_{1*}$ is below $u$, so $v_{1*} \le u_* = t$. Similarly $u_1^* \ge w$. The lattice diagram (Figure 7.1) shows this. The element $u_1$ is totally atomic, and $\kappa(v_1) = u$ has a lower cover, so by Lemma 6.25, $v_1 \in X^\wedge$ — i.e., $v_1$ is a meet of generators. Lemma 7.1 then forces the chain into the connected component of $0$ or $1$. Theorem 7.5's 6-form classification. The 6 forms are: the 11-element diamond at the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (= connected component of $0$ from Example 3.44); a 7-element variant; three 4-element intervals; the 3-element chain; the 2-element chain. The 11-element diamond and 7-element variant only occur at the very top/bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. The other forms can occur anywhere in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ but are bounded in size to ≤ 4 elements. 3-element intervals in detail. Theorem 7.9 constructs them from a totally atomic meet-irreducible $q$ and a finite cover-collection $p_1, \ldots, p_k$. Theorem 7.10 shows every 3-element interval arises this way. Examples 7.11, 7.12, 7.13 give specific infinite families. Corollary 7.14: $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals — a paradoxical-sounding "infinitely many small structures" result. Connected-component classification (Theorem 7.32). A connected component of the covering relation in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — modulo the connected components of 0 and 1 — is a labeled ordered set with cover labels indicating "actual cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$" vs "non-cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (despite Hasse-diagram adjacency)". Theorem 7.32 says these are exactly: 4-element Boolean OR $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ for some $k \ge 1$ OR $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ for some $\langle m, k\rangle \neq \langle 1, 0\rangle, \langle 2, 0\rangle$. So the local structure of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is FINITELY DESCRIBABLE: only countably many isomorphism types of connected components.

PART A · CHAINS OF COVERS — §1

Theorem 7.2: chains of covers have at most 5 elements (FL(3)) or 4 elements (FL(n), n≥4); at most 3 not at top/bottom

The chapter's first structural bound: cover chains in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ are short. The proof uses Chapter VI's Theorems 6.24, 6.25, 6.27 to constrain extensions of cover chains.

§ 1 Chains of Covers

Lemma 7.1 + Theorem 7.2: cover-chain length is bounded. Connected component of $0$ has chains of length 4 in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$, length 3 in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 4$. Outside the top/bottom: at most 3 elements (length 2).

The setup lemma (book p. 151)

Lemma 7.1 · canonical-joinand-of-meetand structure

Let $X$ be a finite set and let $Y$ be a nonempty, proper subset of $X$. In $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, let $a = \bigwedge Y$ and $b = \bigvee X - Y$. Suppose that $a$ is a canonical joinand of a completely meet irreducible element $v$ and $b$ is a canonical meetand of a completely join irreducible element $u$. If $v^* \le u_*$, then either $a \in X$ and $v$ is in the connected component of $1$, or $b \in X$ and $u$ is in the connected component of $0$.

Lemma 7.1 is the structural setup. It says: when an interval $u_*/v^*$ is "wide" (an entire structural span), the corresponding $a, b$ must be generators, forcing $v, u$ into the canonical "connected component of 0 or 1". This is what bounds cover chains away from the connectivity centers.

The chains in connected components of 0 and 1 (book p. 152)

Our description of the connected component of $0$ (Example 3.45) shows that if $X$ is a finite set with at least three elements, then $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains a chain of three covers, and if $|X| = 3$, it contains a chain of four covers. In more detail, if $X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ and $n = |X| \ge 4$, then all the chains of three covers beginning with $0$ are of the form

$$0 \prec \underline{x_i} \prec \underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j} \prec \bigwedge_{k \ne i, j}(\underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j} \vee \underline{x_k})$$

for $i \ne j \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$, where $\underline{x_i} = \bigwedge_{l \ne i} x_l$. If $n = 3$, then the chains of four covers beginning with $0$ are

$$0 \prec x_i \wedge x_j \prec (x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \prec x_i \wedge ((x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \vee (x_j \wedge x_k)) \prec (x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \vee (x_j \wedge x_k)$$

for a permutation $i, j, k$ of $1, 2, 3$. Of course, there are also the dual chains of three or four covers at the top of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, ending with $1$. Moreover, none of these chains is contained in a longer chain of covers.

Theorem 7.2 — the chain-length bound (book p. 152)

Theorem 7.2 · chains of covers are bounded

In a free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, each chain of at least three covers is either a subchain of one of the chains (1) (in the case $|X| \ge 4$) or (2) (in the case $|X| = 3$) or a subchain of the dual of one of these chains.

Intuition · why cover chains are short

The proof is a cascade. Suppose $t \prec u \prec v \prec w$ is a chain of three covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Cases:

  1. $v$ completely meet irreducible AND $u$ completely join irreducible. By Theorem 6.24, the unique canonical joinand $v_1$ of $v$ not below $u$ is totally atomic; dually, the unique canonical meetand $u_1$ of $u$ not above $v$ is totally atomic. The element $v_{1*}$ is below $u$ (otherwise $v = u \vee v_{1*}$ would not be a canonical joinand). So $v_{1*} \le u_* = t$. Similarly $u_1^* \ge w$ (Figure 7.1). Now $u_1$ is totally atomic, $\kappa(v_1) = u$ has a lower cover, so by Lemma 6.25, $v_1 \in X^\wedge$ — i.e., $v_1$ is a meet of generators. Lemma 7.1 then places $v$ in the connected component of $0$ or $1$.
  2. Other cases (Corollary 6.28). If $v$ is completely meet irreducible, $u$ is meet reducible hence completely join irreducible (so we're in Case 1). Dually if $u$ is completely join irreducible. So we may assume $u$ is completely meet irreducible AND $v$ is completely join irreducible. Now $\kappa(v) = v_*$ — but in Chapter VIII a completely join irreducible $v$ with $\kappa(v) = v_*$ is called singular, and Theorem 8.6 will characterize them as occurring only at the very top and bottom. Combined with Lemma 7.1 (Case 1), this places the chain in the connected component of 0 or 1.

Either way, the chain of three covers must be a subchain of the (1) or (2) chains described above. $\square$

Footnote: in Chapter VIII we will define a completely join irreducible element $v$ to be singular if $\kappa(v) = v_*$, and characterize these elements in Theorem 8.6. Since that characterization depends on the result we are proving now, we cannot use it here.

Connection P2 · Theorem 7.2's structural mechanism

Theorem 7.2 is the chapter's central structural bound. Its proof reveals deep machinery from Chapter VI:

(1) Theorem 6.24 forces totally-atomic cover-witnesses. A cover $v \succ u$ where $v$ is completely meet irreducible has a totally-atomic canonical joinand $v_1$ — a structural fingerprint that propagates through the chain.

(2) Theorem 6.27 prevents extension. $\kappa(w)^* = w \vee \kappa(w)$ is not completely meet irreducible. So the chain cannot continue past $u_1^*$.

(3) Lemma 6.25 forces canonical-form structure. If $\kappa(v_1)$ has a lower cover (which it does, since $\kappa(v_1) = u$ has lower cover $u_* = t$), and $v_1$ is totally atomic, then $v_1 \in X^\wedge$ — meet of generators.

(4) Lemma 7.1 places the chain in the component of 0 or 1. The "wide span" condition $a \in X^\wedge$, $b \in X^\vee$ forces the elements to live in the canonical bottom/top connected components.

Combining: any chain of 3 covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ must lie in the connected component of 0 or 1. The chains in those components are explicitly enumerated (formulas (1) and (2) above). So the maximum chain length is bounded by the structure of those specific components.

Concrete bounds:

  • $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ — chains of 4 covers (5 elements) at the bottom, 4 covers at the top. Total maximum chain length: 5 elements, in the very bottom or top.
  • $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 4$ — chains of 3 covers (4 elements) at the bottom and top. Maximum chain length: 4 elements.
  • Outside the connected components of 0 and 1 — at most 2 covers (3 elements). The "interior" of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has very short cover chains.

This is structurally surprising. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is infinite and "wide" (uncountable in many cardinality senses) but its cover-relation graph has very short edges. The Hasse diagram is "flat" — many elements per layer, few cover-relations between layers.

§ 2 Finite Intervals

Theorem 7.3 — uniqueness of meet-irreducible lower cover (book p. 154)

Theorem 7.3 · uniqueness of meet-irreducible lower cover

Any element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ other than $1$ has at most one meet irreducible lower cover.

The proof: the upper cover $x^*$ of a generator has its other lower cover $x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ meet reducible, so we are really concerned with the possibility of two join reducible lower covers. Let $w$ be an element with two distinct join reducible lower covers $u$ and $v$. By Theorem 3.5(1), the unique canonical joinand $v_1$ of $w$ such that $v_1 \not\le u$ is completely join irreducible, and we have $v_1 < v$ and $u = \kappa(v_1)$. Similarly, the unique canonical joinand $u_1$ of $w$ such that $u_1 \not\le v$ is completely join irreducible, $u_1 < u$ and $v = \kappa(u_1)$. Since $w = u^* = v^*$, two applications of the dual of Theorem 3.34 tell us that $u_1$ is a canonical joinand of $u$ and $v_1$ is a canonical joinand of $v$. Now by Theorem 3.5(2), $u$ has a lower cover $t$ such that $u_1 \not\le t$, viz., $t = u \wedge \kappa(u_1) = u \wedge v$. Symmetrically, $v \succ u \wedge v = t$. By Theorem 6.24, $u_1$ is the unique canonical joinand of $u$ such that $u_1 \not\le t$. So $u_1$ is totally atomic. Similarly $v_1$ is totally atomic. Since $u_1$ is totally atomic and the element $\kappa(u_1) = v$ has a lower cover, it follows from Theorem 6.25 that the totally atomic canonical joinand $v_1$ of $\kappa(u_1)$ is a generator. Similarly $u_1$ is a generator and the elements $u, v$ are both coatoms. We get $w = 1$. $\square$

Theorem 7.4 — finite intervals not at top/bottom have ≤ 3 elements (book p. 155)

Theorem 7.4 · finite intervals away from top/bottom

Let $w/u$ be a finite interval of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ not contained in the connected component of either $0$ or $1$. Then $w/u$ is a chain of at most three elements.

The proof: by Theorem 7.2, $w/u$ is a lattice of height at most 2. Since $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is semidistributive, the five-element lattice of height 2, $\mathbf{M}_3$, is not a sublattice of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Hence to prove the theorem it remains only to derive a contradiction from the assumption that $w/u$ is a four-element interval, $w/u = \{u, v, t, w\}$, $u \prec v \prec w$ and $u \prec t \prec w$. By the description of the connected component of a generator in Example 3.43, none of the four elements is a generator, and without loss of generality we can assume that $v$ is meet reducible. By the dual of Theorem 7.3, $t$ is join reducible. Clearly, $t = \kappa(v)$. Let $v_1$ be the canonical meetand of $v$ not above $w$. It is easy to see that $\kappa^d(v_1) < t$ and $\kappa^d(v_1) \vee u = t$. Because the lower cover of $\kappa^d(v_1)$ lies below $u$, it follows that $\kappa^d(v_1)$ is a canonical joinand of $t$. In fact, $\kappa^d(v_1)$ is the only canonical joinand of $t$ not below $u$. By Theorem 6.24, $\kappa^d(v_1)$ is totally atomic. Similarly, $v_1$ is totally atomic. By the dual of Theorem 6.25 we get $v_1 \in X^\vee$. So there exists a nonempty proper subset $Y$ of $X$ such that $v_1 = \bigvee Y$ and $\kappa^d(v_1) = \bigwedge(X - Y)$. Now the Theorem follows from Lemma 7.1. $\square$

Theorem 7.5 — the 6-form classification (book p. 155)

Theorem 7.5 · the 6 finite-interval forms

If $\mathbf{L}$ is a finite interval in a free lattice, then either $\mathbf{L}$ is the one element lattice or it is isomorphic to one of the lattices of Figure 7.3 or the dual of one of these lattices. Moreover:

  1. The first lattice is the connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ and does not occur as an interval in any other free lattice. Nor does it occur anywhere else in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.
  2. The second lattice only occurs as an interval in the connected component of $0$ or of $1$ in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.
  3. The third lattice occurs as an interval in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for each $n \ge 3$, but its least element must be the $0$ of $\mathbf{FL}(n)$.
  4. The fourth lattice occurs as an interval in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$, for $n \ge 3$, only when it is a subinterval of an interval isomorphic to the third lattice or its dual.
  5. The three element chain occurs as an interval infinitely often in each $\mathbf{FL}(n)$, with $n \ge 3$, but there are infinite intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ which do not contain it as a subinterval.
  6. The two element chain is a subinterval of every nontrivial interval.
Intuition · the 6 forms in detail

Figure 7.3 of the monograph shows six lattices. From the description:

(1) The 11-element bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. Example 3.44 — the connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$. Top, bottom, three atoms, three pairwise joins, and three further elements making up the diamond-with-four-coatoms shape.

(2) A 7-element variant. A subinterval of (1), occurring only in the bottom or top connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$.

(3) A 4-element interval. Occurs at the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for any $n \ge 3$ — specifically as $0 \prec \underline{x_i} \prec \underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j} \prec \bigwedge_{k \ne i, j}(\underline{x_i} \vee \underline{x_j} \vee \underline{x_k})$ from Example 3.45.

(4) Another 4-element variant. Restricted to subintervals of (3) or its dual.

(5) The 3-element chain. Infinitely many in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ for $n \ge 3$ (Theorem 7.10 + Corollary 7.14).

(6) The 2-element chain. Day's theorem: every nontrivial interval contains a covering pair.

The taxonomy is striking. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is infinite and complex, yet finite-interval-as-lattice can only be one of these 6 forms (up to duality). The "interesting" intervals are confined to the bottom/top — at the structural extremes. The interior is filled with very small intervals (mostly 3-element chains).

Theorem 7.6 — structural test for 3-element interval (book p. 156)

Theorem 7.6 · 3-element-interval structural characterization

Let $u, v, w$ be three elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $u \prec v \prec w$.

  1. Either $w/u = \{u, v, w\}$ or there exist two elements $r, s$ such that $u \prec r \le s \prec w$ and the interval $w/u$ is the disjoint union $\{u, v, w\} \cup (s/r)$. Moreover, in the latter case, $s/r$ is an infinite interval unless $v$ is an atom or a coatom, in which case $r = s$.
  2. Suppose $v$ is join irreducible, and let $v_1$ be the unique canonical meetand of $v$ not above $w$. We have $w/u = \{u, v, w\}$ if and only if $\kappa^d(v_1) \not\le \kappa(v)$.
  3. If $w/u \ne \{u, v, w\}$, then $s = w \wedge \kappa(v)$ and $r = u \vee \kappa^d(v_1)$.
Corollary 7.7 · structural form for q* ≻ q ≻ p

Suppose $q$ is a completely meet irreducible element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ and $q^* \succ q \succ p$. Let $q_1$ be the canonical joinand of $q$ not below $p$, and suppose $\kappa^d(q) \le \kappa(q_1)$. If $q$ is not a coatom, then the only completely meet irreducible element of $q^*/p$ is $q$.

PART C · THREE-ELEMENT INTERVALS — §3

Theorems 7.9, 7.10: a constructive recipe for 3-element intervals

Theorem 7.9 constructs 3-element intervals from a totally atomic meet-irreducible $q$ and a finite collection of completely-join-irreducibles. Theorem 7.10: every 3-element interval arises this way. Examples 7.11-7.13 give explicit infinite families. Corollary 7.14: $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals.

§ 3 Three Element Intervals

Although there are infinitely many 3-element intervals in each $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $|X| \ge 3$, a detailed description of all such intervals can be given. This description is very useful in later chapters.

Lemma 7.8 + Theorem 7.9 — the construction (book p. 157)

Lemma 7.8 · meet-irreducible totally atomic + canonical joinand below κ^d

If a canonical joinand of a meet irreducible totally atomic element $q \in \mathbf{FL}(X)$ is below $\kappa^d(q)$, then $q < \kappa^d(q)^*$. (Proof: direct consequence of the dual of Corollary 6.12.)

Theorem 7.9 · constructive recipe for 3-element intervals

Let $q$ be a meet irreducible totally atomic element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, $q = q_1 \vee \cdots \vee q_m$ canonically. Let $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ ($k \ge 1$) be a finite collection of completely join irreducible elements satisfying the following three conditions:

  1. $\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k$ is in canonical form;
  2. $q_2 \vee \cdots \vee q_m \le p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k < q$;
  3. If $k = 1$, then $q \wedge (\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1) > p_1$.

Then the element

$$w = q \wedge (\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k)$$

is in canonical form, is completely join irreducible, and is the middle element of the three element interval $\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k / w_*$.

Theorem 7.10 — every 3-element interval arises this way (book p. 158)

Theorem 7.10 · classification of 3-element intervals

Let a join irreducible element $w$ be the middle element of a three element interval $w_* \prec w \prec u$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. Denote by $q$ the canonical meetand of $w$ not above $u$. Let $q = q_1 \vee \cdots \vee q_m$ canonically with $q_1 \not\le w$, and let $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ be the canonical joinands of $u$ that lie below $w$. Then $q$ is totally atomic, $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ are completely join irreducible, and the three conditions (1), (2), and (3) of Theorem 7.9 are satisfied. Moreover:

  1. $\{q_2, \ldots, q_m\} \ll \{p_1, \ldots, p_k\}$.
  2. $u = q \wedge (\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee \cdots \vee p_k)$ canonically.
  3. $\kappa(w) < q$.

Examples and the infinitude theorem (book p. 160)

Example 7.11 · simplest construction

Let $q$ be a meet irreducible totally atomic element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that $q$ is not a generator and $q$ is not the join of two atoms. Let $q = q_1 \vee \cdots \vee q_m$ canonically. With the choice $\{p_1, \ldots, p_k\} = \{q_2, \ldots, q_m\}$, the conditions of Theorem 7.9 are always satisfied. Consequently, the element $q \wedge (\kappa^d(q) \vee q_2 \vee \cdots \vee q_m)$ is the middle element of a three element interval in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Example 7.12 · infinite family in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z, t\})$

Take $q = x \in X$ and $k = 1$. Define recursively:

  • $a_0 = z, b_0 = t$;
  • $a_{i+1} = z \wedge (b_i \vee (y \wedge z)), b_{i+1} = t \wedge (a_i \vee (y \wedge t))$;
  • $c_i = (a_i \vee (y \wedge t)) \wedge (b_i \vee (y \wedge z))$;
  • $p_i = \mu_x c_i$ where $\mu_x$ is the endomorphism of $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z, t\})$ sending each generator to its meet with $x$.

An inductive argument shows that each element $\underline{x} \vee p_i = (y \wedge z \wedge t) \vee p_i$ is in canonical form, and the algorithm in Theorem 3.32 can be used to prove that the elements $p_i$ are completely join irreducible. By Theorem 7.9, $x \wedge (\underline{x} \vee p_i)$ is the middle element of a 3-element interval in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z, t\})$. Conclusion: $\mathbf{FL}(4)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals.

Example 7.13 · infinite family in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$

Take $q = x$. Define $a_0 = x \wedge y$, $b_0 = x \wedge z$, and

$$a_{i+1} = \begin{cases} x \wedge (z \vee a_i) & i \text{ even},\\ x \wedge (y \vee a_i) & i \text{ odd}, \end{cases}$$ $$b_{i+1} = \begin{cases} x \wedge (y \vee b_i) & i \text{ even},\\ x \wedge (z \vee b_i) & i \text{ odd}. \end{cases}$$

By Example 3.40, the (slim) elements $a_i$ and $b_i$ are completely join irreducible. It is also easy to check that for any $i$, $(y \wedge z) \vee a_i \vee b_i$ is in canonical form. This verifies condition (1), while conditions (2) and (3) are both empty. By Theorem 7.9, for any $i$ the element

$$x \wedge ((y \wedge z) \vee a_i \vee b_i)$$

is the middle of a 3-element interval in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$. So $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ also has infinitely many 3-element intervals.

Corollary 7.14 · $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals

For any finite set $X$ of at least three elements, the free lattice $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ contains infinitely many 3-element intervals.

Connection P4 · the 3-element-interval explosion vs cover-chain finitude

Chapter VII delivers a striking structural contrast.

Side A: cover chains are SHORT. Theorem 7.2 says any chain of covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has at most 5 elements (in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$) or 4 elements (in $\mathbf{FL}(n)$, $n \ge 4$). Outside the connected components of 0 and 1: at most 3 elements. So in any "interior" region of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, going up by covers, you can only travel 2 cover-steps before stopping.

Side B: 3-element intervals are INFINITELY MANY. Corollary 7.14 says $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many 3-element intervals, EVEN for $|X| = 3$. The constructions in Examples 7.12, 7.13 build them recursively using slim elements (from Example 3.40 of Chapter III).

Reconciliation: SHORT chains, MANY of them. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has a "flat" Hasse-diagram structure: many elements per layer (uncountably many), but few cover-relations between layers. Each 3-element interval is a "local witness" — a cover-pair $u \prec v$ extended by a single more cover-pair $v \prec w$. There are infinitely many such patterns, all of bounded depth.

Implication for fine-structure analysis. The chapter's classification approach is:

  1. BOUND the local structure (Theorem 7.2: chain length).
  2. ENUMERATE the bounded forms (Theorem 7.5: 6 finite-interval forms).
  3. DESCRIBE the infinite-multiplicity case (Theorems 7.9-7.10: 3-element intervals).

This pattern — bound, enumerate, parametrize — is the chapter's structural template. Section 4 applies it to connected components.

Setup: the labeled-ordered-set framework (book pp. 160–161)

Definition · labeled ordered set, interpretation

A labeled ordered set is an ordered set, each covering pair of which is labeled with either a $1$ (indicating a covering in the free lattice) or a $0$ (indicating a noncover in the free lattice). In diagrams, a covering labeled $0$ will be indicated with a dotted line, while a covering labeled $1$ is indicated with a solid line.

An interpretation of a labeled ordered set $\mathbf{P}$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is an order isomorphism $f$ of $\mathbf{P}$ onto a connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ such that for any pair $a, b$ of elements of $\mathbf{P}$, $f(a)$ is covered by $f(b)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ if and only if $a, b$ is a covering pair labeled $1$ in $\mathbf{P}$.

The aim of §4 is to describe all the labeled ordered sets that have an interpretation in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ for some finite set $X$.

Why the labeled-ordered-set framework? In describing connected components of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$, we should not only indicate the order among the elements, but also which covers in the induced partial order of the connected component are actually covers in the free lattice. For example, the pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ from Example 3.43 has elements $x_*, x_* \vee \underline{x}, x, x^* \wedge \overline{x}, x^*$ with $x_* \prec x_* \vee \underline{x} < x^* \wedge \overline{x} \prec x^*$ — but the middle order-relation $x_* \vee \underline{x} < x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ is NOT a cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (despite being adjacent in the connected component's induced order). The label $0$ on this edge captures the non-covering.

The two infinite families (book pp. 161–163)

Definition · $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$ and $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$

The pentagon, with all covers labeled $1$ EXCEPT for the atom-to-coatom cover (as in Figure 7.5 — the cover between $x_* \vee \underline{x}$ and $x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ in $\mathbf{N}_5$), will be denoted by $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$. (For this purpose, the middle element of a pentagon is the only element which is both an atom and a coatom.) For example, $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(3)$ is pictured in Figure 7.6.

The ordered set is considered to be a labeled ordered set with all labels set to $1$. For example, $\mathbf{C}(2, 1)$ is pictured in Figure 7.7. Theorem 7.32 — the 4-form classification (book p. 169)

Theorem 7.32 · classification of connected components

A labeled ordered set $\mathbf{P}$ is representable as a connected component of a free lattice if and only if one of the following four cases, or one of their duals, occurs:

  1. $\mathbf{P}$ is the four element Boolean algebra, which occurs only as the (unique) connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(2)$.
  2. $\mathbf{P}$ is the connected component of $0$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ with $X$ finite and $|X| \ge 3$, and this representation of $\mathbf{P}$ is the only one. $\mathbf{P}$ has been described in Example 3.44 for $|X| = 3$, and in Example 3.45 for $|X| > 3$.
  3. $\mathbf{P}$ is $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ for some positive integer $k$.
  4. $\mathbf{P}$ is $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ for a pair of nonnegative integers $m$ and $k$ such that $\langle m, k\rangle \ne \langle 1, 0\rangle$ and $\langle m, k\rangle \ne \langle 2, 0\rangle$.
Corollary 7.33 · totally-atomic connected components

The connected component of a totally atomic element $q$ NOT belonging to the connected component of either $0$ or $1$ is always either $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$, if $q$ is completely join irreducible with $k$ canonical meetands, or the dual of $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$, when $q$ is completely meet irreducible with $k$ canonical joinands.

Proof of Corollary 7.33. The result is clear if $q$ is a generator. Otherwise its connected component must contain the configuration diagrammed in Figure 7.10(2), or its dual, and the result follows from Lemma 7.27(2). $\square$

Connection P3 · the labeled-ordered-set framework

The labeled-ordered-set framework (Definition before Example 7.15) is the chapter's technical innovation for §4. Why is it needed?

The discrepancy. A connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ is closed under the cover relation but is not a sublattice. Two elements can be order-comparable in the component's induced order BUT not in cover-relation. The 5-element pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ from Example 3.43 has $x_* \vee \underline{x} < x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ in induced order, but NOT in cover (there's NO covering pair between them in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$).

The label fix. Each cover-pair of the labeled poset gets a label: 1 = "actually covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$", 0 = "induced-order cover but not a cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$". The labeled poset captures BOTH the order structure AND the cover structure simultaneously.

Solid vs dotted lines in diagrams. Figure 7.5 (and others) draws labeled posets with solid lines for label 1 and dotted lines for label 0. The visual distinction is essential — without it, the two structurally distinct posets ($\mathbf{N}_5$ and $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$) would look identical in the Hasse diagram.

Combinatorial novelty. A footnote on book p. 161 notes: "A better term might be a covers labeled ordered set since combinatorists use the term labeled ordered set to mean an ordered set whose elements are labeled." The chapter's terminology (labeling COVERS, not ELEMENTS) is specific to the lattice-theoretic application.

Why the framework yields a clean classification. Theorem 7.32 is one of the cleanest classification theorems in the monograph: only 4 types of connected components (up to duality). Without the labeled framework, there would be more types (because $\mathbf{N}_5$ and $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$ would conflate). With the framework, the structural information is captured exactly.

Example VII.AThe longest cover chain in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ has 5 elements
Verify Theorem 7.2: the chain $0 \prec x_i \wedge x_j \prec (x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \prec x_i \wedge ((x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \vee (x_j \wedge x_k)) \prec (x_i \wedge x_j) \vee (x_i \wedge x_k) \vee (x_j \wedge x_k)$ has 5 elements (4 covers).

Setup. $X = \{x, y, z\}$. Take $i = 1, j = 2, k = 3$ — i.e., $x_1 = x, x_2 = y, x_3 = z$. The chain becomes:

  • $a_0 = 0$;
  • $a_1 = x \wedge y$;
  • $a_2 = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$;
  • $a_3 = x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z))$;
  • $a_4 = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$.

Verify each is a cover. By Example 3.45 of Chapter III (the bottom of $\mathbf{FL}(n)$ is fully described), each consecutive pair is a cover in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. Specifically, $a_0 \prec a_1$ since $a_1 = \underline{x_3} = \underline{z}$, an atom. $a_1 \prec a_2$: $a_1 = x \wedge y$, and $a_2 = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ — the "pairwise join of two atoms". By the bottom-of-$\mathbf{FL}$ analysis: $a_2$ covers $a_1$.

$a_2 \prec a_3$: more delicate, but by Example 3.45's description of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$'s bottom, this is a cover.

$a_3 \prec a_4$: where $a_3 = x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z))$ and $a_4 = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) \vee (y \wedge z)$. Since $a_3 \le a_4$ (the meet with $x$ in $a_3$ makes it smaller), and the structural analysis confirms this is a cover.

Verify the chain extends maximally. By Theorem 7.2, no chain of 5 covers exists in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. So this is at the maximum length. $\square$

Implication. $\mathbf{FL}(3)$'s structural depth in cover-relation terms is exactly 4 covers (5 elements) at the bottom, with the dual at the top. Outside these regions: at most 2 covers. The "interior" of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ is structurally flat in cover-graph terms.

(4) Another 4-element variant. A subinterval of (3) or its dual. Restricted to specific structural locations.

(5) The 3-element chain. $u \prec v \prec w$ — three elements in a chain. Theorem 7.10 + Corollary 7.14: $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ has infinitely many of these.

(6) The 2-element chain. $a \prec b$ — a single covering pair. By Day's theorem (Chapter II §7), every nontrivial interval contains one.

Together with their duals: (1)' = top of $\mathbf{FL}(3)$ (dual of (1)). (2)' = dual of (2). (3)' = dual chain at the top of $\mathbf{FL}(n)$. (4)' = dual of (4). (5), (6) self-dual.

So the FULL list of finite intervals (up to isomorphism): the 1-element trivial interval, plus the 6 forms of Figure 7.3, plus 4 duals = 1 + 6 + 4 = 11 isomorphism types of finite intervals.

Implication. $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — for ANY finite $X$ with $|X| \ge 3$ — has at most 11 isomorphism types of finite intervals. The structural classification is COMPLETE.

Example VII.CAn explicit 3-element interval from Example 7.13
Apply Theorem 7.9 with $q = x$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ to construct an explicit 3-element interval.

Setup. Take $q = x$. By Example 7.13's recursive construction:

  • $a_0 = x \wedge y$, $b_0 = x \wedge z$.
  • $a_1 = x \wedge (z \vee a_0) = x \wedge (z \vee (x \wedge y))$.
  • $b_1 = x \wedge (y \vee b_0) = x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$.
  • $a_2 = x \wedge (y \vee a_1)$, $b_2 = x \wedge (z \vee b_1)$.
  • ...

By Example 3.40, the slim elements $a_i$ and $b_i$ are completely join irreducible.

Apply Theorem 7.9 for $i = 1$. $q = x$, $m = 1$ (since $q$ is a generator), $q_1 = x$. Take $\{p_1, p_2\} = \{a_1, b_1\}$ (so $k = 2$).

Verify the conditions. $\kappa^d(q) = \kappa^d(x) = y \wedge z = \underline{x}$. So $\kappa^d(q) \vee p_1 \vee p_2 = (y \wedge z) \vee a_1 \vee b_1$.

Condition (1): $(y \wedge z) \vee a_1 \vee b_1$ in canonical form. Check: $a_1 = x \wedge (z \vee (x \wedge y))$ and $b_1 = x \wedge (y \vee (x \wedge z))$ are distinct slim elements. $y \wedge z$ is a meet of generators. The three are pairwise incomparable (by canonical form analysis on slim elements), so the join is canonical. ✓

Condition (2): $q_2 \vee \cdots \vee q_m = $ (empty since $m = 1$) $\le p_1 \vee p_2 < q$. The empty side is trivially satisfied. The strict inequality: is $a_1 \vee b_1 < x$? Yes — $a_1 \le x$ (since $a_1 = x \wedge \cdots$) and $b_1 \le x$ similarly, but $a_1 \vee b_1 \neq x$ (Whitman's algorithm checks). ✓

Condition (3): doesn't apply ($k = 2$, not $1$).

The 3-element interval. By Theorem 7.9, the element

$$w = x \wedge ((y \wedge z) \vee a_1 \vee b_1)$$

is the middle of a 3-element interval $((y \wedge z) \vee a_1 \vee b_1) / w_*$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$.

Implication. By varying $i$, we get infinitely many 3-element intervals in $\mathbf{FL}(3)$. This is Corollary 7.14 made concrete: the slim-element-based construction yields a one-parameter family.

Example VII.DThe labeled pentagon $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$ — explicit verification
Verify that the connected component of generator $x$ in $\mathbf{FL}(\{x, y, z\})$ — the pentagon $\mathbf{N}_5$ from Example 3.43 — is the labeled ordered set $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$ (NOT $\mathbf{N}_5$ with all labels 1).

The 5 elements (Example 3.43). $x_* = x \wedge \overline{x}, x_* \vee \underline{x}, x, x^* \wedge \overline{x}, x^* = x \vee \underline{x}$.

The covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. By Example 3.43:

  • $x_* \prec x \prec x^*$ — covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ (label 1).
  • $x_* \prec x_* \vee \underline{x}$ — cover (label 1).
  • $x^* \wedge \overline{x} \prec x^*$ — cover (label 1).

The induced-order edge $x_* \vee \underline{x} < x^* \wedge \overline{x}$. In the connected component's induced order, these two elements are comparable. Are they a COVER in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$? The strict inequality holds (as the component analysis shows). But for it to be a cover, no element of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ must lie strictly between.

Claim: $x_* \vee \underline{x}$ is NOT covered by $x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$. The interval $(x^* \wedge \overline{x}) / (x_* \vee \underline{x})$ contains other elements of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ — outside the pentagon component but still in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Specifically: by Theorem 7.6 + Theorem 7.4, the interval $(x^* \wedge \overline{x}) / (x_* \vee \underline{x})$ is INFINITE — not a finite interval — and contains many "interior" elements not connected by covers to the pentagon's boundary.

So the edge $x_* \vee \underline{x} < x^* \wedge \overline{x}$ in the pentagon's induced order is LABEL 0 (non-cover). Drawn dotted in Figure 7.5.

Conclusion. The connected component of $x$ is the labeled ordered set $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5$ — pentagon with all covers labeled 1 EXCEPT the atom-to-coatom internal edge (labeled 0). Per Theorem 7.32, this is type (3) of the classification — $\overline{\mathbf{N}}_5(k)$ for $k = 1$.

Example VII.E$\mathbf{C}(3, 2)$ — a chain bouquet realization
Apply Example 7.18 to construct an explicit interpretation of $\mathbf{C}(3, 2)$ in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ for sufficient $|X|$.

Setup. $\mathbf{C}(3, 2)$: 3 chains of length 2 plus 2 chains of length 1, all top-identified. Total elements: $3 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 1 + 1 = 9$ elements (with the top shared by all 5 chains). All labels are 1 (all edges are covers in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$).

Construction (Example 7.18). Let $X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_r, y_1, \ldots, y_k\}$ with $r \ge m$ and $r \ge 4$. For $\mathbf{C}(3, 2)$: $m = 3, k = 2$. Take $r = 4$ (minimum), so $X = \{x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4, y_1, y_2\}$ with $|X| = 6$.

Put $\hat{x}_i = \bigwedge_{l \neq i} x_l$ (atom of the $x$-restricted sublattice). $u_i = \bigvee_{l \neq i} x_l$ (coatom of the $x$-restricted sublattice). $v_j = \bigvee_{l \neq j} y_l$ (coatom of the $y$-restricted sublattice). $u = x_1 \vee \cdots \vee x_r = x_1 \vee x_2 \vee x_3 \vee x_4$ and $v = y_1 \vee \cdots \vee y_k = y_1 \vee y_2$.

The connected component $C$ of $a = \hat{x}_1 \vee \cdots \vee \hat{x}_m \vee y_1 \vee \cdots \vee y_k = \hat{x}_1 \vee \hat{x}_2 \vee \hat{x}_3 \vee y_1 \vee y_2$ is isomorphic to $\mathbf{C}(3, 2)$.

The 9 elements of $C$.

  • $a$ — the top, with 5 lower covers.
  • For $i = 1, 2, 3$ — the "x-side" branches: chains of length 2.
    • $a \wedge x_i$ — covered by $a$.
    • $a \wedge [\bigvee_{p=1}^k ((x_i \vee v) \wedge (u \vee v_p) \wedge (u_i \vee v)) \vee \bigvee_{q \ne i, q \le m}((x_i \vee v) \wedge (x_q \vee v))]$ — covered by $a \wedge x_i$.
  • For $j = 1, 2$ — the "y-side" branches: chains of length 1.
    • $a \wedge (u \vee v_j)$ — covered by $a$.

Total: 1 (top) + 3 (x-side mid) + 3 (x-side bottom) + 2 (y-side bottom) = 9 elements. ✓

All edges are covers (label 1). By Lemma 7.27 + Lemma 7.28, $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ has all edges labeled 1 — every induced-order cover IS a cover in $\mathbf{FL}(X)$.

Implication. The chain-bouquet $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ is a "rigid" structural pattern: every connected component of $\mathbf{FL}(X)$ that doesn't contain a pentagon and isn't 4-element Boolean or the connected component of 0/1 must be exactly $\mathbf{C}(m, k)$ for some $\langle m, k\rangle \neq \langle 1, 0\rangle, \langle 2, 0\rangle$.
