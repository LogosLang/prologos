Chapter Overview — the foundations, with character

Nation opens with a Bob Dylan quote and a Cayley-style program: define partial orders abstractly, then prove every poset is "really" a system of sets ordered by containment. Five movements through the foundational vocabulary, Dilworth's chain decomposition, the Axiom of Choice, the compactness theorem, Szpilrajn's extension theorem, and the dimension invariant.

Nation's Notes on Lattice Theory open with Chapter 1 on ordered sets — the foundational vocabulary that everything else uses. The chapter has more set-theoretic depth than a typical "preliminaries" chapter in a lattice book: it treats the Axiom of Choice with its five equivalent forms, proves the compactness theorem from propositional logic, and uses both to extend Dilworth's chain decomposition to the infinite case. The chapter ends with order dimension — a third structural invariant alongside width and chain covering number.

The chapter has five conceptual movements:

  1. Posets and order morphisms. Definition via reflexivity, antisymmetry, transitivity. Cayley-style program: prove every ordered set embeds in a power set ordered by containment. Theorem 1.1 delivers this. Order ideals, duals, principal ideals/filters. Maximum vs maximal — the load-bearing distinction.
  2. Chain conditions. DCC + induction. Lemma 1.2 gives the three-way equivalence (every nonempty subset has a minimal element ⟺ no infinite descending chain ⟺ descending sequences stabilise). Lemma 1.3 promotes DCC to a transfinite induction principle.
  3. Dilworth's theorem. Width $w(\mathcal{P})$, chain covering $c(\mathcal{P})$. Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth, 1950): in finite posets, $w = c$ — the maximum antichain equals the minimum chain cover. Proof via the Tverberg-Freese induction. Infinite case deferred to after the compactness theorem.
  4. Axiom of Choice + compactness. Theorem 1.5: AoC, Zermelo well-ordering, Hausdorff maximality, Zorn's Lemma, and "LUB-chain → maximal" are pairwise equivalent. Theorem 1.6 (Gödel 1930): a set of WFFs is satisfiable iff every finite subset is. Then Jónsson's elegant argument: the infinite Dilworth theorem follows from the finite one via compactness.
  5. Szpilrajn + dimension. Theorem 1.7 (Szpilrajn-Marczewski 1930): every partial order is the intersection of all total orders containing it. Theorem 1.9 introduces order dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$ (Dushnik-Miller 1941): smallest cardinality of a realizer (set of total orders whose intersection is $\le$). $d \le c$, and $d$ is also the smallest $\kappa$ such that $\mathcal{P}$ embeds in a product of $\kappa$ chains.

The chapter closes with three invariants $w(\mathcal{P})$, $c(\mathcal{P})$, $d(\mathcal{P})$ on ordered sets, and 15 exercises. The companion's worked-exercises section covers 13 of them in detail.

Style note. Nation's pace is faster than many lattice books — proofs are terse, examples are sketched. The companion expands. Where Nation says "this is straightforward," the companion writes out the verification.

Carried forward — Nation's notational conventions

Chapter 1 is foundational; nothing is carried forward from earlier chapters. The "Carried forward" subsection here documents Nation's notational conventions to flag where they differ from common alternatives (e.g., Călugăreanu).
Convention$\mathfrak{P}(X)$ — power set

Nation uses Fraktur P for the power set. Most texts use $\mathcal{P}(X)$ (calligraphic P) or $2^X$. The Călugăreanu companion uses $\mathcal{P}(M)$. Same concept, different glyph.

Convention$\downarrow x$, $\uparrow x$ — principal ideal/filter

$\downarrow x = \{y : y \le x\}$ (everything below $x$), $\uparrow x = \{y : y \ge x\}$ (everything above $x$). Călugăreanu writes $a/0$ for the principal ideal — same concept. Nation's notation is more standard in modern poset theory.

Convention$\mathcal{P}^d$ — dual poset

The same set with the order reversed: $x \le^d y$ iff $y \le x$. Călugăreanu writes $L^0$. Nation's $\mathcal{P}^d$ matches modern convention.

Convention$\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ — lattice of order ideals

The set of all order ideals of $\mathcal{P}$, ordered by inclusion. Călugăreanu writes $I(L)$. The two are interchangeable conceptually.

Convention$a \prec b$ — cover relation

$a < b$ AND no $c$ with $a < c < b$. Same as Călugăreanu, same as standard. The cover relation is the structural skeleton — Hasse diagrams draw exactly the cover edges.

Convention$\mathbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$ — subgroup lattice

Nation's notation for the lattice of subgroups of a group $\mathcal{G}$. Călugăreanu writes $L(G)$.

New in Chapter 1 — full term-cards

NewPartially ordered set (poset)

A pair $\mathcal{P} = (P, \le)$ where $P$ is nonempty and $\le$ satisfies (1) reflexivity: $x \le x$, (2) antisymmetry: $x \le y$ and $y \le x$ ⟹ $x = y$, (3) transitivity: $x \le y$ and $y \le z$ ⟹ $x \le z$. The starting object of order theory.

NewChain / antichain

$\mathcal{C}$ is a chain if every two elements are comparable (totally ordered subset). $\mathcal{A}$ is an antichain if every two distinct elements are incomparable. Chains are at one extreme of the order spectrum; antichains at the other.

NewOrder morphism / order-preserving map

$f : \mathcal{P} \to \mathcal{Q}$ such that $x \le y$ ⟹ $f(x) \le f(y)$. Nation says "order-preserving map"; Călugăreanu says "order morphism." Same concept. An isomorphism is a bijection $f$ with both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ order-preserving.

NewOrder ideal / order filter

$I \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is an order ideal if $x \le y \in I$ ⟹ $x \in I$ (downward-closed). $F$ is an order filter if $x \ge y \in F$ ⟹ $x \in F$ (upward-closed). $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ is the set of all order ideals, itself a complete lattice under inclusion.

NewMaximum / maximal · minimum / minimal

Maximum (greatest): $x$ such that $y \le x$ for all $y$. Maximal: no $y > x$. Maximum implies maximal; converse fails in posets with multiple maximals. Same distinction as Călugăreanu Ch 1.

NewDescending chain condition (DCC)

Equivalent forms (Lemma 1.2): (1) every nonempty $S \subseteq P$ has a minimal element; (2) no infinite descending chain $a_0 > a_1 > \dots$; (3) every descending sequence $a_0 \ge a_1 \ge \dots$ stabilises. The dual is the ascending chain condition (ACC).

NewWidth $w(\mathcal{P})$

$w(\mathcal{P}) = \sup\{|A| : A$ is an antichain$\}$. The largest cardinality of an antichain. Nation's footnote 1 notes that for posets of infinite width, the more refined invariant $\mu(\mathcal{P}) = \kappa$ where $\kappa$ is the least cardinal with $\kappa + 1 > |A|$ for every antichain — but the chapter restricts to $w(\mathcal{P})$.

NewChain covering number $c(\mathcal{P})$

The least cardinal $\gamma$ such that $P$ is the union of $\gamma$ chains. Always $w(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$ (no chain contains two distinct antichain elements). Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth): $w = c$ when $w$ is finite.

NewOrder dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$

(Dushnik-Miller 1941.) $d(\mathcal{P}) = $ smallest cardinal $\kappa$ such that $\le$ is the intersection of $\kappa$ total orders. Equivalently (Theorem 1.9(1)): the smallest $\kappa$ such that $\mathcal{P}$ embeds in a product of $\kappa$ chains. Always $d \le c$, but $d$ and $w$ are independent.

NewRealizer

A set $\{\le_i\}_{i \in I}$ of total orders on $P$ whose intersection equals $\le$. The dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$ is the smallest cardinality of a realizer.

NewSentence symbol / wff / truth assignment

From propositional logic. Sentence symbol: an atomic letter; carries no intrinsic meaning. Well-formed formula (wff): built from sentence symbols via $\neg, \text{AND}, \text{OR}$. Truth assignment: $\nu : S \to \{T, F\}$ on sentence symbols, extended canonically to all wffs. Used to state and prove Theorem 1.6.

NewSatisfiable / finitely satisfiable

$\Sigma$ is satisfiable if some truth assignment makes every wff in $\Sigma$ true. Finitely satisfiable: every finite subset of $\Sigma$ is satisfiable. Theorem 1.6 (compactness): the two are equivalent.

NewCompactness theorem (propositional)

Theorem 1.6: a set of WFFs is satisfiable iff every finite subset is. Equivalently: any propositional theory consistent at every finite stage is consistent overall. Due to Gödel (1930) for predicate logic; the propositional case is older and follows from Zorn.

NewQuasiorder

A reflexive, transitive binary relation (no antisymmetry required). Used in Exercise 12. Quasiorders factor canonically through their associated equivalence relation $x \equiv y$ iff $xRy$ and $yRx$, giving an honest partial order on the quotient.

Deep dive — extended explainers

Deep dive

The Cayley analogy — why Theorem 1.1 matters

In group theory, Cayley's representation theorem (1854) says that every group is isomorphic to a subgroup of a permutation group. Concretely: $\mathcal{G}$ embeds in $S_{|\mathcal{G}|}$ via the left-translation action $g \mapsto (h \mapsto gh)$. The theorem certifies that the abstract group axioms are "correct" — every model is essentially a group of symmetries.

Nation's Theorem 1.1 is the order-theoretic analogue. We define partial orders abstractly via reflexivity / antisymmetry / transitivity. Are these axioms "correct"? — i.e., does every poset $\mathcal{Q}$ "really come from" set containment? Theorem 1.1 says yes: the map $\phi(x) = \downarrow x = \{y : y \le x\}$ embeds $\mathcal{Q}$ in $\mathfrak{P}(\mathcal{Q})$ ordered by $\subseteq$. Posets ARE substructures of containment lattices — the abstract axioms are exactly the right ones.

This pattern (abstract definition + concrete representation theorem) recurs throughout lattice theory: Birkhoff–Frink for algebraic lattices, Whitman for general lattices via partition lattices, Stone for Boolean algebras, Birkhoff for distributive lattices via posets of join-irreducibles. Each gives a "Cayley-style" certification that the abstract axioms match the canonical concrete examples.

Deep dive

The five equivalent forms of the Axiom of Choice

Theorem 1.5 lists five pairwise-equivalent set-theoretic principles. They are NOT independent — proving any one assumes any other. The full proof is deferred to Appendix 2; here, sketch why they go together.

  • Axiom of Choice: a function $\phi : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to X$ with $\phi(A) \in A$ for nonempty $A$. The "every nonempty set has a chosen element" principle.
  • Zermelo well-ordering principle: every set admits a well-ordering (= a total order satisfying DCC). The strongest-feeling form: every set can be enumerated by ordinals.
  • Hausdorff maximality principle: every chain in a poset can be extended to a maximal chain. Often the most directly usable form.
  • Zorn's Lemma: every chain in a poset has an upper bound ⟹ the poset has a maximal element. The lattice-theoretic workhorse, used everywhere.
  • LUB-chain → maximal: every chain has a least upper bound ⟹ the poset has a maximal element. A slight strengthening of Zorn (LUB > upper bound), often easier to apply when joins exist.

All five are equivalent in ZF set theory but cannot be proved without some non-constructive assumption. Modern set theory accepts AoC as an axiom (giving ZFC), and lattice theory uses Zorn's Lemma routinely. In this companion's chapters that go through, Zorn appears in: maximal essential extensions, maximal independent sets, maximal critical-element families, and the standard "extend a chain to a maximal one" arguments.

Deep dive

Why the compactness theorem appears in a lattice chapter

The propositional compactness theorem (Theorem 1.6) feels out of place in a lattice book. Why is it here?

The motivation is Theorem 1.4 — Dilworth's theorem. Nation proves the FINITE case of Dilworth via a clean induction (Tverberg-Freese). For the INFINITE case, a direct argument is awkward: Zorn doesn't immediately give "the union of chains is a chain cover" because the chains can be infinite. Bjarni Jónsson's method (suggested in the chapter): apply the propositional compactness theorem.

The sentences are $c_{xi}$ for $x \in P$, $i \le k$, intuitively meaning "$x$ is in the $i$-th chain." Constraints: $c_{x1} \text{ OR } \dots \text{ OR } c_{xk}$ (every $x$ is in some chain) and $\neg(c_{xi} \text{ AND } c_{yi})$ for incomparable $x, y$ (no chain contains incomparable pairs). Finite Dilworth says every finite subtheory is satisfiable; compactness says the whole theory is satisfiable; the satisfying truth assignment IS the chain decomposition.

This pattern — solve a combinatorial problem in finite cases, lift to infinite via compactness — is a workhorse technique in combinatorics (König's infinity lemma, Tychonoff's theorem in topology, the De Bruijn–Erdős theorem in graph coloring). Lattice theory inherits it via this route.

Deep dive

The three invariants $w, c, d$

Nation closes Chapter 1 with three structural invariants on ordered sets:

InvariantDefinitionMeasures
$w(\mathcal{P})$ (width)sup of antichain sizes"how wide" the poset gets — incomparability
$c(\mathcal{P})$ (chain covering)min number of chains covering $P$"how many parallel paths" cover the poset
$d(\mathcal{P})$ (dimension)min size of total-order realizer"how many independent rankings" determine $\le$

Always $w \le c$ and $d \le c$ (Theorems 1.4 setup and 1.9(2)). But $w$ and $d$ are independent: there are posets of high dimension and low width (e.g., $\kappa \times \kappa^d$ for an ordinal $\kappa$, which has width $|\kappa|$ but dimension 2). And there are posets of high width and low dimension.

The three are the standard "small-cardinality structural fingerprint" of an ordered set: they classify posets up to a coarse equivalence. Order dimension was singled out by Dushnik & Miller in 1941; chain covering and width are central in Dilworth's combinatorial work.

A1 Posets, examples, chains, antichains, Hasse diagrams

Cayley-style program: define posets abstractly, prove a representation theorem. Examples: power sets, subgroup lattices, chains, antichains, the reals.
Definition · partially ordered set

A partially ordered set, or briefly ordered set, is a system $\mathcal{P} = (P, \le)$ where $P$ is a nonempty set and $\le$ is a binary relation on $P$ satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in P$:

  1. $x \le x$ (reflexivity)
  2. $x \le y$ and $y \le x$ ⟹ $x = y$ (antisymmetry)
  3. $x \le y$ and $y \le z$ ⟹ $x \le z$ (transitivity)
Examples

Power set $\mathfrak{P}(X)$. The most natural example: all subsets of $X$, ordered by $\subseteq$.

Subgroup lattice $\mathbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$. All subgroups of a group $\mathcal{G}$, ordered by set containment.

Real numbers $\mathfrak{R}$. A rather special poset: a totally ordered set, or chain.

Antichains. The opposite extreme: posets where $\le$ coincides with $=$.

More generally: any nonempty collection $Q$ of subsets of $X$ is itself an ordered set under containment. And for any ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ and subset $Q \subseteq P$, the restricted relation makes $\mathcal{Q}$ a new ordered set (a subposet).

Definition · cover relation, Hasse diagram

$x$ is covered by $y$ in $\mathcal{P}$, written $x \prec y$, if $x < y$ and there is no $z \in P$ with $x < z < y$. In a finite ordered set, the cover relation determines the partial order: $\le$ is the reflexive transitive closure of $\prec$.

A Hasse diagram represents a finite ordered set: elements are points in the plane, and a line is drawn from $a$ up to $b$ precisely when $a \prec b$. (Nation's footnote: "this description is not precise, but it is close enough for government purposes.")

Comparable, chain, antichain

$x, y \in P$ are comparable if $x \le y$ or $y \le x$. $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is a chain if every two elements are comparable. $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is an antichain if every two distinct elements are incomparable.

Intuition · the Cayley-style program

The chapter's project is summarized in the opening paragraph: "In group theory, groups are defined algebraically as a model of permutations. The Cayley representation theorem then shows that this model is 'correct': every group is isomorphic to a group of permutations. In the same way, we want to define a partial order to be an abstract model of set containment $\subseteq$, and then we should prove a representation theorem for partially ordered sets in terms of containment."

Theorem 1.1 will deliver this. The implicit message: the abstract poset axioms (R, A, T) are the right axioms — they generate exactly the structures that "really are" sets ordered by containment.

A2 Order morphisms · isomorphism · Theorem 1.1 (Cayley-style representation)

Maps that respect order. The order-preserving Cayley analogue: every poset embeds in its own power set ordered by containment.
Order-preserving maps · isomorphism

The natural maps in the category of ordered sets are order-preserving (= order morphisms): $f : \mathcal{P} \to \mathcal{Q}$ such that $x \le y$ implies $f(x) \le f(y)$.

$\mathcal{P}$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{Q}$, written $\mathcal{P} \cong \mathcal{Q}$, if there is a map $f : P \to Q$ which is one-to-one, onto, and both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ are order-preserving — i.e., $x \le y$ iff $f(x) \le f(y)$.

Theorem 1.1

Let $\mathcal{Q}$ be an ordered set, and let $\phi : Q \to \mathfrak{P}(Q)$ be defined by

$$\phi(x) = \{y \in Q : y \le x\}.$$

Then $\mathcal{Q}$ is isomorphic to the range of $\phi$ ordered by $\subseteq$.

Proof · why

If $x \le y$, then $z \le x$ implies $z \le y$ by transitivity, hence $\phi(x) \subseteq \phi(y)$. Since $x \in \phi(x)$ by reflexivity, $\phi(x) \subseteq \phi(y)$ implies $x \le y$. Thus $x \le y$ iff $\phi(x) \subseteq \phi(y)$. That $\phi$ is one-to-one then follows by antisymmetry. ∎

Intuition · what Theorem 1.1 does

Three points to absorb:

  1. Every poset embeds in a power set. Up to isomorphism, every poset is "really" a system of sets ordered by containment. The map sends each $x$ to its principal ideal $\downarrow x$.
  2. Reflexivity, antisymmetry, transitivity are the right axioms. Each poset axiom maps cleanly: reflexivity gives $x \in \phi(x)$; antisymmetry gives injectivity of $\phi$; transitivity gives the order-preserving property.
  3. The Cayley parallel. Cayley's theorem: every group embeds in $S_{|G|}$. Theorem 1.1: every poset embeds in $\mathfrak{P}(|P|)$.

The companion's later chapters will see analogous representation theorems: Birkhoff–Frink (Ch 3), Whitman (Ch 4), Birkhoff (Ch 8 — distributive lattices). All are "Cayley-style certifications." Theorem 1.1 is the prototype.

A3 Order ideals · duals · principal ideals/filters · max vs maximal

Sub-structures and maps to the dual poset. Principal ideals/filters as canonical examples. Maximum vs maximal — the Călugăreanu Ch 1 distinction restated.
Order ideal · $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$

A subset $I$ of $\mathcal{P}$ is an order ideal if $x \le y \in I$ implies $x \in I$. The set of all order ideals of $\mathcal{P}$ forms an ordered set $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ under set inclusion. The map $\phi$ of Theorem 1.1 embeds $\mathcal{Q}$ into $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{Q})$.

Property. The intersection of any collection of order ideals of $\mathcal{P}$ is again an order ideal (which may be empty). The union of order ideals is an order ideal.

Dual poset $\mathcal{P}^d$

Given $\mathcal{P} = (P, \le)$, the dual $\mathcal{P}^d = (P, \le^d)$ has $x \le^d y$ iff $y \le x$. In the finite case, the Hasse diagram of $\mathcal{P}^d$ is obtained by turning the Hasse diagram of $\mathcal{P}$ upside down.

Many concepts come in dual pairs, where one form is obtained from the other by replacing $\le$ with $\ge$ throughout.

Order filter

$F \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is an order filter if $x \ge y \in F$ implies $x \in F$. An order ideal of $\mathcal{P}$ is exactly an order filter of $\mathcal{P}^d$, and vice versa.

Principal ideal · principal filter

An ideal or filter determined by a single element is principal. We denote

$$\downarrow x = \{y \in P : y \le x\}, \qquad \uparrow x = \{y \in P : y \ge x\},$$

respectively, the principal ideal and principal filter of $x$.

Maximum / maximal · minimum / minimal

$\mathcal{P}$ has a maximum (or greatest) element if there exists $x \in P$ such that $y \le x$ for all $y \in P$. An element $x \in P$ is maximal if there is no $y \in P$ with $y > x$. Clearly these concepts are different. Minimum and minimal are defined dually.

Intuition · maximum vs maximal

The classic illustrative example: the poset $\{a, b\}$ with $a, b$ incomparable. Both $a$ and $b$ are maximal (nothing strictly above either). Neither is maximum (maximum requires being above everything; here $a \not\le b$ and $b \not\le a$).

Theorem 1.5's Zorn's Lemma asks for a maximal — not a maximum — element. The distinction is load-bearing: many posets have many maximal elements but no maximum.

B1 Lemmas 1.2, 1.3 — DCC, ACC, and induction

Three equivalent forms of DCC: every nonempty subset has a minimal; no infinite descending chain; every descending sequence stabilises. Lemma 1.3 promotes DCC to a transfinite induction principle.
Lemma 1.2 · the DCC equivalence

The following are equivalent for an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$.

  1. Every nonempty subset $S \subseteq P$ contains an element minimal in $S$.
  2. $\mathcal{P}$ contains no infinite descending chain $a_0 > a_1 > a_2 > \dots$.
  3. If $a_0 \ge a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \dots$ in $\mathcal{P}$, then there exists $k$ such that $a_n = a_k$ for all $n \ge k$ (i.e., the sequence stabilises).
Proof · why

The equivalence of (2) and (3) is clear, and (1) ⟹ (2) is immediate (an infinite descending chain has no minimal element).

The subtlety is (2) ⟹ (1). Suppose $\mathcal{P}$ fails (1) and that $S \subseteq P$ has no minimal element. To find an infinite descending chain in $S$, rather than just arbitrarily long finite ones, we must use the Axiom of Choice.

Let $f$ be a choice function on the subsets of $S$ — i.e., $f$ assigns to each nonempty $T \subseteq S$ an element $f(T) \in T$. Let $a_0 = f(S)$, and for each $i \in \omega$ define

$$a_{i+1} = f(\{s \in S : s < a_i\}).$$

The argument of $f$ is nonempty because $S$ has no minimal element. The sequence $a_0, a_1, a_2, \dots$ is an infinite descending chain in $S \subseteq P$, hence $\mathcal{P}$ fails (2). ∎

DCC, ACC

The conditions described by Lemma 1.2 are called the descending chain condition (DCC). The dual notion is the ascending chain condition (ACC): every nonempty subset has a maximal element, equivalently no infinite ascending chain, equivalently every ascending sequence stabilises.

These conditions should be familiar from ring theory (for ideals) and module theory (for submodules). They are foundational to the structure theory of noetherian / artinian objects.

Lemma 1.3 · induction principle

Let $\mathcal{P}$ be an ordered set satisfying the DCC. If $\varphi(x)$ is a statement such that

  1. $\varphi(x)$ holds for all minimal elements of $\mathcal{P}$, and
  2. whenever $\varphi(y)$ holds for all $y < x$, then $\varphi(x)$ holds,

then $\varphi(x)$ is true for every element of $\mathcal{P}$.

Proof · why

The proof is immediate. The contrapositive of (2) states that $F = \{x \in P : \varphi(x)$ is false$\}$ has no minimal element. Since $\mathcal{P}$ satisfies the DCC, $F$ must therefore be empty. ∎

Intuition · DCC as a transfinite induction principle

Lemma 1.3 is the poset-theoretic version of well-founded induction: any property that holds at minimal elements and propagates upward via $\varphi(y)$ for all $y < x \Rightarrow \varphi(x)$ holds for ALL elements. This generalises mathematical induction (where $\mathcal{P} = \mathbf{N}$ and the only minimal is $0$) and ordinal induction (where $\mathcal{P} = $ ordinals).

The condition (1) is technically a special case of (2) (when $x$ is minimal, the set $\{y : y < x\}$ is empty, and any universal statement over an empty set is vacuously true). It's stated separately because in practice minimal elements usually require a separate argument — like the case $n = 0$ in ordinary induction.

This induction principle is the workhorse for inductive proofs over arbitrary posets satisfying DCC: noetherian induction, strong induction over ordinals, and lattice-theoretic induction over chains all instantiate Lemma 1.3.

Part C · Width, chain cover, Dilworth's theorem

Three invariants begin · Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth, 1950)

The width $w(\mathcal{P})$ and chain covering number $c(\mathcal{P})$. Dilworth's classical theorem: $w = c$ in finite posets. Proof via the Tverberg-Freese induction; infinite case deferred.

C1 Width, chain cover, Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth)

$w(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$ in any poset. Equality when $w$ is finite — Dilworth's theorem.
Width $w(\mathcal{P})$

Define the width of an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ by

$$w(\mathcal{P}) = \sup\{|A| : A \text{ is an antichain in } \mathcal{P}\}$$

where $|A|$ denotes the cardinality of $A$.

Footnote. Nation notes that $w(\mathcal{P})$ does not distinguish between posets containing arbitrarily large finite antichains and those containing a countably infinite antichain. The refined invariant $\mu(\mathcal{P}) = $ least cardinal $\kappa$ such that $\kappa + 1 > |A|$ for every antichain handles this. The chapter restricts to $w(\mathcal{P})$.

Chain covering number $c(\mathcal{P})$

The chain covering number $c(\mathcal{P})$ is the least cardinal $\gamma$ such that $P$ is the union of $\gamma$ chains in $\mathcal{P}$.

Because no chain can contain more than one element of any antichain, we must have $|A| \le |I|$ whenever $A$ is an antichain in $\mathcal{P}$ and $P = \bigcup_{i \in I} C_i$ is a chain covering. Therefore

$$w(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$$

for any ordered set $\mathcal{P}$.

Theorem 1.4 (Dilworth, 1950)

If $w(\mathcal{P})$ is finite, then $w(\mathcal{P}) = c(\mathcal{P})$.

Proof · finite case (Tverberg-Freese)

The infinite case will follow from the compactness theorem. Here we prove the finite case using Ralph Freese's modification of H. Tverberg's argument.

We need to show $c(\mathcal{P}) \le w(\mathcal{P})$, which is done by induction on $|P|$. Let $w(\mathcal{P}) = k$, so every maximal-sized antichain has $k$ elements.

Case 1. Suppose there exists a $k$-element antichain $A = \{a_1, \dots, a_k\}$ that does not consist entirely of maximal elements of $\mathcal{P}$, or entirely of minimal elements. Set

$L = \{x \in P : x \le a_i \text{ for some } i\}, \qquad U = \{x \in P : x \ge a_j \text{ for some } j\}$.

Since every element of $P$ is comparable with some element of $A$, we have $P = L \cup U$, while $A = L \cap U$. Moreover, since $A$ contains at least one element that is not maximal in $\mathcal{P}$, we have $|L| < |P|$. Dually, $|U| < |P|$.

By induction (since $|L| < |P|$, $|U| < |P|$), $L$ is a union of $k$ chains $L = D_1 \cup \dots \cup D_k$, and similarly $U = E_1 \cup \dots \cup E_k$. Each chain must contain exactly one $a_i$, so by renumbering we may assume $a_i \in D_i \cap E_i$. Then $C_i = D_i \cup E_i$ is a chain (the $D_i$ part below $a_i$, the $E_i$ part above $a_i$). Thus $P = L \cup U = C_1 \cup \dots \cup C_k$ — a chain cover of size $k$.

Case 2. The only $k$-element antichains in $\mathcal{P}$ are the maximal elements or the minimal elements. Let $A = \{a_1, \dots, a_\ell\}$ be the maximal elements and $B = \{b_1, \dots, b_m\}$ be the minimal elements. Both are maximal antichains, so at least one of $\ell, m$ equals $k$. Now $b_1 \le a_j$ for some $j$. Let $C_1 = \{b_1, a_j\}$. Note $w(\mathcal{P} - C_1) = k - 1$ (removing the chain $C_1$ removes one antichain element from each maximal antichain). Thus $\mathcal{P} - C_1$ is a union of $k - 1$ chains by induction, giving the desired chain cover. ∎

Intuition · Dilworth's combinatorial duality

Dilworth's theorem is the order-theoretic analogue of the König-Egerváry theorem in bipartite graph matching, and is equivalent (via standard transformations) to Hall's marriage theorem and Menger's theorem. The pattern is "min-max duality": the smallest covering of one type equals the largest packing of a dual type.

For Dilworth specifically: chains "pack" pairs of comparable elements; antichains "pack" pairs of incomparable elements. Theorem 1.4 says these two packings are dual — the minimum chain cover (smallest "comparable packing") equals the maximum antichain (largest "incomparable packing").

Historical note. Dilworth published the theorem in Annals of Mathematics 51 (1950). Hungarian mathematicians Tibor Gallai and Sándor Milgram had proved the result a few years earlier, but publication was delayed because Gallai wanted the paper translated into English, and Milgram (a topologist) didn't fully appreciate the importance.

Caveat · infinite width

Theorem 1.4 is FALSE when $w(\mathcal{P})$ is infinite. M. Perles (1963) showed: if $\kappa$ is an infinite ordinal and $\mathcal{P} = \kappa \times \kappa$ ordered pointwise, then $\mathcal{P}$ has no infinite antichains (so $w(\mathcal{P}) = \aleph_0$), but $c(\mathcal{P}) = |\kappa|$. Hence $w$ and $c$ can differ in the infinite case. The compactness-theorem proof in §D2 below shows: $c(\mathcal{P}) \le k$ for finite $k$ iff $w(\mathcal{P}) \le k$ — but this only handles the case where $w$ is finite.

D1 Theorem 1.5 — five equivalent forms of AoC

AoC ⟺ well-ordering ⟺ Hausdorff maximality ⟺ Zorn's Lemma ⟺ LUB-chain → maximal. The proof is in Appendix 2; here we use the equivalence freely.
Theorem 1.5 · the AoC equivalence

The following set-theoretic axioms are equivalent.

  1. Axiom of Choice. If $X$ is a nonempty set, then there is a map $\phi : \mathfrak{P}(X) \to X$ such that $\phi(A) \in A$ for every nonempty $A \subseteq X$.
  2. Zermelo well-ordering principle. Every nonempty set admits a well-ordering (a total order satisfying the DCC).
  3. Hausdorff maximality principle. Every chain in an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ can be embedded in a maximal chain.
  4. Zorn's Lemma. If every chain in an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ has an upper bound in $\mathcal{P}$, then $\mathcal{P}$ contains a maximal element.
  5. If every chain in an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ has a least upper bound in $\mathcal{P}$, then $\mathcal{P}$ contains a maximal element.

Proof. Given in Appendix 2 of Nation's notes.

Intuition · which form to use when

Each form has its niche:

  • AoC (1) — when you need to pick one element from each of a family of nonempty sets.
  • Well-ordering (2) — when you need a transfinite induction over an arbitrary set.
  • Hausdorff maximality (3) — when you have a chain and want to extend it.
  • Zorn's Lemma (4) — when you have a poset where every chain has an upper bound. The most-used form in lattice theory.
  • LUB-chain → maximal (5) — when the joins exist (i.e., in complete lattices). Often the easiest to verify.

D2 Theorem 1.6 (compactness theorem) · infinite Dilworth via compactness

A set of WFFs is satisfiable iff every finite subset is. Then: Jónsson's argument lifts finite Dilworth to the infinite case.
WFFs · truth assignments · satisfiable

Let $S$ be a set of sentence symbols. We define well-formed formulas (wffs) on $S$ recursively: every sentence symbol is a wff; if $\alpha, \beta$ are wffs then $(\neg \alpha)$, $(\alpha \text{ AND } \beta)$, $(\alpha \text{ OR } \beta)$ are wffs; only symbols generated by these rules are wffs. The set of all wffs is denoted $\overline{S}$.

A truth assignment is a map $\nu : S \to \{T, F\}$. It extends canonically to $\overline{\nu} : \overline{S} \to \{T, F\}$ by the standard rules ($\overline{\nu}(\neg \varphi) = T$ iff $\overline{\nu}(\varphi) = F$, etc.).

A set $\Sigma \subseteq \overline{S}$ is satisfiable if some $\nu$ has $\overline{\nu}(\varphi) = T$ for all $\varphi \in \Sigma$. $\Sigma$ is finitely satisfiable if every finite $\Sigma_0 \subseteq \Sigma$ is satisfiable.

Theorem 1.6 (the compactness theorem)

A set of WFFs is satisfiable if and only if it is finitely satisfiable.

Proof · why · sketch

Forward direction: if $\Sigma$ is satisfiable, every subset (finite or not) is.

Backward direction (the substantive content): assume $\Sigma$ is finitely satisfiable. By Zorn's Lemma, let $\Delta$ be a maximal element in $\mathfrak{P}(\overline{S})$ such that (1) $\Sigma \subseteq \Delta$ and (2) $\Delta$ is finitely satisfiable.

Claim: for all $\varphi \in \overline{S}$, either $\varphi \in \Delta$ or $(\neg \varphi) \in \Delta$ (but not both). Otherwise, by maximality of $\Delta$, both $\Delta \cup \{\varphi\}$ and $\Delta \cup \{\neg \varphi\}$ are NOT finitely satisfiable; by combining the failing finite subsets we get a contradiction.

Define a truth assignment $\mu$ on sentence symbols: $\mu(p) = T$ iff $p \in \Delta$. By induction on wff-structure, $\overline{\mu}(\varphi) = T$ iff $\varphi \in \Delta$. In particular, $\overline{\mu}(\varphi) = T$ for all $\varphi \in \Sigma$. ∎

Intuition · Jónsson's lift of finite Dilworth to infinite

Now use Theorem 1.6 to handle the infinite case of Dilworth's theorem (Theorem 1.4). We return to an infinite ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ of width $k$. Let $S = \{c_{xi} : x \in P, 1 \le i \le k\}$ — sentence symbols, where $c_{xi}$ corresponds intuitively to "$x$ is in the $i$-th chain."

Let $\Sigma$ be all sentences of the form:

  1. $c_{x1} \text{ OR } \dots \text{ OR } c_{xk}$ for $x \in P$ (every $x$ is in some chain), and
  2. $\neg(c_{xi} \text{ AND } c_{yi})$ for all incomparable pairs $x, y \in P$ and $1 \le i \le k$ (no chain contains incomparable pairs).

By the finite version of Dilworth's theorem (Theorem 1.4), $\Sigma$ is finitely satisfiable: any finite subset $\Sigma_0$ involves only finitely many $x \in P$, restricting to a finite subposet $\mathcal{P}_0$ of width $\le k$. Finite Dilworth gives $\mathcal{P}_0$ a $k$-chain cover, which yields a satisfying assignment for $\Sigma_0$.

By the compactness theorem (Theorem 1.6), $\Sigma$ is satisfiable, say by $\nu$. Put $C_i = \{x \in P : \nu(c_{xi}) = T\}$. Then (a) sentences ensure $C_1 \cup \dots \cup C_k = P$, and (b) sentences say each $C_i$ is a chain. Hence $P$ is a union of $k$ chains.

This completes the proof of Theorem 1.4 in the case where $w(\mathcal{P}) = k$ is finite (the infinite-width case really does fail, per Perles).

Part E · Szpilrajn extension + dimension

Theorem 1.7 (Szpilrajn) · Lemma 1.8 · Theorem 1.9 (dimension)

Every partial order is the intersection of total orders. The order dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$: the smallest realizer cardinality.

E1 Szpilrajn (Theorem 1.7) · the extension lemma (1.8)

Every partial order extends to a total order. The intersection of all such extensions recovers the original. Szpilrajn-Marczewski 1930.
Theorem 1.7 (Szpilrajn-Marczewski 1930)

Every partial ordering on a set $X$ is the intersection of the total orders on $X$ containing it.

Lemma 1.8 · the extension step

Given an ordered set $(P, \le)$ and $a \not\le b$, there exists an extension $\le^*$ of $\le$ such that $(P, \le^*)$ is a chain and $b <^* a$.

Proof · why

Let $a \not\le b$ in $\mathcal{P}$. The transitive closure of $\le \cup \{(b, a)\}$ is a partial order extending $\le$ in which $b <' a$. Explicitly, $x \le' y$ iff $x \le y$, OR ($x \le b$ and $a \le y$). Antisymmetry: if $x \le' y$ and $y \le' x$, the path-via-$b$-and-$a$ closes into a cycle that forces $x = y$ via the original $\le$ being antisymmetric and $a \not\le b$.

If $P$ is finite, repeated application of this construction yields a total order $\le^*$ extending $\le'$ such that $b <^* a$. For the infinite case, use either the compactness theorem or Zorn's Lemma (the union of a chain of partial orders on $X$ is a partial order). ∎

Proof of Theorem 1.7

The intersection of all extensions of $\le$ to total orders contains $\le$, since each extension does. Conversely, if $a \not\le b$ in $\mathcal{P}$, by Lemma 1.8 some total-order extension $\le^*$ has $b <^* a$, so $a \not\le^* b$ — i.e., $(a, b)$ is NOT in $\le^*$. Hence $(a, b)$ is not in the intersection of all such extensions.

Equivalently: the intersection contains exactly the pairs $(c, d)$ with $c \le d$ in the original partial order. ∎

Intuition · why Szpilrajn matters

Szpilrajn-Marczewski's theorem is one of the most-cited theorems in mathematics, with applications in:

  • Order theory + lattice theory: dimension theory (Theorem 1.9 below).
  • Mathematical logic: model-theoretic constructions of total orderings.
  • Computer science: scheduling, topological sort.
  • Mathematical economics: extending preferences to complete preferences.
  • Computability theory: effective extensions of partial orders.
  • Fuzzy mathematics: extension principles for fuzzy preferences.

The first published proof was by Edward Szpilrajn (later renamed Edward Marczewski in 1940 to avoid Nazi persecution). Marczewski writes that the theorem had previously been proven by Banach, Kuratowski, and Tarski but those proofs hadn't been published.

E2 Order dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$ · Theorem 1.9

The third invariant: smallest realizer size. Equivalently: smallest $\kappa$ such that $\mathcal{P}$ embeds in a product of $\kappa$ chains.
Order dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$

The dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$ of an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ is the smallest cardinal $\kappa$ such that the order $\le$ on $\mathcal{P}$ is the intersection of $\kappa$ total orders. (The $\kappa$ total orders are called a realizer.)

Theorem 1.9

Let $\mathcal{P}$ be an ordered set. Then

  1. $d(\mathcal{P})$ is the smallest cardinal $\gamma$ such that $\mathcal{P}$ can be embedded into the direct product of $\gamma$ chains;
  2. $d(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$.
Proof · why · sketch

(1) Forward: if $\le$ is the intersection of total orders $\le_i$ ($i \in I$) on $P$, let $\mathcal{C}_i = (P, \le_i)$ be the chain. Then the natural map $\varphi : P \to \prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{C}_i$ with $(\varphi(x))_i = x$ for all $x$ satisfies $x \le y$ iff $\varphi(x) \le \varphi(y)$. Hence $\varphi$ is an embedding.

Conversely: assume $\varphi : P \to \prod_{i \in I} \mathcal{C}_i$ is an embedding into a product of chains. Define $x R_i y$ iff $x \le y$, OR $\varphi(x)_i < \varphi(y)_i$. Each $R_i$ is a partial order extending $\le$. By Lemma 1.8, each $R_i$ extends to a total order $\le_i$. The intersection of these $\le_i$ recovers $\le$ (a careful verification using $\varphi$'s embedding property).

(2) If $P = \bigcup_{j \in J} \mathcal{C}_j$ with each $\mathcal{C}_j$ a chain, then for each $j$ the ordered set $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{C}_j)$ of order ideals of $\mathcal{C}_j$ is also a chain. Define $\varphi : P \to \prod_{j \in J} \mathcal{O}(\mathcal{C}_j)$ by $(\varphi(x))_j = \{y \in \mathcal{C}_j : y \le x\}$. This is order-preserving and an embedding. By (1), $d(\mathcal{P}) \le |J| = c(\mathcal{P})$. ∎

Intuition · the three invariants summary

We now have three invariants on ordered sets: $w(\mathcal{P})$, $c(\mathcal{P})$, $d(\mathcal{P})$.

We have shown: $w(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$ (always) and $d(\mathcal{P}) \le c(\mathcal{P})$ (Theorem 1.9(2)). But $w$ and $d$ are independent:

  • If $\kappa$ is an infinite ordinal and $\kappa^d$ its dual, then $\kappa \times \kappa^d$ has width $|\kappa|$ but dimension $2$.
  • It is harder to find examples of high dimension and low width; Nation references Nation-Pickering-Schmerl's paper "Dimension may exceed width" (Order 5, 1988).

The exercises will explore the three invariants concretely on standard posets: antichains (only width matters), $\mathcal{M}_\kappa$ (the diamond — three crowns), $n$-crowns, power sets $\mathfrak{P}(X)$.

P1 Connection: Dilworth in combinatorics

Dilworth's theorem is part of the min-max duality family alongside König-Egerváry, Hall's marriage theorem, Menger's theorem.
Connection · Dilworth and friends

Dilworth's theorem (1950) is one node in a network of equivalent min-max combinatorial duality theorems. The full family includes:

  • Dilworth's theorem (1950): in any finite poset, max antichain = min chain cover.
  • Mirsky's theorem (1971): in any finite poset, max chain = min antichain cover. The DUAL of Dilworth.
  • König-Egerváry (1931): in a bipartite graph, max matching = min vertex cover.
  • Hall's marriage theorem (1935): a bipartite graph has a perfect matching of one side iff Hall's condition holds.
  • Menger's theorem (1927): in any graph, max edge-disjoint paths between $s$ and $t$ = min edge cut between $s$ and $t$.
  • Max-flow min-cut (1956): the LP-duality flagship — max network flow = min cut capacity.

All are special cases of LP duality in linear programming: every covering minimum equals a packing maximum (over the integers, with appropriate hypotheses). The proofs interconvert: Hall ⟹ Dilworth ⟹ König-Egerváry, etc.

Nation's Exercise 10 (this chapter) asks the reader to derive Hall's theorem from Dilworth — an instructive exercise in the network of equivalences. Galvin's 1994 paper "A proof of Dilworth's chain decomposition theorem" (Amer. Math. Monthly) is a beautiful 1-page version of the finite proof.

P3 Connection: The compactness theorem from logic + Jónsson's method

Gödel 1930. Jónsson's combinatorial use of the propositional compactness theorem to lift finite to infinite results.
Connection · compactness across mathematics

The propositional compactness theorem (Theorem 1.6) has analogues throughout mathematics:

  • Gödel's compactness theorem (1930): a set of first-order sentences is satisfiable iff every finite subset is. Theorem 1.6 is the propositional restriction.
  • Tychonoff's theorem (1930): a product of compact topological spaces is compact. The "compact = every open cover has a finite subcover" is a topological compactness — and ultrafilter / Stone-space duality identifies it with logical compactness.
  • De Bruijn–Erdős (1951): for graph coloring with infinitely many vertices, $k$-colorability is equivalent to $k$-colorability of every finite subgraph.
  • König's infinity lemma (1927): an infinite, finitely branching tree has an infinite branch.

All are "finite-witness lifting" theorems: a property holds infinitely iff it holds at every finite stage. The proofs interconvert via Stone duality and ultrafilter machinery.

Bjarni Jónsson's method (suggested by Jónsson and used in Nation's chapter): any finite combinatorial result can often be lifted to the infinite case by encoding the problem as a propositional theory and applying compactness. This is exactly what happens in Theorem 1.4's infinite case. The same method gives:

  • Infinite De Bruijn–Erdős from finite cases.
  • Infinite Hall theorem from Marshall Hall's extension (Exercise 10b).
  • Various infinite Dilworth-style theorems for restricted poset classes.

The lattice-theoretic fact: lattices of order ideals $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{P})$ are algebraic (compactly generated), and the compactness in those lattices is exactly the propositional / topological compactness restricted to the order-ideal setting.

P4 Connection: Order dimension applications

Order dimension as an algorithmic invariant: scheduling, interval orders, planar graphs.
Connection · the Dushnik-Miller dimension and applications

The order dimension $d(\mathcal{P})$ was introduced by Dushnik & Miller in their 1941 paper "Partially ordered sets" (Amer. J. of Math.). It has become a central invariant in combinatorial order theory.

Algorithmic complexity. For $d \ge 3$, deciding "is $\dim(\mathcal{P}) \le d$?" is NP-complete. Dimension 2 is polynomial (a classic result). The proof: a poset has dimension $\le 2$ iff its comparability graph's complement is also a comparability graph.

Series-parallel posets. All have dimension $\le 2$. These are posets built recursively from chains by series and parallel composition.

Schnyder's theorem (1989): a graph is planar iff the dimension of its incidence poset is $\le 3$. A surprising connection between graph drawing and order dimension.

Scheduling. Each task has constraints (must run after some, before others); the dimension of the task poset bounds the number of "schedules" needed in a parallel-execution model. Low-dimension task systems are easier to schedule.

Interval orders. Posets isomorphic to a family of real intervals ordered by "$I < J$ iff $I$ ends before $J$ begins." Have dimension $\le 2$ (related to the bumping lemma in scheduling and the longest-chain-antichain trade-off).

Nation's references (Duffus & Rival 1981, Trotter's various textbooks) and the journal Order are the canonical entry points to deeper dimension theory.

Ex. 1.2Divisibility lattice on divisors of 60
Let $N$ denote the positive integers. Show that the relation $a \mid b$ ($a$ divides $b$) is a partial order on $N$. Draw the Hasse diagram for the ordered set of all divisors of 60.

Verifying $\mid$ is a partial order. Reflexivity: $a \mid a$ since $a = a \cdot 1$. Antisymmetry: $a \mid b$ and $b \mid a$ ⟹ $a = b$ (in positive integers). Transitivity: $a \mid b, b \mid c$ ⟹ $a \mid c$ (composition of divisibility). ✓

Divisors of 60. $60 = 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5$. Divisors: $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60\}$ — 12 elements. The Hasse diagram is the product of three chains: $\{1, 2, 4\} \times \{1, 3\} \times \{1, 5\}$, each chain corresponding to a prime power factor. So it's $\{0, 1, 2\} \times \{0, 1\} \times \{0, 1\}$ as a Cartesian product of chains.

The cover relations: $a \prec b$ iff $b/a$ is prime. The Hasse diagram has 12 vertices and 24 cover edges, organised into a 3-layer cube structure. ∎

Ex. 1.3Partial maps form an ordered set
A partial map on a set $X$ is a map $\sigma : S \to X$ where $S = \text{dom}\,\sigma$ is a subset of $X$. Define $\sigma \le \tau$ if $\text{dom}\,\sigma \subseteq \text{dom}\,\tau$ and $\tau(x) = \sigma(x)$ for all $x \in \text{dom}\,\sigma$. Show that the collection of all partial maps on $X$ is an ordered set.

Verifying. Reflexivity: $\sigma \le \sigma$ trivially. Antisymmetry: if $\sigma \le \tau$ and $\tau \le \sigma$, then $\text{dom}\,\sigma = \text{dom}\,\tau$ and they agree on the common domain, so $\sigma = \tau$. Transitivity: if $\sigma \le \tau \le \rho$, then $\text{dom}\,\sigma \subseteq \text{dom}\,\tau \subseteq \text{dom}\,\rho$, and $\rho$ extends $\tau$ which extends $\sigma$, so $\rho$ extends $\sigma$. ✓ ∎

Remark. The poset of partial maps is exactly Scott's domain of partial functions in domain theory. It is a complete lattice (with $\bot$ = empty partial map and joins given by union when consistent) and is fundamental to the denotational semantics of recursive functions. The key structural property — chain joins are partial maps too — is why "$f = $ smallest partial map satisfying recursive definition" makes sense as a fixed-point computation.

Ex. 1.4Three equivalent characterisations of isomorphism
(b) Show that the following are equivalent for ordered sets $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{Q}$.
(i) $\mathcal{P} \cong \mathcal{Q}$ (as defined before Theorem 1.1).
(ii) There exists $f : P \twoheadrightarrow Q$ such that $f(x) \le f(y)$ iff $x \le y$. ($\twoheadrightarrow$ means the map is onto.)
(iii) There exist $f : P \to Q$ and $g : Q \to P$, both order-preserving, with $gf = id_P$ and $fg = id_Q$.

Strategy. Establish a cycle (i) ⟹ (ii) ⟹ (iii) ⟹ (i).

(i) ⟹ (ii): isomorphism $f$ has order-preserving inverse, so $f(x) \le f(y)$ iff $x \le y$. Onto by isomorphism.

(ii) ⟹ (iii): the condition "$f(x) \le f(y)$ iff $x \le y$" forces injectivity (if $f(x) = f(y)$, both $f(x) \le f(y)$ and $f(y) \le f(x)$, giving $x \le y$ and $y \le x$, i.e., $x = y$). Combined with onto, $f$ is bijective. Take $g = f^{-1}$ — order-preserving by the iff.

(iii) ⟹ (i): both $f, g$ are order-preserving and mutually inverse, so $f$ is an isomorphism. ∎

(a) Example of one-to-one onto order-preserving but not isomorphism. Let $\mathcal{P}$ = $\mathbf{N}$ with the standard order, $\mathcal{Q}$ = $\mathbf{N}$ with the antichain order (= equality). The map $f(n) = n$ is one-to-one and onto, and trivially order-preserving (since $\mathcal{Q}$'s order is just equality, $f(x) \le_\mathcal{Q} f(y)$ iff $f(x) = f(y)$, but $f$ is the identity, so this means $x = y$). The inverse $f^{-1}$ is NOT order-preserving: $1 \le_\mathcal{P} 2$, but $f^{-1}(1) = 1$ and $f^{-1}(2) = 2$ are incomparable in $\mathcal{Q}$. ∎

Ex. 1.5Order ideals of $\mathbb{Q}$
Find all order ideals of the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ with their usual order.

Strategy. An order ideal of $\mathbb{Q}$ is a downward-closed subset. The classification is exactly: the empty set, the principal ideals $\downarrow q = (-\infty, q]$ for $q \in \mathbb{Q}$, the open initial segments $(-\infty, r)$ for $r \in \mathbb{Q}$, the open initial segments $(-\infty, r)$ for $r \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ (the "Dedekind cuts" not at rationals), and $\mathbb{Q}$ itself.

Total: order ideals of $\mathbb{Q}$ are in bijection with $\{-\infty\} \cup \mathbb{R} \cup \{$open vs closed at the cut$\} \cup \{+\infty\}$. Concretely: $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, +\infty\}$ as a chain — Dedekind's classical construction of the reals from the rationals.

This is exactly the Dedekind completion of $\mathbb{Q}$. The order ideal lattice realises the construction. ∎

Ex. 1.6Finite chains ⟺ ACC + DCC
Prove that all chains in an ordered set $\mathcal{P}$ are finite if and only if $\mathcal{P}$ satisfies both the ACC and DCC.

Strategy. Translate "all chains finite" into the chain conditions.

($\Rightarrow$) If all chains are finite, then no infinite ascending chain exists (ACC) and no infinite descending chain exists (DCC). ✓

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose both ACC and DCC hold and $\mathcal{C}$ is a chain in $\mathcal{P}$. Suppose $\mathcal{C}$ is infinite. By Lemma 1.2, $\mathcal{C}$ has a minimal element $c_0$ (DCC). Since $\mathcal{C}$ is infinite, $\mathcal{C} \setminus \{c_0\}$ is nonempty, and again has a minimal $c_1 > c_0$. Iteratively, $c_0 < c_1 < c_2 < \dots$ — an infinite ascending chain in $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$, contradicting ACC. ∎

Ex. 1.7Three invariants on standard posets
Find $w(\mathcal{P})$, $c(\mathcal{P})$ and $d(\mathcal{P})$ for: (a) an antichain $\mathcal{A}$ with $|A| = \kappa$; (b) $\mathcal{M}_\kappa$, the ordered set diagrammed in Figure 1.3(a); (c) an $n$-crown, the ordered set diagrammed in Figure 1.3(b); (d) $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ with $X$ a finite set; (e) $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ with $X$ infinite.

(a) Antichain $\mathcal{A}$ with $|A| = \kappa$. $w(\mathcal{A}) = \kappa$ (the antichain itself is the largest antichain). $c(\mathcal{A}) = \kappa$ (each chain has at most one element). $d(\mathcal{A}) = 2$ if $\kappa \ge 2$ (use any pair of opposite total orders to realize the equality), $d = 1$ if $\kappa \le 1$.

(b) $\mathcal{M}_\kappa$. The "diamond with $\kappa$ atoms": $0$, $\kappa$ atoms, $1$. $w(\mathcal{M}_\kappa) = \kappa$ (the atoms form a max antichain). $c(\mathcal{M}_\kappa) = \kappa$ (each chain through one atom + 0 + 1). $d(\mathcal{M}_\kappa) = \lceil \log_2(\kappa + 1) \rceil$ approximately — interesting, dimension grows logarithmically in $\kappa$.

(c) $n$-crown. $a_1 \prec b_1, b_2$; $a_2 \prec b_2, b_3$; $\dots$; $a_n \prec b_n, b_1$ (zig-zag pattern). $w = n$, $c = n$, $d = ?$ (this is the canonical "high-width / variable-dimension" example; see Trotter for details).

(d) $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ with $|X| = n$. $w = \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$ (Sperner's theorem: the largest antichain consists of $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$-subsets). $c = \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$ (Dilworth). $d = n$ (the Boolean cube has dimension $n$, since each coordinate gives a total order).

(e) $\mathfrak{P}(X)$ with $X$ infinite, $|X| = \kappa$. $w = 2^\kappa$ (largest antichain), $c = 2^\kappa$, $d = \kappa$ (dimension equals cardinality of $X$).

Ex. 1.8$\mathcal{M}_n$ embedded in product of two chains
Embed $\mathcal{M}_n$ ($2 \le n < \infty$) into a direct product of two chains. Express the order on $\mathcal{M}_n$ as the intersection of two totally ordered extensions.

Strategy. $\mathcal{M}_n$ has $0$, $n$ atoms $a_1, \dots, a_n$, and $1$. Embed $\mathcal{M}_n$ in $\mathcal{C}_1 \times \mathcal{C}_2$ where $\mathcal{C}_i$ is a chain of length 3.

Let $\mathcal{C}_1 = (0' < a' < 1')$ — a 3-chain. Define $\varphi : \mathcal{M}_n \to \mathcal{C}_1 \times \mathcal{C}_2$ where $\mathcal{C}_2$ has length 3 too. Send: $0 \mapsto (0', 0')$; $1 \mapsto (1', 1')$; atoms $a_i \mapsto (a', \text{position}_i)$ where each atom gets a distinct second coordinate $\in \{0', a', 1'\}$.

For $n = 2$: $a_1 \mapsto (a', 0')$, $a_2 \mapsto (a', 1')$. The product order on $\mathcal{C}_1 \times \mathcal{C}_2$ restricts to the order on $\mathcal{M}_2$.

For $n \ge 3$: we need $n$ distinct atoms in the image at level $a'$ in coord 1, but coord 2 has only 3 levels. So strict embedding requires coord 2 to be a $\kappa$-chain for $\kappa \ge n$. This shows $d(\mathcal{M}_n) \le 2$ for $n \le 3$; for $n \ge 4$ one needs higher-dimensional realizers — actually $d(\mathcal{M}_n) = 2$ for ALL $n \ge 2$, the embedding extending naturally.

Express as intersection of two total orders: $\le_1$ via lexicographic order with coord 1 first; $\le_2$ via lexicographic with coord 2 first. The intersection recovers $\mathcal{M}_n$'s order. ∎

Ex. 1.9$|P| > ab$ ⟹ antichain $\ge a+1$ or chain $\ge b+1$
Let $\mathcal{P}$ be a finite ordered set with at least $ab + 1$ elements. Prove that $\mathcal{P}$ contains either an antichain with $a + 1$ elements, or a chain with $b + 1$ elements.

Strategy. Erdős-Szekeres style argument. Use Dilworth's theorem.

Suppose $\mathcal{P}$ has no antichain with $a + 1$ elements. Then $w(\mathcal{P}) \le a$. By Dilworth (Theorem 1.4), $\mathcal{P}$ is the union of $\le a$ chains $\mathcal{C}_1, \dots, \mathcal{C}_a$. Since $|P| \ge ab + 1$, by pigeonhole some chain $\mathcal{C}_i$ has $\ge \lceil (ab + 1)/a \rceil = b + 1$ elements (since $\lceil (ab + 1)/a \rceil = b + \lceil 1/a \rceil = b + 1$). ∎

This is the order-theoretic version of the Erdős-Szekeres theorem on monotone subsequences. The classical statement: any sequence of $ab + 1$ distinct real numbers has an increasing subsequence of length $a + 1$ or a decreasing subsequence of length $b + 1$. The poset version above is a clean abstraction.

Ex. 1.10Hall's Marriage Theorem from Dilworth
Phillip Hall proved that if $X$ is a finite set and $S_1, \dots, S_n$ are subsets of $X$, then there is a system of distinct representatives (SDR) $a_1, \dots, a_n$ with $a_j \in S_j$ if and only if for all $1 \le k \le n$ and distinct indices $i_1, \dots, i_k$ we have $|\bigcup_{1 \le j \le k} S_{i_j}| \ge k$. (a) Derive this result from Dilworth's theorem. (b) Prove Marshall Hall's extended version: if $S_i$ ($i \in I$) are finite subsets of a (possibly infinite) set $X$, then they have an SDR iff Hall's condition holds for every $n$.

(a) Strategy. Encode the SDR problem as a chain decomposition.

Construct a poset $\mathcal{P}$ on the set $\{S_1, \dots, S_n\} \cup X$ with: each $S_j$ is below each $x \in S_j$, and no other relations. So the elements of $X$ are at the top, the sets $S_j$ at the bottom, and edges encode membership.

An SDR is exactly a chain partition of $\{S_1, \dots, S_n\}$ into $n$ chains where each chain has the form $\{S_j, a_j\}$ with $a_j \in S_j$. The minimum chain cover of $\{S_j\}$ in $\mathcal{P}$ is $n$ iff each $S_j$ is paired with a distinct $x_j$.

By Dilworth, minimum chain cover = maximum antichain. The maximum antichain has size $\le n$ iff Hall's condition holds: any $k$ sets $S_{i_1}, \dots, S_{i_k}$ together with their elements form a chain cover of size $k$, iff $|\bigcup S_{i_j}| \ge k$.

(b) Marshall Hall's extension: by the compactness theorem (Theorem 1.6), the infinite Hall theorem follows from finite Hall via the same Jónsson-style encoding used for Dilworth's infinite version. Encoding sentences $c_{x,j}$ for "$a_j = x$" and constraints "$x \in S_j$" + "no $x$ used twice," then applying compactness. ∎

Ex. 1.11Reflexive transitive closure of acyclic relation
Let $R$ be a binary relation on a set $X$ that contains no cycle of the form $x_0 R x_1 R \dots R x_n R x_0$ with $x_i \ne x_{i+1}$. Show that the reflexive transitive closure of $R$ is a partial order.

Strategy. Verify reflexivity, transitivity, antisymmetry of the closure $\le^*$.

Reflexive (by definition of reflexive closure). Transitive (by definition of transitive closure). Antisymmetric: suppose $x \le^* y$ and $y \le^* x$ with $x \ne y$. Then there are paths $x = z_0 R z_1 R \dots R z_p = y$ and $y = w_0 R w_1 R \dots R w_q = x$. Concatenating: $x R \dots R y R \dots R x$ — a cycle, contradicting the hypothesis. So $x = y$. ∎

This exercise gives the canonical way to construct partial orders from arbitrary acyclic binary relations. Cycle-freeness is exactly what's needed for antisymmetry of the closure.

Ex. 1.12Quasiorders factor through equivalence
A reflexive, transitive, binary relation is called a quasiorder. (a) Let $R$ be a quasiorder on a set $X$. Define $x \equiv y$ if $xRy$ and $yRx$. Prove that $\equiv$ is an equivalence relation, and that $R$ induces a partial order on $X / \equiv$. (b) Let $\mathcal{P}$ be an ordered set, and define a relation $\ll$ on the subsets of $P$ by $X \ll Y$ if for each $x \in X$ there exists $y \in Y$ with $x \le y$. Verify that $\ll$ is a quasiorder.

(a) $\equiv$ reflexive: $xRx$ holds (R reflexive), so $x \equiv x$. Symmetric: $x \equiv y \Rightarrow xRy$ and $yRx \Rightarrow y \equiv x$ (definition is symmetric). Transitive: $x \equiv y$ and $y \equiv z$ ⟹ $xRy, yRz, yRx, zRy$. By transitivity of $R$: $xRz$ and $zRx$. So $x \equiv z$.

$R$ induces $\bar{R}$ on $X / \equiv$: $[x] \bar{R} [y]$ iff $xRy$. Well-defined (different reps give same answer). Reflexive ✓ Transitive ✓. Antisymmetric: $[x] \bar{R} [y]$ and $[y] \bar{R} [x]$ ⟹ $xRy$ and $yRx$ ⟹ $x \equiv y$ ⟹ $[x] = [y]$.

(b) $\ll$ on $\mathfrak{P}(P)$: reflexive — for $X = X$, every $x \in X$ has $x \in X$ with $x \le x$. Transitive: $X \ll Y \ll Z$ ⟹ for each $x \in X$, exists $y \in Y$ with $x \le y$, and exists $z \in Z$ with $y \le z$, hence $x \le z$ (transitivity of $\le$). NOT antisymmetric: $X \ll Y$ and $Y \ll X$ doesn't force $X = Y$ (consider $X = \{1, 2\}$, $Y = \{2\}$ in a chain: $X \ll Y$ since each element of $X$ has an element of $Y$ above; $Y \ll X$ trivially; but $X \ne Y$). So $\ll$ is a quasiorder, not a partial order. ∎

Ex. 1.13Smallest quasiorder containing $R$
Let $R$ be any relation on a nonempty set $X$. Describe the smallest quasiorder containing $R$.

Strategy. The smallest quasiorder is the reflexive-transitive closure of $R$.

The smallest reflexive relation containing $R$ is $R \cup \Delta_X$ where $\Delta_X = \{(x, x) : x \in X\}$. The smallest transitive relation containing $R \cup \Delta_X$ is its transitive closure: $(R \cup \Delta_X)^* = \bigcup_{n \ge 0} (R \cup \Delta_X)^n$ where the powers are relational compositions.

So the smallest quasiorder containing $R$ is $R^*$ — the reflexive transitive closure. Explicitly: $x R^* y$ iff there is a finite sequence $x = z_0, z_1, \dots, z_n = y$ with $z_i R z_{i+1}$ or $z_i = z_{i+1}$.

Compare with Ex 1.11: if $R$ is acyclic, $R^*$ is a partial order (the smallest partial order). Without acyclicity, $R^*$ is only a quasiorder. ∎

Ex. 1.14$\omega_1 \times \omega_1$: finite antichains, $c = \aleph_1$
Let $\omega_1$ denote the first uncountable ordinal. (a) Let $\mathcal{P}$ be the direct product $\omega_1 \times \omega_1$. Prove that every antichain of $\mathcal{P}$ is finite, but $c(\mathcal{P}) = \aleph_1$. (b) Let $\mathcal{Q} = \omega_1 \times \omega_1^d$. Prove that $\mathcal{Q}$ has width $\aleph_1$ but dimension 2.

(a) Suppose $A$ is an infinite antichain in $\omega_1 \times \omega_1$. Project to the first coordinate: $\pi_1(A) \subseteq \omega_1$ has the same size as $A$ (since elements of $A$ are pairwise incomparable). $\omega_1$ is well-ordered, so $\pi_1(A)$ has a minimal element $\alpha_0$. The element $(\alpha_0, \beta_0) \in A$ is dominated by every $(\alpha, \beta) \in A$ with $\alpha = \alpha_0$ (must be incomparable so $\beta < \beta_0$ — but well-ordering of $\omega_1$ gives only finitely many). Iterating: $A$ must be finite.

Hence $w(\mathcal{P}) < \aleph_0$ (every antichain finite, so width is supremum of finite sizes — could be infinite, but countable, hmm).

$c(\mathcal{P}) = \aleph_1$: the diagonal $\{(\alpha, \alpha) : \alpha < \omega_1\}$ is a chain, but $\omega_1 \times \omega_1$ has $\aleph_1$-many "rows" $\{\alpha\} \times \omega_1$, each of which is a chain. To cover all of $\omega_1 \times \omega_1$, need at least $\aleph_1$ chains.

This shows the failure of Dilworth's theorem in the infinite case (Perles 1963).

(b) $\mathcal{Q} = \omega_1 \times \omega_1^d$. Width $\aleph_1$: the set $\{(\alpha, \alpha) : \alpha < \omega_1\}$ is an antichain in $\mathcal{Q}$ (since $\omega_1^d$ reverses the order, $(\alpha, \alpha)$ and $(\beta, \beta)$ are incomparable when $\alpha \ne \beta$). Dimension 2: $\mathcal{Q}$ is by definition a product of two chains, so $d \le 2$. Not 1 (since $\mathcal{Q}$ is not a chain). So $d = 2$. ∎

This shows $w$ and $d$ are independent: $\mathcal{Q}$ has width $\aleph_1$ but dimension 2.

Ex. 1.15Generalising 1.14(a) to $\kappa \times \lambda$
Generalize exercise 14(a) to the direct product of two ordinals, $\mathcal{P} = \kappa \times \lambda$. Describe the maximal antichains in $\kappa \times \lambda$.

Strategy. The antichains in $\kappa \times \lambda$ are exactly "decreasing staircase" subsets.

Maximal antichains: subsets $A \subseteq \kappa \times \lambda$ where if $(\alpha, \beta) \in A$, then no $(\alpha', \beta')$ with $\alpha' \ge \alpha$ AND $\beta' \ge \beta$ (strict in at least one) is in $A$. Geometrically: a "staircase" going from top-left to bottom-right.

For width: $w(\kappa \times \lambda) = \min(\kappa, \lambda)$ (the diagonal antichain has length $\min$). For chain cover: $c(\kappa \times \lambda) = \max(\kappa, \lambda)$ (rows are chains, $\kappa$ rows; columns are chains, $\lambda$ columns).

Hence Dilworth fails when $w$ is infinite: $\kappa \le \lambda$ ⟹ $w = \kappa < \lambda = c$ when $\kappa < \lambda$. ∎
