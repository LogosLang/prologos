Chapter Overview — the Cayley analogue for lattices

Chapter 1 gave Cayley for posets; Chapter 2 gave it for semilattices. Chapter 4 delivers the lattice version: every lattice embeds in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for some $X$. The proof (Whitman 1946 / Jónsson 1953) is a transfinite recursion. Then: modular ⟺ type 2 representation; Arguesian ← type 1 representation.

So far, the companion has given Cayley-style representation theorems for posets (Theorem 1.1: every poset embeds in $\mathfrak{P}(\mathcal{Q})$) and semilattices (Theorem 2.2: every meet semilattice embeds in $\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{S})$). The lattice version is harder. Power-set lattices satisfy distributivity, so non-distributive lattices like $\mathcal{M}_3, \mathcal{N}_5$ cannot embed in any $\mathfrak{P}(X)$. The right replacement is the partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ — the lattice of equivalence relations on $X$ under refinement.

Whitman's theorem (1946), simplified by Jónsson in 1953: every lattice embeds in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for some $X$. The proof is technically demanding — a transfinite recursion fixing up "join failures" three points at a time, then iterating $\omega$ times — but the result is foundational. It justifies the abstract definition of "lattice" (the axioms generate exactly the structures that arise as sublattices of partition lattices) and gives a "universal example" — namely, $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ — that contains every lattice.

The chapter has three conceptual movements:

  1. Equivalence relations + Eq X. Equivalence relations as binary relations satisfying R, S, T. Kernel of a function. Congruence relation. $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ as an algebraic lattice with many nice structural properties. Relative product $R \circ S$. Three types of representations 1, 2, 3 — differing in how joins are computed.
  2. Whitman's theorem (Theorem 4.1). Every lattice has a type 3 representation. Proof via weak representation + Sublemma 1 (extension by 3 new points) + Sublemma 2 (limit ordinal continuity). Then Theorem 4.2 (Pudlák-Tůma 1980): every finite lattice has a finite representation. Theorem 4.3: every lattice embeds in the subgroup lattice of a group (a corollary of Whitman).
  3. Modular and Arguesian. Lemma 4.4: type 2 representations satisfy the modular law. Theorem 4.5 (Jónsson): a lattice has type 2 representation ⟺ modular. The modular law's history (Dedekind 1890s) and the modular vs distributive distinction. Arguesian law (Schützenberger): stronger than modular, weaker than distributive. Theorem 4.6: type 1 ⟹ Arguesian. Examples (subgroups, abelian groups, ideals, vector subspaces, modules — all Arguesian). Haiman's 1987/1991 theorems on the Arguesian / type 1 gap.

The chapter's tone is more technical than Chs 1-3 — the Whitman proof is intricate, and the modular / Arguesian story has subtle distinctions. The companion expands the proof, isolates the structural insights, and connects to the broader picture: representation theorems are the backbone of universal-algebraic lattice theory.

New in Chapter 4 — full term-cards

NewEquivalence relation

A binary relation $E$ on $X$ satisfying (1) $x E x$ (reflexive), (2) $x E y$ ⟹ $y E x$ (symmetric), (3) $x E y, y E z$ ⟹ $x E z$ (transitive). Equivalently: the relation that partitions $X$ into disjoint blocks (equivalence classes).

NewKernel of a function

For $f : X \to Y$: $\ker f = \{(x, y) \in X^2 : f(x) = f(y)\}$. An equivalence relation. If $X, Y$ are algebras and $f$ a homomorphism, $\ker f$ is a congruence relation — preserved by all operations.

NewEq X — partition lattice

The lattice of all equivalence relations on $X$, ordered by set containment ($R \le S$ iff $R \subseteq S$). Top = universal relation $X^2$; bottom = equality $=$. Algebraic, strongly atomic, semimodular, relatively complemented, simple. Meet = set intersection. Join = transitive closure of union.

NewRelative product $R \circ S$

For relations $R, S$ on $X$: $R \circ S = \{(x, y) : \exists z \in X$ with $x R z S y\}$. For equivalence relations: $R \subseteq R \circ S$ and $S \subseteq R \circ S$ (because of reflexivity). The chain $R \circ S \subseteq R \circ S \circ R \subseteq \dots$ has union $R \vee S$.

NewRepresentation by equivalence relations

A pair $(X, F)$ where $X$ is a set and $F : \mathcal{L} \to \textbf{Eq}\,X$ is a lattice embedding. Whitman's theorem: every lattice has a representation.

NewType 1 / 2 / 3 representations

Differ in how joins of $F(x)$ and $F(y)$ are computed:

  • Type 1: $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y)$.
  • Type 2: $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x)$.
  • Type 3: $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x) \circ F(y)$.

Type 1 ⟹ type 2 ⟹ type 3. Whitman's theorem gives type 3 for every lattice. Modular lattices have type 2 (Theorem 4.5). Arguesian lattices that are type 1 satisfy stronger conditions.

NewWeak representation

A pair $(U, F)$ where $U$ is a set and $F : \mathcal{L} \to \textbf{Eq}\,U$ is a one-to-one MEET homomorphism (meets preserved; joins maybe not). The intermediate object in Whitman's proof: start with a weak representation, extend it $\omega$ times to fix join failures, eventually obtain a (full) type 3 representation.

NewModular law (M)

$x \ge y$ ⟹ $x \wedge (y \vee z) = y \vee (x \wedge z)$. Equivalently: $x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z)$ (form M'). Dedekind 1890s. Special case of distributivity (D); fails in $\mathcal{N}_5$.

NewArguesian law (A)

The lattice identity:

$(a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \le a_2 \vee b_2$ implies $c_2 \le c_0 \vee c_1$,

where $c_i = (a_j \vee a_k) \wedge (b_j \vee b_k)$ for $\{i, j, k\} = \{0, 1, 2\}$. Stronger than modular, weaker than distributive. Modeled after Desargues' Law in projective geometry. Equivalent form (A'): $(a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \wedge (a_2 \vee b_2) \le a_0 \vee (b_0 \wedge (c \vee b_1))$ where $c = c_2 \wedge (c_0 \vee c_1)$.

NewPermuting / commuting equivalence relations

$R, S \in \textbf{Eq}\,X$ permute if $R \circ S = S \circ R$ (Exercise 4: equivalent to $R \vee S = R \circ S$). Sublattices of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ in which all pairs permute give type 1 representations. The lattice of normal subgroups of a group is exactly such a sublattice (cosets permute).

Deep dive — extended explainers

Deep dive

Whitman's proof technique — the Cayley-style construction

Whitman's proof (Theorem 4.1) is a paradigmatic example of transfinite recursion in lattice theory. The structure:

  1. Start with a "weak" representation — meets are preserved, but joins might not be. Specifically, $F(x \vee y)$ might be strictly bigger than $F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x) \circ F(y)$.
  2. Sublemma 1: fix one join failure at a time. If $(p, q) \in F(x \vee y)$ but $(p, q) \notin F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x) \circ F(y)$, extend $U$ to $V = U \cup \{r, s, t\}$ (three new points) and define new equivalence relations $G(z)$ on $V$ that fix this one join failure.
  3. Sublemma 2: limit-ordinal continuity. When the recursion encounters a limit ordinal, define the representation as the union of the previous stages. Verify it's a valid weak representation.
  4. Iterate the join-fixing $\omega$ times. Each iteration introduces new join failures (because of the new points), so we keep going. After $\omega$ iterations, we get a representation $(W, H)$ that is type 3 (Whitman) or type 2 (Jónsson, with appropriate adjustments for modular).

Why three new points? The proof uses a 4-element diagram: $p \to r \to s \to t \to q$ with edges labeled $G(x), G(y), G(x), G(y)$. Two new points are insufficient — Theorems 4.5 and 4.6 (modular and Arguesian) show that with FEWER intermediate points, you'd get stronger laws (modular for 2 intermediate, Arguesian for 1 intermediate).

The proof is reminiscent of Cayley's group representation: build the universal example by adjoining missing structure recursively. For groups, you adjoin permutations; for lattices, equivalence relations.

Deep dive

Dedekind's modular law (1890s) and module theory

Richard Dedekind invented the modular law in the 1890s while studying ideals. Two key papers:

  • Dedekind 1892 (Supplement XI to Dirichlet's Vorlesungen über Zahlentheorie) — first appearance.
  • Dedekind 1897 (Über Zerlegungen von Zahlen durch ihre grössten gemeinsamen Teiler) — systematic development.
  • Dedekind 1900 (Über die von drei Moduln erzeugte Dualgruppe) — the free modular lattice on three generators (28 elements).

Dedekind proved that the lattice of normal subgroups of a group is modular. The lattice of submodules of a module is modular. The lattice of ideals of a ring is modular. The pattern: "normal subobjects" are modular.

The lattice abstraction: a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is modular iff $x \ge y$ ⟹ $x \wedge (y \vee z) = y \vee (x \wedge z)$, equivalently iff $\mathcal{L}$ does NOT contain $\mathcal{N}_5$ as a sublattice (Dedekind 1900; restated as Theorem 9.1 in Nation).

The chapter's modular content (Lemma 4.4 + Theorem 4.5 — Jónsson) connects modularity to the representation theory: a lattice has a type 2 representation iff it is modular. So modular lattices are exactly the sublattices of partition lattices in which joins are computed by the "shorter" formula $R \circ S \circ R$ instead of $R \circ S \circ R \circ S$.

Deep dive

The Arguesian / Desargues / projective-geometry connection

The Arguesian law has a beautiful connection to projective geometry. Desargues' theorem (Girard Desargues, 1648) states: in a projective plane, two triangles are perspective from a point if and only if they are perspective from a line. This is one of the foundational theorems of projective geometry.

Schützenberger 1945: Desargues' theorem can be expressed as a lattice identity (the Arguesian identity) that holds in the lattice of subspaces of a projective space iff the geometry is Desarguesian. So:

Desarguesian projective geometry ⟺ subspace lattice satisfies Arguesian law.

Jónsson 1953: showed the Arguesian identity holds in every lattice of commuting equivalence relations — i.e., in every lattice with a type 1 representation. Theorem 4.6 in Nation is this result.

Examples of lattices satisfying the Arguesian law:

  • Lattice of normal subgroups of any group (cosets permute).
  • Lattice of subgroups of any abelian group (everything commutes).
  • Lattice of ideals of any ring (ideals add commutatively).
  • Lattice of subspaces of any vector space (= subspace lattice of $\mathbb{P}^n$).
  • Lattice of submodules of any module.

Freese-Jónsson 1976: if $\mathcal{V}$ is a variety of algebras with all congruence lattices modular (e.g., groups, rings, modules), then all those congruence lattices are also Arguesian.

Haiman 1987, 1991: showed the converse of Theorem 4.6 fails — there are Arguesian lattices that do NOT have a type 1 representation. So "Arguesian" is strictly weaker than "type 1 representable." The exact characterisation of type 1 representable lattices remains an open problem.

Pálfy-Szabó 1995: subgroup lattices of abelian groups satisfy an identity that does NOT hold in all normal subgroup lattices — refining the variety hierarchy further.

Part A · Eq X and representations

From R, S, T to the structural properties of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. Three flavors of representation, distinguished by how joins are computed.

A1 Equivalence relations · Eq X · relative product

$\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is the algebraic lattice of equivalence relations on $X$. Algebraic, strongly atomic, semimodular, relatively complemented, simple.
Definition · equivalence relation

An equivalence relation on a set $X$ is a binary relation $E$ satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in X$:

  1. $x E x$ (reflexive)
  2. $x E y$ implies $y E x$ (symmetric)
  3. $x E y$ and $y E z$ imply $x E z$ (transitive)

We think of an equivalence relation as partitioning the set $X$ into blocks of $E$-related elements, called equivalence classes. Conversely, any partition of $X$ into a disjoint union of blocks induces an equivalence relation on $X$: $x E y$ iff $x$ and $y$ are in the same block. We write $x E y$ and $(x, y) \in E$ interchangeably.

Kernel · congruence relation

The most important equivalence relations are those induced by maps. If $Y$ is another set and $f : X \to Y$ is any function, then

$$\ker f = \{(x, y) \in X^2 : f(x) = f(y)\}$$

is an equivalence relation, called the kernel of $f$. If $X$ and $Y$ are algebras and $f : X \to Y$ is a homomorphism, then $\ker f$ is a congruence relation (preserved by all operations).

Eq X — the partition lattice

Thinking of binary relations as subsets of $X^2$, the axioms (1)-(3) for an equivalence relation are finitary closure rules. Thus the collection of all equivalence relations on $X$ forms an algebraic lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. The order on $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is given by set containment:

$$R \le S \quad \text{iff} \quad R \subseteq S \text{ in } \mathfrak{P}(X^2) \text{ iff } (x, y) \in R \implies (x, y) \in S.$$

The greatest element of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is the universal relation $X^2$, and its least element is the equality relation $=$. The meet operation in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is set intersection: $(x, y) \in \bigwedge_{i \in I} E_i$ iff $x E_i y$ for all $i \in I$. The join $\bigvee_{i \in I} E_i$ is the transitive closure of $\bigcup_{i \in I} E_i$, so $(x, y) \in \bigvee E_i$ iff there exists a finite sequence of elements $x_j$ and indices $i_j$ such that

$$x = x_0 \, E_{i_1} \, x_1 \, E_{i_2} \, x_2 \dots x_{k-1} \, E_{i_k} \, x_k = y.$$

Properties of Eq X

The lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ has many nice properties: it is

  • algebraic (compactly generated; from finitary closure rules);
  • strongly atomic;
  • semimodular;
  • relatively complemented;
  • simple (no nontrivial congruences).

The terms "relatively complemented" and "simple" are defined in Chapter 10. Proofs of the structural properties are exercises in this chapter (Ex 6) and Chapter 11.

Crawley-Dilworth's Algebraic Theory of Lattices (1973), Chapter 12, gives the full structural account.

Relative product $R \circ S$

If $R$ and $S$ are relations on $X$, define the relative product

$$R \circ S = \{(x, y) \in X^2 : \exists z \in X \text{ with } x R z S y\}.$$

If $R, S$ are equivalence relations, then because $x R x$ we have $S \subseteq R \circ S$; similarly $R \subseteq R \circ S$. Thus

$$R \circ S \subseteq R \circ S \circ R \subseteq R \circ S \circ R \circ S \subseteq \dots$$

and it is not hard to see that $R \vee S$ is the union of this chain.

It is possible, however, that $R \vee S$ is in fact equal to some term in the chain — for example, this is always the case when $X$ is finite. Whitman's proof yields a representation in which this is always the case for any two equivalence relations that represent elements of the given lattice. The "depth" at which this happens is the type of the representation.

A2 Representations of types 1, 2, 3

Three "depths" of join expression. Type 1 ⟹ type 2 ⟹ type 3. Modular lattices give type 2; arbitrary lattices give type 3 (Whitman).
Representation by equivalence relations

To be precise, a representation (by equivalence relations) of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ is an ordered pair $(X, F)$ where $X$ is a set and $F : \mathcal{L} \to \textbf{Eq}\,X$ is a lattice embedding. We say that the representation is

  1. of type 1 if $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y)$ for all $x, y \in L$,
  2. of type 2 if $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x)$ for all $x, y \in L$,
  3. of type 3 if $F(x) \vee F(y) = F(x) \circ F(y) \circ F(x) \circ F(y)$ for all $x, y \in L$.

Clearly type 1 ⟹ type 2 ⟹ type 3.

Why three types?

The "depth" of the join expression measures how hard it is to compute lattice joins inside the partition lattice. The chapter will show:

  • Type 3 (Whitman 1946 / Jónsson 1953): EVERY lattice has a type 3 representation. The "weakest" condition; works for arbitrary lattices.
  • Type 2 (Jónsson): A lattice has a type 2 representation iff it is MODULAR (Theorem 4.5).
  • Type 1 (Schützenberger / Jónsson): A lattice with a type 1 representation satisfies the ARGUESIAN law (Theorem 4.6). The converse fails in general (Haiman 1987, 1991).

So the type hierarchy stratifies lattices by their structural complexity: arbitrary < modular < Arguesian (with strict inequalities on the type-1 side).

P. M. Whitman [1946] proved in Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. 52 that every lattice has a representation. In 1953 Bjarni Jónsson [Math. Scand. 1] found a simpler proof that gives a slightly stronger result.

B1 Weak representation · Sublemma 1 (extension by 3 new points)

Build a "weak" representation that preserves meets but maybe not joins. Sublemma 1 fixes one join failure by adding three new points.
Theorem 4.1 · Whitman / Jónsson

Every lattice has a type 3 representation.

Weak representation · the staging ground

Given a lattice $\mathcal{L}$, we use transfinite recursion to construct a type 3 representation of $\mathcal{L}$. Along the way we work with a more flexible structure:

A weak representation of $\mathcal{L}$ is a pair $(U, F)$ where $U$ is a set and $F : \mathcal{L} \to \textbf{Eq}\,U$ is a one-to-one MEET homomorphism. (Joins not required to be preserved.)

Order weak representations by

$$(U, F) \sqsubseteq (V, G) \quad \text{if} \quad U \subseteq V \text{ and } G(x) \cap U^2 = F(x) \text{ for all } x \in L.$$

I.e., $G$ extends $F$ by acting like $F$ on the original set $U$.

Initial weak representation

We can begin our construction by letting $(U_0, F_0)$ be the weak representation with $U_0 = L$ (the lattice itself, as a set) and $F_0(x)$ defined on $L$ by $(y, z) \in F_0(x)$ iff $y = z$ or $y \vee z \le x$.

Verifications: $F_0(x)$ is reflexive (by $y = y$), symmetric (by symmetry of $\vee$), transitive (by $y \vee z \le x$ and $z \vee w \le x$ giving $y \vee w \le y \vee z \vee w \le x$ in modular — but we don't have modular in general; this needs care). Meets: $F_0(x \wedge y) = F_0(x) \cap F_0(y)$ by direct verification. So $F_0$ is a meet homomorphism.

Joins fail in general: $F_0(x) \vee F_0(y)$ might be strictly larger than what we want. The "crucial step" is fixing these failures up one at a time.

Sublemma 1 · the extension step

If $(U, F)$ is a weak representation of $\mathcal{L}$ and $(p, q) \in F(x \vee y)$, then there exists $(V, G) \sqsupseteq (U, F)$ with $(p, q) \in G(x) \circ G(y) \circ G(x) \circ G(y)$.

Proof · the construction with 3 new points

Form $V$ by adding three new points to $U$: $V = U \cup \{r, s, t\}$, as in Figure 4.1. We want to make

$$p \,G(x)\, r \,G(y)\, s \,G(x)\, t \,G(y)\, q.$$

Accordingly, for $z \in L$ we define $G(z)$ to be the reflexive, symmetric relation on $V$ satisfying, for $u, v \in U$:

  1. $u \,G(z)\, v$ iff $u \,F(z)\, v$,
  2. $u \,G(z)\, r$ iff $z \ge x$ and $u \,F(z)\, p$,
  3. $u \,G(z)\, s$ iff $z \ge x \vee y$ and $u \,F(z)\, p$,
  4. $u \,G(z)\, t$ iff $z \ge y$ and $u \,F(z)\, q$,
  5. $r \,G(z)\, s$ iff $z \ge y$,
  6. $s \,G(z)\, t$ iff $z \ge x$,
  7. $r \,G(z)\, t$ iff $z \ge x \vee y$.

You must check that each $G(z)$ defined thusly really is an equivalence relation, i.e., that it is transitive. This is routine but a bit tedious to write down, so we leave it to the reader. There are four cases, depending on whether or not $z \ge x$ and on whether or not $z \ge y$. Straightforward though it is, this verification would NOT work if we had only added one or two new elements between $p$ and $q$ — see Theorems 4.5 and 4.6 below.

Now (1) says that $G(z) \cap U^2 = F(z)$. Since $F$ is one-to-one, this implies $G$ is also. Note that for $z, z' \in L$ we have $z \wedge z' \ge x$ iff $z \ge x$ and $z' \ge x$, and symmetrically for $y$. Using this with conditions (1)-(7), it is not hard to check that $G(z \wedge z') = G(z) \cap G(z')$. Hence, $G$ is a weak representation of $\mathcal{L}$, and clearly $(U, F) \sqsubseteq (V, G)$. ∎

B2 Sublemma 2 · Theorem 4.1 (Whitman / Jónsson)

The limit-ordinal step + the $\omega$-iterated fix-up. Result: every lattice has a type 3 representation.
Sublemma 2 · limit ordinal continuity

Let $\lambda$ be a limit ordinal, and for $\xi < \lambda$ let $(U_\xi, F_\xi)$ be weak representations of $\mathcal{L}$ such that $\alpha < \beta < \lambda$ implies $(U_\alpha, F_\alpha) \sqsubseteq (U_\beta, F_\beta)$. Let $V = \bigcup_{\xi < \lambda} U_\xi$ and $G(x) = \bigcup_{\xi < \lambda} F_\xi(x)$ for all $x \in L$. Then $(V, G)$ is a weak representation of $\mathcal{L}$ with $(U_\xi, F_\xi) \sqsubseteq (V, G)$ for each $\xi < \lambda$.

Proof · why · sketch

Let $\xi < \lambda$. Since $F_\alpha(x) = F_\xi(x) \cap U_\alpha^2 \subseteq F_\xi(x)$ whenever $\alpha < \xi$, and $F_\xi(x) = F_\beta(x) \cap U_\xi^2$ whenever $\beta \ge \xi$, for all $x \in L$ we have

$$G(x) \cap U_\xi^2 = (\bigcup_{\gamma < \lambda} F_\gamma(x)) \cap U_\xi^2 = \bigcup_{\gamma < \lambda} (F_\gamma(x) \cap U_\xi^2) = F_\xi(x).$$

Thus $(U_\xi, F_\xi) \sqsubseteq (V, G)$. Since $F_0$ is one-to-one, this implies $G$ is also.

It remains to show that $G$ is a meet homomorphism. Order-preservation is clear. For any $x, y \in L$: $G(x \wedge y) \subseteq G(x) \cap G(y)$ trivially. Conversely, if $(u, v) \in G(x) \cap G(y)$, then there exists $\alpha < \lambda$ with $(u, v) \in F_\alpha(x)$, and there exists $\beta < \lambda$ with $(u, v) \in F_\beta(y)$. Take $\gamma = \max(\alpha, \beta)$. Then $(u, v) \in F_\gamma(x) \cap F_\gamma(y) = F_\gamma(x \wedge y) \subseteq G(x \wedge y)$. ∎

Proof of Theorem 4.1 · putting it together

Now we use these two sublemmas to construct a type 3 representation of $\mathcal{L}$.

Step 1: fix all join failures from $(U_0, F_0)$. Start with the arbitrary weak representation $(U_0, F_0)$. Consider the set of all quadruples $(p, q, x, y)$ such that $p, q \in U_0$ and $x, y \in L$ and $(p, q) \in F_0(x \vee y)$. Arrange these into a well-ordered sequence $(p_\xi, q_\xi, x_\xi, y_\xi)$ for $\xi < \eta$. Applying Sublemmas 1 and 2 repeatedly, we obtain a sequence of weak representations $(U_\xi, F_\xi)$ for $\xi \le \eta$ such that:

  1. If $\xi < \eta$, then $(U_\xi, F_\xi) \sqsubseteq (U_{\xi+1}, F_{\xi+1})$ and $(p_\xi, q_\xi) \in F_{\xi+1}(x_\xi) \circ F_{\xi+1}(y_\xi) \circ F_{\xi+1}(x_\xi) \circ F_{\xi+1}(y_\xi)$.
  2. If $\lambda \le \eta$ is a limit ordinal, then $U_\lambda = \bigcup_{\xi < \lambda} U_\xi$ and $F_\lambda(x) = \bigcup_{\xi < \lambda} F_\xi(x)$.

Let $V_1 = U_\eta$ and $G_1 = F_\eta$. If $p, q \in U_0$ and $p F_0(x \vee y) q$, then $(p, q, x, y) = (p_\xi, q_\xi, x_\xi, y_\xi)$ for some $\xi < \eta$, so by (1):

$$F_0(x \vee y) \subseteq G_1(x) \circ G_1(y) \circ G_1(x) \circ G_1(y).$$

Note $(U_0, F_0) \sqsubseteq (V_1, G_1)$.

Step 2: iterate $\omega$ times. Of course, along the way we have probably introduced lots of new failures of the join property that need to be fixed up (because Sublemma 1 added new elements). So repeat this whole process $\omega$ times, obtaining a sequence:

$$(U_0, F_0) = (V_0, G_0) \sqsubseteq (V_1, G_1) \sqsubseteq (V_2, G_2) \sqsubseteq \dots$$

such that $G_n(x \vee y) \subseteq G_{n+1}(x) \circ G_{n+1}(y) \circ G_{n+1}(x) \circ G_{n+1}(y)$ for all $n \in \omega$ and $x, y \in L$.

Finally, let $W = \bigcup_{n \in \omega} V_n$ and $H(x) = \bigcup_{n \in \omega} G_n(x)$ for all $x \in L$. By Sublemma 2 applied to the chain at limit $\omega$, $(W, H)$ is a weak representation of $\mathcal{L}$. By construction, $H$ satisfies $H(x \vee y) \subseteq H(x) \circ H(y) \circ H(x) \circ H(y)$ for all $x, y \in L$. Combined with the trivial reverse inclusion, equality holds — and you get a type 3 representation of $\mathcal{L}$. ∎

Why this is "transfinite recursion"

Whitman's proof structure is a paradigm of transfinite construction in lattice theory:

  1. Successor step: Sublemma 1 fixes one join failure.
  2. Limit step: Sublemma 2 takes unions at limit ordinals.
  3. Outer iteration: Iterate the join-fixing $\omega$ times to handle the new failures introduced by Step 1.

The same pattern recurs in: subgroup-lattice constructions (Theorem 4.3), Birkhoff's theorem on free lattices (Ch 6), Hanf's theorem (Ch 3), and Loewy series in Călugăreanu Ch 5. The trick is always: at each step, fix one local failure; at limit ordinals, take unions; iterate enough times that all failures are fixed.

B3 Theorem 4.2 (Pudlák-Tůma) · Theorem 4.3 (subgroup embedding)

For finite lattices: a finite representation exists (Pudlák-Tůma 1980). Birkhoff's corollary: every lattice embeds in a subgroup lattice.
Theorem 4.2 · Pudlák-Tůma 1980

Every finite lattice has a representation $(Y, G)$ with $Y$ finite.

Why Theorem 4.2 is famous (and hard)

Since the proof of Theorem 4.1 involves transfinite recursion, it produces a representation $(X, F)$ with $X$ infinite, even when $\mathcal{L}$ is finite. For a long time one of the outstanding questions of lattice theory was whether every finite lattice can be embedded into the lattice of equivalence relations on a finite set.

In 1980, Pavel Pudlák and Jiří Tůma showed that the answer is YES. The proof is quite difficult. Their paper Every finite lattice can be embedded in a finite partition lattice (Algebra Universalis 10, 74-95) is one of the celebrated results in 20th-century lattice theory.

The Pudlák-Tůma construction is intricate, using a combinatorial argument that builds a finite $Y$ inductively from the finite lattice $\mathcal{L}$. Modern improvements (Wehrung et al., 2018+) give "tight" embeddings with controlled $|Y|$ in terms of $|L|$.

Theorem 4.3 · Birkhoff's subgroup embedding

Every lattice can be embedded into the lattice of subgroups of a group.

Proof · sketch

One of the motivations for Whitman's theorem was Garrett Birkhoff's observation, made in the 1930's, that a representation of a lattice $\mathcal{L}$ by equivalence relations induces an embedding of $\mathcal{L}$ into the lattice of subgroups of a group.

Given a representation $(X, F)$ of $\mathcal{L}$, let $\mathcal{G}$ be the group of all permutations on $X$ that move only finitely many elements (= the symmetric group with finite support), and let $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$ denote the lattice of subgroups of $\mathcal{G}$. Define $h : \mathcal{L} \to \textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$ by

$$h(a) = \{\pi \in \mathcal{G} : x \,F(a)\, \pi(x) \text{ for all } x \in X\}.$$

I.e., $h(a)$ is the subgroup of permutations that "respect" the equivalence relation $F(a)$. Then it is not too hard to check that $h$ is a lattice embedding. ∎

Two universal examples

Theorems 4.1 and 4.3 give two "universal examples" of lattices:

  • Partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$: every lattice embeds via Whitman.
  • Subgroup lattice $\textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G}$: every lattice embeds via Birkhoff's corollary.

Both are the lattice-theoretic analogues of Cayley's theorem for groups (every group embeds in a permutation group). The partition lattice is more "combinatorial"; the subgroup lattice is more "algebraic." Together, they certify that the abstract definition of "lattice" is "correct" — every model is, up to isomorphism, a sublattice of a partition lattice (or equivalently, of a subgroup lattice).

C1 Lemma 4.4 · Theorem 4.5 (Jónsson) · the modular law

Type 2 representations force modularity. Conversely, every modular lattice has a type 2 representation. Dedekind's modular law placed in context.

Not all lattices have representations of type 1 or 2, so it is natural to ask which ones do. First we consider sublattices of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with type 2 joins.

Lemma 4.4

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with the property that $R \vee S = R \circ S \circ R$ for all $R, S \in L$. Then $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies

$$(M) \quad x \ge y \quad \text{implies} \quad x \wedge (y \vee z) = y \vee (x \wedge z).$$

The implication $(M)$ is known as the modular law.

Proof · why

Assume that $\mathcal{L}$ is a sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with type 2 joins, and let $A, B, C \in L$ with $A \ge B$. If $p, q \in X$ and $(p, q) \in A \wedge (B \vee C)$, then

$$p \,A\, q \quad \text{and} \quad p \,B\, r \,C\, s \,B\, q$$

for some $r, s \in X$ (see Figure 4.2). Since

$$r \,B\, p \,A\, q \,B\, s$$

and $B \le A$, we have $(r, s) \in A \wedge C$. It follows that $(p, q) \in B \vee (A \wedge C)$. Thus $A \wedge (B \vee C) \le B \vee (A \wedge C)$. The reverse inclusion is trivial, so we have equality. ∎

Theorem 4.5 · Jónsson — modular ⟺ type 2

A lattice has a type 2 representation if and only if it is modular.

Proof · why · sketch

($\Leftarrow$) Jónsson 1953 gave a slight variation of the proof of Theorem 4.1 that shows every modular lattice has a type 2 representation. The variation: in Sublemma 1, we add only TWO new points (instead of three) between $p$ and $q$ — yielding a 3-point chain that gives type 2 joins. The transitivity verification works precisely under the modular hypothesis (which is why the standard 3-new-point construction in Sublemma 1 was needed for general lattices — see the comment in Nation's text after Sublemma 1).

($\Rightarrow$) Combine Lemma 4.4 (type 2 ⟹ modular) with the embedding hypothesis. ∎

The modular law and its history

The modular law $(M)$ plays an important role in lattice theory. It was invented in the 1890s by Richard Dedekind, who showed that the lattice of normal subgroups of a group is modular [Dedekind 1892, 1897]. Note that $(M)$ FAILS in the pentagon $\mathcal{N}_5$. In fact, Dedekind proved that a lattice is modular if and only if it does not contain the pentagon as a sublattice (this is Theorem 9.1 in Nation's book — see Călugăreanu Ch 1 for a parallel treatment).

The modular law is equivalent to the equation:

$$(M') \quad x \wedge ((x \wedge y) \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z).$$

It is easily seen to be a special case of (and hence weaker than) the distributive law:

$$(D) \quad x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z).$$

Specifically: $(M)$ says that $(D)$ should hold for $x \ge y$.

C2 Arguesian law · Theorem 4.6 · Haiman

Arguesian: stronger than modular, weaker than distributive. Type 1 ⟹ Arguesian (Schützenberger / Jónsson). Haiman: converse fails.

Note that the normal subgroup lattice of a group has a natural representation $(X, F)$: take $X = G$ and $F(N) = \{(x, y) \in G^2 : xy^{-1} \in N\}$. This representation is in fact type 1 (Exercise 3), and Jónsson showed that lattices with a type 1 representation, or equivalently sublattices of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ in which $R \vee S = R \circ S$, satisfy an implication stronger than the modular law. A lattice is said to be Arguesian if it satisfies

Arguesian law (A)

$$(A) \quad (a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \le a_2 \vee b_2 \quad \text{implies} \quad c_2 \le c_0 \vee c_1$$

where

$$c_i = (a_j \vee a_k) \wedge (b_j \vee b_k) \quad \text{for } \{i, j, k\} = \{0, 1, 2\}.$$

The Arguesian law is (less obviously) equivalent to a lattice inclusion,

$$(A') \quad (a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \wedge (a_2 \vee b_2) \le a_0 \vee (b_0 \wedge (c \vee b_1))$$

where

$$c = c_2 \wedge (c_0 \vee c_1).$$

These are two of several equivalent forms of this law, which is stronger than modularity and weaker than distributivity. The Arguesian law is modeled after Desargues' Law in projective geometry.

Theorem 4.6 · type 1 ⟹ Arguesian

If $\mathcal{L}$ is a sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with the property that $R \vee S = R \circ S$ for all $R, S \in L$, then $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies the Arguesian law.

Corollary

Every lattice that has a type 1 representation is Arguesian.

Proof of Theorem 4.6 · the diagram chase

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a sublattice of $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ with type 1 joins. Assume $(A_0 \vee B_0) \wedge (A_1 \vee B_1) \le A_2 \vee B_2$, and suppose $(p, q) \in C_2 = (A_0 \vee A_1) \wedge (B_0 \vee B_1)$. Then there exist $r, s$ such that

$$p \,A_0\, r \,A_1\, q$$

$$p \,B_0\, s \,B_1\, q.$$

Since $(r, s) \in (A_0 \vee B_0) \wedge (A_1 \vee B_1) \le A_2 \vee B_2$, there exists $t$ such that $r \,A_2\, t \,B_2\, s$.

Now you can check that

$$(p, t) \in (A_0 \vee A_2) \wedge (B_0 \vee B_2) = C_1$$

$$(t, q) \in (A_1 \vee A_2) \wedge (B_1 \vee B_2) = C_0$$

and hence $(p, q) \in C_0 \vee C_1$. Thus $C_2 \le C_0 \vee C_1$, as desired. (This argument is diagrammed in Figure 4.3.) ∎

Examples of Arguesian lattices

It follows that the lattice of normal subgroups of a group is not only modular, but Arguesian (Exercise 3). All these types of lattices are Arguesian:

  • Lattice of normal subgroups of a group (cosets permute).
  • Lattice of subgroups of an abelian group (everything commutes).
  • Lattice of ideals of a ring (ideals add commutatively).
  • Lattice of subspaces of a vector space (= subspace lattice of $\mathbb{P}^n$).
  • Lattice of submodules of a module.

More generally, Ralph Freese and Bjarni Jónsson (1976, Algebra Universalis 6, 225-228) proved that if $\mathcal{V}$ is a variety of algebras, all of whose congruence lattices are modular (such as groups or rings), then the congruence lattices of algebras in $\mathcal{V}$ are Arguesian.

The Arguesian / type 1 gap (Haiman)

Mark Haiman has shown that the converse of Theorem 4.6 is false: there are Arguesian lattices that do NOT have a type 1 representation [Haiman 1987 (Bull. AMS 16), 1991 (Algebra Universalis 28)]. In fact, his proof shows that lattices with a type 1 representation must satisfy equations that are STRICTLY STRONGER than the Arguesian law. It follows, in particular, that the lattice of normal subgroups of a group also satisfies these stronger equations, as do the other types of lattices mentioned in the preceding paragraph.

Interestingly, P. P. Pálfy and László Szabó (1995, Algebra Universalis 33) have shown that subgroup lattices of abelian groups satisfy an equation that does NOT hold in all normal subgroup lattices. So the variety hierarchy is even finer than just "Arguesian / type 1 / modular."

Open question (still as of Nation's notes): Does there exist a set of equations $\Sigma$ such that a lattice has a type 1 representation iff it satisfies all the equations of $\Sigma$? Haiman proved that if such a $\Sigma$ exists, it must contain infinitely many equations. In Chapter 7 we will see that a class of lattices is characterised by a set of equations iff it is closed with respect to direct products, sublattices, and homomorphic images. The class of lattices having a type 1 representation is easily seen to be closed under sublattices and direct products, so the question is equivalent to: Is the class of all lattices having a type 1 representation closed under homomorphic images?

  • $\{\{a, b\}, \{c\}\}$, $\{\{a, c\}, \{b\}\}$, $\{\{a\}, \{b, c\}\}$ — 3 partitions with one 2-block, one 1-block.
  • $\{\{a, b, c\}\}$ — 1 block (top, universal relation).

Total: 5 equivalence relations, forming the lattice $\mathcal{M}_3$ (the diamond): bottom = equality, three atoms (the 2-block partitions), top = universal. So $\textbf{Eq}\,\{a, b, c\} \cong \mathcal{M}_3$.

$|X| = 4$. Bell number $B_4 = 15$. Partitions of $\{a, b, c, d\}$:

  • 1 partition with 4 blocks (equality): bottom.
  • $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ partitions with one 2-block + two 1-blocks.
  • $\binom{4}{2} \cdot \binom{2}{2}/2 = 3$ partitions with two 2-blocks (e.g., $\{\{a,b\}, \{c,d\}\}$).
  • $\binom{4}{3} \cdot \binom{1}{1} = 4$ partitions with one 3-block + one 1-block.
  • 1 partition with 1 block (universal): top.

Total $1 + 6 + 3 + 4 + 1 = 15$. The Hasse diagram is more complex; the lattice has length 3 and is non-modular only if checked carefully — actually $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is always semimodular but typically not modular for $|X| \ge 4$. ∎

Ex. 4.2Find representations in Eq X for $\mathfrak{P}(Y)$, $\mathcal{N}_5$, $\mathcal{M}_n$
Find representations in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ for (a) $\mathfrak{P}(Y)$, $Y$ a set, (b) $\mathcal{N}_5$, (c) $\mathcal{M}_n$, $n < \infty$.

(a) $\mathfrak{P}(Y)$ in some $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. For each subset $A \subseteq Y$, define an equivalence on $X = Y \times \{0, 1\}$ where two elements are equivalent iff they agree on the first coordinate AND if both have second coordinate in some specific way. Concretely: take $X = Y$ and define $F(A) = (A \times A) \cup \Delta_X$ where $\Delta_X$ is the equality relation. Then $F : \mathfrak{P}(Y) \to \textbf{Eq}\,Y$ — but does this preserve joins? $F(A) \vee F(B) = F(A \cup B)$? Verify by computing joins directly. The standard result: yes, $\mathfrak{P}(Y) \cong \{F(A) : A \subseteq Y\} \subseteq \textbf{Eq}\,Y$.

(b) $\mathcal{N}_5$. Pentagon: $0 < a < c < 1$, $0 < b < 1$, $a \| b$, $c \| b$. Need $X$ such that $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ has a sublattice isomorphic to $\mathcal{N}_5$. By Pudlák-Tůma 1980, $|X|$ can be finite. The smallest such $X$: actually for $\mathcal{N}_5$, $|X| = 5$ suffices using a specific construction (Whitman's proof gives an explicit construction).

(c) $\mathcal{M}_n$. $\mathcal{M}_n$ is the diamond with $n$ atoms. Take $X = \{0, 1, \dots, n+1\}$ or similar. The atoms are $n$ different equivalence relations that pairwise generate the full universal relation. Concrete: $X = \mathbb{Z}_n$, atoms $A_i$ = "everything is equivalent to its image under translation by $i$." Or use Pudlák-Tůma's explicit construction. ∎

Ex. 4.3Standard coset representation is type 1
Let $\mathcal{G}$ be a group. Let $F : \textbf{Sub}\,\mathcal{G} \to \textbf{Eq}\,G$ be the standard representation by cosets: $F(H) = \{(x, y) \in G^2 : xy^{-1} \in H\}$. (a) Verify that $F(H)$ is indeed an equivalence relation. (b) Verify that $F$ is a lattice embedding. (c) Show that $F(H) \vee F(K) = F(H) \circ F(K)$ iff $HK = KH$ (= $H \vee K$). (d) Conclude that the restriction of $F$ to the normal subgroup lattice $\mathcal{N}(\mathcal{G})$ is a type 1 representation.

(a) $F(H)$ reflexive: $x x^{-1} = e \in H$. Symmetric: $xy^{-1} \in H$ ⟹ $(xy^{-1})^{-1} = yx^{-1} \in H$. Transitive: $xy^{-1} \in H$ and $yz^{-1} \in H$ ⟹ $xz^{-1} = (xy^{-1})(yz^{-1}) \in H$. ✓

(b) Order-preserving: $H \subseteq K$ ⟹ $F(H) \subseteq F(K)$. Injective: $F(H) = F(K)$ ⟹ $xy^{-1} \in H$ iff $xy^{-1} \in K$ for all $x, y$, hence $H = K$ (taking $y = e$). Lattice: $F(H \cap K) = F(H) \cap F(K)$ direct check. $F(H \vee K)$ corresponds to the subgroup generated by $H \cup K$.

(c) $F(H) \circ F(K) = \{(x, z) : \exists y, xy^{-1} \in H$ and $yz^{-1} \in K\}$. Setting $a = xy^{-1}, b = yz^{-1}$: $xz^{-1} = ab \in HK$. So $F(H) \circ F(K) = \{(x, z) : xz^{-1} \in HK\}$. Similarly $F(H) \vee F(K)$ in $\textbf{Eq}\,G$ corresponds to the smallest equivalence relation containing both, which corresponds to the subgroup $H \vee K$ generated by $H \cup K$. The two are equal iff $HK = H \vee K$, equivalently $HK = KH$.

(d) If $H, K$ are normal in $\mathcal{G}$: $HK = KH = HK$ (cosets commute for normal subgroups), and $HK = H \vee K$. So $F(H) \vee F(K) = F(H) \circ F(K)$ — type 1. ∎

Ex. 4.4Permuting equivalence relations
Show that for $R, S \in \textbf{Eq}\,X$: $R \vee S = R \circ S$ ⟺ $S \circ R \subseteq R \circ S$ ⟺ $R \circ S = S \circ R$. (For this reason, such equivalence relations are said to permute.)

Strategy. Cycle through the three equivalences.

(1) ⟹ (2): If $R \vee S = R \circ S$, then $S \circ R \subseteq R \vee S = R \circ S$. ✓

(2) ⟹ (3): If $S \circ R \subseteq R \circ S$, then by symmetry of the argument (swap $R$ and $S$): $R \circ S \subseteq S \circ R$. Combined: $R \circ S = S \circ R$.

(3) ⟹ (1): If $R \circ S = S \circ R$, compute $R \circ S \circ R \circ S = (R \circ S) \circ (R \circ S) = (R \circ S) \circ (S \circ R)$ (by (3)) $= R \circ (S \circ S) \circ R = R \circ S \circ R$ (using $S \circ S = S$ for equivalences) $= (R \circ S) \circ R = (S \circ R) \circ R = S \circ R \circ R = S \circ R = R \circ S$. So the chain $R \circ S \subseteq R \circ S \circ R \subseteq R \circ S \circ R \circ S \subseteq \dots$ stabilises at $R \circ S$. Hence $R \vee S = R \circ S$. ✓ ∎

Ex. 4.5Complete representation theorem for algebraic lattices
Recall from Exercise 6 of Chapter 3 that a complete sublattice of an algebraic lattice is algebraic. (a) Let $\mathcal{S}$ be a join semilattice with 0. Assume that $\varphi : \mathcal{S} \to \textbf{Eq}\,X$ is a join homomorphism with the properties: (i) for each pair $a, b \in X$ there exists $\sigma(a, b) \in S$ such that $(a, b) \in \varphi(s)$ iff $s \ge \sigma(a, b)$, and (ii) for each $s \in S$, there exists a pair $(x_s, y_s)$ such that $(x_s, y_s) \in \varphi(t)$ iff $s \le t$. Show that $\varphi$ induces a complete representation $\overline{\varphi} : \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S}) \to \textbf{Eq}\,X$. (b) Indicate how to modify the proof of Theorem 4.1 to obtain, for an arbitrary join semilattice $\mathcal{S}$ with 0, a set $X$ and a join homomorphism $\varphi : \mathcal{S} \to \textbf{Eq}\,X$ satisfying (i) and (ii). (c) Conclude that a complete lattice $\mathcal{L}$ has a complete representation by equivalence relations if and only if $\mathcal{L}$ is algebraic.

(a) Define $\overline{\varphi}(I) = \bigvee_{s \in I} \varphi(s)$ for $I \in \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{S})$. By (i) and (ii), this is well-defined and a complete embedding.

(b) Modify Theorem 4.1's construction by allowing the lattice $\mathcal{L}$ to be a join semilattice with 0; the proof goes through with minor adjustments.

(c) $\mathcal{L}$ algebraic ⟺ $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{L}^c)$ (Theorem 3.3) ⟺ by (a) and (b), $\mathcal{L}$ has a complete representation. ∎

Ex. 4.6Eq X is strongly atomic, semimodular, algebraic
Prove that $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ is a strongly atomic, semimodular, algebraic lattice.

Algebraic. Equivalence relations are defined by finitary closure rules ($x R y$ and $y R z$ ⟹ $x R z$, etc.). By Theorem 3.2, the lattice of $\Sigma$-closed subsets of $X^2$ — which is $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ — is algebraic. Compact elements: equivalence relations whose nontrivial classes are finite (= finitely generated equivalences).

Strongly atomic. Let $R > S$ in $\textbf{Eq}\,X$. There is some pair $(p, q) \in R \setminus S$. Define $T$ = smallest equivalence relation containing $S \cup \{(p, q)\}$. Then $T \succ S$ (cover) and $T \le R$. So we have $u = T$ with $R \ge u \succ S$.

Semimodular. If $R \succ R \wedge S$, show $R \vee S \succ S$. The atomic step is: removing one pair from $R$ to get $R \wedge S$, the corresponding step in the join is adding one new equivalence pair to $S$ to get $R \vee S$. ∎

Ex. 4.7Type 1 ⟹ Arguesian inclusion (A')
Prove that a lattice with a type 1 representation satisfies the Arguesian inclusion (A').

Strategy. Build on Theorem 4.6's proof. The Arguesian inclusion (A') is equivalent to (A), but the inclusion form is sometimes easier to verify directly.

(A') states: $(a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \wedge (a_2 \vee b_2) \le a_0 \vee (b_0 \wedge (c \vee b_1))$ where $c = c_2 \wedge (c_0 \vee c_1)$.

Diagram chase: take $(p, q) \in (A_0 \vee B_0) \wedge (A_1 \vee B_1) \wedge (A_2 \vee B_2)$. By type 1: $(p, q) \in A_i \circ B_i$ for each $i$. So there are $r_0, r_1, r_2$ with $p A_i r_i B_i q$.

Use the type 1 hypothesis to combine: $r_i A_i p A_j r_j$ gives $(r_i, r_j) \in A_i \vee A_j = A_i \circ A_j$ in type 1 representation. Similarly for $B$'s. Building the diagram (Figure 4.3 style), derive $(p, q) \in A_0 \vee (B_0 \wedge (C \vee B_1))$ where $C = C_2 \wedge (C_0 \vee C_1)$ — that's (A'). ∎

Ex. 4.88-variable identity beyond Arguesian (Haiman)
Mimicking the proof of Theorem 4.6, find an 8-variable implication, like the Arguesian law, that holds in every lattice of permuting equivalence relations. (The hard part of Haiman's work was to show that such laws are not a consequence of the Arguesian law, nor of each other as more variables are added.)

Strategy. Mimic the Arguesian proof but with FOUR triangles instead of three. Each triangle introduces a new variable pair $(a_i, b_i)$, and the corresponding combinations involve $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ terms $c_{ij} = (a_k \vee a_l) \wedge (b_k \vee b_l)$ for $\{i, j, k, l\} = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$.

The 8-variable implication takes the form:

$(a_0 \vee b_0) \wedge (a_1 \vee b_1) \le a_2 \vee b_2 \vee a_3 \vee b_3$ implies $c_{23} \le c_{02} \vee c_{12} \vee c_{03} \vee c_{13}$ (or similar combinatorial pattern).

The exact form depends on which "diagram" you start with — Haiman's contribution was to show that for each diagram of size 4, 5, 6, etc., you get a NEW identity not implied by the previous ones. So lattices with type 1 representation satisfy an INFINITE family of identities — strictly stronger than Arguesian.

Detailed forms appear in Haiman's papers Arguesian lattices which are not linear (Bull. AMS 16, 1987) and Arguesian lattices which are not type-1 (Algebra Universalis 28, 1991). The hard part: showing each new identity is independent of the previous ones (not derivable from earlier identities). This requires constructing specific lattices where the earlier identities hold but the new one doesn't. ∎

Looking Ahead — to Chapter 5 (Congruence Relations)

Chapter 5 specialises from arbitrary equivalence relations to CONGRUENCES — equivalence relations preserved by algebra operations.

Chapter 4 has established that every lattice embeds in a partition lattice $\textbf{Eq}\,X$ (Whitman). The chapter has also shown that modular and Arguesian lattices admit "shorter" join expressions in their representations — connecting representation theory to algebraic identities.

Chapter 5 (Congruence Relations) specialises the equivalence-relation theory to congruences — equivalence relations on an algebra that are preserved by ALL operations. Key ideas:

  • Congruence lattice $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$. The lattice of all congruences on $\mathcal{A}$. Always algebraic; for groups isomorphic to the normal subgroup lattice; for rings to the ideal lattice.
  • Funayama-Nakayama theorem. $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{L}$ is distributive for any lattice $\mathcal{L}$ — even when $\mathcal{L}$ itself is not.
  • Congruence permutability. Some varieties (groups, rings, modules) have congruences that always permute (= type 1 representations).
  • Congruence modularity / distributivity. Different "Mal'tsev conditions" for varieties.
  • Grätzer-Schmidt theorem. Every algebraic lattice arises as $\text{Con}\,\mathcal{A}$ for some algebra (foreshadowed in Ch 3).

The chapter's connection to Chapter 4: congruence lattices are sublattices of partition lattices (since congruences are equivalences); when the congruences permute, the representation is type 1, and the congruence lattice is Arguesian.
