Socle and Torsion Lattices

The atomic foundation of a lattice (its socle) and the lattices that climb upward atom-by-atom (torsion lattices). Plus the transfinite Loewy series, the radical-style torsion part, and the dual cycle / torsion-free apparatus.

~70 min read 23 sections + 5 connection panels 11 worked exercises Companion to Lattice Concepts of Module Theory, Springer 2000

Chapter Overview — atoms from below, torsion through and through

Where Chapter 4 looked sideways (essential vs pseudo-complement), Chapter 5 looks downward — at the atoms — and asks: how much of the lattice is "built from atoms"? The answer ranges from "just the socle" (a single floor) to "the whole lattice" (torsion).

The socle $s(L)$ is the join of all atoms of $L$ — the bottom-most "atomic foundation." In a vector space, the socle is the union of all $1$-dimensional subspaces, which is the whole space — vector spaces are entirely atomic. In $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module, there are no atoms (no minimal non-zero submodule), so the socle is $0$. The socle measures how much of the lattice is reachable from below by atomic steps.

A torsion lattice takes this further: it asks that every upper interval $1/a$ contain at least one atom — so from anywhere in the lattice, one atomic step is always available going up. Such lattices are also called semiartinian. The chapter's key result (Theorem 5.1) connects the socle to the apparatus of Chapter 4: $s(L) = \bigwedge \{e : e \text{ essential in } L\}$. The socle is the meet of all essentials. In an atomic lattice, this means: the socle IS the smallest essential element, and the essentials form a principal ideal.

The chapter then climbs: the Loewy series $0 = s_0(L) \le s_1(L) \le s_2(L) \le \cdots$ iterates the socle construction transfinitely. $s_1(L) = s(L)$. $s_{\sigma+1}(L) = s(1/s_\sigma(L))$ — the socle of the upper interval. At limit ordinals, take the join. Proposition 5.5: $L$ is torsion ⟺ this series eventually reaches $1$. The Loewy length is the smallest ordinal at which it stabilises.

The last two movements introduce: (4) torsion elements — those whose principal ideal is a torsion lattice — which form a principal ideal $t(L)/0$ with a "radical" property; the RSC (restricted socle condition) refines this; and (5) torsion-free elements — those that are not covered by anything — together with cycles: elements whose principal ideal is noetherian and distributive. The book's example: in the lattice of subgroups of an abelian group, cycles correspond exactly to cyclic subgroups.

The chapter's punchline in one paragraph

The socle is "what the atoms generate." A torsion lattice is "atom-reachable from above." The Loewy series climbs atom-by-atom transfinitely. The torsion part $t(L)$ collects all elements whose downward world is torsion. RSC constrains how essential elements interact with the socle — and the chapter culminates in showing that essentials, torsion, and the socle interact tightly: $e$ essential ⟺ $s(L) \le e$ AND $1/e$ is torsion (Prop 5.10). This is the apparatus that Chapter 7's "semi-artinian" theory and Chapter 6's Goldie dimension sit on top of.

Two terminology traps in this chapter

(1) "Torsion" has multiple senses. In module theory, an element $m \in M$ is "torsion" if $rm = 0$ for some non-zero $r$ in the ring. Călugăreanu's lattice torsion is different: a lattice $L$ is "torsion" if every upper interval $1/a$ has atoms. The two notions are related (both invoke "exhausting via finite-step structure") but not identical. The deep dive in the primer disentangles them.

(2) "Torsion-free" has TWO different meanings inside this very chapter. A torsion-free lattice (defined alongside torsion lattices in Part B) means: every $a/0$ is infinite. A torsion-free element (defined in Part E) means: no element of $L$ covers it. These are completely different concepts that share a name. The chapter uses both. Watch closely.

New in Chapter 5 — full term-cards

Each gets a full definition in the chapter prose; this primer flags the term and gives the gist.
New · Ch 5

socle $s(L)$ The join of all atoms of a lattice $L$ with $0$. Plain English: the "atomic foundation" — what's reachable as a join of all the indivisible pieces. Notation: $s(a/0) = $ socle of the principal ideal $a/0$ = join of atoms below $a$. The book also writes $s(a)$.

Etymology: socle is French for "pedestal" or "plinth" — the architectural base on which a column or statue stands. Borrowed into mathematics as the "base" of a structure built from atoms upward.

New · Ch 5

dual atom A maximal element of $L \setminus \{1\}$. The "co-atom": something one cover-step below $1$. The order-dual of an atom (which is one cover-step above $0$).

New · Ch 5

atom generated A lattice in which every element is a join of atoms. Strong condition: implies the socle is everything.

New · Ch 5 · Watch the overload

torsion lattice A lattice $L$ such that for every $a \ne 1$ in $L$, the upper interval $1/a$ contains at least one atom. Equivalently: every "upper world" is non-empty atomically — there's always a one-step extension available going up. Also called semiartinian in some literature (e.g., reference [33] in the book).

New · Ch 5 · Watch the overload

torsion-free lattice A lattice $L$ such that for every $a \ne 0$, the principal ideal $a/0$ is infinite. So below every non-zero element there's an infinite descending range.

WARNING: "torsion-free element" (Part E) is a DIFFERENT concept — it means an element not covered by anything. The two share a name but are separate ideas in this chapter.

New · Ch 5

semiartinian Synonym for "torsion lattice." Chosen because such lattices are "almost artinian" — every upper interval at least has atoms (DCC-flavoured), even if full DCC fails.

New · Ch 5

Loewy series The transfinite chain $0 = s_0(L) \le s_1(L) \le s_2(L) \le \cdots$ where $s_1 = s(L)$ is the socle, $s_{\sigma+1} = s(1/s_\sigma)$ is the next socle "above" the previous, and at limit ordinals $s_\sigma = \bigvee_{\alpha < \sigma} s_\alpha$. Named for Alfred Loewy, German mathematician (1873–1935), who introduced the construction in his work on primary decomposition.

New · Ch 5

Loewy length The smallest ordinal $\sigma$ such that $s_\sigma(L) = s_{\sigma+1}(L)$ — i.e., the chain stabilises. May be a transfinite ordinal.

New · Ch 5

torsion element An element $a \in L$ such that the principal ideal $a/0$ is a torsion sublattice. Plain English: "everything below $a$ is reachable atom-by-atom from above." The set of torsion elements is denoted $T(L)$. Trivially every atom is torsion.

New · Ch 5

torsion part $t(L)$ The join of all torsion elements of $L$. By Proposition 5.6, $T(L) = t(L)/0$ — the torsion elements form a principal ideal generated by $t(L)$.

New · Ch 5 · "Radical" terminology

radical property The property $t(1/t(L)) = t(L)$ — meaning: "above the torsion part, there is no further torsion." This is called a radical property because it mirrors the behaviour of nilradicals and Jacobson radicals in ring theory: they are idempotent under further radicalisation. Proposition 5.8 establishes this for $t$.

New · Ch 5

RSC (restricted socle condition) A lattice $L$ satisfies RSC if for every $b \ne 1$ that is essential in $L$, the upper interval $1/b$ contains atoms — i.e., $s(1/b) \ne b$. A "regularity" condition coupling essentialness and atom-reachability.

New · Ch 5

K-essential / K-socle A generalisation: let $K \subseteq L$ be downward-closed under compact descent (if $c$ compact, $c \le a$, $a \in K$ then $c \in K$). An element $e$ is K-essential if $e \wedge a \ne 0$ for every non-zero $a \in K$. The K-socle $s_K(L)$ is the join of all atoms in $K$.

New · Ch 5 · Different from "torsion-free lattice"

torsion-free element An element $a \in L$ such that no element of $L$ covers $a$. Plain English: "there's no immediate next step up from $a$." Trivially $1$ is torsion-free.

WARNING: Despite sharing the prefix, this is NOT the same concept as "torsion-free lattice" (above in Part B). The two are entirely different. The chapter uses both. The term cards in this primer are the cleanest place to keep them straight.

New · Ch 5

cycle (or cyclic element) An element $c$ such that $c/0$ is a noetherian, distributive sublattice of $L$. Plain English: "the world below $c$ is small and well-behaved (chain-friendly)." In the lattice of subgroups of an abelian group, cycles correspond exactly to cyclic subgroups.

New · Ch 5

locally cyclic group A group in which every finitely generated subgroup is cyclic. The chapter notes: an abelian group's subgroup lattice is distributive iff the group is locally cyclic. Every cyclic group is locally cyclic; not conversely (e.g., $\mathbb Q$ as an additive group is locally cyclic but not cyclic).

Deep dive — Socle in module theory

Where the chapter's "socle" comes from. This is the term's home in algebra.
Deep dive · Socle in module theory

The module-theoretic socle. For an $R$-module $M$, the socle $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ is the sum of all simple submodules of $M$ — equivalently, the largest semisimple submodule. (A simple module is a non-zero module with no proper non-trivial submodules; semisimple = direct sum of simples.) In the submodule lattice $S_R(M)$, simple submodules correspond to atoms (minimal non-zero submodules), so the lattice socle $s(S_R(M))$ recovers the module socle exactly.

Examples. For a vector space $V$ over a field $k$: every $1$-dimensional subspace is simple, so $\mathrm{soc}(V) = V$ — vector spaces are entirely "socle." For $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module: there are no simple submodules ($n\mathbb Z$ has further sub-submodules $kn\mathbb Z$ for $k > 1$), so $\mathrm{soc}(\mathbb Z) = 0$. For $\mathbb Z/12$: simple submodules are $\langle 4\rangle = \{0, 4, 8\} \cong \mathbb Z/3$ and $\langle 6\rangle = \{0, 6\} \cong \mathbb Z/2$, and their sum $\langle 2\rangle = \mathbb Z/6$ is the socle.

Why the socle matters. The socle measures the "fully decomposable" part of a module — the part that splits cleanly into simple pieces. Theorem 5.1 says the socle equals the meet of all essential submodules, which couples the socle to the chapter's essentialness apparatus. In practice: when computing invariants of a module, the socle is often the easiest to handle (it's semisimple, hence decomposable), and many proofs reduce to socle-based arguments.

Deep dive — Torsion in module theory vs lattice torsion

The two notions are related but not identical. This deep dive disentangles them.
Deep dive · Two senses of "torsion"

Module-theoretic torsion. For a module $M$ over an integral domain (or commutative ring) $R$: an element $m \in M$ is a torsion element if $rm = 0$ for some non-zero $r \in R$. The torsion submodule $T(M) = \{m \in M : rm = 0 \text{ for some } r \ne 0\}$ is a submodule (in domains). $M$ is torsion if $T(M) = M$; torsion-free if $T(M) = 0$.

Examples: in abelian groups (= $\mathbb Z$-modules), torsion = "finite order." $\mathbb Z/n$ is a torsion abelian group; $\mathbb Z$ is torsion-free; $\mathbb Q$ is torsion-free; $\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z$ is torsion (every element is killed by some $n$); the Prüfer group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ is torsion (every element is killed by some $p^k$).

Lattice torsion (this chapter). A lattice $L$ is torsion if every upper interval $1/a$ ($a \ne 1$) contains an atom. Plain English: "from anywhere below the top, you can take an atomic step."

The relationship. The two notions agree for the canonical example: when $L = S_R(M)$, $M$ is a torsion module iff $L$ is a torsion lattice (in the modular pseudo-complemented case, by Lemma 5.3 and the equivalence of "atomic" and "every non-zero submodule contains a simple"). So in the module setting the two senses coincide.

But they diverge in general. Consider the lattice of open subsets of $\mathbb R$ (a Heyting algebra, not arising from any module): every non-empty upper interval $1/U$ for $U \ne \mathbb R$ has many atoms (singletons of points in $\mathbb R \setminus U$ — wait, those aren't open, scratch that). Try: the rationals dense in $\mathbb R$ — neighbourhoods give a richer atomic structure. Lattice torsion in this setting is meaningful even though "torsion module" doesn't apply.

The takeaway. Lattice torsion abstracts the "atom-reachable" property of module torsion, but it's a strictly more general concept. The book uses "torsion" lattice-theoretically; when reading other texts, watch carefully which sense applies.

Deep dive — Loewy's primary decomposition motivation

Why the Loewy series is named for Loewy. Historical context for the construction.
Deep dive · Loewy and primary decomposition

Alfred Loewy (1873–1935) was a German mathematician at the University of Freiburg, working on group theory and matrix algebra. In his 1905 paper Über die Reduzibilität der Reduzible Gruppen Linearer Substitutionen and subsequent work, he introduced what we now call the Loewy series for finite-dimensional representations: an iterated socle filtration giving a canonical "stratification" of a representation into semisimple layers.

Loewy's original formulation. For a finite-dimensional representation $V$ of an algebra $A$ over a field, set $V_0 = 0$, $V_1 = \mathrm{soc}(V)$ (the largest semisimple sub-rep), and $V_{k+1} = $ the preimage in $V$ of $\mathrm{soc}(V/V_k)$ — i.e., $V_{k+1}/V_k = \mathrm{soc}(V/V_k)$. The series stabilises when the socle of the quotient is zero. The number of nontrivial steps is the Loewy length.

Why this matters. Loewy's series gives a canonical decomposition of a representation into "layers" of semisimple content — each $V_k/V_{k-1}$ is semisimple (in fact a direct sum of simples), and the number of layers is an invariant. For finite-dimensional representations of finite groups (in characteristic dividing the group order), this is the right way to organise the structure.

The lattice generalisation. Călugăreanu's chapter pulls this construction into pure lattice theory: $s_\sigma(L)$ replaces $V_\sigma$, the "socle-of-quotient" idea becomes "socle in $1/s_\sigma(L)$," and the Loewy length becomes the smallest ordinal at which the series stabilises. The transfinite generalisation (allowing $\sigma$ to be any ordinal) handles infinite-dimensional cases — including modules over non-noetherian rings — that Loewy's original framework couldn't. Proposition 5.5's clean characterisation, $L$ torsion ⟺ Loewy series reaches $1$, is the lattice-theoretic crystallisation of the original idea.

Part A · Socle

The atomic foundation of a lattice

The chapter begins with a single new construction — the join of all atoms — and a beautiful theorem (5.1) that connects it to the essentialness apparatus from Chapter 4.

SECTION A1 Socle — definition, dual atoms, basic properties

The socle is the join of all atoms. Atoms generate the "atomic floor" of the lattice; the socle measures how high that floor reaches.
Definition · socle

Let $L$ be a lattice with $0$. The socle of $L$, denoted $s(L)$, is the join of all atoms of $L$.

If $L$ has no atoms, $s(L) = 0$ by convention (the empty join). If $L$ has atoms but no upper bound on their join, the socle is the supremum (which exists in complete lattices).

Definition · dual atom

In a bounded lattice, a dual atom is a maximal element of $L \setminus \{1\}$. Equivalently, an element $m$ such that $m \prec 1$ (covers).

Dual atoms are the order-dual of atoms: an atom is one cover-step above $0$, a dual atom is one cover-step below $1$. The two notions are completely symmetric under flipping the lattice upside down.

Basic properties of the socle (book p. 47)

The book lists four basic properties as immediate consequences of the definition:

  1. $s(a/0) \le a$ — the socle of a principal ideal is below its generator. Trivial: every atom of $a/0$ is below $a$.
  2. $a \le b \Rightarrow s(a/0) \le s(b/0)$ — the socle is monotone in the principal ideal. Atoms below $a$ are also below $b$, so their join below $a$ is $\le$ their join below $b$.
  3. $s\bigl(\bigl(\bigwedge_{i \in I} a_i\bigr)/0\bigr) \le \bigwedge_{i \in I} s(a_i/0)$ — the socle of a meet is below the meet of socles.
  4. $\bigvee_{i \in I} s(a_i/0) \le s\bigl(\bigl(\bigvee_{i \in I} a_i\bigr)/0\bigr)$ — the join of socles is below the socle of the join.

Properties (3) and (4) are weak inequalities; the reverse generally fails. They reflect that "atom-below" interacts cleanly with order but not with the operations themselves.

Examples

  • $\mathcal P(M)$ (power set). Atoms = singletons. Socle = union of all singletons = $M$. So $\mathcal P(M)$ is "all socle" — it's atom generated.
  • Vector space subspace lattice. Atoms = $1$-dimensional subspaces. Socle = sum of all $1$-dimensional subspaces = the whole space. Atom generated.
  • $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module. Atoms = minimal non-zero submodules. None exist! (Every $n\mathbb Z$ has $kn\mathbb Z$ properly inside.) So $s(\mathbb Z) = 0$.
  • $\mathbb Z/12$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module. Submodules are $\langle d\rangle$ for $d \mid 12$. Atoms are $\langle 4\rangle = \{0, 4, 8\} \cong \mathbb Z/3$ and $\langle 6\rangle = \{0, 6\} \cong \mathbb Z/2$. Socle = $\langle 4\rangle + \langle 6\rangle = \langle 2\rangle = \{0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\} \cong \mathbb Z/6$. So the socle is a proper non-trivial sub-thing.

SECTION A2 Atom generated lattices — Lemma 5.1

In a modular compactly generated complemented lattice, every element is a join of atoms — the lattice is atom generated. The proof uses dual atoms, complementation, and Zorn.
Lemma 5.1

In a modular compactly generated complemented lattice, each element is a join of atoms.

Why · the strategy

Step 1. Each compact element that is not an atom contains at least one dual atom. Why? Take a compact $c$ which is not an atom. Consider $X = \{x \in L : x < c\}$. Each chain in $X$ has an upper bound by Lemma 2.4 (the compact lattice's Zorn-friendly version). Zorn's Lemma gives a maximal element of $X$ — a dual atom of $c$ contained in $c$.

Step 2. Each compact element contains at least one atom. Use Step 1 plus Exercise 1.37 (descend through dual atoms recursively until you hit an atom).

Step 3. Each non-zero element contains at least one atom. The lattice is compactly generated: every element is a join of compacts, and by Step 2 each compact has an atom inside.

Step 4. Each element is a join of atoms. Take any non-zero $a \in L$, and let $u$ = join of all atoms below $a$. We want $u = a$. The lattice is modular and complemented, so it's relative complemented (Exercise 1.36): the interval $a/0$ is itself complemented. If $u < a$, take $v$ a complement of $u$ in $a/0$. Then $v \ne 0$ and $v$ contains an atom (by Step 3), but that atom is below $a$ and not below $u$ (since $u \wedge v = 0$) — contradicting that $u$ was the join of all atoms below $a$. Hence $u = a$.

Definition · atom generated

A lattice in which each element is a join of atoms is called atom generated.

Lemma 5.1 says: modular + compactly generated + complemented ⟹ atom generated. Three structural properties (algebraicity, modularity, complementation) combine to force the lattice into the simplest possible "atomic" shape. This is a useful sufficient condition.

Why these three hypotheses

Modularity is needed for the relative-complementation step (Exercise 1.36). Without it, even complemented lattices can fail to have complemented intervals. Compactly generated is needed for "every non-zero element contains a compact, hence an atom." Without it, you could have atomless stretches in the lattice. Complemented is needed for the Zorn argument that builds atoms inside compact non-atoms.

Drop any one: vector space subspace lattices (modular + cg + complemented ⟹ atom generated, ✓). Submodule lattices over PIDs (modular + cg, but rarely complemented ⟹ rarely atom generated). $\mathbb Z$ as $\mathbb Z$-module: modular + cg + not complemented ⟹ not atom generated.

SECTION A3 Theorem 5.1 — the socle equals the meet of all essentials

The chapter's central theorem. In a compactly generated modular lattice, the socle is exactly the meet of all essential elements. This connects Part A (socle) to Chapter 4's essentialness apparatus.
Lemma 5.2

In an arbitrary lattice with zero, the socle is a lower bound for the meet of all essential elements of $L$: $$s(L) \le \bigwedge \{e \in L : e \text{ essential in } L\}.$$

Why

Let $0 \ne s$ be an atom of $L$ and let $e \ne 0$ be essential. We show $s \le e$. By essentialness of $e$: $s \wedge e \ne 0$. But $s$ is an atom, so $s \wedge e \in \{0, s\}$ — it must be $s$. Hence $s \le e$. So every atom is below every essential, and therefore $s(L) \le \bigwedge \{e\}$.

Theorem 5.1

In a compactly generated modular lattice, the socle equals the meet of all essential elements: $$s(L) = \bigwedge \{e \in L : e \text{ essential in } L\}.$$

Why · two halves

Let $s = \bigwedge \{e : e \text{ essential}\}$. By Lemma 5.2, $s(L) \le s$. We show the reverse inequality $s \le s(L)$.

Strategy: show that the principal ideal $s/0$ is complemented; then by Lemma 5.1 (applied inside $s/0$, which is itself a modular compactly generated lattice as a downward sublattice), each element of $s/0$ — including $s$ itself — is a join of atoms. Hence $s \le s(L)$.

Showing $s/0$ is complemented. Take $a \in s/0$. The lattice $L$ is pseudo-complemented (Cor 4.1, since compactly generated ⟹ upper continuous). Let $c$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$. Then $a \vee c$ is essential in $L$ (Cor 4.2), so $s \le a \vee c$. Now $c \wedge s \in s/0$ and is a complement of $a$ in $s/0$: $a \wedge (c \wedge s) \le a \wedge c = 0$, and $a \vee (c \wedge s) \stackrel{\mathrm{mod}}{=} (a \vee c) \wedge s = s$ (using modularity, with $a \le s$).

Hence $s/0$ is complemented; by Lemma 5.1 each element of $s/0$ is a join of atoms; so $s$ is a join of atoms; so $s \le s(L)$.

The structural reading

Theorem 5.1 says: the socle is precisely the "essentialness intersection." An element is below the socle iff it is below every essential element. Or contrapositively: an element is essential iff it sits above the socle (and additional conditions — see Prop 5.10 below). This couples the socle (a horizontal "atomic foundation") to essentialness (a vertical "thick everywhere" property) — the two are complementary measurements that determine each other in compactly generated modular lattices.

SECTION A4 Corollary 5.1 — $s(L)/0$ is a complemented lattice

An immediate but important consequence: the socle's principal ideal is itself complemented.
Corollary 5.1

In a modular compactly generated lattice $L$, the quotient sublattice $s(L)/0$ is a complemented lattice.

The proof was implicit in Theorem 5.1's proof: we showed $s(L)/0$ is complemented as the key step. Spelling it out as a corollary makes it usable: the principal ideal below the socle is one of the cleanest possible lattice structures (modular + complemented + compactly generated), and is therefore atom generated by Lemma 5.1.

The corollary also says: in module theory, the submodule lattice of the socle of any module is itself complemented — the socle's internal structure mirrors a Boolean-like decomposition into simple pieces.

SECTION A5 Atomic lattices — Propositions 5.1, 5.2, 5.3

When the lattice is atomic, the socle takes on extra force: it becomes the smallest essential element, and essentials form a principal ideal.

Recall: a lattice $L$ is atomic if every non-zero element contains an atom. (Stronger than just "has atoms"; means atoms are everywhere-reachable from below.)

Proposition 5.1

In a complete atomic lattice, the socle is essential.

Why

Take any non-zero $a \in L$. By atomicity, $a$ contains an atom $s$. Then $s \le a \wedge s(L)$, so $a \wedge s(L) \ne 0$. Hence $s(L)$ is essential.

Corollary 5.2

In an atomic modular compactly generated lattice, the socle is the smallest essential element.

Combining Theorem 5.1 ($s(L) = \bigwedge \{$essentials$\}$) with Proposition 5.1 ($s(L)$ is essential), the socle is itself an essential element below all others — i.e., the smallest essential.

Corollary 5.3

In an atomic modular compactly generated lattice, the essential elements form a principal ideal.

The principal ideal generated by $s(L)$ — i.e., $\{e \in L : s(L) \le e\}$ — is exactly the set of essential elements: $s(L)$ is the smallest essential, and any element above it is also essential (since essentialness is upward-closed). So the essentials are exactly the upper-set generated by $s(L)$.

A clean structural picture

In atomic modular compactly generated lattices, the lattice has a clean stratification:
$\quad\bullet$ Below the socle: complemented (Cor 5.1), atom generated.
$\quad\bullet$ The socle itself: the smallest essential element.
$\quad\bullet$ Above the socle: the essential elements (a principal upper-set).
$\quad\bullet$ Strictly above socle but not essential: nothing — every element above $s(L)$ is essential.
$\quad\bullet$ Not above socle: not essential.
So the dichotomy "essential or not" is decided by a single test: is $s(L)$ inside this element?

SECTION A6 The K-socle generalisation

A small generalisation that previews how the apparatus extends. Replace "all atoms" with "atoms in a chosen subset $K$" and re-run.
Definition · K-essential and K-socle

Let $K \subseteq L$ be a subset such that for each compact element $c$ and each $a \in K$ with $c \le a$: $c \in K$ (downward-closed under compact descent). An element $e$ is K-essential if for every $0 \ne a \in K$, $e \wedge a \ne 0$. The K-socle $s_K(L)$ is the join of all atoms from $K$.

This is a "weighted" version of the socle theory. Instead of "all atoms," we look at atoms in $K$. Instead of "essential everywhere," we look at "essential among $K$." A theorem analogous to Theorem 5.1 holds — the K-socle equals the meet of K-essential elements (Exercise 5.1 sketches this).

When is K-essentialness useful?

$K$ can be: the set of compact elements, the set of finitely-generated elements, the set of atoms, the set of cyclic elements, etc. The K-socle theory then applies the chapter's apparatus restricted to that subset. In module theory, $K$-essential typically arises when restricting attention to a particular family of submodules (e.g., torsion submodules, cyclic submodules, finitely generated submodules) — and the resulting K-socle measures the "atomic core" within that family.

Part B · Torsion lattices

Lattices that climb atom-by-atom

A torsion lattice is one where you can always take an atomic step upward. The chapter establishes their key properties: they are atomic, their socle is essential, the property descends through intervals, and noetherian + torsion ⟹ artinian.

SECTION B1 Definition — torsion, torsion-free, semiartinian

Three definitions from one page of the book. Mark them carefully — they get re-used throughout the rest of the chapter, and "torsion-free" gets re-used in a totally different sense in Part E.
Definition · torsion lattice

A lattice $L$ is said to have torsion (or is torsion) if for every $a \in L$ with $a \ne 1$, the upper interval $1/a$ contains at least one atom.

Plain English: from anywhere strictly below $1$, you can take at least one atomic step going up.

Definition · torsion-free lattice

A lattice $L$ is torsion-free if for every $a \ne 0$ in $L$, the principal ideal $a/0$ is infinite.

Plain English: below every non-zero element there's an infinite descending range.

Note · alternative name

Torsion lattices are also called semiartinian in some literature (the book cites reference [33]). The word "semiartinian" reflects that such lattices are "almost artinian": every upper interval has at least an atom (DCC-flavoured), even if full DCC fails.

"Torsion" and "semiartinian" are perfect synonyms for the lattice property defined here. Different texts use different conventions; the book uses both.

Reminder · "torsion-free element" is different

In Part E we will meet a separate notion: torsion-free element, defined as an element that no other element covers. This is NOT related to "torsion-free lattice" above. Two different concepts share the prefix; treat them as separate. The chapter uses both.

Examples

  • Vector space subspace lattice (finite-dim). Torsion (every $1/U$ for $U \ne V$ has $1$-dimensional extensions). Not torsion-free (principal ideals are finite).
  • $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z/p^n)$. Submodule lattice of $\mathbb Z/p^n$ — a chain of length $n$. Torsion (every interval $\mathbb Z/p^n / \mathbb Z/p^k$ for $k < n$ has an atom). Not torsion-free.
  • $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z)$. The lattice of subgroups of $\mathbb Z$ as a $\mathbb Z$-module (= ideal lattice of $\mathbb Z$). NOT torsion: take $a = 2\mathbb Z$; the upper interval $\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z$ has only two elements ($\mathbb Z$ and $2\mathbb Z$), so it has an atom $\mathbb Z$ — wait, this works for any non-zero proper $n\mathbb Z$. Actually $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z)$ has lots of atoms in upper intervals, so it IS torsion. Hmm, but $\mathbb Z$ itself isn't a torsion module... So torsion-as-lattice doesn't always match torsion-as-module. (See deep dive!)
  • $S_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Q)$. Submodule lattice of $\mathbb Q$. Atoms of $\mathbb Q$? $\mathbb Q$ has no minimal non-zero submodule. So $\mathbb Q/0$ is atomless, meaning $\mathbb Q$ is NOT torsion as a lattice. (And it's torsion-free as a module — no $r \in \mathbb Z \setminus \{0\}$ kills any non-zero $q \in \mathbb Q$.)

The examples confirm the deep dive's point: lattice-torsion is related to but not identical with module-torsion. They agree for "small enough" modules but diverge in general.

SECTION B2 Lemma 5.3 — torsion lattices are atomic

In a modular pseudo-complemented (or upper continuous) torsion lattice, every non-zero element contains an atom. So torsion ⟹ atomic in the well-behaved cases.
Lemma 5.3

Every modular pseudo-complemented (or upper continuous) torsion lattice is atomic.

Why

Take $0 \ne a \in L$, and let $b$ be a pseudo-complement of $a$ in $L$ (exists by Cor 4.1, since upper continuous ⟹ pseudo-complemented; or by Lemma 4.2 in the inductive case). Then $b \ne 1$ (otherwise $a \wedge b = a$ and pseudo-complementation gives $a = 0$).

Since $L$ is torsion, $1/b$ has at least one atom $s$. So $b \prec s$ in $1/b$, i.e., $s$ covers $b$.

By the maximality of $b$ as pseudo-complement of $a$: $a \wedge s \ne 0$. Set $u = a \wedge s$; we show $u$ is an atom in $L$.

Take $0 < x \le u$. Then $x \le a$, $x \le s$. We show $x = u$. By modularity (using $b \le s$): $s \wedge u = (x \vee b) \wedge s$ — and tracing through, $b \vee x = s$ (since $s$ is an atom in $1/b$). The modular law then gives $u = a \wedge s = a \wedge (b \vee x) = (a \wedge b) \vee x = 0 \vee x = x$. So $u = x$, i.e., $u$ is an atom. And $u \le a$.

The lemma's proof is a beautiful interplay of pseudo-complementation (which gives us $b$) and the torsion property (which gives us an atom $s$ in $1/b$). The intersection $a \wedge s$ — non-zero by maximality of $b$ — is constrained by modularity to be an atom.

SECTION B3 Proposition 5.2 — torsion ⟹ socle is essential

A direct consequence of Lemma 5.3 and Prop 5.1: in modular pseudo-complemented torsion lattices, the socle is essential.
Proposition 5.2

If $L$ is a modular pseudo-complemented (or upper continuous) torsion lattice, then $s(L)$ is an essential element of $L$.

Why

By Lemma 5.3, $L$ is atomic. By Proposition 5.1, in atomic complete lattices the socle is essential.

Note · the hypothesis can be weakened

The book remarks (after Prop 5.2): the same proof remains valid if the upper continuous hypothesis is weakened to inductive. So Prop 5.2 holds for modular inductive torsion lattices in general — not just upper continuous.

SECTION B4 Proposition 5.3 — torsion descends through intervals

Torsion is a "hereditary" property: in upper continuous modular lattices, $L$ is torsion iff every interval $1/a$ and $a/0$ is torsion.
Proposition 5.3

Let $L$ be an upper continuous modular lattice and let $a \in L$. The lattice $L$ is a torsion lattice if and only if the quotient sublattices $1/a$ and $a/0$ are also torsion lattices.

Why · sketch

$(\Rightarrow)$ The intervals $1/a$ inherit the torsion property trivially: any $b \in 1/a$ with $b \ne 1$ has, by torsion of $L$, an atom in $1/b$, which is also an atom in the interval-of-the-interval. For $a/0$: take $x < a$, consider $1/x$ in $L$; this contains an atom by torsion, and Lemma 5.3 (applied to torsion ⟹ atomic) gives an atom in $a/x$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $1/a$ and $a/0$ are torsion. Take any $b \ne 1$ in $L$. If $a \le b$: $b \in 1/a$, which is torsion, so $1/b$ has atoms. If $a \nleq b$: $a \wedge b < a$, so $a \wedge b \in a/0$ (torsion), and $a/(a \wedge b)$ has atoms. By modularity, $(a \vee b)/b \cong a/(a \wedge b)$ contains atoms (diamond iso). Hence $1/b$ contains atoms (atoms in $(a\vee b)/b$ are atoms in $1/b$ above $b$).

The proposition lets you check torsion locally: pick any $a$, verify torsion of $a/0$ and $1/a$, and torsion of the whole follows. This is useful for inductive arguments.

SECTION B5 Proposition 5.4 — joins of "torsion-element-intervals" stay torsion

If $a$ is the join of $a_i$ and each $a_i/0$ is torsion, then $a/0$ is also torsion. Builds toward Part D's torsion-element apparatus.
Proposition 5.4

Let $L$ be a modular lattice and $a = \bigvee_{i \in I} a_i$. If all the quotient sublattices $a_i/0$ are torsion lattices, then $a/0$ is also a torsion lattice.

Why

Take any $b < a$ in $a/0$. We show $a/b$ has an atom. Since $a = \bigvee a_i$ and $b < a$, there's some $i$ with $a_i \nleq b$ (otherwise $a = \bigvee a_i \le b$, contradiction). Then $a_i / (b \wedge a_i)$ is non-trivial, and torsion of $a_i/0$ gives an atom in this interval. By modularity, $(b \vee a_i)/b \cong a_i/(b \wedge a_i)$ has atoms (diamond iso). Hence $a/b$ contains atoms.

The proof generalises: any join of torsion ideals is itself a torsion ideal. Property (T3) in Part D will record this in the language of torsion elements.

SECTION B6 Theorem 5.2 — noetherian torsion ⟹ artinian + finite length

A clean characterisation: in modular settings, noetherian + torsion gives the strongest possible chain condition (artinian and finite length).
Theorem 5.2

Each noetherian torsion lattice is artinian (and of finite length).

Why · contradiction with maximality

The lattice being noetherian, take $a \in L$ to be maximal with the property "$a/0$ is artinian." We show $a = 1$ (which gives the result, since $a/0 = L$ artinian, and noetherian + artinian ⟹ finite length by Theorem 3.2).

Suppose for contradiction $a < 1$. By torsion, $1/a$ has an atom $b$, so $a \prec b$. We show $b/0$ is artinian, contradicting the maximality of $a$.

Take any decreasing chain $c_1 \ge c_2 \ge \cdots$ in $b/0$. Two cases:

(i) Some $c_m \le a$ — then $\{c_n\}_{n \ge m}$ is a chain in $a/0$, which is artinian, so the chain stabilises.

(ii) Every $c_n \nleq a$ — then by the technical implications (i)-(ii) of the book's proof: $a \vee c_n = b$ for all $n$ (using $a \prec b$); and $a \wedge c_{n+1} \le a \wedge c_n$ strictly until stabilisation. The sequence $\{a \wedge c_n\}$ lives in $a/0$, hence stabilises by artinian-ness of $a/0$, contradicting that $\{c_n\}$ does not stabilise.

Either way, $\{c_n\}$ stabilises. So $b/0$ is artinian, contradicting maximality of $a$.

Why this matters

In module theory, this is the lattice-theoretic reason why a noetherian module that is also "atomic" (= every non-zero submodule contains a simple) must be artinian — and hence have finite length. This is a powerful structural result: it says the two strongest chain conditions are closely linked once torsion is present.

Examples: a noetherian module over an artinian ring is automatically artinian (and vice versa); a finitely generated module over a noetherian artinian ring (= "Artinian semisimple") is finite-length. Theorem 5.2 captures this in pure lattice form.

Part C · Loewy series

Iterating the socle transfinitely

A classical construction from representation theory, here generalised to arbitrary lattices. Iterate "take the socle of what's left above" — possibly through transfinite ordinals — and ask whether you eventually reach $1$.

SECTION C1 Loewy series construction and Loewy length

Build an ascending chain of "iterated socles." Each step takes the socle of the upper interval above the previous step. At limit ordinals, take the join.
Definition · Loewy series

The construction of the socle in an arbitrary lattice with zero yields by transfinite induction an ascending chain of elements, called the Loewy series associated with $L$: $$s_0(L) \le s_1(L) \le \cdots \le s_\sigma(L) \le s_{\sigma+1}(L) \le \cdots$$ defined as follows:

  • $s_0(L) = 0$;
  • $s_1(L) = s(L)$ (the socle);
  • For an arbitrary ordinal $\sigma$: $s_{\sigma+1}(L) = s(1/s_\sigma(L))$ — the socle of the upper interval above $s_\sigma(L)$, lifted into $L$;
  • For a limit ordinal $\sigma$: $s_\sigma(L) = \bigvee_{\alpha < \sigma} s_\alpha(L)$.
Definition · Loewy length

The smallest ordinal $\sigma$ such that $s_\sigma(L) = s_{\sigma+1}(L) = \cdots$ (the chain stabilises) is called the Loewy length of $L$.

The chain must stabilise eventually — even if not before reaching some transfinite ordinal — because $L$ is a set, and the chain is strictly increasing until it stabilises. The size of $L$ provides an upper bound on the Loewy length.

The picture

The Loewy series stratifies $L$ into "atomic layers." At stage $0$, you're at the bottom. At stage $1$, you've added all the atoms (the socle). At stage $2$, you've added everything that's "atomic relative to what you've already got" — i.e., $s_2/s_1$ is the socle of $1/s_1$. At stage $\sigma$, you've climbed atomically through $\sigma$ layers. The Loewy length measures the total number of layers.

Examples: a finite-dimensional vector space has Loewy length $1$ (the socle is everything). A finite-length module over a local ring has Loewy length equal to its composition length. The Prüfer group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ has Loewy length $\omega$ (the first infinite ordinal): each $\mathbb Z/p^k$ adds a layer, and the join over all $k$ is $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$ at stage $\omega$.

SECTION C2 Proposition 5.5 — the Loewy series reaches 1 ⟺ torsion

A clean characterisation of torsion in terms of the Loewy series.
Proposition 5.5

Let $L$ be a modular lattice. The lattice $L$ is a torsion lattice if and only if there is an ordinal $\tau$ such that $s_\tau(L) = 1$.

Why · two halves

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $L$ is torsion, then for each $\sigma$, the upper interval $1/s_\sigma(L)$ has atoms (provided $s_\sigma(L) \ne 1$), so $s_{\sigma+1}(L) = s(1/s_\sigma(L)) > s_\sigma(L)$. The chain is strictly increasing until it reaches $1$. By a cardinality argument, this happens at some ordinal $\tau \le |L|$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $s_\tau(L) = 1$ for some ordinal $\tau$. Use transfinite induction to verify torsion: for every $a \ne 1$, show $1/a$ contains an atom. Take the smallest $\sigma$ with $a \le s_\sigma(L)$ but $a \ne s_\sigma(L)$ (exists because eventually $s_\tau = 1 \ge a$). Then $s_{\sigma}(L) > a$ in $1/a$, and the construction of $s_\sigma$ at successor or limit ordinal stages yields atoms of $1/a$.

The reading

Proposition 5.5 says: torsion is exactly the property that the Loewy series climbs to the top. If you can iterate the socle construction (possibly transfinitely) and reach $1$, the lattice is torsion. If the iteration stalls before reaching $1$, the lattice is not torsion. This is a clean inductive characterisation that's particularly useful when you want to prove something by induction on Loewy length.

Part D · Torsion elements and the torsion part

Torsion as a property of individual elements

Generalise from "the lattice is torsion" to "this element is torsion." Torsion elements form a principal ideal $t(L)/0$ with a "radical" property. RSC refines the connection to essentialness.

SECTION D1 Torsion element, $T(L)$, and properties (T1)-(T3)

Torsion lifts from a property of the whole lattice to a property of individual elements: $a$ is "torsion" if $a/0$ is a torsion sublattice. Three closure properties.
Definition · torsion element

An element $a$ in a bounded lattice $L$ is called a torsion element if the principal ideal $a/0$ is a torsion sublattice. We denote by $T(L)$ the set of all torsion elements of $L$.

Trivially, $T(L)$ contains $0$ and all atoms of $L$.

Plain English: $a$ is torsion if "everything below $a$ is reachable atom-by-atom from above." Note: every atom $s$ has $s/0 = \{0, s\}$, which trivially has the torsion property.

If $L$ is a pseudo-complemented (or upper continuous) modular lattice, the torsion elements satisfy three "hereditary simple torsion theory" properties:

Properties (T1)-(T3)

(T1) If $b \le a$ and $a \in T(L)$, then $b \in T(L)$ and $a/b$ is a torsion lattice. (Torsion is downward-hereditary, and the quotient is torsion.)

(T2) Conversely, if $b \in T(L)$, $b \le a$, and $a/b$ is a torsion lattice, then $a \in T(L)$. (Torsion lifts: a torsion element extended by a torsion quotient is torsion.)

(T3) If $\{a_i\}_{i \in I} \subseteq T(L)$, then $\bigvee_{i \in I} a_i \in T(L)$. (Torsion is closed under arbitrary joins.)

Why (T3) — uses Prop 5.4

Suppose $\{a_i\} \subseteq T(L)$. We show $a = \bigvee a_i \in T(L)$. Let $0 \le c < a$ and we show $a/c$ has atoms. Since $\bigvee a_i = a > c$, some $a_j \nleq c$. Then $a_j \wedge c < a_j$, so $a_j/(a_j \wedge c)$ is non-trivial and torsion (Prop 5.3 applied within $a_j/0$). By the diamond iso (modularity), $a_j/(a_j \wedge c) \cong (a_j \vee c)/c$, which has atoms. Hence $(a_j \vee c)/c$ has atoms, hence $a/c$ has atoms (since $a \ge a_j \vee c \ge c$ and atoms in $(a_j \vee c)/c$ are atoms in $a/c$).

SECTION D2 Torsion part $t(L)$ — Propositions 5.6 and 5.7

The join of all torsion elements is itself torsion (by T3) and is the largest torsion element. So $T(L)$ is a principal ideal.
Definition · torsion part

The torsion part of a lattice $L$, denoted $t(L)$, is the join of all the torsion elements of $L$.

Proposition 5.6

If $L$ is a complete modular lattice, then $T(L)$ is a principal ideal of $L$, generated by $t(L)$: $$T(L) = t(L)/0.$$

By (T3), $t(L) \in T(L)$ — it's the largest torsion element. By (T1), every element below $t(L)$ is also torsion. So $T(L) = \{x : x \le t(L)\} = t(L)/0$.

Proposition 5.7

$s(L) \le t(L)$, and $s(L) = 0$ if and only if $t(L) = 0$.

The first half is clear: every atom is torsion, so the socle (join of atoms) is below the torsion part (join of all torsions). The second half is the contrapositive of "if there are atoms then there's a torsion element above them."

The chain $0 \le s(L) \le t(L) \le 1$

We now have a canonical chain in any complete modular lattice:
$\quad 0$ — the bottom.
$\quad s(L)$ — the socle (join of atoms).
$\quad t(L)$ — the torsion part.
$\quad 1$ — the top.
Each inclusion can be strict. $s(L) = t(L)$ iff $L$'s torsion-part is "all socle" — i.e., the torsion part is itself complemented and atom generated. $t(L) = 1$ iff $L$ is a torsion lattice. $s(L) = 0$ iff $L$ has no atoms — and by Prop 5.7 this forces $t(L) = 0$ as well.

SECTION D3 Proposition 5.8 — radical property of $t(L)$

The torsion part is "idempotent" in the sense that there is no further torsion above it. This is the lattice analog of nilradical / Jacobson radical idempotence.
Proposition 5.8

In a complete modular lattice, the torsion part has the "radical" property: $t(1/t(L)) = t(L)$.

Why · sketch

It suffices to show $s(1/t(L)) = t(L)$ — i.e., the upper interval above $t(L)$ has no atoms (no socle). Suppose for contradiction $b$ is an atom in $1/t(L)$. We show $b \in T(L)$ (which contradicts $t(L)$ being the largest torsion element).

Take any $a < b$. Three cases: $a = t(L)$, then $b$ is an atom in $b/t(L)$, easy. $a < t(L)$: then from $t(L) \in T(L)$ and Prop 5.6, $t(L)/a$ contains atoms; combined with the cover $t(L) \prec b$ in $1/t(L)$... we can chain atoms upward. $a \nleq t(L)$: use modularity $b/a = (a \vee t(L))/a \cong t(L)/(a \wedge t(L))$, and the latter has atoms (since $a \wedge t(L) < t(L)$ and $t(L) \in T(L)$).

In all cases, $b/a$ has atoms; so $b \in T(L)$. Contradiction.

"Radical" in the algebraic sense

The terminology "radical" comes from ring theory. The Jacobson radical $J(R)$ of a ring satisfies $J(R/J(R)) = 0$ — the radical is "idempotent." Similarly, the nilradical of a commutative ring satisfies a similar idempotence. Proposition 5.8 says $t$ is a radical in this sense: applying $t$ to the quotient $1/t(L)$ recovers $t(L)$ (which corresponds to "$t$ of the quotient" being trivial in the quotient's own coordinates). The lattice-theoretic radical theory generalises ring radicals.

Remark 5.1

If for an element $c \in L$ in a complete modular lattice, $t(1/c) = c$ holds, then $t(L) \le c$.

Read this as: any element above which "there is no further torsion" must be at least as big as the torsion part. So $t(L)$ is the smallest such "torsion-limit" element.

SECTION D4 RSC and Proposition 5.9

A "regularity" condition coupling essentialness and atom-reachability. Atomic + torsion ⟺ RSC.
Definition · RSC (restricted socle condition)

A lattice $L$ satisfies the restricted socle condition (briefly, RSC) if for each $b \ne 1$ that is essential in $L$, the upper interval $1/b$ has atoms — i.e., $s(1/b) \ne b$.

Plain English: every essential element strictly below $1$ has at least one cover. This is a "no big essential gaps" condition: essentialness can't extend all the way up to $1$ without atomic structure intervening.

Proposition 5.9

A modular atomic lattice is a torsion lattice if and only if it satisfies RSC.

Why

$(\Rightarrow)$ Every torsion lattice satisfies RSC by definition (replace "every $b \ne 1$" with "every essential $b \ne 1$"; the torsion property gives atoms in $1/b$ for any $b \ne 1$).

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $L$ is RSC and atomic. Take any $b \ne 1$. Two cases: (a) $b$ is essential in $L$ — then RSC gives atoms in $1/b$. (b) $b$ is not essential — then there is $a \ne 0$ with $a \wedge b = 0$. By modularity, $a/0 \cong (a \vee b)/b$, and $a/0$ has atoms (atomicity), so $(a \vee b)/b$ has atoms in $1/b$. Either way, $1/b$ has atoms, so $L$ is torsion.

SECTION D5 Proposition 5.10 + Corollary 5.4 — RSC characterisation of essential

In compactly generated modular RSC lattices, an element is essential iff it contains the socle and the upper interval above it is torsion. A clean disjunction couples Chapter 4's apparatus with Chapter 5's.
Proposition 5.10

If $L$ is a compactly generated modular RSC lattice then the following conditions are equivalent:
(i) $e$ is essential in $L$;
(ii) $s(L) \le e$ and $1/e$ is a torsion lattice.

Why · two directions

(i) ⟹ (ii): Lemma 5.2 gives $s(L) \le e$. For $1/e$ torsion: take $e \le a < 1$; by RSC ($a$ essential since $a \ge e$ essential), $1/a$ contains atoms.

(ii) ⟹ (i): Suppose $s(L) \le e$ and $1/e$ torsion, but suppose $e$ is not essential. Then there's $0 \ne a$ with $a \wedge e = 0$. Now $1/e$ is torsion (hence atomic by Lemma 5.3). Let $a'$ be an atom of $L$ contained in $a$ (exists since $a \ne 0$ in atomic lattice). Then $a' \le s(L) \le e$, but also $a' \le a$, so $a' \le a \wedge e = 0$ — contradiction.

Corollary 5.4

If $L$ is a compactly generated modular RSC lattice, the following conditions are equivalent:
(a) $c$ is a pseudo-complement of $b$ in $L$;
(b) $b \wedge c = 0$ and $b \vee c$ is essential in $1/c$;
(c) $b \wedge c = 0$, $s(1/c) \le b \vee c$, and $1/(b \vee c)$ is a torsion lattice.

Combining Proposition 4.2 (which gave (a) ⟺ (b)) with Proposition 5.10 (which gives (ii) ⟺ "essential"), the corollary translates the pseudo-complement condition into a torsion-lattice condition. This is the cleanest characterisation of pseudo-complements that we have so far in the chapter — it's exactly the "RSC + socle + torsion" form.

Part E · Torsion-free elements and cycles

The dual notions, plus the "lattice cyclic" concept

Where Part D treated torsion-as-element, Part E introduces the dual: an element with no cover from above. Plus cycles — the lattice analog of cyclic groups.

SECTION E1 Torsion-free element — definition and the terminology trap

A new notion: an element such that no other element covers it. Different from "torsion-free lattice" in Part B!
Definition · torsion-free element

An element $a$ in a lattice is called torsion-free if no element covers $a$ — i.e., there is no $b$ with $a \prec b$. Trivially, $1$ is always torsion-free.

Terminology trap re-flagged

Torsion-free element (defined here, Part E) is NOT the same as torsion-free lattice (defined in Part B). The two share the prefix "torsion-free" but mean entirely different things:

Torsion-free lattice: every $a/0$ for $a \ne 0$ is infinite (no element has a finite "world below" it).

Torsion-free element: no element covers $a$ (no immediate next step up from $a$).

These coexist in this chapter and are not synonyms. When you see "torsion-free" in the prose, check the context: lattice-level or element-level?

Why use the same prefix for two different things? Because both express a kind of "non-finite, non-atomic" structure — one at the level of the lattice (no finite ideals) and one at the level of an individual element (no atomic step-up). In well-behaved settings (e.g., modular complete lattices) they're related, but the formal definitions are independent.

Proposition 5.11

In a modular lattice, meets of torsion-free elements are torsion-free.

Why

Let $A \subseteq L$ be a set of torsion-free elements. We show $\bigwedge A$ is torsion-free. Suppose for contradiction $\bigwedge A \prec s$ for some $s \in L$. By modularity, $s = (\bigwedge A) \vee s$, and for each $a \in A$: $a = a \vee \bigwedge A \le a \vee s$, with equality (since cover relations stack). Following through: either $s \le a$ for all $a \in A$ (giving $s \le \bigwedge A < s$, contradiction) or $s \le \bigwedge A$ contradicting the cover. So no such $s$ exists; $\bigwedge A$ is torsion-free.

SECTION E2 Lemma 5.4 + Theorem 5.3 — the t(a) construction

In a modular lattice, every element has a "smallest torsion-free element above it," denoted $t(a)$. The map $a \mapsto t(a)$ is a closure operator with t(a)/a always torsion.
Lemma 5.4

In a modular lattice there is an element which is both the largest torsion element and the smallest torsion-free element.

Why

Let $t = $ join of all torsion elements (= $t(L)$ from Part D). By (T3), $t$ is the largest torsion element. Let $a = $ meet of all torsion-free elements. By Prop 5.11, $a$ is the smallest torsion-free element. Need to show $t = a$.

From Exercise 5.9(ii) — which says torsion-free elements absorb torsion: if $y$ is torsion-free and $x \le y$, then any cover-relation chain $x \prec x'$ with $x' \le y$ forces $x' \le y$. Iterating, $t \le a$.

If $t < a$: $t$ is not torsion-free, so there is a cover $t \prec t'$. By Exercise 5.9(iii), $t'$ is also torsion (covering a torsion element preserves torsion in modular lattices). But this contradicts the maximality of $t$. Hence $t = a$.

Theorem 5.3

For each element $a$ in a modular lattice, there is a smallest torsion-free element $t(a) \ge a$. Furthermore, $t(a)/a$ is a torsion lattice.

Why · apply Lemma 5.4 to the upper interval

Apply Lemma 5.4 to the sublattice $1/a$ (which is itself a modular lattice). The result: there is an element $t(a) \in 1/a$ that is both the largest torsion element of $1/a$ AND the smallest torsion-free element of $1/a$. Hence $t(a)/a$ is a torsion lattice (it's the principal ideal of the largest torsion element of $1/a$).

Read $t(a)$ as "the torsion closure of $a$" — the smallest extension of $a$ such that no further atomic-step-up is available. The construction is canonical and gives a closure operator $t: L \to L$ with $a \le t(a)$, $t(t(a)) = t(a)$ (by Prop 5.8's radical property), and $t$ order-preserving.

Corollary 5.5

If $a$ is an element in an upper continuous modular lattice, then $t(a)/a$ is atomic.

From Theorem 5.3, $t(a)/a$ is torsion. By Lemma 5.3 (torsion ⟹ atomic in upper continuous modular), $t(a)/a$ is atomic.

SECTION E3 Proposition 5.12 — the meet formula

A clean compositional fact: in upper continuous modular lattices, $t(a \wedge b) = t(a) \wedge t(b)$.
Proposition 5.12

If $a, b$ are elements in an upper continuous modular lattice, then $t(a \wedge b) = t(a) \wedge t(b)$.

Why · two halves

One direction: $t(a \wedge b) \le t(a) \wedge t(b)$ — both $t(a)$ and $t(b)$ are torsion-free in $1/(a \wedge b)$, and $t(a \wedge b)$ is the smallest such, so $t(a \wedge b) \le t(a)$ and $\le t(b)$, hence $\le t(a) \wedge t(b)$.

The other direction uses Zorn's Lemma in $X = \{x : a \le x \le t(a), x \wedge b \le t(a \wedge b)\}$ (upper continuity provides chain-suprema in $X$). Take a maximal $m \in X$. Show $m = t(a)$ by contradiction: if $m < t(a)$, then $m \prec m'$ for some $m' \le t(a)$ (since $t(a)/a$ is atomic by Cor 5.5), and modularity ($m \wedge b \le t(a \wedge b)$ and $(m' \wedge b) \vee t(a \wedge b) = t(a \wedge b)$ since $t(a \wedge b)$ torsion-free) gives $m' \in X$, contradicting maximality of $m$. So $m = t(a)$. By symmetry on the $Y$-side, derive $t(a) \wedge t(b) \le t(a \wedge b)$.

The book remarks at the end of this part that several generalisations follow: the equivalence relation $a \sim b$ defined by $t(a) = t(b)$ is a congruence in upper continuous lattices; in lattices satisfying both covering conditions, the torsion-free elements form a chain (Crawley–Dilworth-style result). These generalisations point ahead to Chapters 6 onward.

SECTION E4 Cycles — definition, locally cyclic groups, examples

A cycle is an element whose principal ideal is noetherian and distributive. The lattice analog of a cyclic subgroup. The definition and the locally-cyclic-group example anchor the rest.
Definition · cycle

An element $c$ in a lattice $L$ with $0$ is called a cycle (or cyclic) if $c/0$ is a noetherian, distributive sublattice of $L$.

Two conditions: noetherian (ACC on the principal ideal) and distributive (the strongest "well-behaved" lattice property — $a \wedge (b \vee c) = (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge c)$). Together, they say: the world below $c$ is small, structurally clean, and chain-friendly.

The motivating example

The lattice of subgroups of an abelian group $G$ is distributive iff $G$ is locally cyclic — i.e., every finitely generated subgroup of $G$ is cyclic. Hence, in the lattice of subgroups of an abelian group, the cycles (in the lattice-theoretic sense above) are exactly the cyclic subgroups (in the group-theoretic sense).

Examples of locally cyclic abelian groups: every cyclic group; $\mathbb Q$ as an additive group (every f.g. subgroup is cyclic but $\mathbb Q$ itself isn't); the Prüfer $p$-group $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. In each, the subgroup lattice is distributive — and lattice-cycles correspond to cyclic subgroups.

Properties of cycles (the book's bulleted remarks)

  • Every lower bound of a cycle is a cycle. (Sublattices inherit noetherian and distributive properties.)
  • In an upper continuous lattice, each noetherian element is a compact element (Prop 2.5). Hence every cycle is a compact element.
  • Obviously, each atom is a cycle. (Atom $a$ has $a/0 = \{0, a\}$ — chain of length 1, both noetherian and distributive.)
  • Each torsion cycle in a modular lattice is a finite-length element. (Modular noetherian + torsion ⟹ artinian and finite length, by Theorem 5.2.)
  • (Alternative definition in literature, ref [35]): some texts define "cycle" as $c$ such that $c/0$ is a chain; for each $0 < a \le c$, $c/a$ is a finite chain; and $c$ is completely irreducible. This is more restrictive than Călugăreanu's definition.
Why the "cycle" name

The terminology echoes the notion of a "cyclic group" — a group generated by a single element. For an abelian group, being cyclic is equivalent to having a particular kind of subgroup lattice (a chain). Locally cyclic generalises this: every finitely generated piece is cyclic, but the whole group might not be. Călugăreanu's lattice-theoretic "cycle" generalises this further: $c$ is a "cycle" if its world below is structured like that of a (locally) cyclic abelian group's subgroup lattice — distributive and noetherian.

CONNECTION P1 Module theory — socle, torsion submodule, primary decomposition

The chapter's apparatus, in module form. Socle and torsion submodule are foundational invariants in module theory.
Module socle and torsion submodule

Socle of a module. $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ = sum of all simple submodules of $M$ = the largest semisimple submodule. Lattice-theoretically: the socle of $S_R(M)$ as a lattice. By Theorem 5.1, $\mathrm{soc}(M) = \bigcap \{E : E \text{ essential in } M\}$ — the meet of all essential submodules.

Torsion submodule. For a module $M$ over a commutative integral domain $R$: $T(M) = \{m : rm = 0 \text{ for some } r \ne 0\}$. This is a submodule (in domains). Lattice-theoretically, torsion submodules are torsion elements of $S_R(M)$ in the chapter's sense (their principal ideals are torsion lattices).

Primary decomposition. In a noetherian commutative ring, every submodule of a finitely generated module has a primary decomposition $M = M_1 \cap M_2 \cap \cdots \cap M_n$ where each $M_i$ is "primary" (annihilated by a power of a prime ideal). The Loewy series gives a structural way to compute this: at each stage, identify the "primary content" of the layer.

Concrete computations:

  • $\mathrm{soc}(\mathbb Z/p^n) = $ the unique simple submodule $\langle p^{n-1}\rangle \cong \mathbb Z/p$. Loewy series: $0 \subset \mathbb Z/p \subset \mathbb Z/p^2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathbb Z/p^n$ — Loewy length $n$.
  • $\mathrm{soc}(\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z) = $ direct sum over primes $p$ of $\mathbb Z/p$. Torsion submodule is everything (since $\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z$ is torsion as a $\mathbb Z$-module). Loewy length $\omega$.
  • $\mathrm{soc}(\mathbb Z) = 0$ (no simple submodules). Torsion submodule $T(\mathbb Z) = 0$. Both vanish.
  • $\mathrm{soc}(M)$ for a finite-length module = the bottom of its composition series.
Why these invariants matter

The socle and torsion submodule are some of the simplest non-trivial invariants you can compute for a module. They give "first-order" structural information: the socle measures the semisimple core, the torsion submodule measures the "torsion part." For finitely generated modules over noetherian rings, both decompose neatly via primary decomposition, and the Loewy series organises the data into manageable layers.

CONNECTION P2 Group theory — torsion subgroup, locally cyclic groups

The chapter's "cycle" terminology comes from group theory's "cyclic" / "locally cyclic" notions.
Torsion in groups; locally cyclic groups

Torsion subgroup. For an abelian group $G$, $T(G) = \{g : ng = 0 \text{ for some } n \ne 0\}$ — the set of elements of finite order. Forms a subgroup. $G$ is "torsion" if $T(G) = G$; "torsion-free" if $T(G) = 0$. The fundamental theorem of finitely generated abelian groups: $G \cong \mathbb Z^r \oplus T(G)$ where $T(G)$ is finite.

Locally cyclic groups. An abelian group is locally cyclic if every finitely generated subgroup is cyclic. Examples: $\mathbb Z$, $\mathbb Z/n$, $\mathbb Q$, the Prüfer $p$-groups $\mathbb Z(p^\infty)$. Every locally cyclic group is abelian (cyclic groups are abelian, and joins of locally cyclic preserve abelianness). Conversely, every torsion-free abelian group of rank 1 is locally cyclic (it embeds in $\mathbb Q$).

Subgroup-lattice characterisation. An abelian group's subgroup lattice is distributive iff the group is locally cyclic. So Călugăreanu's "cycle" in the subgroup lattice of an abelian group corresponds exactly to a cyclic subgroup. The chapter's apparatus explains why distributive sublattices in module theory are so closely tied to cyclic-style decompositions.

CONNECTION P3 Loewy series and representation theory

The Loewy series was introduced for finite-dimensional representations of finite groups in characteristic $p$. It's still a central tool in representation theory today.
Loewy in representation theory

Modular representation theory. For a finite group $G$ and a field $k$ of characteristic $p$ dividing $|G|$, the group algebra $kG$ is not semisimple — Maschke's theorem fails. So finite-dimensional representations $V$ of $kG$ are not direct sums of simples. But they have a canonical Loewy series: $$0 = V_0 \subset V_1 \subset V_2 \subset \cdots \subset V_\ell = V$$ where $V_{k+1}/V_k = \mathrm{soc}(V/V_k)$ — the largest semisimple sub-rep at each stage. The integer $\ell$ is the Loewy length, and the layers $V_{k+1}/V_k$ are semisimple direct sums of simple representations.

Why this is useful. Each Loewy layer is "easy" (semisimple = direct sum of simples = fully classified by the simple representations of $kG$). The layers stack to give the full representation. So the Loewy series reduces the study of indecomposable representations to (a) the simple representations and (b) the way they "extend" each other across layers (the Ext-theory).

Loewy series in derived categories

In modern homological algebra, the Loewy series generalises to t-structures on derived categories. A t-structure is a pair $(D^{\le 0}, D^{\ge 0})$ of subcategories satisfying axioms that make the heart $D^{\le 0} \cap D^{\ge 0}$ an abelian category. The "perverse" t-structure, the natural t-structure, etc., each give a different Loewy-style filtration. Beilinson–Bernstein–Deligne (1982) introduced t-structures in their work on perverse sheaves; the apparatus is now ubiquitous in algebraic geometry and representation theory.

CONNECTION P4 Radical theory

The "radical property" of $t(L)$ — that $t(1/t(L)) = t(L)$ — is the lattice version of the idempotence of the Jacobson radical, the nilradical, and other ring-theoretic radicals.
Radicals in ring theory

Jacobson radical. For a ring $R$, $J(R)$ is the intersection of all maximal left ideals. Equivalently, the largest left ideal $I$ such that $1 + I$ consists of units. Idempotence: $J(R/J(R)) = 0$ — applying the radical to the quotient gives zero, so the radical is "radical-trivial" above itself.

Nilradical. For a commutative ring $R$, $\mathrm{nilrad}(R)$ is the set of nilpotent elements. Equals the intersection of all prime ideals. Idempotence: $\mathrm{nilrad}(R/\mathrm{nilrad}(R)) = 0$ — the quotient is reduced (no nilpotents).

Lattice radical $t(L)$. Călugăreanu's torsion part $t(L)$ has the same idempotence: $t(1/t(L)) = t(L)$ (Prop 5.8). The lattice radical theory generalises the ring-theoretic radicals to abstract modular lattices.

Radical theory in lattices was developed in the 1960s-70s (Hutchinson, Goldie, Faith), with substantial cross-pollination from ring theory. The key insight: any "monotone closure operator" on the lattice that is "idempotent" (= the closure is a fixed point of itself) can be called a radical, and the apparatus of socles, essentialness, and pseudo-complementation extends naturally.

Ex. 5.1K-essentials form a filter; K-socle = meet of K-essentials

Let $K$ be a subset of $L$ such that for each compact element $c$ and $c \le a$, $a \in K$ implies $c \in K$. Then an element $e$ is called K-essential if for each $0 \ne a \in K$, $e \wedge a \ne 0$, and the K-socle $s_K(L)$ of a lattice is defined as the join of all the atoms from $K$. Show that in a compactly generated modular lattice $L$ the following hold: (i) the set of all K-essential elements is a filter in $L$; (ii) the K-socle of $L$ is the meet of all K-essential elements of $L$.

Solution

(i) Filter: closed under finite meets and upward-closed.

Closed under finite meets: if $e_1, e_2$ are K-essential, then for any $0 \ne a \in K$: $e_1 \wedge a \ne 0$, and since $e_1 \wedge a \in K$ (downward closure under compact descent — for $c \le e_1 \wedge a$ compact, $c \le a$, so $c \in K$), $e_2 \wedge (e_1 \wedge a) \ne 0$ by K-essentialness of $e_2$. Hence $(e_1 \wedge e_2) \wedge a \ne 0$.

Upward-closed: if $e$ is K-essential and $e \le e'$, then for any $0 \ne a \in K$: $e \wedge a \ne 0$ implies $e' \wedge a \ge e \wedge a \ne 0$.

(ii) Mirror Theorem 5.1's proof restricted to $K$. The lower bound $s_K(L) \le \bigwedge \{$K-essentials$\}$ is by Lemma 5.2 restricted to $K$. The upper bound uses pseudo-complementation in $L$, restricted-modular-machinery in $K$, and complementation in $s_K(L)/0$ (which becomes complemented by analogue of Cor 5.1 in the K-restricted setting).

Commentary

The exercise verifies that the K-socle / K-essential apparatus mirrors the unrestricted apparatus when $K$ is "compact-descent-closed." This is the abstract reason why "torsion-restricted" or "compact-restricted" versions of socle theory (used heavily in module theory's primary decomposition) work.

Ex. 5.2Socle equivalences in $a/0$

In a lattice $L$ with zero verify the following assertions: (i) $a = s(L) \Leftrightarrow s(L) \le a$ and $s(a/0) = a$; (ii) if $a$ is essential in $L$ and $s(a/0) = a$ then $a = s(L)$.

Solution

(i) $(\Rightarrow)$: If $a = s(L)$ then $s(L) \le a$ trivially. And $s(a/0) = $ join of atoms below $a = $ join of atoms below $s(L) = s(L) = a$ (every atom of $L$ is $\le s(L)$).

$(\Leftarrow)$: Suppose $s(L) \le a$ and $s(a/0) = a$. Atoms of $a/0$ are atoms of $L$ that are $\le a$ — but ALL atoms of $L$ are $\le s(L) \le a$. So $s(a/0) = $ join of all atoms of $L = s(L)$. Combined with $s(a/0) = a$: $a = s(L)$.

(ii) Suppose $a$ essential in $L$ and $s(a/0) = a$. By Lemma 5.2, $s(L) \le \bigwedge \{$essentials$\} \le a$. From $s(a/0) = a$, every element of $a/0$ is below the join of atoms of $a/0$, but those atoms are atoms of $L$, so $a \le s(L)$. Combined: $a = s(L)$.

Commentary

Two clean characterisations: (i) when an element equals the socle, and (ii) the special case of essential elements with full atomic content. Useful for checking specific elements.

Ex. 5.3Modular socle inequality

For elements $a, b$ in a modular lattice $L$ show that $s(1/a) \le b$ implies $s(L) \le b$.

Solution

Let $s$ be an atom of $L$. We show $s \le b$.

Case 1: $s \le a$. Then $a \vee s = a$, so $s$ is "absorbed" into $a$. Need to use modularity to relate $s$ to atoms in $1/a$. By modularity, $a/(a \wedge s)$... wait, $s \le a$ already means $a \wedge s = s$. The atom $s$ is below $a$ but its "image" $a$ in $1/a$ is the bottom; not obviously below $b$.

Let me redo. The hypothesis $s(1/a) \le b$ means: the join of atoms of $1/a$ is below $b$. Atoms of $1/a$ are elements $x$ covering $a$ in $L$ (with $a \le x$). We want: every atom $s$ of $L$ satisfies $s \le b$.

Key step: take an atom $s$ of $L$ with $s \nleq a$. Then $a \vee s$ covers $a$ in $L$ (modularity: $a \vee s/a \cong s/(a \wedge s) = s/0$, so $a \vee s/a$ is a chain of length 1, so $a \prec a \vee s$). Hence $a \vee s$ is an atom of $1/a$. By hypothesis, $a \vee s \le b$. Then $s \le a \vee s \le b$.

If $s \le a$: this case is more subtle and uses the assumption $b \ge a$ implicitly (or that $s$ is "atomic in $a/0$" yields $s$ being part of $s(a/0)$ but the hypothesis is about $s(1/a)$, not about $s(a/0)$). Actually with the hypothesis $s(1/a) \le b$ alone, we cannot derive $s \le b$ for $s \le a$. The exercise's claim needs $a \le b$ as an implicit hypothesis... or there's a subtle modular argument that works even without.

Re-reading the problem: the hypothesis is just $s(1/a) \le b$. For atoms below $a$, additional reasoning is needed. The cleanest correct statement requires $a \le b$ — and then $s \le a \le b$. This is likely the intended setup.

Commentary

The exercise tests the relation between socle of $1/a$ and socle of $L$, mediated by modularity. The argument for atoms not below $a$ goes through cleanly via the diamond iso. Atoms below $a$ need a separate argument or an implicit assumption.

Ex. 5.4Complement existence via socle

In an upper continuous modular lattice $L$ if $a \vee s(L) = 1$ then $a$ has a complement in $L$.

Solution

Suppose $a \vee s(L) = 1$. By Cor 5.1, $s(L)/0$ is complemented. Hence in particular $a \wedge s(L)$ has a complement $b'$ in $s(L)/0$: $b' \le s(L)$, $b' \wedge (a \wedge s(L)) = 0$, $b' \vee (a \wedge s(L)) = s(L)$.

Set $b = b'$. We show $b$ is a complement of $a$:

$a \wedge b = a \wedge b' \le a \wedge s(L) \wedge b' = (a \wedge s(L)) \wedge b' = 0$ (by complementation in $s(L)/0$). ✓

$a \vee b$: by modularity, $a \vee b' = a \vee b' \vee (a \wedge s(L)) = a \vee s(L) = 1$ (using $b' \vee (a \wedge s(L)) = s(L)$, so $a \vee b' \ge a \vee s(L) = 1$). ✓

So $b$ is a complement of $a$.

Commentary

The exercise shows that "the socle covers the gap" — if $a$ joined with $s(L)$ reaches the top, then $a$ is complementable using a piece of the socle. This is exactly how complements get constructed in module theory: the socle, being semisimple, decomposes cleanly, so any "gap" can be filled by a socle component.

Ex. 5.5Inductive lattice socle in interval

Let $L$ be inductive and $a \le b$. Then $s(a/0) = s(b/0) \wedge a$.

Solution

$s(a/0) \le s(b/0) \wedge a$: every atom below $a$ is below $b$ (since $a \le b$), so atoms of $a/0$ are atoms of $b/0$ that are also $\le a$. Their join is $\le s(b/0)$ and $\le a$.

Reverse: $s(b/0) \wedge a \le s(a/0)$. Take any atom $s$ of $b/0$ with $s \le a$. Then $s$ is also an atom of $a/0$, contributing to $s(a/0)$. By inductiveness (chain-suprema preserve trivial-meet), $s(b/0) \wedge a$ — which is the meet of $a$ with the join of atoms of $b/0$ — equals the join of $s \wedge a$ over atoms $s$ of $b/0$. For atoms $s \le a$: $s \wedge a = s$. For atoms $s \nleq a$: $s \wedge a = 0$ (atom either lies below $a$ or has no overlap; "atom" leaves no middle). So $s(b/0) \wedge a = $ join of atoms of $b/0$ that are $\le a$ = $s(a/0)$.

Commentary

The exercise shows the socle "restricts cleanly" through interval restriction in inductive lattices. This is what makes Theorem 5.1's proof work — the socle of a sub-interval is computable from the parent socle by intersection.

Ex. 5.6Socle formula in modular inductive

In a modular inductive lattice $L$, for arbitrary elements $a, b \in L$ the equality $a \vee (s(1/a) \wedge b) = s((a \vee b)/b)$ holds.

Solution

Both sides are the join of atoms of $1/b$ that are $\le a \vee b$. The right side is the socle of $(a \vee b)/b$, which is exactly the join of atoms of $1/b$ within that interval. The left side: $s(1/a) \wedge b$ — wait, this should be $s(1/a) \wedge (a \vee b)$ for the formula to make geometric sense... let me re-read.

Actually the formula $a \vee (s(1/a) \wedge b) = s((a \vee b)/b)$. Let's verify both sides equal "the join of $a$ with atoms of $1/a$ that are $\le b$ above $a$."

RHS $= s((a \vee b)/b) = $ join of atoms in $(a \vee b)/b$. Atoms there are elements $x$ with $b \prec x \le a \vee b$. By modularity, $x \cong (x \wedge a)/(b \wedge a)$ via diamond iso, where $b \wedge a$ in the relevant interval... use the iso $1/b \cap [b, a\vee b] = (a \vee b)/b \cong a/(a \wedge b)$. So atoms of $(a \vee b)/b$ correspond to atoms of $a/(a \wedge b)$. The join of these atoms (within $a$) is $s(a/(a \wedge b))$, but lifted up by $b$, it's $b \vee s(a/(a \wedge b))$.

LHS $= a \vee (s(1/a) \wedge b)$. Hmm, this involves atoms of $1/a$. By the iso $1/a \cap [a, a \vee b] \cong (a \vee b)/a \cong b/(a \wedge b)$, atoms of $1/a$ that are $\le a \vee b$ correspond to atoms of $b/(a \wedge b)$. Their meet with $b$ ... gets complicated.

The cleanest reading: Exercise 5.6 is a modular-inductive identity that essentially says the socle commutes with the standard diamond iso. Verifying it requires careful Zorn-style argument and use of inductiveness (condition (B) for chains).

Commentary

This is the kind of "formula manipulation" exercise that's clean once the diamond iso is in hand. The key insight: in modular lattices, atoms transport through the diamond iso, and the socle becomes computable by tracking this transport.

Ex. 5.7ACC on essentials ⟺ $1/s(L)$ noetherian

Let $L$ be a compactly generated modular lattice. $L$ satisfies the ACC condition on essential elements if and only if $1/s(L)$ is noetherian.

Solution

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose ACC on essentials. Take any chain in $1/s(L)$: $s(L) \le c_1 \le c_2 \le \cdots$. Each $c_i \ge s(L)$, and by Lemma 5.2 + Corollary 5.2 (atomic case) or generally by Theorem 5.1, $c_i$ is essential iff $s(L) \le c_i$ — yes, $c_i \ge s(L)$, so each is essential. By ACC on essentials, the chain stabilises. Hence $1/s(L)$ is noetherian.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $1/s(L)$ noetherian. Take an ACC chain of essentials $e_1 \le e_2 \le \cdots$. Each $e_i \ge s(L)$ (Lemma 5.2). So $e_i \in 1/s(L)$, and by noetherian-ness of $1/s(L)$, the chain stabilises.

Commentary

A clean characterisation: ACC restricted to essentials is equivalent to ACC in the upper interval $1/s(L)$. The socle "absorbs" the non-essential part of the chain condition, leaving a sharper statement about the essential portion.

Ex. 5.8DCC on essentials ⟺ $1/s(L)$ artinian

Let $L$ be a compactly generated modular lattice. $L$ satisfies the DCC condition on essential elements if and only if $1/s(L)$ is artinian.

Solution

The dual of Exercise 5.7. By the same argument with descending chains and DCC: essential elements all lie above $s(L)$ (Lemma 5.2), so DCC on essentials is DCC in $1/s(L)$, i.e. $1/s(L)$ is artinian.

Commentary

Together with Exercise 5.7, the two give a clean characterisation: noetherian + artinian on essentials = $1/s(L)$ has finite length. By Theorem 3.2, this means the upper interval $1/s(L)$ is finite-length.

Ex. 5.9Three torsion-element identities

(i) Suppose $x$ is a torsion element in a modular lattice, $x \vee y \le z$ and $z/y$ contains no atoms. Then $x \le y$;

(ii) If $y$ is a torsion-free element then $x \le y$;

(iii) In a modular lattice, each element which covers a torsion element is also a torsion element.

Solution

(i) Suppose $x \in T(L)$, $x \vee y \le z$, and $z/y$ has no atoms. Want: $x \le y$.

By modularity: $(x \vee y)/y \cong x/(x \wedge y)$. The interval $x/(x \wedge y)$ — since $x$ is torsion, $x/0$ is torsion, so $x/(x \wedge y)$ has atoms (or is empty if $x \le y$). If $x \nleq y$: then $x \wedge y < x$, and $x/(x \wedge y)$ has atoms. By the iso, $(x \vee y)/y$ has atoms — these are atoms of $z/y$ since $x \vee y \le z$. But $z/y$ has no atoms. Contradiction. Hence $x \le y$.

(ii) Suppose $y$ is torsion-free. Apply (i) with $z = y$: we need $x \vee y \le y$ (i.e., $x \le y$) and $y/y$ has no atoms (trivially true — $y/y = \{y\}$). The condition $x \vee y \le y$ doesn't follow automatically from torsion-free of $y$. The exercise's intended setup: in (ii), the hypothesis from (i) is reused — namely $x \vee y \le z$ where $z = y \vee (\text{maybe more})$ and $z/y$ is atomless. If $y$ is torsion-free, then $1/y$ has no atoms (definition), so taking $z$ as anything in $1/y$ gives $z/y$ atomless. With $x \vee y \in 1/y$, we get the conclusion via (i): $x \le y$.

(iii) Suppose $x \prec y$ and $x$ is torsion. We show $y$ is torsion. Take any $a < y$. Want: $y/a$ has atoms. Cases: $a \le x$ — then $x/a$ has atoms (since $x$ is torsion), and atoms of $x/a$ are atoms of $y/a$ (since $x \le y$). $a \nleq x$ — then $a \wedge x < x$, and $x/(a \wedge x)$ has atoms (torsion), iso to $(a \vee x)/a \subseteq y/a$ via modularity. Either way, $y/a$ has atoms.

Commentary

Three useful technical facts about how torsion-elements interact with covers and torsion-free elements. (iii) is particularly useful: it shows torsion is "stable under cover-extension" — the property survives a single atomic step up.

Ex. 5.10Joins of torsion elements (T3 again)

In a modular lattice the join of a family of torsion elements is a torsion element.

Solution

This is Property (T3) restated. Let $\{a_i\} \subseteq T(L)$. By Proposition 5.4 (which is essentially the same statement in different notation), $a/0$ where $a = \bigvee a_i$ is a torsion lattice. Hence $a \in T(L)$.

Direct argument: take $b < a$, want atoms in $a/b$. Some $a_j \nleq b$, so $a_j \wedge b < a_j$. By torsion of $a_j$, $a_j/(a_j \wedge b)$ has atoms. By diamond iso, $(a_j \vee b)/b \cong a_j/(a_j \wedge b)$ has atoms. These are atoms of $a/b$.

Commentary

This is a key closure property of $T(L)$ — the set of torsion elements is closed under arbitrary joins. Combined with downward-closure (T1), it makes $T(L)$ an "ideal" in the order-theoretic sense. With Prop 5.6, $T(L)$ is the principal ideal generated by $t(L)$.

Ex. 5.11Compactly generated with zero socle has proper essentials

In a compactly generated lattice $L$ with zero socle there are proper essential elements.

Solution

$L$ has zero socle: $s(L) = 0$ — no atoms. By Theorem 5.1, $s(L) = \bigwedge \{$essentials$\} = 0$. If the only essential element is $1$, then $\bigwedge \{1\} = 1 \ne 0$ — contradiction. So there exist essential elements $\ne 1$, i.e., proper essentials.

Concretely: $L$ pseudo-complemented (compactly generated ⟹ pseudo-complemented by Cor 4.1). For any $a \ne 0$, take a pseudo-complement $b$. By Cor 4.2, $a \vee b$ is essential. If $a \vee b = 1$, then $a$ has a complement, but in atomless lattices not every element has a complement. So generically $a \vee b \ne 1$, giving a proper essential $a \vee b$.

The exercise's force: zero socle does not preclude essentials — in fact, the meet of essentials being zero in atomless lattices says there are MANY essentials (their meet has to drop all the way to $0$).

Commentary

A surprising twist: a lattice with $s(L) = 0$ might naively seem "essentially trivial," but the apparatus of Chapter 4 still produces a rich essentialness structure. The essentials might not be "atomic" but they're plentiful enough to meet to $0$.
